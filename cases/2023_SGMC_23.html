Introduction

1       The Accused, a 36-year-old Malaysian male, was employed as a lorry driver to transport construction materials into Singapore from Ipoh, Malaysia, about three times a week. On 18 October 2022, at about 7.45am, he was arrested at the Tuas Checkpoint Arrival Cargo Zone 5 (“ACZ 5”) for importing into Singapore 26 dogs and 1 cat without a licence issued by the Direct-General, Animal Health and Welfare pursuant to the Animals and Birds Act 1965 (“ABA”). According to the Prosecution, this has been the most egregious case of live animal smuggling encountered by the National Parks Board (“NParks”) to date.

2       The Accused was charged in court on 19 October 2022 and remains in remand since then as he was unable to raise bail.

3       On 29 March 2023, the Accused pleaded guilty to ten charges for illegal importation of an animal under s 8(1) of the ABA and ten charges for failing to take reasonable steps to ensure that an animal is not confined and conveyed in a manner that subjected the said animal to unnecessary suffering whilst being transported from Malaysia to Singapore under s 41C(1)(a)(iii) of the ABA (“causing unnecessary suffering to an animal during conveyance offence”). The 20 proceeded charges were out of a total of 54 charges faced by him. He consented to having the remaining 34 charges being taken into consideration (“TIC”) for the purpose of sentencing.

4       After hearing both parties on their respective submissions on sentence, I reserved judgment.

For an offence committed under s 41C(1)(a)(iii) read with s 40C(2) of the ABA, the punishment prescribed by law is as set out in s 41(C)(3)(b)(i) of the ABA for a first offender and is the same as that under s 8(3) of the ABA.

Statement of facts

7       The Accused admitted to the Statement of Facts (“SOF”) dated 29 March 2023 without qualification.

8       In essence, the Accused was paid RM340 per delivery from Ipoh, Malaysia to Singapore. Prior to returning to Ipoh, the Accused at times would stop by Johor Bahru to pick up construction cargo for his employer. The Accused would be paid RM340 for his return trip from Singapore to Ipoh. On all his deliveries into and out of Singapore, the Accused drove a Malaysia-registered lorry bearing registration number “AHJ 9392” (“the Accused’s lorry”).

9       Investigations revealed that sometime in 2021, while having lunch at a food stall in Johor Bahru, the Accused was approached by an Indian man who introduced himself as “Dido”. Dido asked the Accused if he was interested in smuggling animals into Singapore and offered the Accused RM250 per live animal that was successfully smuggled. The Accused would not be paid for any animal that died during the transportation process. The Accused agreed to the arrangement.

10     On the first occasion of bringing the animals into Singapore in 2021, the Accused was given specific guidance from Dido and another Chinese man known only to him as “Komo” for the entire delivery process. Upon his return to Malaysia after the first successful delivery, the Accused was paid RM1,000 by Dido. Subsequently, the Accused agreed to continue importing animals on a regular basis for Dido and Komo.

11     After making his first delivery, the Accused would adopt the following arrangement when transporting the animals from Malaysia into Singapore for Dido and Komo:

(a)     During each trip, the Accused would meet Dido and Komo at Bukit Indah highway in Johor Bahru. Their meeting time varied between 2am – 5am;

(b)     The animals would already be pre-packed into either boxes, containers, or zipped laundry/nylon bags and they would be transferred from Komo’s Malaysia-registered car onto the Accused’s lorry. The Accused would then place the animals in the compartments located underneath the sleeping platform behind the driver’s and passenger’s seats as well as an overhead compartment located above the sleeping platform. These compartments were obscured from sight;

(c)     After the animals were loaded and concealed in the Accused’s lorry, the Accused would proceed towards Tuas Checkpoint. Once the Accused had successfully crossed into Singapore, he would proceed to drive to a prearranged location. According to the Accused, the prearranged location each time was dependent on the delivery location of his employer’s construction materials for each trip;

(d)     Upon his arrival at the prearranged location, the Accused would contact Komo who would in turn inform his Singapore contact(s) to proceed to the Accused’s location for collection of the animals;

(e)     Komo would then provide further instructions to the Accused as to the details of the buyers that were arriving (i.e., vehicle number of the buyer) as well as the animal(s) to be handed over to each respective buyer;

(f)     The buyers would then ascertain the number of live animals that made the crossing in the Accused’s presence and make a call to Komo to confirm the same for the purpose of making payment to the Accused;

(g)     Thereafter, the animals would be taken away by their respective buyers and the Accused would then proceed to continue with his regular work.

12     According to the Accused, he imported the animals into Singapore roughly two to three times a month. On average, the Accused would import between seven to 27 animals into Singapore per trip. These animals consisted of dogs, cats and parrots.

Facts to MAC 900037- 90042, 900045-900047 and 900049 of 2023

20     Arising from the examination at the AQC Dr Heng Zhan Pei (“Dr Heng”) prepared the “Animal Health and Welfare Assessment Report” (Annex A in SOF) dated 28 November 2022. In the said report, Dr Heng opined that the condition of the Accused’s lorry was found to be unsuitable for all ten animals referred to in the table above when they were transported from Malaysia to Singapore. This would have caused unnecessary suffering to them, for the following reasons: -

(a)     The space in the three enclosed blue containers where the ten dogs were found were insufficient to hold the number of number of animals recovered;

(b)     As they were kept in cramped conditions in the blue containers, this restricted their movements, the inability to adopt natural positions, as well as being jostled by the movement of the other animals;

(c)     The containers used to transport the animals were also enclosed on all sides with crude openings made on the covers. For adequate ventilation, there ought to be ventilation openings on all four sides of a container;

(d)     The animals were in weak conditions and suffered from medical issues such as lethargy, diarrhoea, poor body condition scores, external parasites, pale mucous membrane, and other inflammatory skin conditions;

(e)     The laboratory results showed that all ten dogs tested positive for Canine Parvovirus (“CPV”). CPV is a highly contagious virus that is transmitted via faecal oral contact and cause gastrointestinal disease in dogs. Clinical signs can develop after five to seven days of infection. Unvaccinated dogs younger than four months old are highly susceptible and more severely affected by the virus. Mortality rate for CPV ranges from 10% to 90%, depending on age, size of dogs and how aggressive and early treatments are given; and

(f)     The cramped and poorly ventilated environment in which the animals were transported in coupled with the unsanitary conditions cause by diarrhoea would expose them to the risks of disease spread and, in all likelihood, facilitate the transmission of CPV among the animals recovered.

21     Investigations revealed that as of 14 November 2022, all ten dogs have passed away despite veterinary treatment as a consequence of CPV infection.

22     Therefore, the Accused, as the person in charge of the ten animals at the material time, had failed to take reasonable steps to ensure that the said animals were not confined and conveyed in a manner that subjected them to unnecessary suffering and was thereby in contravention of a 41C(1)(a)(iii) which is an offence under s 41C(2) and punishable under s 41C(3)(b)(i) of the ABA.

Aggravating factors raised by the Prosecution

A.   Significant suffering caused to the animals

26     The Prosecution submitted that all 27 animals transported by the Accused on 18 October 2022 experienced “suffering” in light of the manner of their transportation. The Prosecution relied on the report by Dr Heng.

27     All except one animal seized were afflicted with Parvovirus (CPV or Feline Parovirus – “FPV”). 18 animals died as a result of the CPV. As for the French Bulldog, it died as a result of gastric dilation and volvulus (GDV). GDV in this case was directly caused by the dog being kept in a nylon bag with insufficient ventilation leading to excessive panting and ingesting large volumes of air, causing its stomach to dilate and twist, ultimately leading to the obstruction of blood circulation and death.

28     The Prosecution highlighted that the death of 19 animals represents more than a 70% loss out of all the animals imported by the Accused on 18 October 2022. This was by far one of the most egregious instances of illicit animal importation, where an offender has failed to take reasonable steps to prevent the infliction of suffering on animals under his charge.

These sellers use these platforms to reach out to a wide audience, including potential interested buyers in Singapore, without revealing their true identity.

D.   Impact on legitimate pet businesses

They are well thought-out with sufficient precautions in place to prevent the identification of the Malaysian suppliers as well as the Singapore buyers or contacts. When arrangements were made for the collection of the illegal pets at pre-arranged collection locations in Singapore, there was an insulating layer between Dido or Komo and the buyers or contacts via the courier to ensure anonymity until the latter came to pick up the animals.

(a)     he committed the offences in connivance with at least 2 others in Malaysia, Dido and Komo. In addition, the local contacts or buyers might have further assisted in the sub-distribution of the animals once in Singapore;

(b)     this was not the first time he had imported pet animals into Singapore. It was not a one-off transaction.;

(c)     there was a structured remuneration system for payment of the Accused for each occasion; and

(d)     there was a structured sale, collection and delivery system for the transportation of the animals from Malaysia into Singapore.

38     Thus, the Prosecution was of the view that there is a need for deterrence to discourage like-minded foreigners from attempting to perpetuate such illicit smuggling offences. Reply to the aggravating factors raised by the Prosecution

44     The Defence submitted that the aggravating factors raised by the Prosecution in its submissions are misplaced.

A.   Level of suffering caused to the animals

45     The Defence did not dispute the manner of suffering caused to the animals as described by Dr Heng and as such a deterrent sentence is necessary in the present matter.

46     It raised the point that whilst Dr Heng was of the view that the cramped conditions under which the animals were transported had in all likelihood facilitated the transmission of the CPV among them, Dr Heng did not make a definitive finding that the transmission had indeed occur during the time that the animals were in the Accused’s vehicle. Moreover, the sole cat was found to be infected with FPV and NParks has confirmed that the cat could not have contracted FPV from the dogs and vice versa.

B.   Inherent public health risk to the general public

47     The Defence did not dispute that the primary focus of the ABA is the concern over public health by safeguarding against the introduction of unwanted animal diseases into Singapore.

48     However, it disagreed that the risk to public health is an aggravating factor but a sentencing factor that is already within the consideration in respect of an offence under s 8(1) of the ABA since this is the very basis of the ABA. This can be gleaned from the precedent cases. On top of that, there is no issue of rabies as raised by the Prosecution as none of the animals were found to have suffered from it.

49     Further, in response to Prosecution’s position that there is an increase in public health risk since the Covid-19 pandemic, the Defence pointed out that there is no evidence that the animals in the present case had contracted Covid-19 or that Covid-19 had been transmitted from such animals even if Covid-19 is a zoonotic disease. There was clearly no actual harm in the present case.

C.   Prevalence of animal smuggling and ready availability to illicitly imported pets

50     The Defence urged the court to consider that the statistics proffered by the Prosecution do not support the Prosecution’s position that there is a recent spike in the prevalence of illicit importation of animals in 2022 as compared to 2020 and 2021.

51     However, the land borders between Singapore and Malaysia were closed from 18 March 2020 and 1 April 2022 due to the Covid-19 pandemic. When the borders were closed, vehicles permitted to enter Singapore could only do so for a limited purpose of transporting goods, food supplies and cargoes. Other modes of transportation like lorries, cars and boats were also not permitted to enter Singapore. There was clearly less vehicular traffic during, which arguably could explain the low figures of illegal importation of animals during in 2020 and 2021. The sufficiency of the statistics put forth by the Prosecution is lacking in evidence and therefore ought to be disregarded (citing Huang Ying-Chun v PP [2019] 3 SLR 606 at [111]).

52     In any case, Defence Counsel highlighted that if there is any issue of prevalence of animal smuggling which necessitates an increase to the punishment prescribed by law for the ABA offences, this is best left for Parliament to legislate as evident from past cases.

53     The Defence also disagreed that the ready availability of illicitly imported pets for sale via online platforms is an aggravating factor. There was firstly no link between the Accused to any vendors found on the online platforms. The Accused never sold any animals on such platforms and none of the animals brought in by the Accused have been found to be linked to these online platforms.

54     Once again, the Defence submitted that if observations made by AVS indeed reveal that there is a prevalence of online platforms offering sale of illegally source animals for a profit, AVS ought to take the necessary action against such perpetrators.

55     As for the potential lucrative financial gain from animal smuggling, the Defence disagreed that this is yet another aggravating factor. First, the estimated profit in respect of the animals brought by the Accused was not stated in the SOF and thus not admitted by the Accused. The Prosecution therefore could not adduce data that is unsubstantiated via the back door.

56     Second, the purpose of illegal importation of animals, like illegal importation of other items, is invariably motivated by some form of financial benefit. Therefore, relying on the High Court decision of PP v Muhammad Ikrimah bon Muhammad Adrian Rogelio Galaura [2020] SGHC 107 at [51]-[52] (“Ikrimah”), an offender’s financial motivation ought to be a neutral factor.

57     Third, as gleaned from the preamble of the ABA, the purpose of the ABA is clearly for the prevention and spread of diseases of animals as well as prevention of cruelty to animals. Financial gain or profits is not a focus of the ABA.

58     Moreover, the Accused himself was a mere courier who was paid RM$250 per live animal which he transported. He was neither the mastermind of the operation nor was he running or assisting to run a business involving the importation or sale of animals. Thus, the Accused has no knowledge of what the buyers stood to save or the sellers or masterminds stood to gain.

D.   Impact on legitimate pet businesses

59     Finally, Defence counsel submitted that any adverse impact on legitimate pet businesses as a consequence of illegal animal importation cannot be regarded as an aggravating factor or even a sentencing consideration. The Defence highlighted that even in cases dealing with illicit importation of contraband cigarettes, the courts have not considered harm to the legal cigarette marketplace as an aggravating factor but the quantity of the contraband items and the harm caused by consuming them: E.g. Yap Ah Lai v Public Prosecutor [2014] 3 SLR 180 at [77] to [79].

E.   Difficulty of detection

60     The Defence highlighted that concealment is inherent in all importation offences. Hence, while the Accused’s act of concealing the animals is indicative of his culpability for an offence under s 8(1) of the ABA, citing Ikrimah, Defence Counsel said that treating all forms of concealment as an aggravating factor would lead to the conclusion that all or almost all offences of importation are aggravated. In the circumstances, difficulty of detection ought not to be a factor which is particularly aggravating in the present case and is one which is common to all the precedents cited.

F.   Transboundary smuggling syndicate

61     The Defence did not dispute that the Accused was part of a transborder syndicate. Nevertheless, it urged the court to consider that this is not a particularly aggravating factor in the present case given that a transborder element would be an inherent factor in illegal importation offences and factors which are inherent in an offence are generally not treated as aggravating but neutral (citing Neo Ah Luan v PP [2018] 5 SLR 1153 at [80]. Besides, the facts in precedent cases would invariably have incorporated some consideration of the involvement of a transnational syndicate.

Also, there were a greater number of eventual deaths of the overall animals in the present matter than in Muguntan.

This is similar to the sentence the Prosecution is seeking per charge for each animal in the present matter. As for the s 41C of the ABA charges, the sentence was eight months’ imprisonment. Thus, the Prosecution’s position of 20 to 24 weeks’ imprisonment per charge is wholly unsupported.

88     S 41C of the ABA is intended to protect the welfare of animals and deter animal abuse in Singapore. Pertinently, when it was enacted, there was no enhancement of the penalties for a first offender, like the Accused, under s 41C(1)(a)(iii) of the ABA in that the maximum punishment prescribed by law is a fine of $10,000 or an imprisonment of up to 12 months under s 41C(3)(b)(i) of the ABA.

89     It is apparent that the legislative intent for the illegal importation offence differs somewhat from that for the causing unnecessary suffering to an animal during conveyance offence. Moreover, s 8(1) of the ABA pertains to licencing, which is a strict liability offence.

90     At the same time, for s 41C of the ABA, the role of the offender when committing the offence is material. The Bill did propose, and which was eventually passed, to establish separate and higher penalties for offenders who have committed the offence in the course of carrying on or employed in animal-related businesses as this is to recognise that businesses are more profit motivated and thus more financially capable of paying the fine such that a higher fine quantum will be a greater deterrent: see s 41C(3)(a)(i) of the ABA. Since the Accused here is not considered to have committed the offence of causing the animals to suffer during conveyance in the course of employment in relation to an animal-related business as defined under s 41 of the ABA, the higher penalty is not applicable to him.

91     For these reasons, I agree with the Defence that as these offences relate to two separately protected interests, in determining the individual sentence for each of them, the sentencing factors ought to be considered separately to avoid double counting.

92     Having said that, in a case of illegal importation, in order to avoid detection from the authorities, the offender will inevitably cause an animal to suffer unnecessarily during conveyance. There is hence an overlap. The issue invariably is a question of the manner, extent and degree as well as whether the animal survives the journey eventually or what is its condition post-trip.

93     With regard to the approach to be taken in determining the aggregate sentence for offenders convicted of multiple charges, is it apposite to seek guidance from the analytical framework as enunciated in the High Court decision of PP v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 799 (“Raveen”) where it was held at [98]:

In summary, the relevant principles in sentencing a multiple offender are as follows:

(a)    The first stage of the sentencing analysis is for the sentencing court to consider the appropriate sentence for each offence. This may be done in a number of ways, including by application of a sentencing framework or benchmark, or by analogy to precedents. In arriving at the individual sentences, the sentencing court will generally have to consider the relevant aggravating and mitigating factors that bear upon each discrete sentence (see [37] above).

(b)    The second stage of the sentencing analysis is to determine how the individual sentences should run. In this regard, the starting point of the analysis is whether the offences are unrelated and this is determined by considering whether they involve a single invasion of the same legally protected interest (see [68]–[70] above). As a general rule, sentences for unrelated offences should run consecutively, while sentences for offences that form part of a single transaction should run concurrently, subject to the requirement in s 307(1) of the CPC. If there is a mix of related and unrelated offences, the sentences for those offences that are unrelated should generally run consecutively with one of the sentences for the related offences (see [53]–[55] above). This general rule may be departed from so long as the sentencing court applies its mind to consider whether this is appropriate and explains its reasons for doing so. Statutory provisions may also abridge the operation of the general rule (see [64]–[67] above).

(c)    The third stage of the sentencing analysis is to apply the totality principle and take a “last look” at all the facts and circumstances to ensure that the aggregate sentence is sufficient and proportionate to the offender’s overall criminality (see [73] above). Specifically, there are two limbs to the totality principle. First, the court should examine whether the aggregate sentence is substantially above the normal level of sentences for the most serious of the individual offences committed. Second, the court should examine whether the effect of the sentence on the offender is crushing and not in keeping with his past record and future prospects. The court should also bear in mind the aggregation principle which provides that the totality principle ordinarily applies with greater force in cases that involve longer aggregate sentences (see [79]–[80] above). If an aggregate sentence is considered excessive, the sentencing court may opt for a different combination of sentences to run consecutively or adjust the individual sentences (see [73] above). In this regard, while it is within the court’s power to select sentences other than the longest individual sentence to run consecutively, the aggregate of such sentences must exceed the longest individual sentence (see Shouffee ([2] supra) at [77]) and, if appropriate, the court should state explicitly the individual sentence that would otherwise have been imposed for the offence but for the adjustment on account of the totality principle (see Shouffee at [66]).

(d)    Across all stages of the analysis, the sentencing court should be careful not to offend the rule against double counting. The central concern of this rule is that a sentencing factor should be given only its due weight in the sentencing analysis and nothing more. The rule underlies all aspects of sentencing. It applies to both aggravating and mitigating factors, and it may arise in cases involving single offences as it does in cases involving multiple offending. However, it does not necessarily violate the rule against double counting if a fact constituting a mitigating factor is again taken into account in the application of the totality principle even though it has been given effect to elsewhere in the sentencing analysis (see [91]–[94] above). [emphasis added]

They can also act as a check to ensure that consistency in sentencing may be maintained, especially given the lack of reported decisions in the present case. As stated in Jennifer Toh at [33]-[34]:

However, it has been cautioned that they serve as mere guidelines only. In Soong Hee Sin v Public Prosecutor [2001] 1 SLR(R) 475 at [12], this court stressed that “every case which comes before the courts must be looked at on its own facts, each particular accused in his own circumstances, and counsel be kept constantly and keenly apprised of the fact that it is just not possible to categorise cases based simply on mere numerals and decimal points”. The truth of this should not be understated.

No two cases are ever alike. The value of a particular sentencing precedent in determining the sentence to be imposed in a subsequent case is ultimately dependent on the degree of factual similarity between the two cases, including the offence-specific and offender-specific factors identified. Making bare references to outcomes in precedent cases without consideration of the detailed reasoning leading to those outcomes is ultimately an unproductive exercise. [emphasis added]

109    Having considered the relevant sentencing factors as well as the precedent cases for the said offence, I now determine the appropriate sentence to be imposed on the Accused.

110    I first deal with the offence-specific factors pertaining to the culpability of the Accused and the harm caused.

A.   Role of the Accused

111    The Accused was a courier who helped to smuggle 27 live animals into Singapore from Malaysia. He did so by driving his lorry which was provided by his employer for the original purpose of transporting construction material into Singapore.

112    I accept the Defence’s submission that the Accused was not a mastermind or even an agent of the transnational syndicate who was running or assisting to run an unlicenced business involving the sale of illegally imported live animals, whether by way of any online platforms or otherwise. Where the offender is a mastermind or perhaps even an agent who was running the business, his culpability would be higher.

B.   Knowledge of the licensing requirement

113    The Accused was acutely aware that it was illegal for him to smuggle the live animals into Singapore without a valid licence. This was because the Accused knew that the animals were already pre-packed into either boxes, containers or zipped laundry/nylon bags and they would have to be hidden inside the various compartments of the Accused’s lorry to avoid detection from the authorities when crossing the land borders between Singapore and Malaysia.

114    Moreover, when the Accused was arrested for the present offence, this was not his first trip as previously, he had made successful trips for the syndicate since 2021 and he would make about two to three trips a month. This further indicates the difficulty to detect such an offence.

C.   Scale of the operation

115    By being a courier, he was part of a transnational syndicate involved in illegal animal smuggling. His contact in Malaysia included Dido and Komo. The Accused took delivery of the live animals as well as instructions from them for the entire delivery process to the buyers from Malaysia to Singapore.

116    As for Prosecution’s submission on the prevalence of illegal pet sales available on online sales platforms, I am of the view that little or no weight ought to be given since this is unsubstantiated and there is no evidence before this court that there is a discernible link between the Accused and the illegal online sellers. For the same reason, neither is the article on pet shops in Johor offering pet smuggling services into Singapore from Channel News Asia dated 4 February 2023 produced by the Prosecution of any assistance to the court.

D.   Importation for the purpose of sale or trade

117    The Accused would receive a financial gain for each trip by the syndicate. He would be paid RM250 per live animal that was successfully smuggled and delivered to the buyers in Singapore. Therefore, for this trip if the Accused was successful, he would have earned RM6,750. The more animals he smuggled, the more he would earn. Yet, the higher risk he would need to take to avoid detection.

118    Once again, I did not consider the statistics adduced by the Prosecution on the price savings potential buyers would have gained if the animals were smuggled into Singapore successfully by the Accused. The prices stated in the table were approximates based on the Prosecution’s horizon scanning exercise conducted between 7 November 2022 and 11 November 2022 by taking a random sampling across two media platforms. They are not supported by any formal findings and presented before this court.

119    In any case, there is no evidence that the figures reflect the actual financial gain or profit of the Accused in the present case and the Accused has not admitted to them as his potential financial gain in the SOF.

120    I accept that illegal pet trading is likely to be a financially profitable business such that syndicates are motivated to take calculated risks to smuggle the animals into Singapore. To attract potential buyers, it is inevitable that the illegal pet businesses will price themselves cheaper than the legitimate businesses, particularly with the ready accessibility and convenience using online platforms. Otherwise, these buyers would simply have opted to purchase their pets from legitimate local pet businesses. Accordingly, the fact that the illicit pet trade is lucrative and is readily available online and thereby potentially affecting legitimate pet business adversely ought not be considered as an aggravating factor against the Accused as proposed by the Prosecution but a neutral factor.

121    Moreover, the legislative intent of the ABA is to prevent and control animal-related diseases into and in Singapore as well as to protect the health of Singaporeans from transmissible animal-related diseases more effectively. To deter these illegal online pet businesses, there may be a need for a more robust enforcement to shut down or remove them from the various online sales or social media platforms. This will remove the accessibility for potential buyers to purchase pets through illegal sources.

122    There should also be public education to inform the public not to purchase pets from such illegal pet businesses but only from licenced or accredited business operators in the interest of protecting public health from unwarranted animal-related diseases through the necessary quarantine, vaccinations and health checks done for the animals.

E.   Harm caused to the animal including any potential risk of infection or transmission of diseases

123    I will deal with the harm caused to the animals collectively. The number of live animals involved was significant. There were 26 dogs and one cat, totalling 27 animals.

124    One dog was found dead when recovered from the Accused’s lorry placed in a nylon bag. The cause of death was due to GDV, which is a life-threatening condition where the stomach of an animal dilates and twists due to excessive panting and ingesting large volumes of air from insufficient ventilation leading to obstruction of blood circulation and thereafter death.

125    Out of the remaining 26 live animals, 16 dogs were tested positive for CPV and the cat was tested positive for FPV at the time of admission into AQC. Some of the animals recovered were in a very weak condition or had medical issues. Eventually, all 24 dogs developed CPV infection with varying degrees of severity. Within about a month, 15 dogs passed away while three dogs were euthanised due to welfare reasons. The cause of death all arose from CPV infection. As such, there was a high mortality rate as a total of 19 animals out of 27 animals died (about 70%).

126    I accept Dr Heng’s opinion that none of the animals could have been infected by CPV after they were seized by the authorities. I further accept his opinion that it is possible that the transmission of the CPV could already have occurred prior to the dogs being placed in the Accused’s lorry since symptoms can arise three to 15 days after infection. Nevertheless, I note too that cramped and poorly ventilated plastic container boxes or nylon bags in which the animals were transported in to avoid detection coupled with the unsanitary conditions caused by diarrhoea would in all likelihood facilitate the transmission of CPV among the dogs recovered.

127    Additionally, for the ten proceeded charges, all the dogs have died.

128    Therefore, for each proceeded charge, having analysed the offence-specific sentencing factors and compared them with the precedents, I assess the indicative starting sentence of the Accused at two months’ imprisonment.

129    I now turn to the offender-specific factors.

130    For aggravating factors, the Accused has 17 TIC illegal importation charges in respect of the remaining 17 out of 27 animals. For these 17 animals, 1 was the white French bulldog, which was dead on arrival, five were subsequently dead, three were euthanised for welfare reasons and eight were alive but tested positive for CPV.

131    As for the mitigating factors raised by the Accused, I have taken into consideration his early plea of guilt, remorsefulness, cooperation with the authorities since his arrest and the fact that he is a first offender. This is similar to the offenders for the precedents cited by parties.

132    The Prosecution submitted that the Accused should not be regarded as a first offender because he has been charged with multiple offence by citing Chen Weixiong Jerriek v PP [2003] SGHC 2 SLR(R) 334 (“Jerriek”). I am of the view that the facts in Jerriek can be distinguished as the offender had committed the seven offences comprising of three different types of offences separately on different occasions as well as against different victims, the Accused here had committed the two types of offences on 27 animals arising out of one occasion. This was effectively a single transaction.

133    On the other hand, given the aggravating factor in this case, as highlighted at [21] of Jerriek, the present situation appears to be such that the mitigating effect afforded by the Accused’s plea of guilt is entirely outweighed by the clear need for a deterrent sentence.

134    Therefore, in weighing the aggravating and mitigating factors, I decline to give any downward adjustment to the indicative starting sentence but impose a sentence of two months’ imprisonment per charge.

135    In comparison with the precedents, the sentence imposed is a rather significant uplift from the three weeks’ imprisonment in Muguntan for 13 dogs (2 died), four weeks’ imprisonment in Desmond Lee for abetting Muguntan as well as 6 weeks’ imprisonment in Yogalingam and Siva for 17 dogs (1 died). It is also higher than the usual tariff of between four to six weeks’ imprisonment.

143    Having considered the relevant sentencing factors as well as the precedent cases for the said offence, I now determine the appropriate sentence to be imposed on the Accused.

144    As mentioned earlier, I am alive to the rule against double counting since in a case of illegal importation, the offender will invariably cause an animal to suffer unnecessarily during conveyance to avoid detection from the authorities.

145    Once again, I first deal with the offence-specific factors pertaining to the culpability of the Accused and the harm caused.

A.   Level of involvement of the Accused

146    The Accused was a courier for a syndicate. He took the pre-packed animals from one Komo in Gelang Patah, Malaysia, hid them in his lorry and transported them into Singapore before disturbing to the buyers at a pre-assigned location with the instructions of Komo or Dido from Malaysia.

147    The Defence highlighted the case of PP v Li Heyi Mitchell Edberg (“Mtichell”) because that offender was punishable under s 41C(3)(a)(i) of the ABA as he had committed the offence under s 41C(1)(a)(iii) of the ABA while carrying on an animal related business. Thus, under s 41C(3)(a)(i) of the ABA, the offender was liable to a high prescribed punishment of a fine not exceeding $40,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 year or both.

148    In Mitchell, the offender had entered Singapore from the Woodlands Checkpoint from Malaysia in a rented Singapore registered motorcar. He smuggled in 22 live animals without the relevant licences or permits and they were kept in separate plastic containers and hidden in a sealed biscuit tin and a cereal box in a car without ventilation for at least five hours. There was no evidence as to the condition of the said animals or whether any of them died after the journey. For each of the five proceeded s 41C(1)(a)(iii) of the ABA charge, the offender was sentenced to one month’s imprisonment with the sentences for two of the charges to run consecutively, giving a global sentence of two months’ imprisonment.

149    The Defence submitted that given the culpability of the offender’s role in Mitchell, the Accused’s culpability as a courier in the present case cannot be higher than that in Mitchell.

150    To my mind, given that the punishment prescribed by law for such an offender is greater under s 41C(3)(a) as compared to an offender in any other case under s 41C(3)(b) of the ABA, one will have to be cognizant of the fact that for a s 41C(1) of the ABA offence, the culpability of the offender is greater when he does so in the course of carrying on, or employment or purported employment with an animal related business as defined under s 41 of the ABA.

B.   Manner of confining and transporting the animal

151    In order to avoid detection of the animals during transportation, 22 dogs were cramped into three separate plastic containers. From the photos, it can be seen that only several small holes were poked at the top of the plastic covers of the containers to provide minimal ventilation for them. The Accused then hid the containers at various compartments in his lorry. Five animals were bundled up in five nylon bags with inadequate ventilation and hidden in another compartment of the lorry.

C.   Any steps taken to prevent unnecessary suffering caused to the animal

152    According to Dr Heng, the manner of transporting the animals in the Accused’s lorry were found to be unsuitable. It would have been inevitable that this would cause unnecessary suffering and significant discomfort to them. There was no evidence before me that the Accused took active steps to prevent this.

D.   Reason or motivation for causing the pain or suffering to the animal

153    The Accused committed the act of confining or conveying the animals in a manner that subjected them to unnecessary suffering for a financial gain using the lorry provided to him by his company so as to smuggle them illegally into Singapore.

E.   Duration of the inadequate care causing unnecessary suffering

154    As for the harm caused, the duration of the inadequate care which resulted in the unnecessary suffering of the animals in the Accused’s lorry was the journey from Johor in Malaysia to Singapore.

F.   Extent of Harm caused to the animal including any disease, disability or death

155    The extent of pain or suffering caused to the animals was also significant given the cramped, poorly ventilated and unsanitary conditions the animals were subjected to during the entire journey. There was further a lack of food and water. The animals were found to be in a weak condition and suffered from various medical issues including diarrhoea, poor body condition score, external parasites, inflamed ears with discharge and inflammatory skin conditions.

156    As stated earlier, one dog was found dead on arrival. 25 puppies were found to be infected with CPV while the cat was infected with FPV. The cramped and poorly ventilated environment coupled with the unsanitary conditions caused by diarrhoea from several of the dogs would in all likelihood had facilitated the transmission of CPV among the dogs recovered. Eventually, 18 out of the remaining 26 live animals recovered died (3 were euthanised) from CPV infection. Only seven dogs and the cat remained well.

157    Furthermore, since the dogs for all the proceeded charges have died, having analysed the offence-specific sentencing factors and compared them with the precedents, I assess the indicative starting sentence for each of the charge at four months’ imprisonment.

158    Coming to the offender-specific factors, the aggravating and mitigating factors are similar to those discussed under s 8(1) of the ABA.

159    Once more, weighing these factors, I decline to give any downward adjustment to the indicative starting sentence but impose a sentence of four months’ imprisonment per charge.

160    When compared with the precedents, the sentence imposed on the Accused shows an appreciable uplift from the eight weeks’ imprisonment in Muguntan for 13 dogs (2 died) as well as ten weeks’ imprisonment in Yogalingam and Siva for 17 dogs (1 died). It is also the same as the sentence imposed on Yeun Jian Iun and Cheow Yong Siong for the consolidated s 41C(1) charge involving 23 dogs (11 died). Notwithstanding, it is higher than the usual tariff of eight to ten weeks’ imprisonment.

The global sentence

161    Given that a sentence of imprisonment has been imposed for each of the 20 proceeded charges, pursuant to s 307(1) of the Criminal Procedure Code 2010 (“CPC”), I have to run the sentences for at least two charges consecutively.

162    For the final step to determine the global sentence to impose, I apply the totality principle and take a “last look” at all the facts and circumstances to ensure that the aggregate sentence is sufficient and proportionate to the Accused’s overall criminality.

163    I have made comparison with the cases where the offenders were convicted and sentenced based on consolidated charges for all the animals involved. The overall sentence imposed in Yeun Jian Iun and Cheow Yon Siong for 23 dogs (11 died) was eight months’ imprisonment respectively. The overall sentence imposed was 32 weeks’ imprisonment in Kelvin Phua for 12 dogs (3 died) and 30 weeks’ imprisonment in Ravinchandran for 11 dogs (none died).

164    Hence, I agree with both parties to run the sentences for two charges under s 8(1) of the ABA and two charges under s 41C(1)(a)(iii) of the ABA consecutively. The aggregate sentence will therefore be (2 months x 2) + (4 months x 2) = 4 months + 8 months = 12 months’ imprisonment.

165    As can be seen, the overall sentence of 12 months’ imprisonment represents a 1/3 uplift from the sentences in Yuen Jian Iun and Cheow Yan Siong as well as an approximate 40% uplift from the sentences in Kelvin Phua and Ravinchandran as a reflection of the Accused’s increased criminality in the present case. Moreover, it serves as an effective deterrent sentence.

166    When compared with cases where the offenders were convicted and sentenced based on a single charge for each animal, the overall sentence of 12 months’ imprisonment represents an almost 60% uplift from the sentences in Muguntan for 13 dogs (2 died) and Yogalingam as well as Siva for 17 dogs (1 died).

167    Furthermore, the aggregate sentence of eight months’ imprisonment imposed on the Accused for the s 41C(1)(a)(iii) of the ABA offence is similarly an approximate 40% uplift from the sentence imposed in Ravinchandran as well as Kelvin Phua. It is also a 75% uplift from the sentence imposed in Mitchell for 22 animals (0 died) under s 41C(3)(a)(i) of the ABA.

168    The aggregate sentence of four months’ imprisonment imposed on the Accused for the s 8(1) of the ABA offence is an approximate 50% uplift from the sentence imposed in Desmond Lee for 13 dogs (2 died) and who had abetted Muguntan to import the animals illegally for his unlicenced animal business.

169    All things considered, I am therefore of the view that a global sentence of 12 months’ imprisonment in the present case is just and appropriate.

Conclusion
