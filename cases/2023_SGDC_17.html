District Judge Tan May Tee:

Introduction

1       The plaintiff is a director of a property investment firm, S Y Lee Pte Ltd, that deals with the rental, purchase and sale of various properties across Singapore[note: 1]. The first defendant is a plastic surgeon. He practises at the second defendant’s clinic[note: 2].

2       In October 2013, the first defendant had performed a procedure on the plaintiff’s eyelids called an upper and lower blepharoplasty with lateral canthopexy. Blepharoplasty is a procedure for the removal of sagging skin and excess fatty tissue at the eyelids. Lateral canthopexy is a procedure to tighten the lower eyelids by the use of sutures. Blepharoplasty with lateral canthopexy is said to be an accepted standard treatment to correct droopy upper and lower eyelids[note: 3].

3       The plaintiff’s complaint pertains to the outcome of the surgery on his upper eyelids which was not what he had expected. His primary dissatisfaction was because after the surgery he was left with high and prominent upper eyelid creases.

4       The plaintiff claims that the first defendant had negligently advised him and provided him inadequate and/or improper medical treatment. In consequence, he alleges that he suffered injury and has been put to considerable inconvenience and expense for which he seeks damages.

5       The first defendant denies all of the plaintiff’s allegations. He says that he had fully discharged his duty of care to the plaintiff. As for the second defendant, it is contended that no cause of action has been established. The defendants accordingly ask for the plaintiff’s claims to be dismissed with costs.

Chronological background

Consultation prior to surgery and up to discharge from hospital

6       The plaintiff had been referred to the first defendant by one Dr Alvin Hong (“Dr Hong”), a neurosurgeon, on 24 September 2013. The plaintiff, then 53 years of age, had consulted Dr Hong as he wanted to remove a lump on his forehead.

7       According to the plaintiff, the lump had formed after he hit his forehead on a glass wall in January 2011. It had grown larger over time and in April 2011, the plaintiff consulted a general surgeon. After undergoing a computed tomography (“CT”) scan, he was advised that it was a bony lump that need not be removed. In August 2013, the plaintiff decided he wanted the bony lump removed after it had become noticeable to his friends. He was then referred to Dr Hong[note: 4].

8       Dr Hong assessed that the lump was an osteoma, ie a benign bony growth which had likely developed in 2011 after the plaintiff had bumped his forehead on glass. Since it was a benign growth, the plaintiff was advised that the osteoma could be left alone, or it could be removed. As the plaintiff wanted it removed for cosmetic reasons, Dr Hong then advised the plaintiff on the possible surgical approaches for its removal. One approach was an incision across the scalp behind the hairline which would result in a very long scar covered by hair when it grew back. The second approach was a cut directly over the osteoma, but the scar would be visible on the forehead. The plaintiff opted for the direct approach to minimise scarring.

9       To achieve the best aesthetic outcome, Dr Hong advised the plaintiff to get a plastic surgeon to suture up the wound after the osteoma was removed. Dr Hong then referred the plaintiff to the first defendant as their respective clinics were in the same building at Mount Elizabeth Medical Centre, and he was available to see the plaintiff that same day[note: 5].

10     The plaintiff and the first defendant differ vastly in their accounts of what had transpired during that first consultation. Their respective versions will be elaborated upon later in this Judgment. What is undisputed is that after the plaintiff had consulted the first defendant, it was agreed that Dr Hong would carry out the surgery for the removal of the osteoma with the first defendant suturing the wound after which the first defendant would also perform surgeries on the upper and lower eyelids of the plaintiff within the same sitting while the plaintiff was still under general anaesthesia. The surgeries were scheduled for 11 October 2013.

11     On the eve of the surgery, 10 October 2013, the plaintiff returned to Dr Hong’s clinic for the pre-operative assessment. He was seen by Dr Hong, and it was confirmed that the operation could proceed the next day. The plaintiff signed a consent form at Dr Hong’s clinic. He then went to the first defendant’s clinic where he was attended by the clinic staff. He signed another consent form and also made payment. The consent forms had the printed heading “CONSENT FOR OPERATION OR PROCEDURE”[note: 6].

12     The surgeries proceeded on 11 October 2013 as planned. The plaintiff was admitted to Mount Elizabeth Hospital in the morning. The first defendant met him in the operating theatre holding area where he carried out pre-operative markings on the plaintiff for each of the procedures[note: 7].

13     The removal of the osteoma was carried out first. The first defendant made the incision on the plaintiff’s forehead and Dr Hong drilled away the osteoma until the surface of the skull was smooth. The first defendant then sutured the wound after which Dr Hong left the operating theatre while the first defendant continued with the cosmetic procedures on the plaintiff’s upper and lower eyelids[note: 8]. The procedures started at 9.10am and ended at 1.10pm.

14     After the surgeries, the plaintiff was seen in the ward by both Dr Hong and the first defendant. Although the original plan was for the plaintiff to be discharged that day, it was decided that he would stay in hospital overnight as his eyes were swollen which affected his vision. The first defendant left instructions that ice packs be applied to the plaintiff’s eyes to help reduce the swelling[note: 9].

15     The following day, 12 October 2013, the plaintiff was again seen by both Dr Hong and the first defendant. His condition was noted by the first defendant to have improved, and he was discharged with post-operative care instructions and medication. A follow-up appointment with the first defendant was scheduled on 16 October 2013[note: 10].

Post-operative consultations and commencement of action

16     On 16 October 2013, the plaintiff attended the first post-operative consultation with the first defendant. There was still swelling on his forehead and eyelids from the surgery. The first defendant decided to delay the removal of the sutures on both the plaintiff’s forehead and eyelids until the next appointment on 18 October 2013[note: 11].

17     At the second post-operative consultation on 18 October 2013, seven days after the surgery, the first defendant noted that the plaintiff was healing well with the swelling reduced. He then proceeded to remove the sutures on both the forehead and eyelids. The plaintiff was prescribed topical antibiotic cream for the suture lines and advised not to swim or do strenuous activities for another week. He was also asked to return in the first week of November for further follow up[note: 12].

18     The plaintiff attended the third post-operative consultation with the first defendant on 4 November 2013. This was 23 days after the surgery. He was happy with the outcome of the surgeries on his forehead and lower eyelids. He was, however, unhappy with the look of his upper eyelids which were still swollen. The swelling had caused his eyelid creases to be higher and more prominent.

19     According to the plaintiff, he had during this consultation raised concerns about the condition of his eyes and the slow rate of recovery. He was unhappy with the thickness of his double eyelids, the purple discolouration and the swelling around his eyes. He was merely told to be patient and was assured that his upper eyelids would heal in time and the swelling would definitely go away[note: 13].

20     The first defendant noted that the wounds were healing well. He explained to the plaintiff that there was still post-operative swelling and that he should wait for the swelling to resolve which would result in a more natural look. The plaintiff informed the first defendant that he had been using “traditional therapy” with eggs to reduce the swelling and was advised against it. The plaintiff requested topical creams for scar therapy as he was returning to China. The first defendant prescribed them with instructions to begin application one month from the date of surgery (ie from 11 November 2013)[note: 14].

21     The plaintiff claimed that he did not receive any assurance from the first defendant during the consultation on 4 November 2013. He left this consultation feeling scared and worried that his eye area would not heal and that he would be left with thick double eyelids (ie with high and prominent upper eyelid creases), a purple discolouration and swelling around his eye area[note: 15].

22     It was subsequently revealed that the plaintiff had surreptitiously recorded his discussion with the first defendant during this consultation. A transcript of the conversation was produced at the trial[note: 16].

23     The plaintiff did not attend the follow-up appointment fixed for 4 December 2013. He had called the first defendant’s clinic to postpone the appointment to 12 December 2013, and subsequently called the clinic again to reschedule the appointment to a later date[note: 17].

24     It transpired that the plaintiff had gone to consult other doctors instead of going back to the first defendant. According to the plaintiff, this was because he no longer trusted the first defendant and was not comfortable consulting him after the third post-surgery consultation with him on 4 November 2013[note: 18].

25     In January 2014, the plaintiff consulted another plastic surgeon, Dr Martin Huang Hsiang Shui (“Dr Huang”), at his clinic, MH Plastic Surgery, on 25 January 2014. He informed Dr Huang of the surgeries carried out by Dr Hong and the first defendant without disclosing the latter’s identity. He complained of numbness and swelling of his upper eyelids, that his upper lid creases were too high, and that the earlier procedure made him look sleepy[note: 19].

26     According to Dr Huang’s clinical examination, the plaintiff had residual swelling of the upper eyelids from the recent surgery, high upper eyelid lid creases, upper eyelid ptosis as well as brow ptosis with prominent frown lines.

27     Dr Huang advised him that it was possible that the eyelid ptosis had either been created surgically or was a pre-existing condition prior to his surgery. He also advised the plaintiff that further surgery could be performed six months or later after the initial surgery when the swelling had subsided if the plaintiff found the eyelid creases too high for his liking and the ptosis was still present. In addition, Dr Huang discussed with the plaintiff the option of using botulinum toxin (Botox) in treating his frown lines and to lift his eyebrows[note: 20].

28     On 30 January 2014, the plaintiff went back to see Dr Huang and was given Botox injections to his brow and glabellar regions and crow’s feet area to treat his frown lines, smile lines and to lift his eyebrows[note: 21].

29     The plaintiff returned to see the first defendant on 11 February 2014, exactly four months after the surgeries were performed. He was again armed with a recording device to record his conversation with the first defendant without the latter’s knowledge.[note: 22]

30     At this consultation, other than the aesthetic appearance of his upper eyelids, he also complained of eye irritation, discomfort and sensitivity to light, and asymmetric upper eyelids. The first defendant found it difficult to examine the plaintiff due to his squinting and blinking. He noted that the swelling of the upper eyelids had resolved but the plaintiff had asymmetric eyelid creases. The first defendant was unable to find the cause of the plaintiff’s eye irritation and discomfort and offered to refer him to an ophthalmologist after which he would see the plaintiff again.

31     During the course of the consultation, the plaintiff disputed much of what the first defendant claimed to have advised him at the first consultation on 24 September 2013. The plaintiff did not disclose to the first defendant that he had consulted Dr Huang and had received Botox injections at his brow region and crow’s feet area on 30 January 2014.

32     The plaintiff did not return thereafter to see the first defendant. He had instructed his first set of solicitors in February 2014 who wrote to the first defendant for his medical records of the plaintiff as a precursor to commencement of action.

33     In response, the first defendant had provided two sets of clinical notes of the plaintiff’s consultations referred to as Set A and Set B in these proceedings on 19 May 2014 and 26 May 2014 respectively. The first defendant had explained that it was his clinic’s practice to scan the clinical notes after each patient’s consultation. After the consultation with the plaintiff on 11 February 2014, he had made further entries to the original records for 24 September 2013 as he noted that the earlier notes did not capture all the relevant information. As for the entry on 11 February 2014, he had re-written the notes as the earlier set was illegible due to the difficult session with the plaintiff on that occasion. While he had omitted to state the dates when the additions and amendments were made (resulting in the Set B notes), they nonetheless contained accurate information. There was no attempt on his part to mislead anyone as both versions had been kept by his clinic and supplied to the plaintiff’s previous solicitors when requested[note: 23].

34     The plaintiff has taken issue with the first defendant’s retrospective additions and amendments made to the clinical notes. He has accused the first defendant of dishonest conduct by the inclusion of these entries and allege that the retrospective changes amounted to an attempt by the first defendant to conceal his failures in the performance of his duty as a surgeon[note: 24].

35     Besides Dr Huang, the plaintiff also consulted various other doctors in Singapore, Shanghai and Bangkok. The plaintiff commenced proceedings through his present solicitors on 3 August 2015 against the defendants based on a medical report dated 28 July 2014[note: 25] issued by one Dr Nattawut Wanumkarng (“Dr Wanumkarng”) of Bumrungrad International Hospital, Bangkok, Thailand.

36     Notably, the plaintiff’s original action had also named Dr Hong and Dr Hong’s practice as defendants. He had alleged in his original pleaded claim that Dr Hong had conspired with the first defendant to cause loss to the plaintiff. The actions against Dr Hong and Dr Hong’s practice were discontinued on 3 October 2018 with costs[note: 26] paid by the plaintiff.

The Defendants’ defence

38     The defendants deny all the allegations of negligence in the statement of claim. Their Defence pleaded that the first defendant had conducted a full clinical examination of the plaintiff on 24 September 2013 when the plaintiff sought his advice on the removal of his eye bags and treatment to provide a fresher look for his upper eyelids.

39     As the clinical examination revealed that the plaintiff had inter alia brow ptosis, the first defendant had suggested a browlift procedure for the upper eyelids which was rejected by the plaintiff. The plaintiff had instead opted for the alternative proposal of an upper blepharoplasty which the first defendant had explained would be less effective[note: 28].

40     The blepharoplasty procedures and accompanying risks were explained by the first defendant in detail to the plaintiff with the aid of drawings and pre- and post-procedure photographs. The blepharoplasty was not intended to, and did not, construct double eyelids for the plaintiff as he originally had double eyelids.

41     While it was conceded that the defendants owed a general duty of care to the plaintiff, the first defendant denied that he failed to treat the plaintiff in a proper and professional manner, or that the treatment given had fallen below the relevant standard of care just because the aesthetic results were not in line with the expectations of the plaintiff. As for the retrospective entries made by the first defendant to the clinical notes, they were factually accurate, and the making of such entries did not amount to negligence[note: 29].

42     It is the defendants’ case that the injury, pain and suffering alleged by the plaintiff were not caused, or contributed to, by any negligent act or omission on the part of the first defendant.

43     It is also the defendants’ case that even if the first defendant were found negligent, the plaintiff’s condition was caused, or contributed to, by his failure to return for the follow-up consultation scheduled on 4 December 2013, and his self-treatment of “traditional therapy” using eggs to reduce the swelling in his eyelids without first seeking medical advice from the first defendant[note: 30].

No cause of action against second defendant

44     From the outset of the proceedings, the defendants had questioned the basis of the plaintiff’s cause of action against the second defendant[note: 31]. Although the then counsel for the plaintiff, Mr Palaniappan, had informed the court that the plaintiff’s case against the second defendant was premised on vicarious liability, he had conceded that this was not pleaded[note: 32]. No amendments have been made to the plaintiff’s statement of claim up to the stage of closing submissions.

45     In their closing submissions, the defendants have reiterated their position on the plaintiff’s pleaded case and further pointed out that no evidence had been led at the trial vis-à-vis the second defendant[note: 33]. The defendants have accordingly submitted that the plaintiff’s claim against the second defendant must be dismissed with costs.

46     It is trite that the legal basis of a defendant’s liability for another person’s tort can be founded on the principles of vicarious liability, agency, or the existence of a non-delegable duty (see Noor Azlin Binte Abdul Rahman v Changi General Hospital and Ors [2019] 1 SLR 834 at [111]). A plaintiff should, however, plead his case clearly so that a defendant may answer it and the court make its finding on the evidence adduced.

47     The plaintiff here has not pleaded any case, and no evidence has been led, against the second defendant. Neither has his counsel alluded to any basis of liability as against the second defendant in his closing submissions or made any attempt to reply to the defendants’ submission seeking dismissal of the claim. As such, the plaintiff’s claim against the second defendant has to be dismissed with costs. The rest of this judgment will deal with the claim against the first defendant only.

Applicable legal principles on liability in medical negligence

48     The first defendant does not dispute that he owed a duty of care to the plaintiff as his patient in the medical care and treatment provided. The dispute is whether there has been a breach of his duty of care. To determine whether the requisite standard of care has been breached, it will be helpful to first set out the applicable legal principles.

49     As explained by the Court of Appeal in its seminal judgment in Hii Chii Kok v Ooi Peng Jin London Lucien and another [2017] 2 SLR 492 (“Hii Chii Kok”), medical care has three aspects namely diagnosis, advice and treatment. They ought not to be treated as monolithic and it would be artificial to assess all three aspects by reference to a single test. The material difference between the three aspects of medical care lies in the dynamics of the doctor-patient relationship. With regard to the aspects of diagnosis and treatment, a patient plays a more passive role. In contrast, when it comes to advice, after a diagnosis has been formed and the treatment is recommended, the patient must assume an active role since the decision is ultimately his to make whether to proceed with the treatment recommended.

50     The Bolam test with the Bolitho addendum has oft been cited as the test to determine if a doctor has been negligent. Under the Bolam test (laid down in Bolam v Friern Hospital Management Committee [1957] 1 WLR 582), a doctor will not be found negligent if he has acted in accordance with a practice accepted at the time as proper by a responsible body of medical opinion, even if other doctors might have adopted a different practice. The Bolitho addendum refers to the qualification to the Bolam test that in assessing whether a body of opinion is responsible, reasonable or respectable, the court has to be satisfied that the experts have, in forming their views, directed their minds to the question of comparative risks and benefits and have reached a defensible conclusion on the matter (see Bolitho v City and Hackney Health Authority [1998] AC 232).

51     While the Bolam test (with the Bolitho addendum) continues to apply with regard to the aspects of diagnosis and treatment, the Court of Appeal in Hii Chii Kok held that a more patient-centric approach should be adopted with regard to the aspect of advice. Drawing reference from the decision of the UK Supreme Court in Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board [2015] UKSC 11, the Court of Appeal formulated a three-stage test to apply in determining the standard of care in relation to the provision of advice by a doctor to his patient, see Hii Chii Kok at [132] to [134]:

(a)     First, the patient is required to identify the exact nature of the information that he alleges was not given to him and establish why it would be regarded as relevant and material. Information which doctors ought to disclose is (a) information that would be relevant and material to a reasonable patient situated in the particular patient’s position, or (b) information that a doctor knows is important to the particular patient in question. This stage of the inquiry is undertaken from the perspective of the patient.

(b)     Second, the court would determine whether the information (as found relevant and material pursuant to the first stage) was in the possession of the doctor at the relevant time. This stage is a factual inquiry.

(c)     Third, if the court finds that the doctor did possess the information, it then has to determine whether the doctor was justified in withholding the information from the patient. This stage of the inquiry is to be undertaken from the doctor’s perspective, having regard to the standards of a reasonable and competent doctor.

The expert witnesses

52     In support of his claim, the plaintiff relies on the expert report of one one Brian Leatherbarrow (“Dr Leatherbarrow”) who is a specialist in ophthalmology sub-specialising in oculoplastic surgery. Dr Leatherbarrow had previously worked as a consultant ophthalmic, oculoplastic and orbital surgeon at Manchester Royal Eye Hospital from 1992 until 2014 before going into private practice and is now the director of the Face & Eye Clinic in Manchester, United Kingdom. He has also authored a textbook on Oculoplastic Surgery as well as published in several medical journals and papers on various topics related to oculoplastic and reconstructive surgery[note: 34].

53     In his statement of claim, the plaintiff had also referred to the report of Dr Wanumkarng who appears to also be an ophthalmologist sub-specialising in oculoplastic surgery. He was, however, not called as a witness at the trial.

54     The first defendant called two expert witnesses:

(a)     Professor Tan Bien Keem (“Prof Tan”), a senior consultant and the former head of department of the Department of Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery of Singapore General Hospital. Prof Tan has been in practice as a qualified plastic surgeon for about 20 years with experience in plastic and reconstructive surgery, including aesthetic surgery and has performed more than 200 eyelid surgeries[note: 35].

(b)     Associate Professor Sunny Shen (“A/Prof Shen”), a specialist in ophthalmology with a subspecialty in oculoplastic surgery. A/Prof Shen is a senior consultant and the head of the Oculoplastic Department at the Singapore National Eye Centre (“SNEC”), the head of the SNEC Eye Clinic at Changi General Hospital, and the Vice-President of the Singapore Society of Ophthalmic Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery[note: 36].

55     As stated by A/Prof Shen, as oculoplastic surgeons, their training background is different from that of plastic surgeons[note: 37]. While Dr Leatherbarrow undoubtedly has impressive credentials in the field of oculoplastic surgery[note: 38], in terms of satisfying the Bolam test, in my assessment the opinion rendered by Prof Tan as to whether the first defendant’s treatment and management of the plaintiff was in accordance with the accepted standards and practice of a responsible body of medical men skilled in the particular field in question, ie plastic surgery, should carry greater weight.

Issues

56     From the parties’ cases as pleaded, the issues which the court had to decide were the following:

(a)     whether the first defendant had failed to advise or warn the plaintiff of the likely or possible consequences of the upper blepharoplasty procedure undertaken;

(b)     whether the first defendant had failed to plan and discuss with the plaintiff the various treatment options available to him;

(c)     whether the first defendant had failed to carry out appropriate investigations and tests to ascertain the correct diagnosis;

(d)     whether the treatment provided to the plaintiff by the first defendant in the upper blepharoplasty procedure was reasonable and adequate even if the aesthetic result did not meet the plaintiff’s expectations;

(e)     whether the injuries alleged by the plaintiff were caused by the acts or omissions of the first defendant;

(f)     if the first defendant were found negligent, whether the plaintiff’s actions had caused and/or contributed to his injuries; and

(g)     what damages or losses, if any, should be awarded to the plaintiff if the first defendant were found liable.

57     By reference to the three aspects of medical care referred to in Hii Chii Kok – diagnosis, advice and treatment – issues (a) and (b) have to do with the aspect of advice (or termed as informed consent by the plaintiff) and will be dealt with together. Tied in with the issue of what advice had been given is the issue raised by the plaintiff on the propriety of the first defendant’s conduct in the making of retrospective amendments and additions to his clinical notes and whether the entries were factually accurate. The determination of issue (c) falls under the aspect of diagnosis while issue (d) falls under the aspect of treatment. Issue (e) deals with causation, another pre-requisite to liability in negligence. Issue (f) deals with the defence of contributory negligence. Lastly, as the trial has not been bifurcated, issue (g) deals with proof of loss and damages. Each of the issues will be dealt with sequentially.

Plaintiff’s allegation of defendants’ failure to advise

Was there informed consent?

58     Under the three-stage test formulated in Hii Chii Kok, it is incumbent on the plaintiff to identify the exact nature of the information that he alleges was not given to him and establish why it would be regarded as relevant and material. The plaintiff’s statement of claim did not plead that the first defendant had failed to give him sufficient information of his clinical condition to enable him to make an informed decision. What can be gleaned from the particulars furnished of the allegation of the first defendant’s failure to advise is merely that:

(a)     the plaintiff was advised that he had asymmetrical eyes which could be corrected by way of a simple day surgery to remove a bit of skin which would be completed within two to two and half hours[note: 39]; and

(b)     the plaintiff was not advised that he had brow ptosis prior to the surgery[note: 40].

59     From the closing submissions[note: 41] filed by the plaintiff’s counsel, Mr Shiever, his case on there being no informed consent is premised on the plaintiff’s testimony that:

(a)     the first defendant had not informed him of the detailed procedure and the risks and complications of the surgery;

(b)     the first defendant made no mention to him of an alternative procedure of a brow lift;

(c)     the first defendant made no mention to him that he had brow ptosis; and

(d)     the first defendant had merely informed him that he was going to remove a bit of skin to correct his asymmetric eyes (ie one big eye and one small eye) in a simple operation that could be done in his office.

60     The crux of the plaintiff’s case is that all the pertinent information on his condition, the blepharoplasty surgery together with its possible complications, and the alternative procedure of a brow lift were told to him only after he had questioned the first defendant on the unsatisfactory outcome of the surgery[note: 42]. The plaintiff has sought to rely on certain snippets of the conversations that he had surreptitiously recorded on 4 November 2013[note: 43] and 11 February 2014[note: 44] and the fact of the first defendant having made retrospective amendments to his clinical notes to infer that the first defendant had admitted to his failure to properly advise him of his condition, the blepharoplasty procedure and its complications.

61     As earlier mentioned, the plaintiff’s narrative as to what had transpired in his first consultation on 24 September 2013 differs vastly from the first defendant’s account. Both versions will now be examined to determine what in fact the plaintiff had been advised pertaining to his condition and the first defendant’s recommendation for treatment.

The plaintiff’s version

62     In his AEIC, the plaintiff recounted the events of 24 September 2013 as follows[note: 45]:

(a)     He agreed to see the first defendant to suture the incision on his forehead following the removal of the osteoma after Dr Hong had informed him that the first defendant was very good at suturing incisions.

(b)     He was not aware, and Dr Hong did not inform him, that the first defendant was a plastic surgeon. He only found this out when he went down to see the first defendant and discussed the suturing of his forehead with him.

(c)     Upon learning that the first defendant was a plastic surgeon, he then inquired with the first defendant on various cosmetic procedures that could be carried out to his face such as the removal of sagging skin around his neck and the sides of his face, and the removal of milia seeds in the under-eye area of his face.

(d)     After their discussion, he informed the first defendant that he only wanted him to suture the incision from the osteoma removal and carry out the procedure to remove the eyebags from his under-eye area whilst he was still under general anaesthesia.

(e)     Unexpectedly, the first defendant then remarked that his eyes were asymmetrical (ie his left eye was bigger than the right eye) and offered to correct them by a simple procedure.

(f)     The first defendant informed him that:

(i)       the procedure to correct his asymmetric eyes would take around two to two and a half hours to complete and would not require an overnight stay in the hospital;

(ii)       the procedure involved removing a little bit of skin and could even be performed in his clinic;

(iii)       the wrinkles around his eyes would become less pronounced after the procedure although they would not disappear completely;

(iv)       he might feel tightness around his eyes after the procedure; and

(v)       he might experience swelling in his upper eyelids after the procedure, but the swelling would subside, and his upper eyelids would return to normal in approximately two weeks, or in more complicated cases up to six weeks, after the procedure.

63     According to the plaintiff, the first defendant had also shown him the before and after photographs of a former patient who had undergone a similar procedure. Based on the information given by the first defendant (as set out in [62(f)] above) together with the photographs of the former patient, the plaintiff then agreed to undergo the procedure to correct his asymmetric eyes while he was under general anaesthesia for the osteoma removal[note: 46]. However, he had no intention to deepen his upper eyelid creases to make his existing double eyelids more prominent. He understood that the procedure would correct his asymmetric eyes to make them symmetrical and leave him with an “Asian look”, namely double eyelids but with a low upper eyelid crease.

64     The plaintiff claims that during the consultation on 24 September 2013, the first defendant did not[note: 47]:

(a)     inform or advise him of any other risks and/or complications and/or side effects associated with the procedure to correct his asymmetric eyes;

(b)     inform him that he would experience numbness and bleeding as a result of the procedure;

(c)     take any measurements of his face or eyes;

(d)     inform him as to how much skin he was going to remove from the upper eyelids;

It must be noted, however, that apart from the medical records (including the first defendant’s Set A and Set B clinical notes), Dr Leatherbarrow’s opinion was prepared based on his examination of the plaintiff on 1 May 2018 and the plaintiff’s account in his AEIC which had been set out verbatim in his report. His criticism of the first defendant was particularly focused on the details which, in his view, ought to have been recorded in the first defendant’s clinical notes but were omitted such as:

(a)     the plaintiff’s concerns and what he expected to achieve from any surgical treatment,

(b)     the plaintiff’s past ophthalmic history including a history of contact lens wear, laser eye surgery, blepharitis or a dry eye problem,

(c)     the plaintiff’s past aesthetic history including a history of botulinum toxin injections or dermal filler injections, and a past dermatology history,

(d)     the plaintiff’s past medical history of obstructive sleep apnoea and hypertension and had in fact recorded that the plaintiff had no history of hypertension,

(e)     the plaintiff’s past surgical history and a drug (medications) history, and

(f)     as the plaintiff was a patient seeking cosmetic eyelid surgery, a basic ophthalmic examination or the plaintiff’s visual acuity.

102    Dr Leatherbarrow had essentially taken issue with the first defendant’s clinical examination records as he noted that in relation to a patient for whom a brow lift was recommended, the first defendant had failed to document the position of the plaintiff’s hairline, and the height and curvature of the forehead. The first defendant had also failed to document that he had taken pre-operative photographs which would form an essential part of the clinical records of any patient undergoing cosmetic surgery. He further opined that the first defendant should have established and documented the plaintiff’s precise complaints, hopes and expectations of the surgery. Depending on the outcome the plaintiff was seeking, there were different options available – if the objective was merely to achieve better symmetry in the appearance of his upper lids and, assuming that the plaintiff preferred the appearance of his left upper lid, the first defendant should have advised him that a very conservative right upper lid skin muscle blepharoplasty alone would have sufficed. Had the plaintiff preferred the appearance of his right upper eyelid, a left blepharoptosis operation could have been considered instead. In this case, the simpler option with the least risk and the fastest recovery period would have been a right upper lid skin muscle blepharoplasty.

103    Dr Leatherbarrow also criticised the first defendant for failing to inform the plaintiff that the most serious risk of the surgery was visual loss, including blindness, and he further noted that the risks of the surgery were not documented on the surgical consent form. It was also his opinion that the plaintiff should have been advised about all the risks and potential complications of a bilateral upper lid blepharoplasty, namely:

(a)     visual loss including blindness (an extremely remote risk of less than 1:10.000);

(b)     blepharoptosis;

(c)     dry eye problem from an incomplete reflex blink requiring the long-term use of topical lubricants;

(d)     infection;

(e)     bleeding;

(f)     development of a haematoma requiring a return to the operating theatre;

(g)     asymmetry;

(h)     scarring;

(i)     failure to achieve the desired result; and

(j)     the possible need for further surgery.

My findings on informed consent

104    From my assessment of the evidence, I make the following findings:

(a)     On 24 September 2013, the first defendant had conducted a clinical examination of the plaintiff’s face and found inter alia that the plaintiff had brow ptosis in addition to asymmetrical upper eyelid creases and lateral hooding.

(b)     The first defendant had informed the plaintiff that he had low brows (brow ptosis) and had proposed a brow lift procedure to the plaintiff, but the plaintiff had declined this procedure as he found it was too complex.

(c)     The first defendant had proposed to the plaintiff the alternative of an upper blepharoplasty procedure to remove excess skin which was explained with a diagram as well as showing him before and after photographs of other patients.

(d)     The first defendant had explained to the plaintiff the risks and possible complications that could arise from the upper blepharoplasty procedure including swelling, bleeding, asymmetry and scarring.

105    Counsel for the plaintiff had submitted that the first defendant could not have examined and advised the plaintiff of all his findings and recommendations as he claimed since the consultation on 24 September 2013 had lasted only 20 to 25 minutes. The first defendant had explained that his clinical examination of the plaintiff entailed a visual and physical examination of the plaintiff’s face and eye area together with taking of photographs and drawing of diagrams to explain his findings and the recommended procedures. As no complex procedures or tests were required, it is speculation that 20 to 25 minutes would not have been sufficient for the first defendant to conduct the examination and explain the findings and recommendations to the plaintiff who had appeared to understand what was communicated to him and had opted for the upper blepharoplasty instead of a brow lift. Furthermore, as explained by the first defendant, the plaintiff had stopped him halfway through the explanation on the brow lift procedure on which he was not keen on having[note: 83]:

Q    When you say he declined a brow lift procedure, can you tell what did you explain to him, a brow lift procedure?

A    Um, I actually didn’t get much time to explain it because once I explained the complexity of cutting it above the hairline and having to lift the skin, lift the brow and then fix it, um, the patient had expressed that it was too complex for him. And, actually, the discussion about the brow lift stopped there.

106    From the transcripts of the conversations that the plaintiff had surreptitiously recorded and my observation of the plaintiff’s behaviour in the courtroom, the plaintiff comes across as someone who is quite impatient and quick to interrupt the other person. The first defendant’s account that the plaintiff had stopped any further discussion on the brow lift procedure when he found it too complex and was not interested in pursuing is certainly very plausible and, more likely than not, what actually took place.

107    As for Dr Leatherbarrow’s opinion that the first defendant’s pre-operative assessment of the plaintiff was not of an acceptable standard, it should be pointed out that his opinion was based on the version of events recounted by the plaintiff in his AEIC which I have rejected in favour of the first defendant’s version. Further, it also appears that Dr Leatherbarrow’s opinion as to the acceptable standard of care was made from the perspective of an ophthalmologist sub-specialising in oculoplastics rather than as a plastic surgeon per se. Examples of this can be seen in his criticism of the first defendant’s failure to record a past ophthalmic history from the plaintiff or to conduct a basic ophthalmic examination or a test for visual acuity. They lend support to the submission made by counsel for the first defendant, Mr Chong, that Dr Leatherbarrow had not applied his mind to what was the acceptable standard of care applicable to a body of plastic surgeons when preparing his expert opinion as required under the Bolam test. It seemed that Dr Leatherbarrow’s criticisms of the first defendant’s treatment and management of the plaintiff was made solely by reference to his own personal clinical practice.

108    It also appears that Dr Leatherbarrow had, without any factual basis, taken the position that the first defendant’s retrospective entries in his clinical notes were false or misleading merely because they were not dated nor signed. The fact that the first defendant had retained the original set of his notes (albeit in digital form) and had provided both sets to the plaintiff showed that he had nothing to hide. While the first defendant should be called to account as to why there were two sets of notes, it must be noted that he had readily admitted that he should have dated and initialled the amendments and additions. Neither the plaintiff nor Dr Leatherbarrow has explained what mandatory requirement has been breached by the first defendant’s failure to do so.

109    The first defendant had explained that the amendments and additions were made as the earlier notes were not complete and/or illegible, and the plaintiff has not provided any evidence to show that the retrospective entries are factually incorrect. As pointed out earlier, on the specific issue of brow ptosis, both Set A and Set B notes clearly indicate presence of bilateral brow ptosis.

110    Prof Tan had also opined that in practice it is quite common for doctors to make retrospective entries as during consultations they have to go through a lot of facts and some critical information might be missed out originally which they will then go back to fill up. As long as the information inserted was factual and could be verified with other sources, he disagreed with the plaintiff’s characterisation of the retrospective entries as being intended to “mislead” the reader. He had no reason to doubt the veracity of the first defendant’s amended notes as the additions made were consistent with the overall clinical picture of an aging face which was further supported by the contemporaneous photographs taken of the patient[note: 84].

111    Dr Leatherbarrow had also unfairly questioned the authenticity of 1ABD57, the letter written by the first defendant to Dr Hong and labelled the retrospective entries in the clinical notes by the first defendant as surreptitious[note: 85]. He appeared to have championed the plaintiff’s cause in questioning the authenticity of the letter based on what he had read in the plaintiff’s AEIC as reproduced in his expert report. It was only during cross-examination when he was referred to Dr Hong’s AEIC which confirmed that he had received the letter dated 25 September 2013 on that day that he accepted that he had taken an unreasonable position[note: 86]. Dr Leatherbarrow’s criticisms of the first defendant without ascertaining the full facts and relying solely on the plaintiff’s version showed a lack of objectivity on his part.

112    Notably, Dr Leatherbarrow, had during cross-examination also admitted that based on his clinical examination, the plaintiff did have brow ptosis at the temporal region, but he had omitted to state this finding in his report[note: 87]. Furthermore, he had also agreed that when referred to the pre-operative photograph of the plaintiff taken by the first defendant on 24 September 2013, the plaintiff did have ptosis at the outer aspects of the brow[note: 88].

113    As to whether the first defendant should have advised the plaintiff of the option of a right upper skin muscle blepharoplasty alone or a left blepharoptosis operation, it is Prof Tan’s opinion that a unilateral blepharoplasty (ie operating on either the left side or right side alone) is not advisable as there would be asymmetry owing to differences in the eyelid fold (usually deeper on the operated side), swelling in the acute phase, and presence of a scar on the operated side. It was therefore reasonable for the first defendant to have advised a bilateral upper blepharoplasty for symmetry of the eyelid folds given the common factors of surgical intervention, swelling and wound healing associated with it[note: 89].

114    In cross-examination, Prof Tan had explained that he would not even “go down the road” of advising a unilateral upper eyelid surgery as it would be more difficult to achieve symmetry. In this case where both sides were found to be sagging, one side more than the other, both sides needed to be operated on for the best symmetrical outcome.

115    In regard to the list of risks and potential complications outlined in Dr Leatherbarrow’s opinion[note: 90], as pointed out by Prof Tan in his Supplementary Expert Opinion, these are the standard risks and complications related to each procedure. The degree of emphasis placed on each risk differs from case to case, depending on the age of the patient, diagnosis, the patient’s co-morbidities, as well as the type of procedure.

116    I found the first defendant’s account that he had duly advised the plaintiff of the associated risks and complications of the bilateral upper blepharoplasty procedure to be credible and consistent with the overall evidence. There was nothing to indicate that the plaintiff had not understood what was communicated to him.

117    Notably, when Prof Tan was questioned as to whether the patient in this particular case was thoroughly explained as to the procedures to be adopted for his condition, he was clear that having gone through the notes as well as the medical reports, the explanation that had been given to the plaintiff was adequate for a standard blepharoplasty procedure which was to be performed.

118    Accordingly, on the first two issues, the plaintiff has failed to prove on a balance of probabilities that:

(a)     the first defendant had failed to advise or to warn him of the likely or possible consequences of the upper blepharoplasty procedure undertaken; and

(b)     the first defendant had failed to plan and discuss with him the various treatment options available to him.

119    There was informed consent on the part of the plaintiff when he decided to proceed with the upper blepharoplasty procedure.

First defendant’s case

123    The first defendant’s evidence[note: 93] is that he had conducted a thorough facial examination of the plaintiff. This entailed firstly an examination of the forehead where the osteoma was to confirm its position. There was then a close examination of the plaintiff’s mid-face and eyelids as the plaintiff had requested whether improvements could be done to his looks. It was noted that the plaintiff had upper and lower blepharochalasia. It was also noted that the plaintiff had asymmetrical upper eyelid creases with the left lid crease higher than the right, lateral hooding of the upper eyelids and brow ptosis indicating laxity of the skin on the forehead. In the lower eyelids, it was noted that the plaintiff had excess fat, skin and muscle, lax lower lid tone and deep nasojugal folds. His facial skin was also lax and there was jowling.

My finding

125    The first defendant had conducted a comprehensive facial examination of the plaintiff as outlined in his AEIC. His evidence remained intact after cross-examination.

126    In satisfaction of the Bolam test, the first defendant had relied on the opinion of Prof Tan, whose credentials as a plastic surgeon with the relevant experience, was not challenged by the plaintiff. Prof Tan had reviewed all of the available medical records and opined that the first defendant conducted an appropriate examination of the plaintiff. No further examinations, tests and/or investigations were required for him to come to his diagnosis, and his diagnosis of asymmetrical upper eyelid creases, lateral hooding of the upper lids and brow ptosis was reasonable.

127    The opinion given by Prof Tan is sound and logical given that the plaintiff’s consultation with the first defendant was in essence to seek an improvement to the look of his eyelids. The plaintiff has not shown how from a plastic surgeon’s perspective, the facial examination carried out was inappropriate and/or inadequate. I agree with Mr Chong that the opinion rendered by Prof Tan had also satisfied the Bolitho addendum of the Bolam test[note: 94]. Mr Shiever in his submissions has failed to rebut this.

128    In the circumstances, the plaintiff’s allegation that the first defendant had failed to carry out appropriate investigations and tests to ascertain the correct diagnosis is not proven.

Plaintiff’s allegation of defendants’ failure to treat correctly

Whether the treatment provided to the plaintiff by the first defendant in the upper blepharoplasty procedure was reasonable and adequate

Plaintiff’s case

129    The plaintiff’s complaint is essentially that the first defendant had removed an excessive amount of upper eyelid skin resulting in high and prominent upper eyelid creases.

130    He relies on Dr Leatherbarrow’s opinion that the upper lid surgery performed by the first defendant was not of an appropriate standard due to the following[note: 95]:

(a)     The skin crease incisions were made far too high for a male patient and the resultant post-operative feminisation of the upper lids with an unsatisfactory appearance was predictable.

(b)     Far too much skin was removed from the plaintiff’s upper lids. The first defendant should have left at least 10mm of skin between the inferior aspect of the brows and the uppermost skin incision. The failure to do so resulted in the plaintiff’s problems with eyelid closure and his corneal exposure symptoms and signs. These problems were also predictable because of the overly aggressive nature of the skin resection.

(c)     The first defendant’s operation record was very poor and did not document the extent of the resection of upper lid orbicularis oculi muscle. He had merely documented that a skin muscle flap was excised. It was more likely than not that the same amount of upper lid orbicularis oculi muscle was resected as skin.

(d)     The first defendant’s medical report dated 25 February 2014, documented that the upper lid skin excision was 5mm in the mid pupillary line and 7mm at the lateral canthus. Such a skin resection would be very unusual as in an upper lid blepharoplasty, less skin is usually taken laterally than is taken centrally as the skin is resected in a crescentic fashion.

(e)     The first defendant’s surgery was performed too deeply and had damaged the plaintiff’s levator aponeurosis resulting in a bilateral blepharoptosis (drooping of the upper eyelids).

First defendant’s case

131    The first defendant disagreed that he had removed an excessive amount of skin. He explained that the judgment of how much skin to excise would depend on the height of the patient’s natural eyelid crease, the amount of excess skin superior to the crease and the patient’s eyelid aperture. This had been discussed with the plaintiff prior to the cosmetic procedures with the aid of pre-operative and post-operative photographs of other cases. For the plaintiff, the first defendant had exercised his judgment and removed 5mm in the mid-pupillary line and 7mm at the lateral canthus in consideration of the above factors.

132    The first defendant relied on the opinions given by both Prof Tan and A/Prof Shen to support his case that the procedure carried out was reasonable:

(a)     Prof Tan’s expert opinion, given from a plastic surgeon’s perspective, was that the first defendant did not remove an excessive amount of skin from the plaintiff’s upper eyelids. As could be seen from the photographs taken by Dr Huang on 25 January 2014 (4 months post-operative) and 15 September 2016 (2 years 11 months post-operative), the plaintiff’s eyebrows and eyelid creases were noted to be symmetrical, and the plaintiff was able to close his eyes. If the skin had been excessively removed, the plaintiff may have difficulty closing his eyes[note: 96].

(b)     In response to Dr Leatherbarrow’s findings and opinion, A/Prof Shen who had examined the plaintiff on 20 October 2020 (about 2.5 years after Dr Leatherbarrow had examined him on 1 May 2018), did not find that the plaintiff had an incomplete reflex blink. He also found that the plaintiff could close his eyes completely (as seen in the photograph at 3BAEIC page 329)[note: 97].

(c)     On whether more skin should have been excised laterally as opposed to centrally (which Dr Leatherbarrow had described as “unusual”), A/Prof Shen had testified that for patients of the plaintiff’s age, due to natural aging of the whole face from forehead to eyebrow, and eyebrow to eyelid, there is usually more redundant skin (referred to “hooding”) laterally. Hence, it would be the usual practice to remove more skin laterally for patients beyond 50 years old like the plaintiff. Furthermore, the fact that there are wrinkles on the side of the patient’s eyes shows that there is reserve skin enough to close the eyes. If the surgery had been overdone, the skin would be taut and tight[note: 98].

My findings

133    The plaintiff’s case that an excessive amount of skin had been removed from his upper eyelids was not put to the first defendant in cross-examination.

134    The plaintiff also did not challenge Prof Tan’s opinion that the first defendant had not removed an excessive amount of skin. Mr Shiever’s cross-examination of Prof Tan had centred on several technical aspects of the surgery and the measurements. Prof Tan had explained that it is not necessary to measure the entire area ie between the brow and ciliary margin of the eyelash. While the recommended practice was to leave 1cm to 1.5cm of intact skin between the brow and the upper incision, what is necessary is to ensure that the patient has sufficient skin laxity to close the eyes. After deciding on the lower skin incision and marking out the incision lines, to ensure there is sufficient skin redundancy, this would be done by pinching the intended incision lines together see if there is any tightness. If there is none, he could proceed without having to make the direct measurements from the incisions to the brow[note: 99].

135    Further, according to A/Prof Shen’s measurements, the plaintiff’s brow to lash distance was 23mm on the right and 21mm on the left. This was well within the normal range of 16mm to 23mm for the Chinese ethnic group and would have allowed for the 10mm of skin between the brow and the upper skin incision that Dr Leatherbarrow had insisted on. It had also been pointed out that the 10mm prescribed by Dr Leatherbarrow did not quite apply to Oriental patients as published in an article “Anthropometry Analysis of Beautiful Upper Eyelids in Oriental: New Eyelid Crease Ratio and Clinical Application” in the Aesthetic Plastic Surgery Journal (2020) 44:3492-410[note: 100].

136    Neither did the plaintiff dispute A/Prof Shen’s evidence of his clinical examination findings that the plaintiff has sufficient skin to close his eyes properly[note: 101].

137    On the other hand, there were aspects of Dr Leatherbarrow’s opinion which did not stand up to scrutiny. He had asserted that the first defendant’s surgery was performed too deeply and had damaged the plaintiff’s levator aponeurosis when there was no evidence of such damage. Notably, the levator function of 15mm which he had measured would be considered as good by convention, according to A/Prof Shen’s evidence[note: 102]. Dr Leatherbarrow had also asserted that because the first defendant had merely documented that “skin muscle flap was excised”, it was therefore more likely than not that the same amount of upper lid orbicularis oculi muscle had been resected as skin. During cross-examination, he was referred to his own textbook wherein he had referred to a “myocutaneous flap” which is synonymous with a skin and muscle flap, and he then conceded that it is actually standard practice to remove less muscle, but this need not be explicitly spelled out.

138    Weighing the evidence of the experts from both sides, I find that the plaintiff has failed to prove that the first defendant had excised an excessive amount of skin from his upper eyelids.

Q    Okay, so you are trying to tell me that the picture here, 11th of February 2014 is a good picture?

A    By the look of this picture, it’s acceptable.

Q    This is acceptable?

A    I mean, if you were to say that---

Q    Thank you.

A    I see---

Q    Yes, alright. So, to you, this look on your face is acceptable to you from a cosmetic aspect, yes or no?

A    Based on this picture, I can accept.

140    From a plastic surgeon’s perspective, Prof Tan had opined that the asymmetry seen on the plaintiff’s eyelids by reference to the post-surgery photographs is within the normal range bearing in mind that there are minor asymmetries in our eyelids[note: 105]. It was also the first defendant’s view that the aesthetic outcome for the plaintiff’s upper eyelids was acceptable even though it was not what the plaintiff subjectively had in mind[note: 106].

141    It also appears from the measurements of the plaintiff’s eyelid creases taken at various times by the different doctors that the asymmetry was either not present or negligible. See for example the measurements taken at SNEC on 10 March 2014 (bilateral symmetrical eyelid crease of 9mm), at Bumrungrad International Hospital on 28 July 2014 (1mm asymmetry of the bilateral upper eyelid crease) and by A/Prof Shen at SNEC on 20 October 2020 (10mm bilaterally measured with eyes gently closed).

143    In so far as the ptosis of the upper eyelids was concerned, it was Prof Tan’s opinion that the eyelid ptosis seen in February 2014 was in all likelihood caused by the injection of Botox in January 2014. Since Dr Huang had not assessed any eyelid ptosis in September 2016 which was about three years after the blepharosplasty procedure, the subsequent finding of ptosis by Dr Leatherbarrow in May 2018 and by A/Prof Shen in October 2020 had to be due to another cause[note: 107].

In particular, the inadvertent diffusion of Botox injected into the plaintiff’s brow area could have resulted in upper eyelid ptosis, and the brow lifting effect of Botox could also have caused the plaintiff’s upper eyelid creases to look higher than before the Botox treatment was administered[note: 108].

145    A/Prof Shen had attributed the plaintiff eyelid ptosis to the aging process which was not challenged by the plaintiff[note: 109]. In any case, eyelid ptosis was not included as one of the injuries pleaded in the statement of claim so the court is precluded from making any finding in this regard even if there was evidence to support it.

Causation

Whether the injuries alleged by the plaintiff were caused by the acts or omissions of the first defendant

146    It is trite that a claimant in negligence is obliged to show affirmatively that the defendant’s breach of his duty of care has caused him to suffer injury or loss. In this regard, the injuries pleaded in the plaintiff’s statement of claim are:

(a)     discomfort and irritation to the eyes;

(b)     sensitivity to light, sun and wind;

(c)     thick eyelid crease;

(d)     moderate to severe blepharitis on both eyes; and

(e)     right eyebrow ptosis causing right upper eyelid skin fold weighing down.

147    The first defendant submits that even if he were found to have breached his duty of care, the plaintiff has not adduced evidence to prove that the injuries that he allegedly suffered was caused by the first defendant’s alleged breach(es).

148    In his closing submissions, the plaintiff does not appear to have addressed the issue of causation in relation to the injuries pleaded, save for some references to discomfort to his eyes and/or eyelids following the surgery. It is not clear whether the discomfort suffered is linked to the complaint of blepharitis which is defined as inflammation that involves the eyelid margin primarily and is a common cause of chronic ocular irritation[note: 110].

Discomfort and irritation to the eyes

149    I have to agree with the submission from the first defendant that the allegation of discomfort is too general to be regarded as an injury caused by the alleged breach of duty. Some level of discomfort has to be expected after a surgical procedure involving cutting away of skin and tissue at the eyelid area and during the healing phase. The first defendant testified that it was his usual practice to advise his patients that there would be a feeling of tightness which is a type of discomfort due to swelling after the eyelid surgery and he would have advised the plaintiff of this[note: 111]. He was not challenged during cross-examination. Furthermore, the plaintiff had confirmed in his own AEIC that he had been informed that he would feel tightness around his eye after the procedure[note: 112]. He had also confirmed this in the transcripts of his secretly recorded conversations with the first defendant on 4 November 2013[note: 113] and 11 February 2014.

Sensitivity to light, sun and wind; moderate to severe blepharitis

150    The complaint of sensitivity to light, sun and wind as well as irritation to the eyes appear to be associated to the diagnosis of moderate to severe blepharitis. There is no evidence to show that these symptoms have been caused by the upper blepharoplasty surgery carried out by the first defendant:

(a)     Prof Tan’s opinion was that chronic blepharitis and sensitivity to light are not usually caused by blepharoplasty surgeries as these procedures only correct the skin and superficial muscle. Blepharitis is an inflammation of the base of the eyelashes, which is not where the upper blepharoplasty was carried out.

(b)     A/Prof Shen’s expert opinion is that anterior and posterior blepharitis normally occur independent of the types of cosmetic procedure that the plaintiff had received. From his examination of the plaintiff’s medical records, it could have developed between May 2014 (when the plaintiff was examined by Dr Fong Kee Siew, his regular ophthalmologist at Eye Surgeons @Novena) and July 2014 (when the plaintiff was examined by Dr Wanumkarng), as it was not diagnosed by either ophthalmologists, A/Prof Audrey Looi of SNEC or Dr Fong Kee Siew in March and April 2014 respectively. It was therefore A/Prof Shen’s view that the blepharitis is unrelated to the blepharoplasty surgery done by the first defendant as it was only diagnosed some nine months after the surgery.

151    Dr Leatherbarrow had also accepted in cross-examination that the first time the plaintiff developed problems with his corneal surfaces, and was diagnosed with blepharitis, was sometime in July 2014. In his Supplementary Report, he had also agreed with A/Prof Shen that the plaintiff’s blepharitis and dry eye problems were not caused by the blepharoplasty surgery[note: 114]. He did, however, qualify his opinion to state that the blepharoplasty had caused the plaintiff to have an incomplete reflex blink and exposure keratopathy which has exacerbated his dry eye problem.

152    I could not accept Dr Leatherbarrow’s opinion as he did not produce any documentary evidence to support his diagnosis of incomplete reflex blink in the plaintiff. His assertion that the plaintiff had an incomplete reflex blink is at odds with the medical records adduced by the plaintiff from the various ophthalmologists that the plaintiff consulted from 2014 to 2020 at SNEC, his regular ophthalmologist at Eye Surgeons @Novena or even Dr Wanumkarng and his colleague at Bumrungrad International Hospital, Bangkok.

Thick eyelid crease

153    The plaintiff’s claim that he has a thick eyelid crease post-surgery is a reference to the aesthetic outcome of an increased pretarsal show following the upper blepharoplasty surgery. Notably, both the first defendant as well as Prof Tan as specialist plastic surgeons were of the view that the plaintiff’s upper eyelids post-surgery looked acceptable. Even more significant is the fact that the plaintiff himself had admitted in cross-examination that based on the photograph taken of him on 11 February 2014, his appearance was acceptable. In any case, I have to agree with the first defendant’s submission that just because the outcome of a cosmetic procedure did not meet the plaintiff’s personal and subjective expectations, this could not be considered a loss or injury recognised in law.

Right eyebrow ptosis causing right upper eyelid skin fold weighing down

154    This alleged head of injury is based on the medical report of Dr Wanumkarng. The diagnosis made was that of “right eyebrow ptosis [causing] right eyelid skin fold weighing down. Leading to asymmetric eyelid crease.” It is clear, from reading Dr Wanumkarng’s report, that the asymmetric eyelid crease seen on 28 July 2014 was caused by the right eyebrow ptosis[note: 115].

155    Mr Shiever’s closing submissions suggests that the plaintiff is claiming that the right eyebrow ptosis developed from the surgery performed by the first defendant[note: 116]. If so, no medical evidence has been adduced in support of this assertion. During cross-examination, the plaintiff revealed that Dr Wanumkarng had been requested to be his expert witness but he had declined as he knew some of the doctors here. In any case, on the face of Dr Wanumkarng’s report, it is clear that his diagnosis supports the first defendant’s case. He had been asked to review the first defendant’s pre-operation diagnosis on whether the plaintiff “had asymmetric eyes and what this is due to” by Dr Beng Lin Liam of Shanghai East International Medical Center[note: 117]. His reply was as follows[note: 118]:

Regarding to pictures (preoperatively), he has asymmetrical crease (smaller in right side) probably from right eyebrow ptosis.

156    Dr Leatherbarrow had also accepted (see [110] above) that the plaintiff’s right eyebrow ptosis was a pre-existing condition as diagnosed by the first defendant during the first consultation on 24 September 2013. The first defendant had advised the plaintiff of the option of a browlift to correct the brow ptosis, but this had been declined by the plaintiff. The plaintiff cannot now claim damages for a pre-existing condition, for which he was offered treatment and had rejected for his own reasons.

157    Causation of the right eyebrow ptosis leading to right upper eyelid ptosis (eyelid skin fold weighing down) has not been proven.

158    As for the plaintiff having eyelid ptosis after the surgery, the first defendant has shown that post-operatively there was no ptosis when the plaintiff was examined on 4 November 2013. Both Prof Tan as well as A/Prof Shen had testified that from the post-operative photographs of the plaintiff taken by Dr Huang, they did not find any eyelid ptosis. As Dr Huang had not supported his finding with any objective measurements, I accept the views expressed by Prof Tan and A/Prof Shen[note: 119]. The plaintiff did not develop eyelid ptosis from the blepharoplasty performed by the first defendant and the ptosis which was seen on 11 February 2014 was most likely due to the Botox treatment that the plaintiff had been given on 30 January 2014 by Dr Huang. Based on the photograph taken by Dr Huang in September 2016 which did not show any eyelid ptosis, the eyelid ptosis that was diagnosed by Dr Leatherbarrow in May 2018 is most likely to the aging process as opined by A/Prof Shen[note: 120].

Contributory negligence

159    Since the plaintiff has not proven his case in negligence, there is no necessity for me to deal with the issue whether the plaintiff was contributorily negligent. For completeness, in case I have erred in my finding against the plaintiff, there is clear evidence from the plaintiff’s own admissions that he had:

(a)     failed to return for the follow-up consultation with the first defendant scheduled on 4 December 2013; and

(b)     employed self-treatment by using “traditional therapy” with eggs to reduce the swelling in his eyelids.

160    Instead of returning to see the first defendant as scheduled on 4 December 2013, the plaintiff had sought treatment from another plastic surgeon, Dr Huang, and had received Botox treatment to the glabellar region of his face which had caused him to have eyelid ptosis.

161    He had without first seeking medical advice from the first defendant as his primary treating doctor used “traditional therapy” and massaged his eyelids with using eggs purportedly to help reduce the swelling. This could have caused irritation, discomfort or infection to the operation site and compromised the healing process.

162    The first defendant had also raised the matter of the plaintiff having undergone a perm of his eyelashes by a beautician again without seeking medical advice. As this allegation has not been pleaded in the Defence (Amendment No 3), there is no basis for me to make any finding in this regard. It is noted however that the transcript of the plaintiff’s recorded conversation with the first defendant did contain an express reference to this action by the plaintiff.

163    The plaintiff’s failure to return for a follow-up scheduled on 4 December 2013, and his failure to inform the first defendant on 11 February 2014 that he had received Botox treatment by another plastic surgeon would have made it difficult for the first defendant to properly examine and diagnose his condition. Employing unconventional treatment with heated eggs could also have contributed to the discomfort that he suffered.

Damages

164    The issue of damages does not arise for determination since the plaintiff has failed to prove his claim that the first defendant was negligent. Notably, Mr Shiever did not even address this issue in his closing submissions. Even in his reply submissions[note: 121] to the detailed arguments on quantum of damages made in Mr Chong’s closing submissions, he had merely responded with a statement that the plaintiff would rely on the matters raised in his AEIC. There was no further elaboration save to repeat that the outcome of the surgery was not the expected promised result.

165    Looking at the statement of claim and leaving aside the question of general damages for the various heads of injury alleged (for which causation is not proven), I have to agree with Mr Chong that the plaintiff has failed to properly plead his claim for special damages as well as his claim for future medical and care expenses. As such, even if the court had found the first defendant liable to any degree, the various sums claimed in the plaintiff’s AEIC cannot be awarded.

Summary and conclusion

166    For the plaintiff to succeed in his claim in negligence against the first defendant, he had to prove that the first defendant had breached his duty of care:

(a)     in failing to advise or warn the plaintiff of the likely or possible consequences of the upper blepharoplasty procedure undertaken for the treatment of the plaintiff’s upper eyelids;

(b)     in failing to plan and discuss with the plaintiff the various treatment options available to him;

(c)     in failing to carry out appropriate investigations and tests to ascertain the correct diagnosis; and

(d)     in failing to the carry out the upper blepharoplasty procedure and treatment on the plaintiff’s upper eyelids in a manner which was in accordance with the practice accepted by a responsible body of plastic surgeons.

167    On the evidence adduced, I have found that the plaintiff had been adequately advised on the condition of his brows and upper eyelids, and the treatment procedures available, including a brow lift which he declined. The plaintiff had also been informed of the risks and possible complications of the upper blepharoplasty procedure. The plaintiff has not shown how the diagnosis by the first defendant and the manner in which he had carried out the upper blepharoplasty procedure on the plaintiff’s upper eyelids was not in accordance with the practice accepted by a responsible body of plastic surgeons.

168    The plaintiff has also failed to prove that the injuries he claimed to have suffered were in any way caused by the first defendant’s diagnosis and treatment of his condition. The plaintiff’s dissatisfaction with the aesthetic result of his eyelids after the procedure is his subjective opinion which does not provide a legal basis for a negligence claim against the first defendant. Notably, he has resiled from his initial position since he did admit in cross-examination that he could accept his post-operative look as seen in the photographs.

169    In the result, the plaintiff’s case against both defendants is dismissed with costs. On the issue of costs, the parties are directed to file their respective written submissions within 14 days from the release of this judgment.
