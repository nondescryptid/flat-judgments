10 April 2023

District Judge Lim Wee Ming:

Introduction

1       The accused pleaded guilty to a charge of dealing with duty unpaid cigarettes weighing 1,501.656 kilogrammes on which excise duty of $794,185.84 was not paid.

2       The accused was sentenced to 34 months’ imprisonment. An enhanced sentence of 81 days was further imposed, as the offence was committed while he was subject to a remission order. The accused has appealed against the sentence.

Charge

3       The accused pleaded guilty to the following charge:

YOU, … are charged that you, on the 9th day of December 2022, at about 9.03 am, in 1 Sunview Road, Ecotech@Sunview, #08-29, Singapore, were concerned in dealing with uncustomed goods, to wit, 2996 cartons x 160 sticks and 4506 cartons x 200 sticks of assorted brands of duty unpaid cigarettes, weighing 1,501.656 kilogrammes, on which excise duty of $794,185.84 was not paid, with intent to defraud the Government of the excise duty thereon, and you have thereby committed an offence under section 128I(1)(b) of the Customs Act 1960 punishable under section 128L(4) of the same Act.

And further, that you, from 11 June 2021 to 20 May 2023, were subject to a remission order made by the Commissioner of Prisons under Division 2 or 3 of Part VB of the Prisons Act (Cap. 247, Rev. Ed. 2014) which remission order is subject to the basic condition under Section 50S(1) of the Prisons Act and, while the remission order is in effect, you, on 9 December 2022, committed the aforesaid offence and upon conviction and the imposition of a sentence reflected under section 50S(1)(b) of the Prisons Act, shall be deemed to have breached the basic condition of your remission order, and you are thereby liable to be punished under Section 50T(1)(a) of the Prisons Act with an enhanced sentence of imprisonment for a term not exceeding the remaining duration of the remission order of 163 days from 9 December 2022 to 20 May 2023 for that offence.

4       The accused agreed to a charge under the Goods and Services Tax Act 1993, to be taken into consideration for sentencing.

Statement of facts

5       The relevant extracts from the statement of facts are set out below.

The relevant persons

6       Other than the accused, there were three other co-accused persons as follows:

(a)     Loo Siew Mun (“Loo”),

(b)     Muhammad Nur bin Mohd Yunos (“Nur”), and

(c)     Muhammad Syafiiy Salim (“Syafiiy”).

Facts pertaining to the charge

7       On 9 December 2022, at about 8.52am, customs officers arrived at 1 Sunview Road, Ecotech@Sunview, #08-29, Singapore (“the Unit”) to arrest persons concerned in activities involving duty unpaid cigarettes. At about 9.00am, the officers entered the Unit and sighted duty unpaid cigarettes scattered around the unit. At 9.03am, the officers sighted the accused and Nur emerging from a staircase landing outside the Unit and running away. The officers gave chase and detained them. At about 9.18am, customs officers sighted Syafiiy along Pioneer View Rd near 1 Sunview Road. The officers detained Syafiiy and brought all three accused persons back to the Unit.

8       In the presence of the accused, Nur and Syafiiy, the officers conducted a search in the Unit and found a total of 2996 cartons x 160 sticks and 6100 cartons x 200 sticks of assorted brands of duty unpaid cigarettes. The accused, Nur and Syafiiy admitted knowledge of the cigarettes found in the Unit and were arrested.

Investigation findings

9       In late November 2022, the accused responded to a job advertisement and was contacted by an unidentified person known to him as “Ann”. Ann informed the accused that the job involved unpacking of goods and promised to pay the accused $1,000 for every job completed. The accused agreed to the job offer. On 8 December 2022, at around 11.00pm, Ann informed the accused that there was an unpacking job at Ecotech@Sunview, 1 Sunview Road, Singapore and instructed the accused to meet at the location.

10     On 9 December 2022, at about 3.00am, the accused arrived at Ecotech@Sunview as instructed and met Nur, Syafiiy and two other unknown men for the first time. Ann then created a WhatsApp group chat consisting of Ann, the three accused persons and the two unknown males. In the group chat, Ann informed that a Malaysian lorry would be delivering four different brands of duty unpaid cigarettes to Ecotech@Sunview and provided the breakdown of the quantity and the brand of cigarettes. The accused then became aware that the job involved duty unpaid cigarettes and confirmed the same with the four other persons waiting with him. Nonetheless, the accused continued to wait for the arrival of the Malaysian lorry containing the duty unpaid cigarettes.

11     Shortly thereafter, a Malaysian lorry driven by Loo arrived and the accused proceeded to unload the duty unpaid cigarettes from the vehicle with the other four persons. The accused, together with the other four persons, transferred the boxes of duty unpaid cigarettes from the lorry to two unidentified vans parked nearby at the loading/unloading bay of Ecotech@Sunview. The two vans were then driven up to the Unit by Syafiiy and the boxes of cigarettes were unloaded into the Unit.

12     After all the boxes of cigarettes were moved to the Unit at about 8am, Loo left in the lorry while the accused, Nur, Syafiiy and the two unknown persons proceeded to the Unit to unpack and arrange the boxes of cigarettes according to brands as instructed by Ann. Of all the duty unpaid cigarettes seized from the Unit, investigations revealed that Ann had instructed the members of the WhatsApp group, including the accused, to only repack and arrange 2996 cartons x 160 sticks and 4506 cartons x 200 sticks assorted brands of duty unpaid cigarettes (“C1”) in the Unit. At about 9.00am, the accused was in the midst of unpacking C1 when he became aware of the presence of customs officers at the Unit and attempted to flee before he was detained at 9.03am.

13     The accused knew that the excise duty leviable on the seized cigarettes were unpaid at the material time the offences were committed.

14     The excise duty leviable on C1 weighing a total of 1,501.656 kilogrammes in the charge is $794,185.84.

Breach of basic condition of remission order

15     Further investigations revealed that the accused, from 11 June 2021 to 20 May 2023, was subjected to a remission order made by the Commissioner of Prisons under Division 2 or 3 of Part VB of the Prisons Act (Cap 247, Rev. Ed. 2014) which remission order is subject to the basic condition under section 50S(1) of the Prisons Act. While the remission order was in effect, the accused had, on 9 December 2022, committed the aforesaid offences and, upon conviction and the imposition of a sentence reflected under section 50S(1)(b) of the Prisons Act, shall be deemed to have breached the basic condition of the remission order. The accused is thereby liable to be punished under section 50T(1)(a) of the Prisons Act with an enhanced sentence of imprisonment for a term not exceeding the remaining duration of the remission order of 163 days from 9 December 2022 to 20 May 2023 for that offence.

Sentencing submissions

Prosecution’s submissions

16     The prosecution sought a global sentence of 34 to 35 months’ imprisonment with an enhanced sentence of 81 days’ imprisonment.

17     The prosecution highlighted the following sentencing considerations in support of their submission of 34 to 35 months’ imprisonment:

(a)     The quantity of duty unpaid cigarettes involved was significant at 1,501.656 kg and the excise duty evaded on these cigarettes was $794,185.84.

(b)     While the accused did not have any management control or profit share in the operations, he was a paid worker who performed the physical role of repacking the duty unpaid cigarettes on the syndicate’s instructions. He did so for monetary gains and was promised $1000 for each packing job he completed.

(c)     The co-accused, Nur, was convicted of the same offences and sentenced on 11 January 2023 before Senior District Judge Bala Reddy. For the same quantity of duty unpaid cigarettes, Nur was sentenced to 34 months’ imprisonment for the duty charge and 10 months’ imprisonment for the Goods and Services Tax (“GST”) charge. Both sentences were run concurrently. The prosecution submitted that a similar sentence would be appropriate for the accused, given that both Nur and the accused were paid workers engaged to perform the same job of unloading, packing and rearranging the cigarettes in the Unit.

(d)     The proposed sentencing range is consistent with the precedents of Yap Ah Lai v Public Prosecutor [2014] 3 SLR 180 (“Yap Ah Lai”) and Public Prosecutor v Pang Shuo [2016] 3 SLR 903.

(e)     There is one other charge taken into consideration for sentencing. This charge concerns the GST evaded for the same duty unpaid cigarettes. The GST evaded was $61,246.40.[note: 1]

18     For the enhanced sentence, the prosecuted submitted that it should amount to 50% of the outstanding remission period of 163 days, equivalent to 81 days, on the basis that the offence was of moderate severity.[note: 2]

Mitigation plea

19     The accused was in person. He pleaded for leniency and raised the following matters in mitigation:

(a)     He was last released from prison in June 2021 for offences relating to drugs and voluntarily causing hurt.

(b)     He married on 25 November 2021 after his release and has a 6 year old step-son under his care.

(c)     He is living with his 66 year old mother who is suffering from illness and is in need of constant medical attention.

(d)     His wife is currently in custody and due to be released by the end of this year.

(e)     He is the sole breadwinner for the house.

(f)     After his release from prison, he managed to stop taking drugs, worked as a safe distancing ambassador and took up courses.

(g)     He committed the offence herein when facing strain and in a moment of weakness, allowed himself to be influenced by negative peers.

(h)     He admitted his wrongdoing at the outset and cooperated fully with the police during investigations.

(i)     He sought a global custodial sentence of 20 to 25 months’ imprisonment and hoped to return back to his family and resume his responsibilities as a breadwinner.

The court’s decision on sentence

20     I agreed with the prosecution’s submission on sentence.

Section 128I(1)(b) of the Customs Act

21     In Yap Ah Lai, the High Court held that:

(a)     The primary factor to be considered in sentencing for the offence of smuggling is the quantity of tobacco products that has been illegally imported. The greater the amount, the greater is the injury to the public policy of restricting the consumption of such goods and the greater is the amount of the putative loss to the revenue (at [27]).

(b)     The second factor is the offender’s antecedents (at [30]).

(c)     The third factor is whether the offender was acting on his own or was involved in a syndicated operation (at [31]).

(d)     The final factor is the role of the offender (at [32]).

22     The High Court further set out (at [46]), a graduated scheme that cross-references the quantity of tobacco with the duration of the imprisonment term that can be expected to be imposed as a starting point. Where the quantity of tobacco was at the highest band of more than 400 kg, the starting point sentencing range would be between 30 to 36 months’ imprisonment.

23     The quantity of tobacco in the present case is 1,501.656 kg, almost four times more than the base quantity of more than 400 kg for the highest band in Yap Ah Lai, where the starting point sentencing range would be between 30 to 36 months’ imprisonment. With that large quantity in mind, an indicative starting point sentence would be at the upper end of that range. I am of the view that an indicative starting point sentence of 36 months’ imprisonment is reasonable, taking into consideration the multiple times the tobacco involved in this case is above the base quantity for the aforesaid sentencing range.

24     The accused pleaded for a global custodial sentence of 20 to 25 months’ imprisonment. No authority was cited to support this sentencing position that was well below the starting point sentencing range of 30 to 36 months for the quantity of tobacco involved.

25     As for the mitigating factors raised by the accused, the most pertinent is his admission of wrongdoing at the outset and his cooperation in the investigation. Nevertheless, considering that the accused was caught red-handed by customs officers and that he had attempted to run away when they arrived at the scene of the crime, the discount to be given for this factor would have to be tempered.

26     I would also have to weigh any discount against the antecedents of the accused. In Yap Ah Lai, it was held that where the prior offence is not a Customs Act offence, “the fact of the offender’s antecedents should rightly be considered within the rubric of general aggravating factors” (at [30]). The antecedents of the accused included drug offences in 2014 and 2018 and voluntarily causing hurt by dangerous weapons in 2018.[note: 3] Weighing the mitigating factors against the antecedents of the accused, I am of the view that a two month discount in this case is reasonable. This brings the sentence to 34 months’ imprisonment. I note that this is consistent with the sentence that was imposed on the co-accused, Nur.

27     Accordingly, the accused was sentenced to 34 months’ imprisonment.

Enhanced sentence

28     The prosecution further sought an enhanced sentence of 81 days, being 50% of the outstanding remission period of 163 days.

29     In Abdul Mutalib bin Aziman v Public Prosecutor [2021] 4 SLR 1220, the General Division of the High Court held that so long as the fresh offence committed is more than de minimis, that would suffice in general to justify the imposition of an enhanced sentence (at [51]). It was further held (at [53]) that the sentencing factors to be considered can be classified in three broad categories:

(a)     Factors going towards the gravity of the offence committed by the offender while on remission. This may be gleaned from the underlying sentence imposed for that offence (at [55]).

(b)     Factors going towards the offender’s rehabilitative prospects, taking into consideration:

(i)       The timing of the fresh offence.

(ii)       The similarity between the fresh offence and the original offence.

(c)     All other relevant circumstances.

30     In relation to the gravity of the offence, in Public Prosecutor v Mohamad Faizal bin Mohamad Haffir [2021] SGDC 121 (“Mohamad Faizal”), the district judge was of the view that the gravity of the offence was moderate, taking into consideration the quantity of duty unpaid cigarettes for the relevant charge in that case was 401.209kg (at [29(b)]). In the present case, the prosecution has submitted that the gravity of the offence is moderate as well.[note: 4]

31     The quantity of tobacco involved in the present case is almost four times that for the relevant charge in Mohamad Faizal. Furthermore, as I have pointed out earlier, the quantity of tobacco involved in the present case is almost four times the base of the highest band in Yap Ah Lai. The starting point sentencing range for that band is between 30 to 36 months’ imprisonment. The sentence that I have imposed is 34 months’ imprisonment. In the premises, I am of the view that the prosecution’s submission that the gravity of the fresh offence in this case is moderate, is reasonable.

32     On the other hand, the fresh offence was committed in December 2022, about 18 months after the accused was released from prison in June 2021. The remaining remission period of 163 days is about 5 months. Accordingly, the fresh offence was committed after the accused had completed more than two-thirds of his remission period. I also note that the fresh offence is not similar to the original offence. In the circumstances, I am of the view that the rehabilitative prospects of the accused are moderate.

33     Weighing the gravity of the fresh offence and the rehabilitative prospects of the accused, I agree with the prosecution that the appropriate sentencing band is in the moderate range. Under this band, the remaining duration of the remission period that may be imposed would be between one-third to two-thirds.

34     The prosecution referred to Mohamad Faizal in support of their submission that an appropriate point within that range would be 50%. In Mohamad Faizal, the High Court determined an enhanced sentence of 53% of the remission period was appropriate. In that case, the offence was committed just 20 days after the offender’s release from prison. However, as set out earlier, the quantity of tobacco involved in the present case is multiple times higher than in Mohamad Faizal.

35     Although the High Court in Mohamed Faizal did not release the full grounds for its decision, its brief grounds are set out in the LawNet Editorial Note of the District Court’s decision. I found it useful to consider this case, in the absence of any other similar precedents cited by the parties. In the brief grounds therein, it appears that the High Court considered the gravity of the underlying sentence was within the lower end of the moderate band but gave an uplift to the middle of the band, in view of the early re-offending and history of offending.

36     In the circumstances, I am of the view that the prosecution’s submission of an enhanced sentence of 50% of the remission period was reasonable, weighing the much higher quantity of tobacco in the present case against the earlier re-offending in Mohamad Faizal’s case. 50% of the remission period amounts to 81 days, based on the remaining remission period of 163 days.

37     As 81 days is significantly less than the sentence of 34 months imposed for the underlying offence, there is no further adjustment required to take into consideration the totality principle. Accordingly, an enhanced sentence of 81 days was imposed against the accused.

Conclusion

38     In conclusion, I sentenced the accused to a total of 34 months’ imprisonment and an enhanced sentence of 81 days’ imprisonment. As the accused was in remand, the sentence was backdated to the date of arrest.
