Building and Construction Law – Building and construction related contracts – Guarantees and bonds

Judgment reserved

District Judge James Leong Kiu Yiu:

Introduction

1       This is the contractor claimant’s application to restrain the first defendant beneficiary’s call on a performance bond (“bond”) issued by the second defendant. The bond was issued with respect to works that the first defendant homeowner engaged the claimant contractor to undertake under a Letter of Award (“LOA”) dated 5 October 2020 for the continuation of the proposed erection of a 3-Storey Envelope Control Detached House on the first defendant’s property. In response to the call on the bond for $ 147,988.80 on 12 September 2022, the claimant filed the present application and DC/SUM 2924/2022 (“SUM 2924”) for an interim injunction against the first and second defendants. SUM 2924 was heard in the presence of all parties and granted by the Learned District Judge Tan May Tee on 23 September 2022.

2       After hearing submissions from counsel for the claimant and the first defendant (with counsel for the second defendant in attendance content to abide by the decision of the court), I find myself in broad agreement with the submissions of counsel for the claimant on the authority of the High Court decision in CEX v CEY and another [2021] 3 SLR 571 (“CEX”) that the call is rooted in illegality and therefore unconscionable.

Facts

3       Under the LOA, the value of the works was $ 1,479,988.00, the designated Architect was Mr Freddie H K Chia (“Mr Chia”) of Freddie H K Chia Architects while the Quantity Surveyor was BKG Consultants Pte Ltd. The LOA provided that the form of contract would be the Singapore Institute of Architects’ Articles and Conditions of Building Contract (Without Quantities) (2016) – 1st Edition (Updated 2017) (“SIA Conditions”).

4       Pursuant to the LOA, the claimant was engaged by the first defendant in October 2020 to take over the building works from the first defendant’s previous contractor following the demise of Mr Juan Peck Foon (“Mr Juan”), who was the Design Consultant for the project to redevelop the first defendant’s existing family home. The claimant had been recommended to the first defendant by Ms Frances Ng Poh Joo, the wife of Mr Juan who agreed to assist and was involved in the project between July 2020 and late February 2021.

5       The Building and Construction Authority Permit to Commence Works (“BCA Permit”) for the project was issued on 12 November 2020. It listed Mr Chia as the Qualified Person for Architectural Work (“Architectural QP”).

6       The LOA dated 5 October 2020 provides for a contract period of eight (8) calendar months from the date of the BCA Permit. Based on a Final Quotation dated 7 October 2020, the claimants say it should be nine (9) calendar months. Given that the LOA was signed on 5 October 2020, eight (8) months appear to be what was contractually agreed although nothing turns on this for present purposes since both dates have been clearly exceeded.

7       This was followed by a series of events that culminated in the first defendant’s call on the bond and termination of the contract on 12 September 2022. While parties dispute the effect of the correspondence, they do not dispute the fact of the correspondence as outlined below.

(a)     By a letter dated 15 July 2022, the first defendant informed the claimant that Mr Ho Yan Yoy (“Mr Ho”) of YY Architects had been appointed to “… take over Freddie H K Chua Architects (sic) as the Architect for the purposes of the contract…”.[note: 1]

(b)     By a letter dated 18 July 2022, Mr Ho informed the claimant that he had been “… appointed as Architect to assist Freddy H K Chia to supervise, complete the project and obtain TOP and CSC for the client.”[note: 2]

(c)     On 27 July 2022, Mr Ho issued a Delay Certificate under Clause 24 (1) of the SIA Conditions indicating that the contract completion date was 11 July 2021 which had not been met till date.

(d)     On 8 August 2022, Mr Ho issued an Architect’s Direction to the claimant that was followed by a site meeting on 15 August 2022.

(e)     On 17 August 2022, the claimant issued a notice by email to the first defendant to “… temporarily suspend our work because of the non payment and non issuance of interim valuation.”[note: 3]

(f)     On 18 August 2022, BKG Consultants recommended that no monies should be paid to the claimant under their Progress Claim No. 8 dated 5 August 2022 for $ 136,610.40.

(g)     On 18 August 2022, the claimant received a payment certificate from Mr Chia stating that no payments were due to the claimants. This was subsequently clarified to have been sent in error.

(h)     The claimant subsequently received a payment certificate from Mr Ho dated 29 August 2022 stating that no payments were due to the claimants.

(i)     On 9 September 2022, Mr Ho issued a Termination Certificate stating that the owners were entitled to terminate the contract.

(j)     On 12 September 2022, the claimant received the first defendant’s notice of termination and call on the bond.

The claimants’ position

8       As outlined in the claimant’s written submissions and elaborated at the hearing, the claimants submit that:

(a)     The firstdefendant’s call on the bond is rooted in illegality on the authority of CEX as the appointment of Mr Ho to take over as Architect on 15 July 2022 was not notified to the BCA as required under s 8(2) of the Building Control Act 1989 (2020 Revised Edition) (“BCA 1989”). The BCA Permit therefore lapsed, rendering any work on the project and the actions of Mr Ho illegal.

(b)     The capacity of the original Architect Mr Chia to serve as the Architectural QP for the purposes of the BCA Permit was also called into question given that as at 14 September 2022, Mr Chia had been living in a nursing home for almost a year with faltering memory and possible dementia.

(c)     The first defendant was responsible for various delays to the works including securing utilities supply and making changes to the original designs for plumbing and sanitary works, aluminum works, façade and timber door works, electrical works, swimming pool and courtyard works. There were issues with the lightning protection system that the claimant says was completed and certified by Mr Edward Woo (“Mr Woo”) the Professional Engineer on 25 April 2022 although Mr Woo has since revised his opinion on 31 October 2022. There were also issues regarding delays to the lift works that the claimant contends were caused by Covid-19 supply issues and the nominated lift sub-contractor.

(d)     The first defendant’s current stance of seeking liquidated damages from 15 July 2021 is inconsistent with oral assurances not to impose such damages which the claimant relied upon in not applying for extensions of time, and the letter of 6 December 2021 from the first defendant that liquidated damages would only be imposed from 15 December 2021.

(e)     The first defendant’s losses are subject to the threshold of reasonableness, and it is evident that the two quotations relied upon are inflated.

(f)     There is no failure to make full and frank disclosure for the interim injunction hearing and even if there is, the court nevertheless has discretion to continue and/or grant a fresh injunction.

The first defendant’s position

9       As outlined in the first defendant’s written submissions and elaborated at the hearing, the first defendant submits that:

(a)     The call on the performance bond is justified because he is entitled to impose liquidated damages on the claimant as the project is incomplete and in delay; there is no application for nor are the contractors entitled to an extension of time; and he is entitled to rely on the delay certificate issued by Mr Ho.

(b)     The call on the performance bond is justified because he is entitled to damages for rectification work and completion of the project.

(c)     The claimant failed to make full and frank disclosure when applying for the ex-parte interim injunction.

Legal principles and issues

10     Counsel for the claimant and counsel for the first defendant agreed at the outset that the legal test to be applied is a strong prima facie case of unconscionability. They also agreed on the application of the three-step framework propounded by Lee Seiu Kin J (“Lee J”) in CEX at [11] that involved (a) identifying the nature of the performance bond, (b) ascertaining whether the call falls within the terms of the bond and (c) evaluating whether the overall tenor and entire context of the parties support a strong prima facie case of unconscionability. Counsel further confirmed that there is no dispute regarding the first two steps and the only issue is whether there is unconscionability.

11     Accordingly, the key issue for determination is whether the call on the performance bond is unconscionable. At the hearing, counsel for the claimant suggested the following sub-issues that I have edited for clarity:

(a)     whether there is illegality leading up to the call on the bond;

(b)     whether the first defendant is entitled to impose liquidated damages on the claimant;

(c)     who caused delay and whether the claimant is entitled to extension of time for the works;

(d)     whether the first defendant is entitled to terminate the contract on 12 September 2022 and whether the first defendant is entitled to claim completion cost against the claimant and whether the costs is inflated to justify the call; and

(e)     whether there is any failure of the claimant to make full and frank disclosure when obtaining the interim injunction and whether this affects the claimant obtaining a permanent injunction.

12     Counsel for the first defendant added that these sub-issues could be condensed as below although parties were unable to agree on this:

(a)     whether the first defendant is entitled to impose liquidated damages on the claimant; and

(b)     whether the first defendant is entitled to terminate the contract and what is the measure of damages they can claim.

13     In my view, having regard to the merits principle cited by Lee J at [20] of CEX, there is no need for me to determine each and every one of the sub-issues highlighted by the parties. There is similarly no need to rule on every one of the submissions of the parties. That said, the submissions and sub-issues are an important part of the overall tenor and entire context of the conduct of the parties that I must examine to determine whether a strong prima facie case of unconscionability is established.

14     In undertaking this examination, I am guided by Lee J’s observations at [21-22] of CEX that while some authorities appear to discuss the substantive allegations of breach of contract, ostensibly departing from the merits principles, they:

“…simply stand for the proposition that if [the obligor] were able to demonstrate conclusively that there had in fact been no breach, then that would lend weight to its assertions that [the beneficiary] had acted unconscionably: Leighton Contractors (Singapore) Pte Ltd v J -Power Systems Corp and another [2009] SGHC 7 at [9]. Put another way, if the beneficiary’s basis for calling on the bond was baseless, an inference may be raised that the call on the bond was unnecessary and abusive. The court however, is not obliged to always draw this inference…”.

15     In deciding whether there is a breach or whether the call is baseless as above, I am also guided by the decision of the Court of Appeal in Eltraco International Pte Ltd v CGH Development Pte Ltd [2000] 3 SLR (R) 198 at [32] that where there are genuine disputes between the parties, one is entitled to call upon a bond to protect one’s interest. I am also mindful of the high threshold for establishing unconscionability as per [21] of the decision in BS Mount Sophia Pte Ltd v Join-Aim Pte Ltd [2012] 3 SLR 352 (“Mount Sophia”) where the Court of Appeal noted:

21.    When determining if a strong prima facie case has been made out, the entire context of the case must be thoroughly considered, and it is only if the entire context of the case is particularly malodorous that such an injunction should be granted. We must emphasise that the courts’ discretion to grant such injunctions must be sparingly exercised and it should not be an easy thing for an applicant to establish a strong prima facie case.

16     With the above in mind, I proceed to evaluate the overall tenor and entire context of the conduct of the parties while applying the merits principle.

Whether there is illegality

17     As submitted by counsel for the claimant, the facts in our case are akin to that in CEX where the High Court ruled at [45] that the intention of ss 6 and 8 of the BCA 1989 is to ensure that there is a qualified person for building works at all times and where a qualified person does not or is unable to act, the developer must appoint a replacement. Any permit to carry out structural works granted lapses when the qualified person under ss 8 or 11 to supervise the building work ceases to be such. The High Court further ruled at [48] that the permits are not transferable and the specific procedure to appoint a replacement must be followed. As this was not done in CEX, the call was unconscionable as the developer was responsible for at least part of the delay by not appointing a replacement qualified person and it would be illegal to proceed with construction works without a valid permit for the project at the time of the call, as per [67], [68] and [71] of CEX.

18     Given that the appointment of Mr Ho was not notified to the BCA and Mr Chia remained the Architectural QP on record as late as September 2022, I find on the authority of CEX that there is illegality leading up to the call on the performance bond. My finding is fortified by the unanswered question surrounding the capacity of Mr Chia to serve as Architectural QP prior to his replacement by Mr Ho, as his incapacity would lead to an even earlier lapsing of the BCA Permit. As counsel for the claimant has noted, Mr Chia has not filed any affidavit to address his capacity to serve and neither has the first defendant sought to rebut the claimant’s contention.

19     With respect, I see no merit in the contention of counsel for the first defendant that a BCA Permit was not needed when Mr Ho was appointed since structural works had already been completed. This contention is unsupported by any controlling authority and as submitted by counsel for the claimant, incongruent with the legislative intent and framework of the BCA 1989 highlighted at [9] of CEX, to have a qualified person for building works at all times, due to the important duties imposed on such qualified persons. Whether the structural works had been completed by then is also unclear.

20     I also see no merit in the submission that the claimant is not entitled to challenge the appointment of Mr Ho since they did not raise any objections earlier. Firstly, there is no evidence that the claimant was aware of the irregularity in the appointment. Secondly, I am of the view that the claimant is entitled to assume that the statutory formalities would be adhered to by the first defendant whose duty it is to make the appointment.

21     As for whether the Architect under the SIA conditions must also be the Architectural QP, I agree with counsel for the claimant that this is envisaged and required under the regulatory framework having regard to the legislative intent and framework mentioned at [19] above. The first defendant’s submission that this is permitted is also not supported by any controlling authority. That said, even if I am wrong on this and the appointment of Mr Ho for the purposes of the contract is valid, the unanswered question regarding the capacity of the Architectural QP remains, rendering the works illegal in light of s 8 (2) of the BCA 1989 that mandates the appointment of another qualified person if any qualified person becomes unwilling or unable to act, by reason of termination or any other reason. As there is no evidence of Mr Chia’s capacity to perform his role while residing at a nursing home, the validity of the BCA Permit prior to the appointment of Mr Ho is highly questionable, even if Mr Ho’s appointment as architect for the purposes of the contract is valid.

Whether there is material non-disclosure

22     On the issue of material non-disclosure, I agree with counsel for the first defendant on the authority of the Court of Appeal decision in Bintai Kindenko Pte Ltd v Samsung C&T Corp and another [2019] 2 SLR 295 at [80] that the claimants have failed to make full and frank disclosure when applying for the ex-parte summons for an interim injunction by:

(a)     Not drawing the court’s attention to the contractual requirements for an extension of time;

(b)     Claiming that the lift contractor was the first defendant’s contractor rather than the claimant’s nominated sub-contractor; and

(c)     Stating they were not aware that YY Architects was the Project Architect while omitting to include all correspondence with YY Architects following the appointment.

23     The duty to make full and frank disclosure is well established and clear, and the submission of the claimant that none of the non-disclosures would have affected the granting of the ex-parte interim injunction in view of the illegality misses the point and does not excuse non-disclosure. That said, the effect of the non-disclosure is mitigated to an extent by the fact that notice of the hearing was given to the first defendant who attended and contested the ex parte application for the interim injunction.

24     Furthermore, as highlighted by counsel for the claimant, the court does have a discretion to continue or grant a fresh injunction if required by the justice of the case even if there was failure to make material full and frank disclosure as noted at [48] of the Court of Appeal decision in Dauphin Offshore Engineering & Trading Pte Ltd v The Private Office of HRH Sheikh Sultan bin Khalifa bin Zayed Al Nahyan [2000] 1 SLR (R) 117. In my view, the justice of the case calls for a permanent injunction and the non-disclosure is best addressed by an appropriate cost adjustment, rather than setting aside in the circumstances of our case.

Other genuine disputes

25     As for the remaining sub-issues and submissions concerning the causes of the delays, the entitlement of the parties to seek extensions of time, suspend and/or terminate the contract and claim liquidated and other damages, applying the merits principle, I am of the view that these are best left to arbitration. Given the conflicting positions of the parties and the equivocal nature of the evidence in support of the various contentions, I am unable to say with any clarity that one side is responsible for the breach and/or that the call was baseless. In this regard, I am inclined to find that they evidence a genuine dispute between the parties that would have ordinarily entitled the first defendant to make the call but for my findings in relation to unconscionability.

Conclusion

26     For the reasons above, having considered the context of the case in its entirety, I find that while there are genuine disputes between the parties on several issues, there are also strong prima facie indications of illegality in the project administration and inconsistency in the stance of the first defendant that are particularly malodorous. These render the call on the bond unconscionable and warrant the grant of the injunction sought.

27     I will hear parties on costs.
