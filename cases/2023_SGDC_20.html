Introduction

1       Before me are two closely related applications that arise from a seemingly innocuous mistake. The plaintiff initiated a claim against the developer and contractor of a development, seeking damages for defects in his property within the development. About a year after the action was initiated, the contractor applied to strike out the plaintiff’s claim, arguing that he had no locus standi to continue pursuing the claim as he had failed to join his wife, a joint tenant of the property, as a co-plaintiff. In a bid to cure the “defect”, the plaintiff applied to amend his pleadings to join his wife as a party. The defendants fervently resist the amendment, arguing that it would irremediably prejudice their accrued limitation defence.

Background facts

2       In 2011, Mr Loo Kim Chwee (“the Purchaser”) and his wife, Mdm Koh Siew Cheek (“Mdm Koh”) entered into a Sale and Purchase Agreement (“SPA”) to purchase a strata house within a development known as Thomson Grand (“the Development”) from Luxury Green Development Pte Ltd (“the Developer”).

3       The Development was issued its Temporary Occupation Permit (“TOP”) on 16 October 2015,[note: 1] and the Purchaser received the Notice of Vacant Possession (“NVP”) in November 2015.[note: 2]

4       Clause 17 of the SPA provided that within “12 months from the date the Purchaser receives the [NVP] in respect of the Unit”, the Purchaser had the right to notify the Developer of the defects in the Unit. Within one month of receiving a notice from the Purchaser of such defects, the Developer “must make good any such defect”, failing which the Purchaser would have the right to cause the rectification works to be carried out and to recover the cost of the rectification works from the Developer (“the Defects Liability Clause”).

5       The Purchaser took possession of his purchased property (“the Property”) around December 2015, and began to reside there in June 2016. According to him, he then discovered “a large number of defects, including several defects which were very serious in nature” (“the Alleged Defects”).[note: 3] As such, from July 2016, he began giving the Developer and Paul Y. – Lian Beng JV Pte Ltd (“the Contractor”), notice of the Alleged Defects.[note: 4] Both the Developer and Contractor dispute this version of events, and the Developer avers that any relevant defects were rectified.[note: 5]

6       In any case, the Purchaser proceeded to engage a building surveyor to inspect the Property. The surveyor released his report in January 2018, wherein he identified 71 areas that required rectification and remedial works, which he estimated would cost S$91,250.[note: 6]

7       Despite repeated discussions, parties reached an impasse.[note: 7] On 15 October 2021, being one day shy of six years after the TOP had been obtained, the Purchaser commenced the present action against the Developer and the Contractor. By this action, he is seeking damages against them for breaches of contract, statutory duty, and duty of care due to the Alleged Defects that remain unrectified.

Applications before the Court

8       On 16 September 2022, the Contractor took out Summons No 2612 of 2022 to strike out the claim (“the Striking Out Summons”) on the basis that the Purchaser, who holds the Property as a joint tenant with his wife, has “no locus standi to commence this action in his sole capacity”.[note: 8]

9       About one month after the Striking Out Summons was taken out, and in apparent response to the objection raised therein, the Purchaser filed Summons No 3131 of 2022 to amend his Writ of Summons and Statement of Claim (Amendment No 1) to add Mdm Koh as a co-plaintiff, and to introduce an “additional claim in respect of latent defects” that he purportedly discovered around May 2022 (“the Amendment Summons”).[note: 9]

10     Both the Developer and Contractor (collectively, “the Defendants”) resist this amendment, arguing that it amounts in substance to a joinder of a party, which cannot be allowed because the claims premised on the Alleged Defects are time-barred. As regards the amendment for the latent defects, it is submitted that this should also not be allowed because this claim only accrued after the commencement of the action, and so the Purchaser ought to commence a fresh action in respect of those defects.[note: 10]

11     The issues arising from the two applications are as follows:

(a)     First, whether the Purchaser has locus standi to commence the claim in his sole capacity as one of two joint tenants of the Property. If it is determined that he has standing, the Striking Out Summons must fail.

(b)     Secondly, if the Purchaser lacks locus standi to sue in his own capacity, whether he should be allowed to join Mdm Koh as a co-plaintiff. This issue raises several sub-issues centred on whether the claims raised by the Purchaser are time-barred.

(c)     Thirdly, regardless of whether the Purchaser’s present claims are time-barred, whether he should be permitted to introduce the additional claim in respect of the purported latent defects discovered in May 2022.

12     I consider the issues in turn.

Whether the Purchaser has locus standi

13     The issue that lies at the heart of the Striking Out Summons is whether the Purchaser, as one of two joint tenants of the Property, is capable of pursuing this action without joining the other joint tenant of the Property, Mdm Koh, as a co-plaintiff.

Nature of a joint tenancy

14     It is a well-established principle that “each joint tenant’s interest in the property is indistinguishable. Each joint tenant holds nothing by himself; he holds the whole estate with the other joint tenants” (Chan Lung Kien v Chan Shwe Ching [2018] 4 SLR 208 at [29]). As a consequence of the manner of their holding, joint tenants must act jointly to bind their estate (Goh Teh Lee v Lim Li Pheng Maria and others [2010] 3 SLR 364 (“Goh Teh Lee”) at [17]):

Since the interest of each joint tenant is the same in extent, nature and duration, a necessary consequence is that each and every joint tenant must partake in any dealings with the whole legal estate before such dealings may effectively bind the entire estate. An action by one joint tenant, or even by some joint tenants but short of all of them collectively, will not suffice to bind the estate, as the whole estate does not reside in the single joint tenant or the select few (as the case may be).

[emphasis added in bold]

15     “Arising from the requirement that joint tenants must act jointly before the estate may be bound by their actions is the rule that in cases involving actions as to the joint estate, one joint tenant may not sue or be sued without joining the others” [emphasis added] (Goh Teh Lee at [21]). This is echoed in Halsbury’s Laws of Singapore vol 14 (LexisNexis, 2023 Reissue) at para 170.0035:

In actions as to the joint estate one joint tenant may not sue or be sued without joining the others, and a judgment for possession against one joint tenant is not an effective judgment. One joint tenant may not part with the title deeds of the property without the consent of the other. Nor can one of several joint lessees surrender rights held jointly before the full period of the lease has run… [emphasis added]

16     Hence, in Lui Yuk Yin v Lam Chuen [2006] HKCU 359, the plaintiff wife who sought a declaration that she and her husband were beneficial joint tenants of a property was held to lack locus standi to bring the action as her husband was not a party to the action. In Goh Teh Lee, the appellant lacked locus standi to appeal against the Strata Titles Board’s decision to grant an order for the collective sale of a development because his ex-wife, a co-owner of his unit within the development, had agreed to the collective sale.

Action improperly commenced in the Purchaser’s sole name

17     Returning to the facts here, the Purchaser’s claim against the Defendants is for damages caused by the Alleged Defects in the Property that he jointly holds with Mdm Koh, who is at present not a party to the proceedings.

18     This is closely analogous to the case of Williams v British Gas Corporation [1981] 1 EGLR 165 (“Williams”), an authority that was followed by the Court of Appeal in Goh Teh Lee (at 366). In Williams, the claimant brought a claim against the defendant for the pollution of lakes which allegedly depreciated the value of his property. According to the claimant, the corporation was negligent, and he wished to claim against them under Part I of the Land Compensation Act 1973 (UK). The tribunal found that he lacked the locus standi to commence or maintain the claim against the defendant because, among others, he had failed to join his wife, the joint owner of the property, as a claimant (at 165):

[The claimant] said that his wife was not willing to be joined with him as claimant. He was not willing to join her as a respondent. …

…

It is clear law that, although as between themselves joint tenants (and, therefore, joint owners) have separate rights, as against everyone else they are in the position of a single owner. There is absolute unity between them and together they form one person. Apart from equitable remedies inter se, one of the joint tenants cannot commence any proceedings without the aid of the other or others.

…

The claim which [the claimant] seeks to put forward … is either a claim for compensation for injurious infection … or a claim for compensation for depreciation caused by use of public works… In either case he has, in our judgment, no locus standi to commence or maintain the proceedings unless his wife be joined with him as a claimant. …

[emphasis added in bold]

19     Faced with the weight of the authorities which make amply clear that a joint tenant like the Purchaser is incapable of seeking relief in respect of the Property without joining the other joint tenant(s), the Purchaser appears to accept, albeit belatedly, that it was improper for him to sue the Defendants in his sole name. In his affidavit for the Amendment Summons, he candidly admits that the amendment to include his wife “would allow [him] to correct the present defect in the Writ of Summons … and stave off a challenge by the Defendants as to whether [he has] locus standi to bring the present claim.”[note: 11]

20     Before me, counsel for the Purchaser, Mr Lam Kuet Keng Steven John (“Mr Lam”) also admitted that based on the authorities, the Purchaser has “no locus standi to commence [the] action in his own name alone”. I was not pointed to any cases that have detracted from the consistent line of authorities summarised above, which speak singularly in supporting the Defendants’ submission that unless and until all the joint tenants are parties to the proceedings, the claim in respect of their property (jointly held) simply cannot proceed. Indeed, in Williams, after the tribunal observed that the claim “must be struck out unless [the claimant’s wife,] Mrs Williams[,] be joined as a claimant with her husband”, they proceeded to make clear that the claimant would be given time for such a course to be taken (Williams at 166). As such, I turn to consider whether the Purchaser can join his wife as a co-plaintiff.

Whether the Purchaser’s wife can be joined as a party

Joinder of party under an amendment application

21     To join his wife, the Purchaser has taken out the Amendment Summons, seeking leave to amend the Writ of Summons to add Mdm Koh as the 2nd Plaintiff pursuant to O 20 r 5(1) of the Rules of Court 2014 (“ROC 2014”).

22     An “amendment” to join parties was also sought in Management Corporation Strata Title Plan No 3322 v Mer Vue Developments Pte Ltd [2016] 4 SLR 351 (“Mer Vue (CA)”). There, the management corporation (“MCST”) brought proceedings against the developer of the condominium for building defects. Midway during the proceedings, the MCST sought to amend the further and better particulars to add another 113 subsidiary proprietors. This amendment would significantly increase the damages recoverable by the MCST if the contractual cause of action succeeded. However, by the time the amendment application was filed, the contractual claim was time-barred, and so the High Court judge disallowed the amendment.

23     In upholding the decision to bar the addition of the 113 subsidiary proprietors, the Court of Appeal observed that the proposed amendment was in substance a joinder of 113 new parties (Mer Vue (CA) at [19]). Given that “[t]he court would not allow the joinder of a claimant as a party if the relevant limitation period of that claim has expired at the time of the application” (Mer Vue (CA) at [18]), the proposed “amendment” to add the 113 subsidiary proprietors could not be permitted.

24     As in Mer Vue (CA), the effect of the proposed amendment to add Mdm Koh is to join her as a party to the action. In the circumstances, O 15 r 6 of ROC 2014, which provides for the joinder of parties, is the applicable provision (see Mer Vue (CA) at [15]). This is so even though the Amendment Summons also engages O 20 r 5 because the Purchaser has sought to add a claim for latent defects, which he says surfaced sometime in May 2022, after the present claim had been commenced; as the court observed in Mer Vue (CA) at [12], “it is possible for a set of proposed amendments to engage both [O 15 r 6 and O 20 r 5 of ROC 2014] … in so far as the amendment constituted both a joinder of new parties and an amendment of pleadings” [emphasis added].

The rule on joinder is subject to limitation

25     The court’s denial of the “amendment” to include additional parties after the expiry of the limitation period in Mer Vue (CA) is not unique.

26     In the earlier case of Abdul Gaffer bin Fathil v Chua Kwang Yong [1994] 3 SLR(R) 1056 (“Abdul Gaffer”), the court similarly barred the joinder of a claimant after the limitation period had expired. There, the plaintiff, who was involved in a road accident, initially commenced proceedings against the employer of the driver who caused the collision only. During the trial, the driver admitted that he had been negligent, and the plaintiff thus sought to add the driver as a co-defendant. Disallowing the application, the Court of Appeal held that “[u]ntil limitation stepped in, it was a matter of choice for the [plaintiff] to decide whether to sue the [driver]”. However, O 15 r 6(2) was subject to, and did not override limitation. Hence, it was not “necessary” to add the driver as a defendant (Abdul Gaffer at [16]).

27     In Ernest Ferdinand Perez De La Sala v Compania De Navegacion Palomar, SA and others and other appeals [2018] 1 SLR 894 (“Ernest Ferdinand”), the Court of Appeal broke down the elements of O 15 r 6(2)(b) of ROC 2014 into two separate stages. For ease of reference, the provision is set out:

(2)    Subject to the provisions of this Rule, at any stage of the proceedings in any cause or matter, the Court may, on such terms as it thinks just and either of its own motion or on application —

…

(b)    order any of the following persons to be added as a party, namely:

(i)    any person who ought to have been joined as a party or whose presence before the Court is necessary to ensure that all matters in the cause or matter may be effectually and completely determined and adjudicated upon;

(ii)   any person between whom and any party to the cause or matter there may exist a question or issue arising out of or relating to or connected with any relief or remedy claimed in the cause or matter which in the opinion of the Court it would be just and convenient to determine as between him and that party as well as between the parties to the cause or matter.

[emphasis added in italics and bold italics]

28     According to the court, O 15 r 6(2)(b) contains two alternate bases to add parties. Both sub-para (i) (“the necessity limb”) and sub-para (ii) (“the just and equitable limb”) are controlled by a non-discretionary requirement that the joinder be done “at any stage of the proceedings”, meaning that the power to add a party only exists if “something still remains to be done in a case” (Ernest Ferdinand at [195] and [202]). Once this threshold is crossed, and regardless of whether the necessity limb or the just and equitable limb is relied upon, the court must “consider all the factors which are relevant to the balance of justice in a particular case” in deciding whether the joinder should be allowed (Ernest Ferdinand at [205]).

29     Saliently, the court highlighted the importance of the applicable limitation period, holding that “[w]here the claim by or against the party sought to be joined is time-barred at the date of application, joinder will not be allowed” [emphasis added] (Ernest Ferdinand at [206], citing Abdul Gaffer at [16] and Mer Vue (CA) at [18]). This is because “the rule on joinder of parties in O 15 r 6(2) [of ROC 2014] is ‘subject to limitation’ and should not be applied to ‘override the limitation period’” (Mer Vue (CA) at [18], citing Abdul Gaffer at [16]).

30     Given the decisions in Mer Vue (CA), Abdul Gaffer, and Ernest Ferdinand, Mr Lam conceded that if the Purchaser’s claims are time-barred, he would not be able to join his wife as a co-plaintiff to “rectify the defect” in his cause papers.

Whether the claim is time-barred

31     The Defendants submit that the six-year limitation period for the Purchaser’s claim began to run, at the latest, from 16 October 2015, when the TOP was issued. As such, the limitation period expired on 16 October 2021, about one year before the Amendment Summons was taken out on 28 September 2022.

32     By contrast, the Purchaser submits that the limitation period only began to run after the defects liability period (“DLP”) expired. In this regard, clause 17.1 of the SPA provides that the DLP of 12 months begins to run “from the date the Purchaser receives the [NVP]”. As the NVP was given to the Purchaser in November 2015, the DLP of 12 months ran until November 2016. Therefore, the six-year limitation period only expired in November 2022 and the Amendment Summons, filed in September 2022, was within time.

33     In considering the parties’ submission, it is important to clearly delineate the claims raised by the Purchaser:

(a)     First, he seeks damages from the Developer for breaches of the SPA for, among others, failing to build the Property in a “good and workmanlike manner”, failing to ensure that the Property is “fit for [its] intended purpose”, failing to carry out the works and/or repairs of the Property with proper skill and care, and failing to rectify the defects during the DLP upon notification by the Purchaser (collectively, “the Contractual Claims”).[note: 12]

(b)     Second, he seeks damages from both Defendants for breaches of their duty of care and/or statutory duty for, among others, failing to construct the Property free of defects, failing to rectify any defects expeditiously and effectively before the expiry of the DLP, and failing to adhere to the building regulations when carrying on the works at the Development (collectively, “the Tortious Claims”).[note: 13]

34     Parties accept that the Contractual and Tortious Claims are both subject to s 6 of the Limitation Act 1959 (“Limitation Act”), which provides that “actions founded on a contract or on tort” “shall not be brought after the expiration of 6 years from the date on which the cause of action accrued” [emphasis added]. I pause at this juncture to observe that while the Purchaser’s Tortious Claims are separately premised on the tort of negligence (ie, breach of duty of care) and breach of statutory duty, the latter head remains a tort, and is subject to the six-year time-bar: see Management Corporation Strata Title Plan No 2911 v Tham Keng Mun and others [2011] 1 SLR 1263 at [65]–[68].

35     When then do the causes of action accrue? The test differs for the Contractual and Tortious Claims.

Contractual Claims

36     A cause of action framed in contract accrues when the alleged breach occurs (Lian Kok Hong v Ow Wah Foong and another [2008] 4 SLR(R) 165 (“Lian Kok Hong”) at [22]).

37     In Management Corporation Strata Title Plan No 3322 v Mer Vue Developments Pte Ltd and others (King Wan Construction Pte Ltd and others, third parties) [2016] SGHC 28 (“Mer Vue (HC)”), the developer submitted that the contractual claims for the building defects began to run from the date of completion of the building. On the other hand, the MCST submitted, on behalf of the subsidiary proprietors, that time only began to run from the date its expert conducted the initial site inspections. Chan Seng Onn J (as he then was) considered that there were several possible dates that could be taken as the latest date of accrual for the contractual claims for building defects, namely (a) the date of issuance of the TOP; (b) the date of service of the NVP to the original purchasers; or (c) the date of issuance of the Certificate of Statutory Completion (“CSC”). In suggesting that the date of TOP was the appropriate reference point, Chan J observed as follows (Mer Vue (HC) at [33]):

Clearly, the alleged contractual breaches of design and construction duties would necessarily have occurred during the design and construction phases of the development process, before the issuance of TOP, NVP or CSC. The performance of the contractual duties of supervision of the design and construction by [the developer], including the performance of the contractual obligations of the design and construction by [the developer’s] own contractors would have been over by the time the TOP, NVP or CSC was obtained. However, the date that the TOP was granted would probably be the last conceivable date of the alleged contractual breaches by [the developer] and thus could be taken as the latest possible date of accrual of the contractual actions by the additional subsidiary proprietors. NVPs are issued by developers after TOPs are granted, and are usually close to, but still possibly after, the TOP date.

[emphasis in original]

38     Chan J then explained that, on the facts before him, the limitation period would have expired even if he took a later start date, such as the date of expiry of the DLP (Mer Vue (HC) at [34]):

Here, the TOP for the Development was issued in two stages on or about the same dates the NVPs were issued to the original purchasers by [the developer]: on 22 April 2008 and 28 May 2008. These were clearly more than six years before 30 June 2015 (the date on which the [MCST] filed its application to amend the F&BPs in SUM 3193 [to add more subsidiary proprietors]). Even if I were to take the Plaintiff’s case at its highest and considered either the date of legal completion on 24 December 2008 or the date of the expiry of the 12 months defects liability period (that began after the issuance of NVP and would have ended by April and May 2009) as relevant, the limitation period would still have expired before the date of the Plaintiff’s application on 30 June 2015.

[emphasis added]

39     Understandably, while counsel for the Defendants focused on [33] of Mer Vue (HC), counsel for the Purchaser resorted to [34] of the same judgment as support for his submission that the limitation period only began to run from the date of expiry of the DLP. According to Mr Lam, before the DLP expired, any breach would not have crystallised as, pursuant to the Defects Liability Clause, the Developer would have the opportunity to rectify any defects upon notice by the Purchaser. Put another way, it is submitted that the Purchaser was “precluded from commencing action against the Defendants prior to the expiry of the DLP ([during which] the Defendants were given the opportunity to rectify the defects in the Property)”.[note: 14] In my judgment, this is misconceived.

40     In Sandy Island Pte Ltd v Thio Keng Thay [2020] 2 SLR 1089 (“Sandy Island”), the Court of Appeal considered a similar defects liability clause. As in this case, clause 17 of the sale and purchase agreement there required the developer to make good any defects that became apparent within the DLP of 12 months. The court recognised the practical utility of defects liability clauses, noting that “[p]rior to the introduction of these kinds of clauses, contractors had no right to return to site to rectify defects” (Sandy Island at [43]). Nonetheless, such clauses are not a “complete code that will cater for all defects” because, for example, a “defects liability clause does not cover latent defects or defects that will manifest after the DLP” [emphasis in original] (Sandy Island at [48]). Defects liability clauses thus provide an additional right or source of remedies for an aggrieved party, and are not intended to be in substitution for the common law rights to sue for damages upon discovery of defects during the DLP (Sandy Island at [69] and [70]):

[Hudson’s Building and Engineering Contracts (Sweet & Maxwell, 11th Ed, 1994)] stated, at para 5-053, that in the absence of express provision, the remedies under maintenance clauses were in addition to, and not in substitution of the common law rights:

It is always a question of construction whether the rights under the maintenance clause are intended to supplant the right to damages at common law altogether. In the absence of express provision, the remedies under these clauses are in addition to and not in substitution for the common law rights, and even where the defects have appeared within the period the owner may sue for damages rather than call on the contractor to do the work, subject, in that event, to the possibility of the owner’s damages being limited, if he has acted unreasonably in the light of the discussion in the preceding paragraphs, to the cost to the contractor of doing the work at that time, rather than the possibly greater cost of bringing in another contractor either then or at a later date.

70    Similarly, [Keating on Construction Contracts (Sweet & Maxwell, 8th Ed, 2006)] made clear at para 10-023 that:

The contractor’s liability in damages is not removed by the existence of a defects clause except by clear words, so that in the absence of such words the clause confers an additional right and does not operate to exclude the contractor’s liability for breach of contract.

[emphasis in original removed, emphasis added in bold]

41     As such, unless the contract expressly provides otherwise, the Purchaser’s right to seek damages for breach of contract arising from the Alleged Defects is, conceptually speaking, separate and in addition to his right to seek relief under the Defects Liability Clause. Here, there was no legal impediment in the SPA or otherwise that precluded the Purchaser from suing for damages discovered during the DLP while simultaneously notifying the Developer of the defects and seeking rectification of the same. If the Developer failed to rectify the defects upon notification, this would constitute a separate breach of the Defects Liability Clause, provided the notifications were made during the DLP. These notifications, if made, would not prejudice the Purchaser’s initial and existing common law rights to seek damages for the defects discovered prior to the notification of said defects.

42     Given the distinct nature of a claim for pre-existing defects and a claim for breaches of a Defects Liability Clause, Ms Hannah Chua, who represented the Developer, helpfully reclassified the Contractual Claims into two “buckets” or sub-categories:

(a)     first, the Contractual Claims pertaining to defects existing at the time of TOP (“the Original Breaches”); and

(b)     second, the Contractual Claims pertaining to the Developer’s alleged breaches of its obligation to rectify under the Defects Liability Clause (“the Defects Liability Clause Breaches”).

(1)   The Original Breaches

43     As regards the Original Breaches, I am strongly persuaded by Chan J’s reasoning in Mer Vue (HC) ([37] supra) at [33], of which I am bound, that breaches of the Developer’s design and construction duties would necessarily have occurred during the development process, and thus precede the issuance of the TOP or NVP. In other words, the latest date that the Original Breaches could occur was on the date the NVP was issued. From that date, construction activities would have ceased, although the Purchaser could, within the DLP, issue notices of defects of the Original Breaches. Upon such notice, the Developer would be obliged to rectify the defects within one month of the notice, failing which the Developer could be additionally liable for rectification costs incurred by the Purchaser.

44     I recognise that Chan J then went on to observe that “[e]ven if I were to take the Plaintiff’s case at its highest and considered either the date of legal completion … or the date of the expiry of the 12 months defects liability period … as relevant, the limitation period would still have expired” (Mer Vue (HC) at [34]). In my judgment, the words “even if” clearly demonstrate that his Honour was not regarding the later dates as the proper reference points for when the contractual cause of action accrued. Rather, he was explaining that even if he took the latest possible date of accrual, namely the expiry of the DLP, the time-bar would still have set in. That this was not intended to defer the date of accrual of the contractual cause of action is made eminently clear by the preceding paragraph, where Chan J had already concluded that “the date that the TOP was granted would probably be the last conceivable date of the alleged contractual breaches by [the developer] and thus could be taken as the latest possible date of accrual of the contractual actions by the additional subsidiary proprietors” [emphasis in original] (Mer Vue (HC) at [33]).

45     Since the Development received its TOP on 16 October 2015,[note: 15] and the Purchaser received the NVP in respect of his Property in November 2015, the cause of action for the Original Breaches would have accrued, at the latest, by November 2015. The Amendment Summons was only taken out in September 2022, more than six years after November 2015. Accordingly, the claim for the Original Breaches is time-barred.

(2)   The Defects Liability Clause Breaches

46     Turning then to the Defects Liability Clause Breaches, the Purchaser’s pleaded case is that he had notified the Developer of the defects in his Property from 5 July 2016 to 1 April 2022.[note: 16] Bearing in mind that the DLP ran for 12 months from the date of receipt of the NVP (ie, from November 2015 to November 2016), the only relevant notices of defects (per the Purchaser’s own pleaded case) were issued on 5 July 2016 and 20 August 2016. All other notices were sent after the expiry of the DLP, and thus could not constitute fresh breaches of the Defects Liability Clause, which had ceased to be effectual by then.

47     In this regard, clause 17.2 of the SPA required the Developer to make good any defects “within one month of … receiving a notice from the Purchaser” of such defects. Hence, the latest possible breach of the Defects Liability Clause occurred on or around 20 September 2016, one month after the notice of defects was issued on 20 August 2016. However, six years from 20 September 2016 is 20 September 2022, which is a few days shy of 28 September 2022, when the Amendment Summons was taken out. Therefore, even if I were to accept that the Purchaser gave the Developer notice of the defects for which he now claims on 20 August 2016, such a claim would remain time-barred.

48     In sum, the Purchaser’s Contractual Claims are time-barred by s 6(1)(a) of the Limitation Act because both the Original Breaches and Defects Liability Clause Breaches accrued more than six years before the Amendment Summons was filed.

Tortious Claims

49     The Purchaser has also claimed against both Defendants for breaches of duties of care and/or statutory duties.

50     Claims in tort accrue when the victim suffers damage due to the tortfeasor’s breach of duty of care. In the context of a claim in tort for defects in a building, such damage is suffered “when the defects manifest themselves in the form of physical damage to the building”: Millenia Pte Ltd (formerly known as Pontiac Marina Pte Ltd) v Dragages Singapore Pte Ltd (formerly known as Dragages et Travaux Publics (Singapore) Pte Ltd) and others (Arup Singapore Pte Ltd, third party) [2019] 4 SLR 1075 (“Dragages”) at [478]– [480].

51     In Pirelli General Cable Works Ltd v Oscar Faber & Partners (A Firm) [1983] 2 AC 588 (“Pirelli”), the defendants, a firm of consulting engineers, were engaged by the plaintiffs to advise on the design and provision of a chimney. The chimney was built around July 1969, but cracks began to develop by April 1970. The damage was only discovered by the plaintiffs in November 1977, and it was found that they could not with reasonable diligence have discovered it before October 1972. In October 1978, the plaintiffs commenced a claim against the defendants for, among others, damages for negligence in relation to the design of the chimney. The defendants argued that the claim was time-barred as the cause of action in tort accrued more than six years prior to the commencement of the action.

52     Agreeing with the defendants, Lord Fraser of Tullybelton (issuing the authoritative judgment of the House of Lords) made clear that “[t]he plaintiff’s cause of action will not accrue until damage occurs, which will commonly consist of cracks coming into existence as a result of the defect even though the cracks or the defect may be undiscovered and undiscoverable” [emphasis added] (at 603). In preferring the date of damage to the date of discoverability, his Lordship referred to the decision of Lord Pearce in Cartledge v E. Jopling & Sons Ltd [1963] AC 658 at 778–779, which in his view showed the absurdity of resorting to the plaintiff’s knowledge (or the date of discoverability) as the decisive factor for when a tortious cause of action accrues:

It would be impossible to hold that while the X-ray photographs are being taken he cannot yet have suffered any damage to his body, but that immediately the result of them is told to him, he has from that moment suffered damage. …

53      Pirelli was followed by the Court of Appeal in Lian Kok Hong ([36] supra). Focusing on the date the damage occurred, the court in Lian Kok Hong held at [24] that the negligent act of failing to exercise closer supervision and more frequent inspection happened when the contractor carried out the allegedly unsatisfactory and unacceptable works, which necessarily occurred on or before 19 March 2022, when the contract was terminated.

54     The date the damage occurred was also the reference point for determining when the tortious claim accrued in Management Corporation Strata Title Plan No 2827 v GBI Realty Pte Ltd and another [2014] 3 SLR 229 (“GBI Realty”). In that case, the development was completed in 2003. Between 2004 and 2005, the MCST began making complaints to the contractor of the development about defects around the development caused by sinking ground. In 2007, the MCST engaged a surveyor to produce a report on the problem of soil settlement, and a report was produced in July 2007. Subsequently, in 2009, the MCST commenced proceedings against the developer, and in 2013, they sought to join the contractor as a co-defendant. This was disallowed by an assistant registrar, and the decision was upheld by Woo Bih Li J (as he then was), who held that the tortious claim against the contractor was time-barred (GBI Realty at [27]):

On the strength of Pirelli, it is likely that the cause of action accrued as early as 30 March 2004. That was the date the MCST first complained of the sunken driveway. The sinking of the driveway was akin to the “cracks coming into existence as a result of the defect”. But taking the MCST’s case at its highest, the damage must have come into existence, at the latest, by the date Mr Casimir conducted the site inspection on 29 May 2007. The damage would have come into existence by then as it was observed by Mr Casimir during his inspection.

55     Returning to the present case, the Purchaser avers that owing to the Defendants failure to carry out the works with reasonable skill and care, his Property presented with defects that have caused him to suffer loss. In this regard, it is important to recall the difference between the date of materialisation of the defect and the date the defect becomes discoverable; the tortious cause of action accrues from the date of materialisation, not discoverability: see Pirelli ([51] supra).

56     As explained in Mer Vue (HC) ([37] supra) at [33], it is likely that any defects in construction would have occurred during the design and construction phases of the development process, which precedes the issuance of the TOP. Hence, it is likely that the defects arose even before the TOP was issued in October 2015. Considering that the damage is suffered when “defects manifest themselves in the form of physical damage to the building” (Dragages ([50] supra) at [480]), it is likely that the damage was suffered on or around the date of TOP in October 2015.

57     It is irrelevant in this regard that the Purchaser only moved into the Property around June 2016. According to him, this is when he moved into the Property, and discovered the Alleged Defects.[note: 17] Such defects caused him to engage a building surveyor “to assist him with the proper identification and listing of these defects”.[note: 18] Hence, per his own case, the surveyor’s task was limited to identifying defects that were already existing, or which had already materialised, within the Property. The belated discovery of the Alleged Defects does not defer the date on which the Tortious Claims accrued. Nonetheless, even if I were to take the date when he moved into the Property (June 2016) as the date his Tortious Claims accrued, this would still be more than six years before he took out the Amendment Summons in September 2022. By any appropriate measure, his Tortious Claims are therefore time-barred.

58     For completeness, I observe that while the Purchaser has sought to include a new claim for latent defects, he has not alleged that any of the present claims are in respect of latent defects that had not manifested on or around June 2016.

59     Nonetheless, even if there were latent defects that only arose around the time the surveyor was appointed, any claim for such latent defects would also be time-barred by s 24A(3)(b) of the Limitation Act. That provision stipulates that any claim for latent defects must be brought “within 3 years from the earliest date on which the plaintiff” had “the knowledge required for bringing an action”. As explained in Lian Kok Hong ([36] supra) at [42], the requisite knowledge for the time to begin running under s 24A(3)(b) is simply that the plaintiff must know the factual essence of his complaint. Here, the extensive list of defects for which the Purchaser seeks compensation is set out in the surveyor’s report that is annexed to his Statement of Claim.[note: 19] Hence, he must have known of the factual essence of his complaint, including any claims for the latent defects not existing when he moved into the Property in June 2016, by January 2018, when the surveyor’s report was released. Applying the extended limitation period in s 24A(3)(b), the three-year limitation period for the claims for latent defects (if any) would similarly expire by January 2021, more than a year before he took out the Amendment Summons.

60     Accordingly, the Purchaser’s Tortious Claims are time-barred pursuant to ss 6(1)(a) and/or 24A(3)(b) of the Limitation Act.

61     Given the operation of the time-bar in respect of both the Contractual and Tortious Claims, I agree with the Defendants that the belated application to join the Purchaser’s wife as a co-plaintiff cannot be allowed. To do so would be to effectively allow the Purchaser to prosecute his Contractual and Tortious Claims when such claims would be time-barred if brought under a new writ, irremediably prejudicing the Defendants’ limitation defence (Mer Vue (CA) ([22] supra) at [48(a)]).

Whether the Purchaser can add a claim for latent defects

62     The final prong of the Purchaser’s Amendment Summons is to add a claim for latent defects, which allegedly arose on or about the end of May 2022, when he discovered a water leakage from the jacuzzi balancing tank on the Property.

63     This amendment, the Developer submits, is an ill-disguised attempt to “hook” a claim that is not time-barred onto the more critical amendment of joining Mdm Koh as a party to cure the Contractual and Tortious Claims.[note: 20] Be that as it may, I proceed to consider whether such an amendment should be allowed.

64     In Singapore Civil Procedure vol 1 (Cavinder Bull, gen ed) (Sweet & Maxwell, 2021) at para 20/8/3, it is noted that an amendment to pleadings “takes effect, not from the date when the amendment is made, but from the date of the original document which it amends”. Because of this, “a plaintiff may not amend his writ by adding a cause of action which has accrued to him since the issue of the writ”.

65     This principle that a party cannot introduce a cause of action which did not exist at the date of the writ emanates from the decision of Eshelby v Federated European Bank, Limited [1932] 1 KB 254 (“Eshelby”). There, the plaintiff commenced a writ in November 1930, seeking payment of a first instalment from the defendant. Subsequently, in March 1931, the referee granted the plaintiff leave to amend his claim to add the amount of a second instalment which fell due in January 1931. Reversing the referee’s decision to allow the amendment for the second instalment claim, Swift J noted (at 262):

… The Court has amended the statement of claim which is endorsed upon the writ, but it has not amended the writ itself; and, indeed, it could not have done so, because in order to make this action on the second instalment one which would come within the writ, the date of the writ would have had to be altered from November 27, 1930, upon which it was issued and soon after it was served, up to some date after January 15, 1931[, when the second instalment fell due]. I do not think the Court could possibly alter the writ in that way. …

66     The rule in Eshelby was endorsed by the Court of Appeal in The “Jarguh Sawit” [1997] 3 SLR(R) 829 (“Jarguh Sawit”), where it was held (at [58]) that “a new cause of action may not be introduced if it arises after the date of the writ”.

67     This position was subsequently doubted in Galistan Gerard Clive Martin trading as Rick’s Technical Services v L & M Prestressing Pte Ltd [1999] SGHC 77 (“Galistan”). In that case, Lim Teong Qwee JC observed that the House of Lords had in Ketteman and others v Hansel Properties Ltd and others [1987] 1 AC 189 observed (at 220) that “[w]hether an amendment should be granted is a matter for the discretion of the trial judge and he should be guided in the exercise of the discretion by his assessment of where justice lies”. Accordingly, Lim JC formed the view that “the ‘rule’ in the Eshelby case is inconsistent with these developments and I think it is no longer valid” (Galistan at [12]).

68     Notwithstanding the observation in Galistan, the rule in Eshelby remains applicable in Singapore. Galistan, as can be observed, was a High Court decision, whereas Jarguh Sawit was a decision of the Court of Appeal. While the apex court was subsequently invited to review the correctness of Jarguh Sawit and its endorsement of Eshelby in Chuang Uming (Pte) Ltd v Setron Ltd [2000] 1 SLR 166 (“Chuang Uming”), it declined to do so (Chuang Uming at [73]–[74]). Recently, in DyStar Global Hospitals (Singapore) Pte Ltd v Kiri Industries Ltd and others and another suit [2018] 5 SLR 1, the Singapore International Commercial Court cited the decisions of Eshelby and Jarguh Sawit as standing for “the principle that a cause of action must be established as at the date when proceedings are instituted, ie, the date of the writ” (at [136]); parties in that case accepted this as the operative principle.

69     Therefore, the position remains that a party cannot be allowed to amend its pleadings to add a cause of action that did not exist when the writ was first issued. Before me, Mr Lam conceded that the authorities were with the Defendants in this regard. As such, the Purchaser’s attempt to amend his Statement of Claim to add his claim for the latent defects which did not exist at the time the writ was filed cannot be allowed.

70     Even if I accept that I have the discretion (per Galistan) to allow such an amendment, I do not think that this is a case that merits such an amendment. As I have held, the present claims raised by the Purchaser are time-barred. The effect of the amendment to include the claim for latent defects is therefore to revive an otherwise empty claim. Furthermore, such an amendment would lead to the curious outcome whereby the cause of action for latent defects would revert back to the date of the writ, which pre-dates the accrual of that cause of action (see [65] above). The claim for latent defects is also not time-barred, and so there is nothing preventing the Purchaser from re-initiating the claim by filing a fresh writ with his wife as a co-plaintiff. This, in my view, would be a neater and more principled outcome than if I were to allow the present claim to stand on a fresh last leg.

Conclusion

71     In conclusion, I find that the Purchaser, as one of two joint tenants of the Property, lacks the locus standi to carry on the proceedings against the Defendants in his sole name. While he has sought to cure this defect by applying to add his wife as a party to the action, he cannot be allowed to do so because his existing claims against the Defendants, whether in contract or in tort, are time-barred. Furthermore, I do not allow his application to add a new cause of action for latent defects as this arose after the writ of summons was filed, and cannot be added per the rule in Eshelby ([65] supra), which remains the law in Singapore.

72     For the foregoing reasons, I dismiss the Amendment Summons in its entirety. Consequently, the fundamental defect in the Purchaser’s claim cannot be remedied, and his claim against the Defendants is struck out in its entirety.

73     In closing, I record my appreciation for counsel for their useful submissions and for directing my attention to the relevant authorities which I have referred to over the course of this judgment.
