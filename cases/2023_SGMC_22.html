12     I now set out the grounds of my decision in full. 

Elements of the offence

13     To prove its case on the eight proceeded charges, the Prosecution had to establish beyond a reasonable doubt all the elements of the present charges namely:

(a)     The accused did the said acts as set out in the charges intending thereby to cause hurt, or knowing that she is likely thereby to cause hurt to both Joan and Jenefer;

(b)     Joan and Jenefer suffered hurt, defined to include “bodily pain” as a result of the accused’s acts;

(c)     Joan and Jenefer were domestic maids and

(d)     The accused was either “an employer of a domestic maid or a member of the employer’s household”.

The Prosecution's Case

14     It was the Prosecution's case that the accused had voluntarily caused hurt to Joan on three occasions in October 2018 as well as sometime in 2018 when she kicked Joan on her chest, used her hand to hit Joan’s head and hit Joan’s torso with a stick. Jenefer had witnessed the abuse on the first two instances when Joan was kicked in her chest and when she was hit on her head by the accused. Both incidents happened on the same day as Joan was assisting the accused to put on her socks and her shoes. No one else was present during the incident when Joan was hit on her torso with her stick by the accused.

15     Further, it was the Prosecution’s case that the accused had voluntarily caused hurt to Jenefer on five occasions sometime in September 2018 when she pinched her right bicep, pinched her stomach, pinched her chest, pinched her left arm, and pinched her right thigh. Four of the incidents occurred when Jenefer was massaging the accused while the remaining incident occurred when Jenefer was rearranging a paper lantern in the garden. In some of these incidents when the injuries manifested in bruises, Jenefer took photographs of these bruises[note: 2] using her mobile telephone.

The Defence's Case

16     The accused’s defence is one of a complete denial of the victims’ allegations. Throughout the trial, she maintained her shock at being prosecuted for these offences.

Issues to be determined

17     The issues to be determined were largely factual namely:

(a)     whether the accused voluntarily caused hurt to Joan on three occasions as stated in the charges when she kicked Joan on her chest, used her hand to hit Joan’s head and hit her torso with a stick;

(b)     whether the accused voluntarily caused hurt to Jenefer on five occasions as stated in the charges when she pinched various parts of her body namely her right bicep, her stomach, her chest, her left arm and her right thigh;

(c)     whether both Joan and Jenefer suffered hurt including bodily pain as a result of the accused’s acts.

Undisputed facts

18     The following facts are undisputed:

(a)     The accused’s husband Mr Sim Guan Huat had employed Joan as a domestic maid sometime in October 2015. He also employed Jenefer sometime in August 2018.

(b)     At the material time, both Joan and Jenefer worked as domestic maids at 24 Paradise Island and the accused is a member of their employer’s household. The accused, her husband and their two children reside at 24 Paradise Island.

(c)     Although the accused’s husband was Joan’s and Jenefer’s employer, it was the accused who primarily managed them by giving them the necessary instructions in respect of their household duties. Compared to the average household, the accused’s requirements in terms of cleanliness were higher due to her various allergies.

(d)     The two victims, Ms Gaborne Clayrimae Balasa (“Clayr”) and Ms Zin Mar Phyo (“Mar Phyo”) who worked at 24 Paradise Island had different work hours compared to other domestic maids. They did not have fixed sleeping time and would sleep in the day whenever the accused was asleep as they had to attend to the accused’s needs whenever she returns home at night or the early hours of the morning until she had gone to sleep or when she was awake.

(e)     The other domestic helpers had previously worked for the accused’s family include Clayr who was employed from 20 March 2017 to August 2018, Mar Phyo who was employed from 2015 to 2016 and Hnin Pwint Aye (“Emily”) who was employed from around July or August 2018 to 8 October 2018.

(f)     The domestic maids employed to work at 24 Paradise Island were expected to do household chores such as cooking, washing, ironing cleaning, gardening as well as to take turns to massage the accused until she fell asleep. At the material time, the accused suffered from bodily pain as she had been involved in two traffic accidents and required massages to relieve her pain.

(g)     In Jenefer’s standard employment contract[note: 3], it was provided that her employer was to provide her with eight hours of continuous rest daily (except for occasions of special care cases), with reasonable rest periods during working hours.

(h)     The incidents of abuse first surfaced on 17 October 2018 when Ms Francisco Ma Erica Sedano (“Erica”) called the Ministry of Manpower (“MOM”) to report that her friend who was working at 24 Paradise Island was beaten by her employer. She had called MOM as her friend Jennelyn sought her help as her sister, Jenefer was mistreated by her employer. On the same day at about 9:50am, one Ms Nurul from the MOM Employment Standard Branch made a 999 call to the police to make a report[note: 4].

(i)     On 17 October 2018, a party of police officers including ASP Lynn Tan Wei Ping (“IO Lynn Tan”) from Clementi Division visited 24 Paradise Island. IO Lynn Tan interviewed both Joan and Jenefer and directed the police photographer Lim Juay Hong Leslie to take photographs[note: 5] of the scene, its vicinity as well as of the two victims. Subsequently, she left 24 Paradise Island and brought both Joan and Jenefer to Alexandra Hospital for medical examination.

(j)     On 17 October 2018, both Joan and Jenefer were seen by Dr Tai Zhao Ming (“Dr Tai”) at about 3:18pm at the Urgent Care Centre of Alexandra Hospital. Dr Tai prepared two medical reports on Joan dated 21 October 2018[note: 6] and 2 November 2018[note: 7] as well as a medical report on Jenefer dated 21 October 2018[note: 8].

(k)     There were various CCTV cameras installed at 24 Paradise Island as indicated by Clayr in the set of photographs[note: 9]. The CCTV cameras were used to monitor the domestic maids and could store recorded footages.

(l)     On 19 October 2018, the police seized two CCTV servers from the accused’s home[note: 10] to ascertain if there were footages of the incidents of abuse. When the two servers were inspected by the police, it was discovered that there were no hard disks in the two servers.

19     The present case turns primarily on my determination of whether the victims’ account or the accused’s account of the events at the material time should be preferred. In my assessment of their credibility, I was guided by the case of Farida Begam d/o Mohd Artham v Public Prosecutor [2001] 3 SLR(R) 592 where the High Court has set out three key considerations in assessing a witness’s credibility namely:

(a)     her demeanour;

(b)     the internal consistency, or lack thereof, in the contents of her evidence; and

(c)     the external consistency, or lack thereof, between the contents of her evidence and the extrinsic evidence eg the evidence from other witnesses.

20     Where there is no other evidence is available, a complainant’s testimony can constitute proof beyond reasonable doubt when it is so unusually convincing as to overcome any doubts that might arise from the lack of corroboration (AOF v PP [2012] 3 SLR 34 (“AOF”) at [111]). Where the complainant’s evidence is not unusually convincing, an accused’s conviction is unsafe unless there is some corroboration of the complainant’s story.

21     In the present case, to prove its case against the accused, the Prosecution had relied on the direct evidence of the victims, documentary evidence and extrinsic evidence from independent witnesses.

Prosecution’s Evidence

The victims’ evidence

Joan’s testimony

22     Joan testified that she worked for the accused’s household at 24 Paradise Island from October 2015 until October 2018. The accused would give her instructions on what to do and at times, her husband would instruct her with the accused’s permission. During her period of employment, the other domestic maids who worked at 24 Paradise Island included Mar Phyo, Emily, Clayr, Jenefer and one other Myanmar national. Subsequently from August 2018, it was only Jenefer and Joan who remained until 17 October 2018.

23     Joan further testified that aside from cooking, washing, cleaning the house, ironing of clothes, she also cleaned the car, did gardening, fixed some electrical wirings, cleaned the air conditioners, painted walls or the whole house as well as massage the accused. Her sleep time depended on whether the accused was sleeping as if she was awake, the domestic maids were not allowed to sleep regardless of the time of the day. On weekdays from Mondays to Fridays, only the accused and the domestic maids would be in the house as her husband would go to work while her daughters would be in school.

24     Joan also testified that she would normally eat two meals a day as there were so much household chores to do. According to her, there was not enough food as although the accused went to the market, the domestic maids were not allowed to cook the food unless the former had instructed them what to cook and they would eat the leftovers or instant noodles. The domestic maids were not allowed to cook their own food as the accused disliked or could not take the smell of their food. If they made mistakes or cooked their food with the accused’s permission their salary would be deducted.

25     When describing her relationship with the accused, Joan testified that sometimes it was good and sometimes it was not as the accused would hit or beat her and used words like ‘Stupid’. She was unable to recall the number of times the accused had hit her but was able to recall four incidents. She further testified that the accused began hitting her in 2016. According to Joan, she was able to recall four occasions where the accused had kicked her chest, beat her head with a ring on her finger, beat her head with a stick and beat her hips with a stick.

26     During her cross-examination, Joan testified that she did not tell anyone about these incidents or call for help as she did not have any physical evidence of the assaults. When the police came on 17 October 2018, she saw it as a chance to tell the police about these four incidents which she could not forget as she was hurt.

Amended first charge – kicking Joan in the chest

27     Joan had testified that on 12 October 2018, at the front porch of 24 Paradise Island, the accused kicked her chest as she was putting on the accused’s socks and fixing the accused’s shoes. According to her, the kick was painful, and she could still feel the pain after five days. She explained that the accused got angry with her for putting on her socks wrongly.

28     At [5] of Joan’s statement recorded on 17 October 2018 at 3.52pm[note: 11], she stated that on 12 October 2018 between 7:20pm to 7:35pm she was at the front porch bending down to help the accused put on her socks then her shoes. The accused told Joan that she had put on the socks wrongly. She wanted Joan to take it off and to put them on properly. The accused suddenly kicked Joan on her chest using her right leg.

29     Joan’s account was corroborated by Jenefer who was present. She testified that she saw the accused kick Joan once while Joan was squatting down and putting on the accused’s shoes. She further testified that while she did not see where the kick landed, she thought that the accused had kicked Joan on Joan’s chest. According to her, this happened at the main door of the house, as shown in photograph P32. She thought that Joan made a mistake putting on the accused’s shoes and explained that the accused got angry and kicked Joan.

Amended second charge – Hitting Joan’s head with her hand

30     Joan had testified that in October 2018, while she was standing on the red floormat[note: 12], the accused used her finger with a ring on it to hit her head hard. She felt pain when the accused hit her. She was almost bending down to put on the accused’s socks when the accused hit her head. She explained that the accused was very angry and had been screaming for her socks.

31     At [6] of Joan’s statement recorded on 17 October 2018 at 3.52pm, she stated that there was another incident while the accused was preparing to leave the house. She had been waiting at the end of the stairs to help the accused wear her socks. She was unable to recall what exactly happened, but the accused suddenly hit her head using her hand and she was wearing rings on that hand.

32     Joan’s account was corroborated by Jenefer who testified that she saw the accused knocking on Joan’s head once using her hand while wearing a ring. She heard a sound which was “not very loud” when this happened. According to her, this happened at the staircase, while Joan was standing on the red mat. She was unable to recall whether it took place in September or October 2018 and could not remember why the accused had hit Joan. She was shocked by the incident but did not do anything about it.

Incident where Joan was hit with a stick in the kitchen

33     Joan testified that she was able to recall a third incident sometime around September or October 2018 in the wet kitchen where the accused had used a stick to hit Joan’s hand as she was cooking the accused’s food. She also testified the stick was as long as her arm and was as thick as her small finger. The stick came from the branch of a plant belonging to the neighbour. Nevertheless, when probed further by the Prosecution on the incident in the kitchen, Joan gave evidence that she was hit on her head with a stick and felt pain. According to her the accused was unhappy with the way Joan had cooked her food. She was unable to recall if anyone else was in the kitchen with them at the time of the incident.

Amended third charge – hitting Joan’s torso with a stick

34     Joan had testified that in 2018, the accused hit her hips with a stick at Mr Sim’s balcony of his room on the third floor. She felt “just a bit” of pain. She explained that the accused was angry because there was lizard or bird faeces at the floor of the balcony. She further testified that her feelings were hurt because she had already cleaned the balcony. Assessment of Evidence and Findings

95     To prove its case on the eight proceeded charges, the Prosecution led evidence from eight witnesses while the accused and her husband were the witnesses for the Defence. The Prosecution had relied on the victims’ testimonies and extrinsic evidence in the form of documentary evidence as well as the evidence of independent witnesses.

96     The accused’s defence was a complete denial of the victims’ allegations. It was the Defence’s position that the evidence of both Joan and Jenefer had to be unusually convincing for the Prosecution to sustain a conviction against the accused. The Defence had also sought to impeach the credit of four of Prosecution’s witnesses namely Jenefer, Joan, Clayr and Mar Phyo on account of the material inconsistencies between their oral testimony and their previous statements.

97     In my assessment of the victims’ evidence before me, I was mindful that although Joan alleged that the accused began hitting her in 2016, the relevant period of abuse for Joan was in October 2018 for the first two amended charges and in 2018 for the third amended charge while for Jenefer, the relevant period of abuse was between September to October 2018. The trial commenced in December 2020 and lasted 22 days until 12 September 2022. Jenefer gave evidence over two tranches namely on 9 to 10 December 2020 and 10 February 2021 while Joan gave evidence over two tranches from 4 to 6 April 2022 and 12 September 2022. There was a lapse of more than four years for Joan and more than two years for Jenefer from the occurrence of the events in question to their testimonies in court. Hence, it was necessary that their testimonies should be assessed with this context in mind.

98     It was observed by the Court of Appeal in Sandz Solutions (Singapore) Pte Ltd and others v Strategic Worldwide Assets Ltd and others [2014] 3 SLR 562 at [56] that caution should be exercised when relying on the uncorroborated recollection of a witness. Conversely, a witness should not be found to be less credible merely because of gaps in his memory, particularly where a long period has passed since the occurrence of the events in question.

99     In addition, the High Court in Public Prosecutor v Singh Kalpanath [1993] 3 SLR(R) 158 at [60] held that adequate allowance must be accorded to the human fallibility in retention and recollection. It is also common to have varying account of the same incident by the same person. No one can describe the same thing exactly in the same way over and over again: see Chean Siong Guat v PP [1969] 2 MLJ 63 at [63] to [64].

Assessment of Joan’s evidence

100    In the present case, I found Joan to be a truthful witness who gave a credible and candid account of the two incidents on 12 October 2018 save for the incident in 2018 at the balcony of Mr Sim’s bedroom.

101    In respect of the first amended charge, I found her testimony to be internally consistent with her written statement recorded on 17 October 2018 at 3.52pm[note: 31], where she stated that the accused had suddenly kicked her on her chest using her right leg as she had put on the accused’s socks wrongly. Her account was externally consistent and corroborated by Jenefer who was present who she saw the accused kick Joan once while Joan was squatting down and putting on the accused’s shoes.

102    Joan’s account was also externally consistent with Dr Tai evidence that he noted tenderness over Joan’s sternum when he examined Joan on 17 October 2018. He had testified that his examination findings of tenderness over her sternum was consistent with a kick in the chest. His impression was that Joan sustained musculoskeletal central chest pain.

103    Dr Tai gave evidence that Joan had reported to him that she was kicked in the chest by her employer and experienced pain around her sternum. This was also reflected in his case notes that Joan had told him that the accused kicked her because the accused was angry with how she had put on the accused’s socks.

104    Although the accused had denied kicking the Joan in the chest over the wearing of her socks, it was undisputed that Joan had assisted the accused to wear her socks at the material time. It was clear that the accused was unhappy with Joan as she testified that she merely nagged and scolded Joan because Joan “can’t even wear socks after being so long here”. It was also undisputed by the Defence that Jenefer was present in the vicinity at the material time.

105    In the present case, I found that the inconsistencies in Joan’s evidence in respect of the first amended charge as highlighted by the Defence in their written submissions were not material but were minor and insignificant. As set out above, it is common to have varying account of the same incident by the same person. No one can describe the same thing exactly in the same way repeatedly particularly when subjected to the rigours of a lengthy cross-examination. Given the lapse of time, in my view such discrepancies were not unexpected and did not affect Joan’s credibility. Hence, I agreed with the Prosecution that Joan’s overall evidence that she was kicked once in the chest had satisfied the key elements of the first amended charge beyond a reasonable doubt. I rejected the accused’s evidence that she did nothing further beyond nagging and scolding her.

106    In respect of the second amended charge, I found her testimony to be internally consistent with her statement recorded on 17 October 2018 at 3.52pm at [6] where she stated that there was another incident where the accused suddenly hit her head using her hand and she was wearing rings on that hand.

107    In addition, Joan’s account was externally consistent and corroborated by Jenefer who testified that she saw the accused knocking on Joan’s head once using her hand while wearing a ring. She heard a sound which was “not very loud” when this happened.

108    In the present case, I found that the inconsistencies in Joan’s evidence in respect of the second amended charge as highlighted by the Defence in their written submissions were not material but were minor and insignificant. As set out above, it is common to have varying account of the same incident by the same person. Given the passage of time between the incident and her testimony, such discrepancies were not unexpected and did not affect Joan’s credibility.

109    Hence, I agreed with the Prosecution that Joan’s overall evidence that the accused had hit once on the head using her hand with a ring had satisfied the key elements of the second amended charge beyond a reasonable doubt. Hence, I rejected the accused’s bare denial that she would not hit anybody using an expensive ring as it would be spoiled.

110    In respect of the third amended charge involving the incident of abuse in 2018 at the balcony of Mr Sim’s bedroom, Joan testified that in 2018, the accused hit her hips with a stick. During cross-examination, when asked what time the incident occurred, Joan testified that it was ‘very late in the afternoon almost night-time’. She then clarified that it was either around 12:00am to 1:00am or 2:00am to 3:00am. She initially testified that she felt “just a bit” of pain but during cross-examination, she agreed with the Defence that she felt no pain but had felt being hit. Unlike the incidents on 12 October 2018, Jenefer was not present, nor did Joan inform Dr Tai about this incident during her medical examination. According to Joan, Mr Sim was not in his room and only she and the accused were in his room at the material time.

111    The Prosecution had relied on her statement recorded on 21 December 2018 at 5.10pm[note: 32] where she stated that the accused would get angry for not cleaning and used the stick to hit different parts of her body. The Prosecution had also relied on Jenefer’s statement recorded on 21 December 2018 at 6.10pm[note: 33], where she stated that Joan did inform her that the accused used the stick to hit different parts of Joan’s body.

112    In respect of the third amended charge, I was of the view that Joan’s evidence had to be unusually convincing before the elements of the charge was made out as it was uncorroborated by extrinsic evidence. I found that both Joan and Jenefer’s statements were not corroborative as it related to unspecified instances of abuse involving different parts of Joan’s body. I was also troubled by the inconsistent timings provided by Joan to as when the incident took place. She had initially testified that it happened very late in the afternoon almost night-time. She then clarified that it was either around 12:00am to 1:00am or 2:00am to 3:00am.

113    In addition, although Joan initially testified that she felt “just a bit” of pain, during cross-examination, she agreed with the Defence that she felt no pain but had felt being hit. As the Prosecution bears the onus of establishing all the elements beyond a reasonable doubt, I was of the view the element of hurt was not satisfied as Joan testified that she felt no pain. I agreed with the Defence that the amended third charge was not made out at all as the key element ie that hurt was caused had not been proven.

114    On the facts, I found her evidence in respect of the amended third charge internally inconsistent and uncorroborated by extrinsic or objective evidence. I also did not find Joan’s evidence on this incident so unusually convincing as to overcome the doubts I had from the lack of corroboration. Hence, I found that the amended charge had been proven by the Prosecution beyond a reasonable doubt.

Impeachment proceedings

162    The Defence had also sought to impeach the credit of four of Prosecution’s material witnesses namely Joan, Jenefer, Clayr and Mar Phyo on account of the material inconsistencies between their oral testimony and their previous statements.

163    As submitted by the Prosecution, in Kwang Boon Keong Peter v Public Prosecutor [1998] 2 SLR(R) 211, the High Court explained at [18] that “the credit of a witness refers to his character and moral reliability whereas the credibility of a witness refers to his mental capacity and power to be a witness of veracity”. The High Court also explained the meaning and purpose of impeachment as follows at [19]:

There is a close relationship between the credit and credibility of a witness. The credit or discredit of a witness relates to his credibility. This brings us to the question of what it means by impeaching a person’s credit. The Oxford English Dictionary (2nd Ed) defines the word, “impeach”, as “to challenge, cast an imputation upon, attack”. To impeach a witness’ credit is to disparage or undermine his character and moral reliability and worth. The purpose of the impeachment of a witness’s credit is to undermine his credibility by showing that his testimony in court should not be believed because he is of such a character and moral make-up that he is one who is incapable of speaking the whole truth under oath and should not be relied on. …

164    For the credit of a witness to be successfully impeached under s 157(c) of the Evidence Act 1893, the following requirements must be met (Peter Kwang at [21]-[26]):

(a)     The witness’s former statement must be proved;

(b)     There must be an actual inconsistency between the witness’s oral evidence and the statement which is not a minor difference or an apparent discrepancy;

(c)     The inconsistency must be so serious or material as to affect the credit of the witness;

(d)     The conflicting versions must be carefully explained to the witness, and he or she must have a fair and full opportunity to explain the difference; and

(e)     The witness is unable to provide a convincing or credible explanation for the difference.

165    Material inconsistencies are defined as inconsistencies that go to the crux of the charges against the accused person: see Peter Kwang at [26] and Mohammed Zairi bin Mohamad Mohtar v Public Prosecutor [2002] 1 SLR(R) 211 (“Mohammed Zairi”) at [34]).

166    As submitted by the Prosecution, the High Court cases of Ang Lilian v Public Prosecutor [2017] 4 SLR 1072 (“Ang Lilian”) and Tay Wee Kiat v Public Prosecutor [2018] 4 SLR 1315 (“Tay Wee Kiat”) provide useful guidance on what could amount to material inconsistencies in maid abuse cases:

(a)     First, the starting point is that adequate allowance must be accorded to human fallibility in retention, recollection, and description, especially when a significant period of time has lapsed, or the witness had been abused on many occasions (Ang Lilian at [29] and Tay Wee Kiat at [31], referencing PP v Singh Kalpanath [1995] 3 SLR(R) 158 at [54] and [60]).

(b)     Second, inconsistencies as to dates and times are generally minor and immaterial (Ang Lilian at [29] and Tay Wee Kiat at [32]).

(c)     Third, inconsistencies in the lead-up to the assault may not be material. In Tay Wee Kiat, Tay was charged with pulling the victim out of the car. Although the victim was inconsistent as to whether her luggage had been searched before the assault, the three-judge High Court found that this was not a material inconsistency and explained that the circumstances leading up to the abuse were “at best, peripheral matters which do not go to the heart of the charge” (at [35]).

(d)     Fourth, inconsistencies on the details of the assault may not be material. Four examples from Tay Wee Kiat and Ang Lilian:

(i)       Tay was charged with directing the victim and another helper to slap each other ten times. In her statements to the police, the victim told the police that “the Prayer Incident” involved Tay using a cane to hit her but testified during her examination-in-chief that “the Prayer Incident” culminated in Tay directing her and the other helper to slap each other ten times. The High Court accepted the victim’s oral testimony despite the inconsistency as it was corroborated by the other helper. It considered that the victim’s candid disclosure of the inconsistency on the stand showed that she was a “generally honest witness who was concerned with telling the truth” (at [36]).

(ii)       Tay was initially charged with slapping the victim, kicking her, pulling her hair, grabbing her chin, and pushing her against a wooden cabinet. However, during cross-examination, the victim said there was no slapping or kicking (Tay Wee Kiat at [37]). The Prosecution amended the charge to remove the reference to slapping and kicking. The High Court found that the victim’s inconsistency on this did not cast doubt on the conviction, because her evidence “fell squarely within the particulars of the amended charge” (at [38]).

(iii)       Tay was also charged with hitting the victim with a cane. During her examination-in-chief, the victim testified that a bundle of three canes was used. However, under cross-examination, she said that a bamboo stick was used. The High Court found at [39] that this inconsistency was not material to the charge as it “did not affect her evidence that she was hit with a type of stick instrument, whether a bunch of three canes or a bamboo stick … [both of which] could fall within the wording of the charge”.

(iv)       Ang was charged with dragging the victim to the toilet, spraying water at her eyes with a hose, and slapping her more than once. During her examination-in-chief, the victim testified that the spraying of water lasted “[a]bout 5 minutes, 10 minutes. 5 to 10 minutes”. Under cross-examination, the victim testified that the “whole incident” lasted “5-10 minutes”. The victim eventually conceded that she was not able to tell how long her face/eyes were sprayed with water. Despite this inconsistency, the High Court accepted at [35] that this detail was not material as “it did not detract from the fact that she has maintained throughout her testimony that her eyes [were] sprayed with water”.

(e)     Fifth, inconsistencies as regards the sequence of events for the assault may not be material. In Ang Lilian, the victim’s evidence during her examination-in-chief was that she was hit once on her head with a three-pin-plug, pulled by her hair, pushed to the floor, and then slapped three times on her cheeks. During her cross-examination, she said she was pulled by her hair, pushed to the floor, hit on her head with the three-pin plug, and then slapped on her cheeks. The High Court found at [37] that this inconsistency in the sequence of events was minor “given that the nature of the individual acts and the attack as whole remained the same”.

167    Nevertheless, I was mindful that even if the witness’s credit is successfully impeached, this does not mean that all his or her oral testimony will have to be disregarded (Public Prosecutor v BZT [2022] SGHC 91 at [176]). The court will still have to scrutinize the whole of the evidence to determine what is true and which aspects should be disregarded (Teo Geok Fong v Lim Eng Hock [1996] 2 SLR(R) 957 at [42]).

168    In the present case, I found that Joan’s credit was not impeached as she was largely consistent in her accounts about the key elements of the first amended charge and the second amended charge. Although I was not convinced that the element of hurt was satisfied in respect of the third amended charge, this did not mean that she lacked credibility or that her entire evidence of the physical abuse she suffered should be rejected in its entirety. This is because her evidence in respect of the two other charges were internally and externally consistent and was corroborated by extrinsic or objective evidence as set out above.

169    I considered that for Joan there was a long lapse of time of between three and a half to more than four years between the incidents of abuse in 2018 and her oral testimony on 4 to 6 April 2022 as well as 12 September 2022. Similarly, there was a long lapse in time between the dates on which her police statements were recorded ie 17 October 2018 and 21 December 2018 and her testimony. Furthermore, Joan was abused on multiple occasions from 2016 to 2018 such that she could only recollect four incidents. It would therefore be extremely difficult for her to remember the details of each incident with a high degree of accuracy.

170    In addition, I agreed with the Prosecution that testifying in court can be a very stressful experience even for witnesses who are able to understand English and follow court proceedings. Even more so for Joan, a foreigner who had to rely on the assistance of a Tagalog interpreter. Furthermore, the stress and pressure applied on her during cross-examination was immense as over the course of about two and a half days, Joan was repeatedly accused of lying and grilled on the details and different aspects of her evidence.

171    As stated at [99] above, I found Joan to be a truthful witness who gave a credible and candid account of the of the two incidents on 12 October 2018 save for the incident in 2018 at the balcony of Mr Sim’s bedroom. On a careful and holistic analysis of the facts, I found that Joan’s evidence in respect of the first two amended charges were not only internally and externally consistent on crucial points, but it was also corroborated by extrinsic evidence in the form of documentary evidence as well as independent witnesses. Hence, I found the various inconsistencies raised by the Defence in respect of the said charges involving Joan were minor and not significant and did not go to the crux of the charges. In any event, these discrepancies can be explained by the fallibility of human memory.

172    In the present case, I found that Jenefer’s credit was not impeached as she was largely consistent in her accounts about the key elements of the fourth amended charge to the eighth amended charges. As a whole, I found that none of the inconsistencies raised by the Defence as regards the five charges involving Jenefer were material inconsistencies. Jenefer’s evidence was consistent throughout that she was pinched by the accused, and for some of the charges the bruises or mark on her the respective parts of her body were caused by the accused’s pinching.

173    I considered that there was a lapse of time ie more than two years between the incidents of abuse and Jenefer’s oral testimony which was given over two tranches on 9 and 10 December 2020 and 10 February 2021. Similarly, there was also a lapse in time between the dates on which her police statements were recorded namely, 17 October 2018 and 21 December 2018 and her testimony. Furthermore, Jenefer was abused on multiple occasions. It would therefore be extremely difficult for her to remember the details of each incident with a high degree of accuracy.

174    In addition, I agreed with the Prosecution that testifying in court can be a very stressful experience even for witnesses who are able to understand English and follow court proceedings. Even more so for Jenefer, a foreigner who had to rely on the assistance of a Tagalog interpreter. Furthermore, the stress and pressure applied on Jenefer during cross-examination was immense as over the course of about two and a half days, Jenefer was repeatedly accused of lying and grilled on the details and different aspects of her evidence. I considered the fact that Jenefer broke down more than once during the process of cross-examination and was also in visible distress at certain points during cross-examination.

175    As stated at [114] above, I found Jenefer to be a truthful witness who gave a credible and candid account of the various pinching incidents as set out in the five amended charges. On a careful and holistic analysis of the facts, I found that Jenefer’s evidence was not only internally and externally consistent on crucial points, but it was also corroborated by extrinsic evidence in the form of documentary evidence as well as independent witnesses. Hence, I found the various inconsistencies raised by the Defence in respect of the five charges involving Jenefer were minor and not significant and did not go to the crux of the charges. In any event, these discrepancies can be explained by the fallibility of human memory.

176    It was also the Defence’s position that Clayr was not a reliable witness, and her credit should be impeached. It was submitted that Joan had repeatedly testified that she was only kicked once by the accused. However, Clayr claimed to have witnessed the accused assault Joan far more frequently. Clayr testified that between March 2017 and around 9 August 2018, she had seen the accused kick Joan 50 or more times and seen the accused use a stick to hit Joan almost every day during the about 18 months that she worked for the accused’s family.

Sentencing

Prescribed Penalty

184    The prescribed penalty for the present charge of voluntarily causing hurt under s 323 of the Penal Code is a term of imprisonment of up to two years, or with a fine which may extend to $5,000 or with both. As the offence was committed against a domestic maid, s 73(2) of the Penal Code provides that the penalty is one and a half times the amount of punishment to which he or she would otherwise have been liable for that offence.

Antecedents

185    The Accused had no antecedents.

Prosecution’s Submissions on Sentence

186    Essentially, the Prosecution had sought individual sentences ranging from three months’ imprisonment to eight months’ imprisonment. In support of its sentencing position, the Prosecution relied on the sentencing framework set by a 3-judge High Court in Tay Wee Kiat v Public Prosecutor [2018] 4 SLR 1315 (“Tay Wee Kiat”) for sentencing cases of domestic maid abuse.

Harm caused to Joan and Jenefer was both physical and psychological

187    Applying Step 1 of the Tay Wee Kiat framework, the Prosecution had submitted that the harm caused to Joan and Jenefer was both physical and psychological. In this regard, a critical factor in assessing whether psychological harm had been caused is whether there was a pattern of abuse. As explained in Tay Wee Kiat at [69], “[e]ven incidents of physical or verbal abuse that might seem individually mild can have a profound psychological impact upon the victim if they form part of a pattern or campaign of abusive conduct that is sustained over a period of time”.

188    It was the Prosecution’s position that Joan suffered psychological harm for the following reasons:

(a)     Based on Joan’s evidence, the accused had beat or hit her so many times that she could not remember how many times it was. The violence started around 2016 and took place almost every day. This was corroborated by the evidence of Clayr, Mar Phyo as well as Rueles.

(b)     Joan also testified that the abuse affected her emotionally:

It affects me so much emotionally and physically and it hurts my ego also, my---my pride as a human, it hurts me.

(c)     Joan explained that she worked for the accused for about 3 years despite the abuse because whenever the accused scolded her, the accused would also threaten to send her back to the Philippines and blacklist her from coming back, which made her continue to work for the accused because she was afraid to lose her job.

(d)     Joan’s working conditions were also oppressive and exploitative. For example, she sometimes had to stay awake the whole night to complete the household chores or massage the accused. She would usually eat only two meals a day and did not have enough food sometimes. The accused also deducted Joan’s salary on a regular basis, whenever she made a mistake or if the accused was not satisfied with her work performance.

189    It was also the Prosecution’s position that Jenefer too suffered psychological harm for the following reasons:

(a)     In the short period of time that she worked for the accused – about 3 months – she was physically abused at least 5 times.

(b)     Jenefer was verbally abused by the accused and she was scolded “almost every day”.

(c)     Jenefer was afraid of reporting the abuse to MOM or the police because she was afraid that the accused would send her back to the Philippines and blacklist her.

Compensation order

200    The Prosecution had submitted that in Tay Wee Kiat and another v Public Prosecutor and another appeal [2018] SGHC 114 has set out that for maid abuse cases, compensation orders should be generally given on four heads of damages. The first is pain and suffering caused to the victim, the second is medical expenses incurred by the victim, the third is loss or damage to the victim’s property and the fourth is prospective earnings, which is basically the income that the victim would have earned but for her being unemployed pending the investigation and the trial of the offences.

201    In the present case, the Prosecution was seeking compensation under the limbs of pain and suffering as well as loss of prospective earnings. In Tay Wee Kiat, the High Court had awarded a compensation sum of $500 to the victim in that case for physical and psychological harm caused by a hard slap on the victim’s cheek and there was no indication of injury. Similarly in Public Prosecutor v Lim Toon Leng and Seah Lei Sie Linda [2019] SGMC 12, the court had also awarded compensation of $500 to the victim for her pain and suffering from being punched in the head twice which caused her bodily pain and there was no evidence of the bodily injury caused. It was submitted that from the precedents, the starting point for pain and suffering where there is no significant or serious injury caused should be $500.

202    In relation to Joan, the compensation amount sought was $2,500 after considering her pain and suffering as well as her loss of income. For her pain and suffering, the Prosecution sought $500 per charge for MCN 901317-2020 and MCN 901318-2020. 203    For Jenefer, the compensation amount sought was $5,100 after considering her pain and suffering as well as her loss of income. For her pain and suffering, the Prosecution sought $500 per charge for MCN 901320-2020 to MCN 901324-2020. Jenefer was unemployed from 8 months from 18 October 2018 to 12 June 2019 ie about 8 months but at [21] of Tay Wee Kiat, the High Court accepted that four months represents a reasonable estimation of time that a domestic helper would have reasonable have required to take on alternative employment. Hence, capped at four months, her loss of income was $2,600 ie 4 x $650 which she testified as being her salary.

Decision on Sentence

Sentencing principles for domestic maid abuse

219    In my consideration of the appropriate sentence to be imposed, I was conscious that the principles of deterrence and retribution take centre stage in domestic maid abuse cases. This was articulated by the Court of Appeal in ADF v Public Prosecutor [2010] 1 SLR 874 which observed as [55]:

In a case of domestic maid abuse, ordinarily, the principles of deterrence and retribution take precedence. A deterrent sentence signifies that there is a public interest to protect over and above the ordinary punishment of criminal behaviour. The protection of domestic maids from abuse by their employers is always a matter of public interest, given their vulnerable status and the prevalence of such relationships in Singapore. No employer or household member has the right to engage in abusive behaviour against a domestic maid. All maids should be treated fairly, with dignity and respect. Indeed, domestic maid abuse not only causes harm to the victim and calls for deep public indignation; it could also harm Singapore’s bilateral relations with other countries.

220    As such, custodial sentences are invariably the starting point for domestic maid abuse cases. Further, the Court of Appeal held in ADF at [91]:

An employer’s unhappiness with a domestic maid’s work or conduct should never justify any form of abuse. All such offences are, in the eyes of the law, unprovoked.

221    Hence, a custodial sentence is almost invariably warranted in cases of domestic maid abuse where there has been any manner of physical abuse. In the present case, I did not find any extenuating or exceptional circumstances warranting a departure from this position and I disagreed with the Defence’s submissions for the imposition of fines in respect of all seven charges. It was also clear from the written plea in mitigation that the accused was unremorseful for her actions and had sought to downplay the seriousness of the abuse suffered by Joan and Jenefer by submitting that they were isolated events during a short window period, there was no evidence of systematic abuse and that the victims only suffered transient and/or minimal pain.

222    I did not place much weight on the 30 character references submitted by the Defence, particularly those which urged the court to reconsider its verdict or those who have indicated their disbelief that the accused had been abusive to the two victims in view of my decision to convict her on 9 December 2022. Furthermore, it is well established that little weight should be given to an accused’s previous good behaviour where serious offences particularly those where the principles of deterrence and retribution takes precedence are committed.

223    In my determination of the appropriate sentence to be imposed in respect of each charge, I am further guided by the sentencing framework for cases of domestic maid abuse established by the 3-judge High Court panel in Tay Wee Kiat v Public Prosecutor [2018] 4 SLR 1315 at [70] to [75] which is as follows:

(a)      Step 1: Determine whether the harm caused to the victim is predominantly physical or both physical and psychological.

(b)      Step 2: If the harm is both physical and psychological, the court should identify the degree of harm caused in relation to the charge under s 323 r/w s 73(2) Penal Code with regard to the following indicative ranges:

 

Less serious physical harm

More serious physical harm

Less serious psychological harm

3–6 months’ imprisonment

6–18 months’ imprisonment

More serious psychological harm

6–18 months’ imprisonment

20–30 months’ imprisonment



(c)      Step 3: To adjust the sentence to be imposed having regard to the aggravating and mitigating circumstances.

224    Finally, I would have to determine which sentences should run consecutively and which concurrently.

225    For Step 1, I had to determine whether the harm caused to the victim was predominantly physical, or both physical and psychological. If the offence does not form part of any broader trend or history of abusive conduct, and does not involve particularly degrading or humiliating treatment, the harm would probably be predominantly physical (in addition to the distress that the victim would invariably feel from being physically assaulted). In that case, the court should consider the degree of harm as well as other aggravating and mitigating factors in determining the appropriate sentence, bearing in mind other maid abuse precedents, and that “a custodial sentence is almost invariably warranted in cases of domestic maid abuse where there has been any manner of physical abuse”. Where the harm is both physical and psychological, the second step is for the court to identify the degree of harm caused in relation to each charge.

226    In the present case, I agreed with the Prosecution that the harm caused to both Joan and Jenefer were both physical and psychological. In respect of Joan, I found that there was psychological harm in view of the sustained pattern of abuse namely, the multiple incidents of the accused causing hurt to her. Joan testified that the accused had beat and hit her many times from 2016 until October 2018 and the abuse had affected her emotionally. This was also corroborated by both Clayr and Mar Phyo.

227    I disagreed with the Defence’s submissions that the difficult working conditions do not equate to psychological harm and are more appropriately dealt with by MOM as they deal with separate interests from those protected by s 323 of the Penal Code. In my view, I was entitled to consider whether there was a sustained pattern of abuse in determining whether there was psychological harm caused to both domestic maids in the present case based on the totality of the evidence before me.

228    I further disagreed with the Defence that I should not accept Joan’s evidence that she hit with a stick many times before the incident in respect of the third amended charge which involved Joan being hit her on her torso with a stick as I had acquitted the accused. As explained earlier, I had found that Joan had not given a clear and consistent account of the facts relating to the third amended charge and it was not corroborated by any extrinsic or objective evidence. This did not mean that Joan’s evidence should be rejected in its entirety on the grounds that she was not a credible witness. In the present case, I had accepted Joan’s evidence in relation to the other two amended charges that she was kicked on her chest and hit on her head by the accused with her hand with a ring on her finger by the accused as I found her evidence to be credible and internally consistent. Furthermore, it was also externally consistent with the extrinsic evidence from the various Prosecution witnesses.

229    Hence, I agreed with the Prosecution that her working conditions was oppressive and exploitative based on Joan’s evidence of her long working hours, the insufficient amount of food provided as well as the salary deductions on a regular basis whenever the accused was not satisfied with her work performance. For instance, sometimes she had to stay awake the whole night to complete the household chores or massage the accused. She would usually eat two meals a day and sometimes did not have enough food.

230    Similarly, I agreed with the Prosecution that Jenefer’s working conditions was also oppressive and exploitative. She would sleep at 1:00 or 2:00 pm and wake up at about 5:00 to 6:00 pm. At times, she did not have sufficient time to eat and sleep. Although Jenefer had worked for the accused for about three months, she was physically abused on at least five occasions as set out in the charges. She was verbally abused by the accused as she was scolded by the accused almost every day. She was afraid of reporting the abuse to MOM, the police, and the authorities as she feared being sent back to the Philippines and blacklisted. Both Joan and Jenefer had also suffered physical harm as a result of the accused’s actions towards them as both had felt bodily pain when the accused caused hurt to them.

231    For Step 2, I had to identify the degree of harm caused in relation to each charge. In respect of MCN 901317-2020 and MCN 901318-2020 involving Joan, I agreed with the Prosecution that the degree of psychological harm was significant as she was abused on a regular basis for about two years against a backdrop of miserable working conditions. The physical harm which involved a kick on Joan’s chest and hitting her on her head was not serious as she had not sustained any visible injuries. Hence, the relevant sentencing range would be six to 18 months’ imprisonment.

232    In respect of the remaining five charges, namely MCN 901318-2020 to MCN 901322- 2020 involving Jenefer, I agreed with the Prosecution that the degree of psychological harm was not very serious as she had only worked for the accused for about three months against the backdrop of regular verbal abuse and miserable working conditions. The physical harm which involved the accused pinching her arms, her thigh and her chest were not serious in view of the nature of injuries she suffered which manifested in bruises in some instances. Hence, the relevant sentencing range would be three to six months’ imprisonment.

233    For Step 3, I had to adjust the sentence of each charge to take into account other aggravating or mitigating factors. In the present, there were no significant aggravating factors, and no mitigating factor applies as the accused had claimed trial to all eight charges. After carefully considering the facts of the case as well as the submissions by both the Prosecution and the Defence, I imposed the following sentences:

(a)     MCN 901317-2020 – six months’ imprisonment;

(b)     MCN 901318-2020 – six months’ imprisonment;

(c)     MCN 901320-2020 – four months’ imprisonment;

(d)     MCN 901321-2020 – three months’ imprisonment;

(e)     MCN 901322-2020 – four months’ imprisonment;

(f)     MCN 901323-2020 – four months’ imprisonment;

(g)     MCN 901324-2020 – three months’ imprisonment.

234    Having determined the individual sentences for each charge, the next step is for the court to decide which sentences to run consecutively and which concurrently, in accordance with the principles set out in Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998. Where an offender faces multiple charges of maid abuse, the court should consider the duration and frequency of abuse in determining whether to order more than two sentences to run consecutively.

235    In the present case, I was of the view that the sentences in MCN 901317-2020 and MCN 901320-2020 should run consecutively as they both involve separate victims. The sentences of the five remaining charges were to run concurrently. Hence, the global sentence imposed was ten months’ imprisonment.

Compensation order

236    I now turn to the issue of compensation. In my assessment of the present case, I agreed with the Prosecution that the victims ought to be compensated for the pain and suffering they underwent as a result of the offences. In my determination of the appropriate amount to ordered against the accused, I was guided by the position taken by the High Court in the supplementary judgment of Tay Wee Kiat and another v Public Prosecutor and another appeal [2018] 5 SLR 438 at [19] where a sum of $500 was determined to be a rough and ready starting point for each incident of abuse.

237    Pursuant to s 359 Criminal Procedure Code 2010 and in line with the “rough and ready” approach adopted the High Court in Tay Wee Kiat, I ordered the accused to pay compensation to the victims.

238    For the charges involving Joan, for her pain and suffering, I fixed the amount at $500 per charge for MCN 901317-2020 and MCN 901318-2020. She was unemployed for about 2.5 months before she was reemployed. Based on her monthly salary of $600, I agreed with the Prosecution that a sum $1,500 was appropriate for her loss of come. Hence, I ordered $2,500 as the total amount of compensation payable by the accused to Joan.

239    For the charges involving Jenefer, similarly for her pain and suffering I fixed the amount at $500 per charge for MCN 901320-2020 to MCN 901324-2020. Although she was unemployed for about 8 months, she has a duty to mitigate her losses. I was of the view that the period of loss of income should be capped at 4 months as I was guided by the High Court’s assessment in Tay Wee Kiat that a period of four months is a reasonable estimation of the time a victim would reasonably require to take on alternative employment. Based on her monthly salary of $650, her loss of income amounted to $2,600. Hence, I ordered $5,100 as the total amount of compensation payable by the accused to Jenefer.
