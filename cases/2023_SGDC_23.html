District Judge Clement Seah Chi-Ling:

Introduction

1       DC/OSS 22/2022 (“OSS 22”) is the Plaintiff’s application for the following orders:

(a)     An order that the Defendant pay the sum of S$165,508.70 to the Plaintiff forthwith pursuant to the Plaintiff’s demand on the Security Deposit Guarantee (Bond No. DBPBSNA00001501900) dated 7 May 2019 (the “Performance Guarantee”) for the sum of S$165,508.70 on 25 November 2021, or such further order as this Honourable Court deems fit;

(b)     The Plaintiff be at liberty to apply for further orders;

(c)     The costs of and incidental to OSS 22 to be paid forthwith by the Defendant to the Plaintiff; and

(d)     Such further and/or other relief(s) be granted as this Honourable Court deems fit.

2       The crux of the present dispute centered on whether the Performance Guarantee was valid and subsisting at the time of the Plaintiff’s demand on the Performance Guarantee, or whether it had been earlier terminated by a non-renewal letter allegedly sent by the Defendant to the Plaintiff.

Background Facts

The Parties

3       The Plaintiff (hereinafter, referred to as the “Plaintiff” or “SICC”), is a professionally managed club in the business of providing a recreational space and facilities for club members. The Plaintiff is incorporated in Singapore, and has its registered office at 180 Island Club Road, Singapore 578774.

4       The Defendant is a Singapore-incorporated insurance company, having its registered office at 3 Anson Road, #16-00, Springleaf Tower, Singapore 079909. The Defendant provided the Performance Guarantee in favour of the Plaintiff.

The Project

5       The Plaintiff is the employer for a project involving refurbishment works for a 50-metre pool, a 25-metre pool and a kids’ play pool located at the SICC’s premises (the “Project”).

6       The Plaintiff’s contractor for the Project is Tienrui Design & Construction Pte. Ltd. (“Tienrui”). Tienrui was awarded the Project under a letter of award dated 8 March 2019 issued by the Plaintiff. SICC and Tienrui subsequently entered into a formal agreement dated 28 August 2019 for the Project (the “Contract”).

7       The Contract provides, inter alia, that:

(a)     Tienrui is contractually obliged to furnish the Plaintiff with a security deposit, by way of either a cash deposit or a guarantee provided by a bank or insurance company, as security for its due performance of its obligations under the Contract;

(b)     The Plaintiff is entitled to utilize the security deposit to make good any loss or damage sustained, or likely to be sustained, as a result of any breach of contract by Tienrui, including any liquidated damages; and

(c)     The amount of the security deposit to be furnished is 10% of the Contract Sum, i.e. S$165,608.65 (the “Guaranteed Sum”).

The Performance Guarantee

8       Pursuant to its obligations under the Contract, on or around 8 March 2019, Tienrui procured the Defendant to provide the Performance Guarantee for the Guaranteed Sum of S$165,508.70 in favour of the Plaintiff. The Performance Guarantee is an on-demand and unconditional security. Clause 1 of the Performance Guarantee[note: 1], reads as follows:

“1. We shall unconditionally pay to the Employer any sum or sums up to a maximum aggregate of Singapore Dollars ONE HUNDRED SIXTY FIVE THOUSAND FIVE HUNDRED EIGHT AND CENTS SEVENTY ONLY (S$165,508.70) (the “Guaranteed Sum”) upon receiving your written notice of claim for payment made pursuant to Clause 4 of this Guarantee without any proof of actual default on the part of the Contractor and without need to satisfy any other condition.”

9       The Performance Guarantee has an automatic extension provision. Clause 3 of the Performance Guarantee provides that the Performance Guarantee is to remain in effect until 5 May 2021, after which, the expiry date is automatically extended for successive periods of one month, unless the Defendant gives SICC ninety days’ written notice prior to the (extended) expiry date of the Defendant’s intention not to extend the Performance Guarantee in respect of any future extension.

10     Clause 3 of the Performance Guarantee reads as follows:

“3.    Our liability under this Guarantee shall continue and this Guarantee shall remain in full force and effect from 6th May 2019 until 5th May 2021 provided always that the expiry date of this Guarantee and our liability under this Guarantee shall be automatically extended for successive periods of 1 month unless we give you 90 days’ written notice prior to the expiry of our liability (the “Notice Period”) of our intention not to extend this Guarantee in respect of any future extension and provided further that you shall be entitled –

(a)     upon receiving such notice of our intention either to:

(i)     make a claim under this Guarantee; or

(b)    direct us (within the Notice Period) to extend the validity of this Guarantee for a further period not exceeding 1 month (and this Guarantee shall then expire at the end of such further period).” [Emphasis added]

The parties’ case

11     Over the course of the Project, the Plaintiff alleged that Tienrui had failed to perform its contractual obligations and/or committed numerous breaches of contract, which caused the Plaintiff to sustain (and/or likely sustain) loss or damage. The Plaintiff’s claim for loss or damage against Tienrui, arising from Tienrui’s breaches of the Contract, included:

(a)     A claim for liquidated damages;

(b)     A defects claim; and

(c)     A clawback claim to recover monies wrongly awarded to Tienrui in SOP AA/242 of 2020.

12     In view of the loss and damage suffered, the Plaintiff, by way of a letter dated 25 November 2021, demanded that the Defendant pay the full Guaranteed Sum of S$165,508.70 to the Plaintiff within thirty (30) business days from the Defendant’s receipt of the said letter (the “Demand”)[note: 2].

Plaintiff’s case

13     The Plaintiff’s case is that it has complied with all the requirements for calling on the Performance Guarantee. Under the terms of the Performance Guarantee, the Defendant was thus obliged to make payment on the Plaintiff’s demand without question. In relation to the Defendant’s contention that the Performance Guarantee had been terminated as a result of an earlier Non-Renewal Letter dated 5 February 2021 sent by the Defendant pursuant to clause 3 of the Performance Guarantee, the Plaintiff’s position is that the said Non-Renewal Letter: (a) was not sent out by the Defendant, (b) was not received by the Plaintiff, and/or (c) was not brought to the Plaintiff’s notice.

14     Specifically, the Plaintiff contends that the Defendant has failed to prove that the Non-Renewal Letter was actually mailed to the Plaintiff and/or that the Non-Renewal Letter was actually received by the Plaintiff. In this connection, it is undisputed that the postal acceptance rule does not apply to the case at hand following the High Court decision of 1L30G Pte Ltd v EQ Insurance Co Ltd [2017] 5 SLR 1106 (“1L30G”). Accordingly, mere proof that the Non-Renewal Letter was mailed to the Plaintiff is insufficient for the Defendant to avoid liability – it is necessary for the Defendant to prove that the Non-Renewal Letter was received by the Plaintiff. Plaintiff contends that there is insufficient proof that the Non-Renewal Letter was in fact received by the Plaintiff, or that the Plaintiff had actual notice of it. The Performance Guarantee was thus automatically extended at the end of each expiry period, and remained valid at the time of the Plaintiff’s Demand. The Plaintiff additionally claimed that the Defendant had failed to comply with the condition precedent for terminating the Performance Guarantee by failing to give the Plaintiff at least 90-days’ notice before terminating the Performance Guarantee.

Defendant’s case

15     The Defendant submits that the Plaintiff’s application ought to be dismissed as the Plaintiff’s demand on the Performance Guarantee was out of time and therefore invalid. The Defendant contends that pursuant to clause 3 of the Performance Guarantee, and by way of a letter dated 5 February 2021 sent by the Defendant to the Plaintiff by AR Registered post on 29 January 2021 (the “Non-Renewal Letter”)[note: 3], the Defendant has given the Plaintiff the requisite 90-days’ written notice of the Defendant’s intention not to extend the Performance Guarantee upon its expiry on 5 May 2021. Although the Defendant’s letter was dated 5 February 2021, the Defendant asserts that it was in fact issued on 29 January 2021 and post-dated to 5 February 2021. This was to factor in time for the letter to be delivered to the Plaintiff so that the Plaintiff would receive the 90-day notice as required by the terms of the Performance Guarantee. The Defendant tenders a Yellow Card[note: 4] endorsed by Singapore Post which purportedly shows that the Non-Renewal Letter was delivered to SICC on 2 February 2021.

16     The Defendant’s position is that since the Defendant has informed the Plaintiff of its intention not to extend the expiry date of the Performance Guarantee beyond 5 May 2021 via the Non-Renewal Letter, the Performance Guarantee expired on 5 May 2021. As the Plaintiff’s Demand was only made on 25 November 2021, the Defendant is not liable to the Plaintiff under the Performance Guarantee.

Procedural History

17     I should note that the Defendant had in the initial stages of the proceedings submitted that the Plaintiff’s application was wrongly commenced by way of an originating summons (“OS”) and ought to be dismissed outright. The Defendant submitted that the proceedings should instead have been commenced by way of a writ action given the number of factual disputes raised in the case.

18     Under Order 28 r 4(3) of the Rules of Court 2014, the court may, in the event it determines that there is a dispute as to fact, order that “the originating summons shall be heard on oral evidence or partly on oral evidence and partly on affidavit evidence, with or without cross examination of any of the deponents, as it may direct”.

19     Both counsel agreed that instead of converting the proceedings into a writ action, a more just, expeditious and economical way of addressing the Defence Counsel’s objections was to allow for witnesses to be called and be examined on the factual issues in the context of the OS proceedings. Directions were thus given for the following witnesses to be called and for their affidavits to be filed, to the extent they have not already been filed in these proceedings.

20     The witnesses called by the Plaintiff were:

(a)     Mr. Cham Meng Kuan (“PW1”), the Acting General Manager and Director of Finance of SICC;

(b)     Ms. Rajita Suntharalingam (“PW2”), the Director of Corporate Service of SICC;

(c)     Mr. Ming Yat Nam (“PW3”), an Office Attendant of SICC;

(d)     Ms. Saemah Bte Satli (“PW4”), an Office Attendant of SICC.

21     The witnesses called by the Defendant were:

(a)     Mr. Rajah Raman S/o Surenderamugam (“DW1”), a District Manager of Singapore Post, who was subpoenaed by the Defendant;

22     The examination of the witnesses took place over two days on 16 August 2022 and 22 November 2022.

Issues

23     Based on the parties’ closing submissions, the issues that arise for consideration in the present case are as follows:

(a)     Whether the Non-Renewal Letter was the document that was mailed out by the Defendant on 29 January 2021;

(b)     Whether the Non-Renewal Letter was received by the Plaintiff such that the Plaintiff had actual knowledge of the Defendant’s intention not to renew the Performance Guarantee; and

(c)     Whether one of the conditions precedent to the termination of the Performance Guarantee – namely, the requirement that the Defendant gives 90-days’ written notice of its intention not to renew the guarantee prior to the expiry of the Defendant’s liability under the Performance Guarantee – was fulfilled.

My decision

24     I agree with the Plaintiff’s Counsel that the High Court decision of 1L30G Pte Ltd v EQ Insurance Co Ltd [2017] 5 SLR 1106 (“1L30G”) is a relevant and binding precedent for the determination of the above issues, especially the issues in paragraphs 23(a) and (b), given that the facts and issues canvassed in 1L30G’s case bore significant similarities to the present case. Pertinently, the automatic extension and termination clause of the performance bond in 1L30G’s case was almost word-for-word identical to the automatic extension and termination clause in Clause 3 of the Performance Guarantee in the present case.

25     A point of distinction between 1L30G’s case and the present case is that the notice of non-renewal in 1L30G was sent by Registered Mail, whereas the Non-Renewal Letter in the present case was sent via Advice of Receipt Registered Mail (“AR Registered Mail”). However, as will become apparent in my discussions below, the distinction is not material for present purposes.

1L30G’s case

26     Briefly, 1L30G dealt with a similar factual matrix in which the issuer of a performance bond had refused to pay out on the plaintiff-beneficiary’s call on the performance bond on the ground that the defendant-insurer had purportedly served a prior notice of non-renewal on the plaintiff-beneficiary by registered post. The plaintiff-beneficiary claimed not to have received the notice of non-renewal.

27     In 1L30G, the issues which His Honour, Lee Seiu Kin J, had to consider were:

(a)     Whether the defendant-insurer posted the notice of non-renewal;

(b)     Whether under clause 3 of the performance bond in that case, it was sufficient that the defendant-insurer had sent the notice of non-renewal by registered mail, or whether it was necessary for the plaintiff-beneficiary to have actually received the letter, i.e. whether the postal acceptance rule applied; and

(c)     If clause 3 of the performance bond required actual receipt, whether the plaintiff-beneficiary actually received the notice of non-renewal.

28     Lee J eventually found in favour of the plaintiff-beneficiary. On the three issues summarized above, His Honour found as follows:

(a)     First, Lee J found, on a balance of probabilities, that the document sent to the plaintiff-beneficiary via registered mail was in fact the notice of non-renewal. The court reasoned that the defendant-insurer only needed to show that the notice of non-renewal was sent to the plaintiff on a balance of probabilities. His Honour considered the fact that the defendant's affidavit evidence that the notice of non-renewal was the document posted out was not directly contradicted by the plaintiff, and was in fact supported by the registered mail receipt. This rendered it likely that the defendant-insurer did in fact send the notice of non-renewal to the plaintiff.

(b)     On the second issue, Lee J found that clause 3 of the performance bond in that case was in effect a termination clause, as it allowed the defendant to give notice of non-renewal of the performance bond. The effect of any exercise of the right to terminate was, however, accompanied by the plaintiff-beneficiary’s rights to make an immediate claim on the performance bond, or to extend the performance bond by a final 180 days. Lee J found that as a matter of construction of the performance bond, the plain meaning of the word “notice” as used in clause 3 must mean that the other party must actually be informed, especially since the notice of non-renewal triggered various rights for the benefit of the plaintiff-beneficiary. In this connection, Lee J held that the postal acceptance rule did not apply. His Honour reasoned that the postal acceptance rule only applied in cases where both parties intended for it to apply. The contractual language in 1L30G’s case was clear in indicating that the plaintiff-beneficiary must have actual notice of the notice of non-renewal in order to benefit from his entitlements under clause 3. The fact that clause 3 of the performance bond concerned termination rather than acceptance of contractual rights might also indicate that the parties did not intend the postal acceptance rule should apply.

29     Accordingly, the court in 1L30G held that clause 3 of the performance bond was “only satisfied when the plaintiff has actually received the notice of non-renewal whether by letter, fax, email or any other means. If the defendant chooses to use postal communication, it assumes the risks that are associated with the post” (at [35]) (emphasis added).

30     On the facts of 1L30G, the court found that the plaintiff-beneficiary did not have actual notice of the notice of non-renewal:

(a)     While the court found that the defendant did in fact post out the notice of non-renewal based on the registered mail receipt, this was insufficient to prove that the plaintiff had actual notice of the document since the postal acceptance rule did not apply; and

(b)     The court found that the evidence supported the conclusion that the plaintiff had not received the notice of non-renewal. The plaintiff’s director’s affidavit evidence to this effect was consistent with the plaintiff’s conduct of asking a third party about the extension of the bond. This finding was also corroborated by the acts of two third parties who were copied on the notice of non-renewal. First, the party whose performance was guaranteed by the bond had filed for an injunction against the first demand on the bond on 5 June 2015, which it would not have done if it had received the 27 June 2014 notice of non-renewal: see 1L30G at [11(c)]. Secondly, the plaintiff had also emailed the insurance broker on 12 December 2014 to enquire if the performance bond would be automatically renewed. Likewise, the plaintiff would not have bothered to make these enquiries if it had already received the 27 June 2014 notice of non-renewal and was apprised of the insurer’s intention to terminate the bond: see 1L30G at [37] and [39].

The present case

31     The holdings of 1L30G are of significant precedential value in the present case as the auto-extension and termination clause in 1L30G and the present case are almost completely identical. Clause 3 of the Performance Guarantee in the present case provides as follows:

(a)     The Defendant’s liability under the Performance Guarantee is between 6 May 2019 to 5 May 2021;

(b)     The Defendant’s liability under the Performance Guarantee will automatically extend past the original expiry date of 5 May 2021 for successive periods of one (1) month unless ninety (90) days’ written notice (of the Defendant’s intention not to extend) is given prior to the expiry date; and

(c)     Upon receiving such notice of intention not to renew, the Plaintiff is entitled to either: (i) make a claim under the Performance Guarantee; or (ii) direct the Defendant to extend the validity of the Performance Guarantee for a further period not exceeding 1 month.

32     Given that the High Court has in 1L30G held that the postal acceptance rule did not apply to an almost identical clause, I am bound to find that the postal acceptance rule does not apply to the present case as well. I therefore turn to the main issues to be decided in the present case, namely: (a) Whether the Non-Renewal Letter was the document mailed out by the Defendant; and (b) Whether the Non-Renewal Letter was received by the Plaintiff such that the Plaintiff had actual notice of the Defendant’s intention not to renew the Performance Guarantee.

Issue 1:   Whether the Non-Renewal Letter was the document mailed out by the Defendant

33     On a balance of probabilities, I find that the document that was mailed by the Defendant by AR Registered Mail on 29 January 2021 is in fact the Non-Renewal Letter. I considered the following.

(i)   Affidavit evidence of the Defendant’s staff

34     The Defendant’s staff deposed affidavits in support of the Defendant’s contention that the Non-Renewal Letter was in fact mailed out on 29 January 2021.

35     DW6 Kelly Ho testified that she had generated the Non-Renewal Letter on 29 January 2021 and post-dated it to 5 February 2021 for the reasons stated above (see [15] above). As she had been working from home, she emailed the Non-Renewal Letter to her colleague Wong Jian Wei (DW4, “Wong”)) and requested the latter to print and thereafter to send the Non-Renewal Letter out by AR Registered Mail. Wong confirmed at paragraph 6 of his affidavit that upon receipt of the email from Kelly Ho, he printed the Non-Renewal Letter, folded the letter and placed it in a sealed envelope. He then arranged for the company’s dispatch staff to send the Non-Renewal Letter by AR Registered Mail to the Plaintiff. He stated that he subsequently received the Yellow Advice of Receipt Card (the “Yellow Card”)[note: 5] from Singapore Post.

36     DW2 Chionh Hock Siong (“Chionh”), the Defendant’s delivery dispatch staff, deposed that he was the person who went to the Shenton Way branch of Singapore Post on 29 January 2021 to arrange for the Non-Renewal Letter to be sent by AR Registered Mail to the Plaintiff. He exhibited at pages 29 to 31 of his affidavit dated 19 July 2022 copies of the Posting Form for Registered Services (“Posting Form”) which he had prepared before going to the Shenton Way post office on 29 January 2021. Item 7 of the Posting Form listed the Plaintiff’s name and address. A picture of the AR Registered Mail addressed to SICC purportedly containing the Non-Renewal Letter, with Reference Number RC126293202SG (the “Reference Number”), is included in the Defendant’s Supplementary Bundle of Documents (“DSBD”) at page 3. Chionh also exhibited at pages 29, 31 and 32 of his affidavit a copy of the Singapore Post (Shenton Way Branch) Transaction Receipt dated 29 January 2021, item 7 of which bore the Reference Number. The Defendant’s witnesses’ evidence, viewed as a whole, supported the inference that the Non-Renewal Letter was in fact mailed out by way of AR Registered Mail at the Shenton Way post office.

(ii)   Email to Gim Insurance Agency

37     I also noted that Kelly Ho had on 29 January 2021 emailed Gim Insurance Agency (“Gim”), Tienrui’s insurance brokers, a copy of the Defendant’s Non-Renewal Letter for Gim to forward to Tienrui[note: 6]. The fact that Kelly Ho’s email of 29 January 2021 to Gim attached a copy of the Non-Renewal Letter dated 5 February 2021 rendered it likely that the same document was sent by Kelly Ho, with the assistance of Wong and Chionh, by way of AR Registered Mail to SICC earlier on the same day.

38     Based on the foregoing, I find, on a balance of probabilities, that the document mailed out by the Defendant to the Plaintiff on 29 January 2021 is in fact the Non-Renewal Letter.

Issue 2:   Whether the Non-Renewal Letter was actually received by the Plaintiff

39     As noted above, 1L30G’s case is binding authority, for present purposes, that the postal acceptance rule does not apply. Hence, the mere fact that it has been shown that the Non-Renewal Letter has been posted by Defendant does not without more lead to the conclusion that the Plaintiff must therefore have received it. The burden of proof lies on the person alleging notice was given (i.e. the Defendant) to show actual communication of the Non-Renewal Letter to the Plaintiff: see Ho Miaw Ling v Singapore Island Country Club [1997] 1 SLR(R) 640 at [20].

40     I preface my discussion of this issue by stating that I agree with the Defendant Counsel’s submission that receipt of the Non-Renewal Letter by an employee of the Plaintiff at the registered office is sufficient to constitute actual notice of the Non-Renewal Letter by the Plaintiff. There is no need for the Non-Renewal Letter to be served on, or be received by, any particular officer of the Plaintiff. Indeed, the Performance Guarantee does not stipulate to whose attention the Non-Renewal Letter must be brought for notice to be effected.

41     Notwithstanding the foregoing, I find that the Defendant has not discharged its burden of showing that the Plaintiff has actually received and has actual notice of the Non-Renewal Letter. I considered the following.

(i)   The evidence provided by the Plaintiff’s witnesses

42     First, the Plaintiff’s witnesses have consistently deposed on affidavit and during cross-examination that the Non-Renewal Letter was not received and did not come to the actual notice of the Plaintiff:

(a)     Ms. Rajita Suntharalingam (“Ms. Rajita”), the Plaintiff’s Director of Corporate Services, confirmed that the Plaintiff did not receive the Non-Renewal Letter;[note: 7]

(b)     Mr. Cham Meng Kuan (“Mr. Cham”), the Plaintiff’s Director of Finance, also confirmed that the Plaintiff did not receive the Non-Renewal Letter; [note: 8]

(c)     Mr. Ming Yat Nam (“Mr. Ming”), the Plaintiff’s dispatch staff, confirmed that he did not sign the Yellow Card and he did not collect the Non-Renewal Letter;[note: 9] and

(d)     Ms. Saemah Bte Satli (“Ms. Saemah”), SICC’s other dispatch staff, confirmed that she had not gone to the Ang Mo Kio Post Office on 2 February 2021 and thus did not collect the Non-Renewal Letter.[note: 10]

43     The uncontradicted testimonies of the recipient’s staff was a factor that the High Court in 1L30G considered in determining that the plaintiff there did not receive the notice of non-renewal: see 1L30G at [36]. In the present case, the Plaintiff’s staff were consistent in stating that they did not actually receive the Non-Renewal Letter.

(ii)   Plaintiff’s arrangement with Singapore Post for the collection of AR Registered Mail

44     Secondly, it became clear in the course of the cross-examination of the witnesses that the Plaintiff had an arrangement with Singapore Post, whereby the Plaintiff collected all of its mail (both ordinary and AR Registered Mail) from the Ang Mo Kio Post Office. Non-registered mail will be collected by the Plaintiff’s office attendants from the Plaintiff’s P.O. Box at Ang Mo Kio Post Office, and registered mail at the counter of Ang Mo Kio Post Office. This was confirmed by: (a) the affidavit evidence and witness testimony of the Plaintiff’s dispatch staff, i.e. Mr. Ming[note: 11] and Ms. Saemah[note: 12]; (b) the affidavit evidence and witness testimony of the Plaintiff’s Director of Corporate Services, Ms. Rajita[note: 13]; and (c) the correspondences exchanged between the Plaintiff and Singapore Post – i.e. the Plaintiff’s letter to Singapore Post dated 5 July 2022[note: 14] and the email response from Singapore Post dated 11 August 2022 confirming this arrangement[note: 15]. Mr. Raman, the Singapore Post representative also confirmed during cross-examination that this is the mailing arrangement agreed between SICC and Singapore Post[note: 16].

45     In light of this arrangement, I agree with the Plaintiff Counsel’s submission that Singapore Post would never have delivered any mail, including the Non-Renewal Letter, to the Plaintiff’s registered address. Thus, the Defendant’s evidence, in particular the Yellow Card, which on its face suggested that the Non-Renewal Letter had in fact been delivered to the Plaintiff’s registered address, is inaccurate.

(iii)   The Yellow Card

46     The signed Yellow Card was also, in my view, insufficient to prove that the Non-Renewal Letter was received by the Plaintiff or brought to the Plaintiff’s actual notice.

47     The Yellow Card in the present case was signed by a Singapore Post officer under the heading - “The recipient has acknowledged receipt of the item on the delivery receipt but declines to sign this card”. This had initially created the impression that the mail had in fact been delivered to SICC’s registered address by a Singapore Post officer and received by a SICC employee at the said address, who then declined to sign on the Yellow Card. However, as noted above, in light of the Plaintiff’s arrangement with Singapore Post, the Non-Renewal Letter would not have been delivered to the Plaintiff’s registered address. Thus, the Yellow Card did not, in and of itself, prove that the Non-Renewal Letter was in fact received by someone at the Plaintiff’s registered address.

48     Furthermore, the Yellow Card was also not signed by the Plaintiff’s dispatch staff to acknowledge collection of the Non-Renewal Letter at the Ang Mo Kio Post office. Singapore Post’s representative, Mr. Rajah Raman, testified during cross examination that since the COVID-19 pandemic, Singapore Post does not deliver AR Registered mail directly to the recipient’s doorstep, but instead delivers such mail to the recipient’s letter box at the relevant post office[note: 17]. All registered mail will then be handed to the recipient over the counter[note: 18]. Mr Raman goes on to clarify that when an AR Registered Mail is handed over to a customer at the post office, Singapore Post will not ask the customer to sign on the Yellow Card. Instead, the counter staff will assist and endorse on the right-hand side of the Yellow Card when they hand over the item to the customer[note: 19]. When it was put to him that it was strange that when collections were made in person at the counter, the Singapore Post staff would not ask for the customer’s signature to be affixed on the left-hand acknowledgement section, Mr. Raman admitted that it was strange[note: 20].

49     This revelation has two implications: (a) first, the notation on the Yellow Card that “the recipient has acknowledged receipt of the item on the delivery receipt but declines to sign this card” is in fact factually wrong and misleading; and (b) despite the return of a signed Yellow Card to the Defendant, there was in fact no acknowledgement by a personnel from SICC that the mail was in fact collected personally by him/her.

50     In this connection, Mr. Raman’s contention that the counter staff would not ask the customer to acknowledge receipt on the Yellow Card despite the customer collecting in person at the counter was in fact contradicted by the Plaintiff’s Mr. Ming and Ms. Saemah’s consistent testimonies to the effect that it was standard practice that they would be required to sign on the Yellow Card if the AR Registered Mail was in fact handed to them personally at the counter. In the present case, they confirmed that the Yellow Card did not bear their signatures, which in turn suggested that the mail was not collected by either of them.

Lack of clarity and consistency as to who signed the Yellow Card

51     I also agree with Plaintiff’s Counsel that, viewed as a whole, there was a lack of clarity and consistency in Mr. Raman’s evidence as to who from Singapore Post signed the Yellow Card, and under what circumstances. During his examination in chief, Mr. Raman stated that it was Singapore Post’s counter staff that will sign on the Yellow Card when the mail is handed over to a customer at the Post office[note: 21]. However, during cross examination, Mr. Raman contradicted his earlier testimony by stating that the Yellow card is signed off by “either the delivery staff or the participating counter staff”[note: 22]. Ultimately, Mr. Raman confirmed that he did not know for sure who signed on the Yellow Card and he was merely “guessing”[note: 23].

52     The lack of clarity as to the person who affixed the signature on the Yellow Card highlights a more fundamental problem, namely the significant ambiguity as to the actual practice adopted by Ang Mo Kio Post Office with regards the transmission of AR Registered Mail to the end customer. In light of such ambiguity, I agree with Plaintiff’s Counsel that the mere fact that Yellow Card was endorsed by someone from Ang Mo Kio Post Office does not, without more, show that the Non-Renewal Letter must have been handed over to a representative of the Plaintiff. This is all the more so given the fact that the Plaintiff’s dispatch staff have deposed on affidavit that it is “standard practice” for them to sign on the Yellow Card (or copy thereof) to acknowledge receipt of any AR Registered mail collected by them. Their affidavit evidence was maintained under cross-examination and was not impeached. Indeed, as a matter of logic, if the AR Registered Mail was in fact collected by a SICC office attendant over the counter, there was, in my mind, no good reason why Singapore Post would not ask the collecting party to sign on the left hand potion of the Yellow Card to confirm receipt[note: 24].

53     The net effect of the testimonies of the different witnesses is that the probative value of the signature on the Yellow Card to prove that the AR Registered Mail was in fact delivered to the Plaintiff is significantly diminished.

(iv)   The “Status Update: RC126293292SG” (the “Status Update”)

54     The Defendant also relied on the status update produced by Singapore Post which purportedly showed that the Registered Mail was collected by SICC on 2 February 2021 (the “Status Update”)[note: 25]. Mr. Raman’s testimony was that the Status Update is a system-generated status report from Singapore Post’s own internal system which tracks the status of trackable mail items, by scanning a barcode on the mail, at each location from the time the mail item is lodged at the post office for posting until the time it is delivered to the recipient[note: 26]. Row 2 of the Status Update states the mail item was “Collected by Customer” on 2 February 2021 at 09:00:00.000 hours.

55     A perusal of the Status Update however raised questions as to the accuracy of the data and information reflected in the Status Update. In particular, there are two entries in the Status Update – namely the entries described as (i) “received by AMK Post Office” and (ii) “collected by customer” – which reflected the exact same timing of 9.00am sharp down to the second and millisecond. Construed literally, the Status Update showed that the Registered Mail arrived at and was scanned by Ang Mo Kio Post Office on 2 February 2021 at 09:00:00.000. It was thereafter purportedly collected by the customer (SICC) at 09:00:00.000 on the same day as well. I accepted Plaintiff’s Counsel’s submission that it was highly improbable that both the receipt of the Registered Mail at the Ang Mo Kio Post Office from the distribution base, and the collection by the customer over the counter, would coincide down to the exact millisecond. This suggested that the data and information contained in the Status Update are not reliable, thus raising doubts as to whether the Registered Mail was in fact “collected by the customer” as the Status Update appears to suggest.

56     The unreliability of the Status Update is accentuated by the Plaintiff’s evidence that the Plaintiff’s office attendant, Mr. Ming, left the Plaintiff’s address at 9.52am on 2 February 2021 to go to the Ang Mo Kio Post Office to collect SICC mails. This was backed by several pieces of documentary evidence:

(a)     First, there was documentary evidence in the form of the Comfort Transportation taxi receipt dated 2 February 2021[note: 27], read with an extract from the Plaintiff’s transport claims system[note: 28], which showed that the Plaintiff’s Mr. Ming only departed from the Plaintiff’s address at 9.52am. Mr. Ming therefore could not have collected the AR Registered Mail from the Ang Mo Kio Post Office counter-staff at 9.00am sharp as the Status Update suggested.

(b)     Mr. Ming also confirmed, during cross-examination, that he usually left the Plaintiff’s address after 9.30am to go to the Ang Mo Kio Post Office[note: 29].

(c)     The Plaintiff’s “CPS Corporate Card Control / Claim Form” also showed that Ang Mo Kio Post Office was the second location on Mr. Ming’s trip on 2 February 2021[note: 30] – i.e., Mr. Ming did not make a direct trip to the Ang Mo Kio Post Office. This further suggested that Mr. Ming could not have been at Ang Mo Kio Post Office at 9.00am, again rendering it unlikely that Mr. Ming collected the AR Registered Mail at 9.00 am sharp.

57     Viewing the Status Update together with all the evidence and surrounding circumstances, I agree with Plaintiff’s Counsel that the Status Update was inadequate to prove that the Non-Renewal Letter was collected by SICC staff on 2 February 2021. In fact, it pointed to the contrary, and raised the question of who the registered mail was actually handed over to at or after 9.00am at Ang Mo Kio Post Office on 2 February 2021.

(v)   The Plaintiff’s conducted as further corroborative evidence that the Plaintiff did not have actual notice of the Non-Renewal Letter

58     In 1L30G’s case, the High Court took cognizance of the conduct of the plaintiff post the alleged dispatch of the notice of non-renewal to support its finding that the plaintiff did not have actual notice of the notice of non-renewal. Similarly, in the present case, I found that the Plaintiff’s conduct was consistent with the Plaintiff’s position that it did not have actual notice of the Non-Renewal Letter.

(A)   Plaintiff’s correspondence with their counsel

59     It is undisputed that if the Non-Renewal Letter had indeed been sent out by the Defendant on 29 January 2021 and received by the Plaintiff on 2 February 2021, the Plaintiff would have up to 5 May 2021 to make a Demand on the Performance Guarantee.

60     The exchange of emails between Plaintiff’s counsel, Mr. Paul Wong of Dentons Rodyk & Davidson LLP, and the Plaintiff’s Ms. Rajita on 19 April 2021 however suggested that the Plaintiff was unaware that the last day for making a claim was 5 May 2021. Mr. Wong had in his 19 April 2021 email to Ms. Rajita stated that “I think we are ok for time because the performance bond provides for automatic renewal upon expiry unless the insurance company gives SICC 90 days’ notice of termination. I presume SICC has not received any notices from the insurance company?”[note: 31]. Ms. Rajita, in an email of the same date, replied that “I have just checked with our Finance Department who has since confirmed that they are not aware of any termination. For your information, please.”[note: 32] This exchange supported the inference that Plaintiff was in all probability unaware of the issuance of the Non-Renewal Letter by the Defendant on 29 January 2021.

(B)   Failure to call on the Performance Guarantee earlier

61     The fact that the Plaintiff did not call on the Performance Guarantee immediately after 2 February 2021, or at any rate by 5 May 2021, also supported the inference that the Plaintiff was unaware of the issuance of the Non-Renewal Letter by the Defendant. Based on paragraph [7] to [29] of Ms. Rajita’s affidavit of 19 July 2022[note: 33], which were unchallenged at trial, the following points may be gleaned:

(a)     The Plaintiff already had accrued claims for liquidated damages arising out of the late completion of works by Tienrui as at December 2019 or January 2020 amounting to some $55,000 (see [9]-[13] of Ms. Rajita’s affidavit[note: 34]).

(b)     There were further claims for defects post the issuance of the Certificate of Substantial Completion (CSOC) on 3 January 2020[note: 35], which was accompanied by two Handover Defects Lists dated 27 December 2019[note: 36], for which SICC had expended various sums by way of rectification costs, and/or procured quotations in contemplation of carrying out rectification works (see paragraph [14] - [23] and page 76 of Ms. Rajita’s affidavit[note: 37]). The fact that Plaintiff did not call on the Performance Guarantee to recover these liquidated damages and rectification costs when the Performance Guarantee had not yet expired, again suggested that the Plaintiff was laboring under the belief that the Performance Guarantee was being automatically renewed, and that no Non-Renewal Letter had yet been served.

(C)   Plaintiff’s failure to return the original bond and discharge letter

62     Thirdly, although the Non-Renewal Letter expressly requested the Plaintiff to “return the original bond … and discharge letter to discharge [the Defendant’s] liability”,[note: 38] the Plaintiff did not issue the original Performance Guarantee and/or the “discharge letter” to the Defendant. In fact, the Plaintiff did not issue any response to the Non-Renewal Letter. This again suggested that the Non-Renewal Letter was not received by the Plaintiff.

Conclusion on whether Plaintiff had actual notice of the Non-Renewal Letter

63     As noted by the High Court in 1L30G’s case, the Plaintiff must be shown to have had actual notice of the Non-Renewal Letter for the Performance Guarantee to be validly terminated. The burden of proof was on the Defendant to prove, on a balance of probabilities, that the Plaintiff was in fact notified of the non-renewal of the Performance Guarantee.

64     In this regard, I agree with Plaintiff’s Counsel that the Defendant had done the bare minimum to bring the Non-Renewal Letter to the attention of the Plaintiff. The Defendant’s Kelly Ho confirmed that she was aware that as at February 2021, due to the Covid pandemic, postmen could not deliver registered mail directly to the recipients; the registered mails were instead placed in letter boxes and the yellow card chopped and signed by the postman[note: 39]. Despite having such knowledge, Kelly Ho did not take other proactive steps to ensure that the Non-Renewal Letter, which she conceded was an important document with “significant financial impact”[note: 40], was brought to the attention of the Plaintiff, such as by delivering personally to SICC. She could, alternatively, have faxed a copy of the Non-Renewal Letter to SICC, or sent a copy by email to SICC (as was done for Gim). The Defendant chose to do none of these. Since the Defendant had chosen to use AR Registered Mail, it assumed the risks of any non-delivery or misdelivery that are associated with the post: see 1L30G at [35].

65     Having regard to all of the foregoing, I find that the Defendant has failed to show that the Plaintiff has received and has actual notice of the Non-Renewal Letter on a balance of probabilities.

Issue 3:   Whether the requirement that the Defendant give 90-days’ written notice prior to the expiry of the Performance Guarantee was fulfilled (the “Condition Precedent Issue”).

66     The Condition Precedent Issue concerns the issue of whether by sending a Non-Renewal Letter dated 5 February to the Plaintiff, the Defendant has effectively given the Plaintiff only 89-days’ notice of its intention not to renew the Performance Guarantee upon its expiry on 5 May 2021, as opposed to the contractually required 90-days.

67     The Defendant’s case is that the Non-Renewal Letter was in fact dispatched on 29 January 2021, but post-dated to 5 February 2021 to provision for a time buffer arising from the mailing process. The Defendant’s case is that the Non-Renewal Letter was collected by SICC representatives from Ang Mo Kio post office on 2 February 2021.

68     In light of my finding that there was no proof that the Non-Renewal Letter was actually received by the Plaintiff on 2 February 2021, it followed that the Non-Renewal Letter did not serve to provide actual notice that the Performance Guarantee would be terminated on 5 May 2021. Instead, the Performance Guarantee was automatically renewed in accordance with Clause 3 of the Performance Guarantee. The issue of whether the Condition Precedent was fulfilled therefore does not arise for consideration.

69     If I am wrong on the notice issue (i.e. the Non-Renewal Letter was in fact received by the SICC on 2 February 2021), then the fact that the Non-Renewal Letter was dated 5 February 2021 does not affect the conclusion that, even by Plaintiff’s method of calculation, at least 90-days prior written notice had been given to the Plaintiff prior to the Defendant’s election not to renew the Performance Guarantee upon its expiry on 5 May 2021. However, given my findings above, the Condition Precedent Issue is moot.

Orders

70     In light of my findings above, I make the following orders:

(a)     The Defendant shall pay the sum of S$165,508.70 to the Plaintiff forthwith pursuant to the Plaintiff’s demand on the Security Deposit Guarantee (Bond No. DBPBSNA00001501900) dated 7 May 2019 for the sum of S$165,508.70 on 25 November 2021;

(b)     The Defendant shall pay the Plaintiff interest at 5.33% per annum on the sum in (a).

(c)     The costs of and incidental to OSS 22 is to be paid forthwith by the Defendant to the Plaintiff. In the event such costs cannot be mutually agreed, parties may write in to the court for costs to be fixed.
