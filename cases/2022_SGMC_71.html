16 December 2022

District Judge Kamala Ponnampalam:

1       This is an appeal against sentence by the accused person, Mr Steven Guo Shenzhong (“the Accused”). The Accused pleaded guilty to one charge under section 11(1) of the Remote Gambling Act 2014 (No 34 of 2014) (“RGA”). One other charge under section 7 of the Common Gaming Houses Act (Chapter 49) (“CGHA”) was taken into consideration (“TIC”) for purposes of sentencing. The Accused was sentenced to eighteen months' imprisonment and a fine of $20,000, in default one month's imprisonment on the proceeded charge. The Accused’s sentences of imprisonment and fine have been stayed pending the hearing of this appeal. He is presently released on bail pending appeal.

Charge

2       The proceeded charge is reproduced below for ease of reference.

3       MAC 904299-2019:

…you, are charged that you, between 1st September 2018 and 7th September 2018, in Singapore did provide a Singapore-based remote gambling service by providing a gambling service to punters for them to participate in gambling by use of remote gambling, to wit, facilitating participation by others, a number of whom were physically present in Singapore, in remote gambling in a club, “Wicked G”, on the PPPoker mobile application, involving bets of SGD407,373.86, in accordance with arrangements made by the accused, which service was in the course of carrying on a business, when the said remote gambling service was not provided by an exempt operator or a person otherwise exempt under Section 40 of the Remote Gambling Act 2014 (No. 34 of 2014), and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under s.11(1) of the RGA.

Facts

4       The Accused is a male, Singapore citizen, aged 25 years. The offences were committed in September 2018 when he was 21 years old. The circumstances of the offence are set out in the Statement of Facts (“SOF”) which is reproduced below.

BACKGROUND FACTS

(c)    Sometime prior to 7 September 2018, the police were investigating an online gambling ring which involved the use of a mobile phone application “PPPoker”. This application allowed users to play online poker games (which is a game of chance) by providing individual platforms (or “clubs”) for players to come together. “PPPoker” is a remote gambling service that is not provided by an exempt operator, or a person otherwise exempt under s 40 of the Remote Gambling Act 2014 (No. 34 of 2014) (“RGA”) from ss 10 and 11 of the said Act.

(d)    The clubs were managed by “hosts” and “managers”, who have the authority to accept players to gamble within their club and are able to initiate online poker games by creating a virtual table for players to join and game by using virtual chips as stakes, which are purchased for actual currency and can be cashed out. The exchange rate of 1 virtual chip is equivalent to S$1.

(e)    Separately, managers are also able to appoint people as agents. These agents would then source for players to be introduced to the managers, who would accept them into the club. Agents would ordinarily earn a cut of the profits won by players introduced by them to the club. The hosts and managers would also receive a specific commission (referred to as a “rake”) would then depend on the individual arrangements between the host, manager and the various agents.

(f)    Based on preliminary investigations into “PPPoker”, one Leonard Cheong Weng Han (“Leonard”) was identified and asked to report to the Criminal Investigation Department (“CID”). Upon his arrival at the CID, Leonard was interviewed and subsequently placed under arrest for remote gambling activity. During investigations, Leonard identified David as his upstream manager, i.e. Leonard was managed by David as an agent.

(g)    On 8 September 2018 at about 12.55 a.m., a party of officers conducted follow-up investigation and raided Blk 889C Woodlands Drive 50 #XXX, Singapore. David was located and arrested for remote gambling activity.

(h)    During investigations, David identified and implicated the accused as the host for the club called ‘Wicked G’ on the mobile application, ‘PPPoker’.

(i)    On 26 September 2018, the accused was arrested at Singapore Changi Airport, Terminal 4.

FACTS RELATING TO MAC 904299-2019

(j)    The accused was the host of a club called “Wicked G” on “PPPoker”. The accused created the club sometime in 2017 and recruited David to be the manager of “Wicked G” in April 2018. David was the host of another club on “PPPoker” and brought over 20 players to join “Wicked G” from his earlier club.

(k)    As the host, the accused managed David, who in turn accepted players into the club and gave out chips to the players for the purpose of playing games on “Wicked G”. David also had other agents under him, who also had players under them. In total, the accused had a total of 175 players under him, a number of whom were physically present in Singapore. The accused earned from the enterprise by taking a 10 % “rake”[note: 1] from David and at the time of his arrest, a sum of $8,000 was found to be associated with earnings from “Wicked G”.

(l)    The accused renewed the validity of the club every month. He also opened tables within the club, kicked out players, tagged players to various agents and bestowed titles on agents. When the accused was not available, David would assist the accused in doing this.

(m)    The accused therefore was providing a remote gambling service to these players by organising remote gambling in accordance with arrangements made by the accused and was providing the service in the course of carrying on a business in Singapore.

(n)    Between 1st September 2018 and 7th September 2018, the accused facilitated a remote gambling service for the players in “Wicked G” on the PPPoker mobile application, by providing facilities in accordance with arrangements made by the accused, which service was in the course of carrying on a business in Singapore. A total of S$407,373.86 of bets was placed during this time. The remote gambling service was not provided by an exempt person or a person otherwise exempt under Section 40 of the Remote Gambling Act 2014 (No. 34 of 2014).

(o)    By virtue of the above, the accused has thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 11(1) of the Remote Gambling Act. He is charged accordingly.

(p)    David pleaded guilty to a charge under Section 9(1)(d) of the Remote Gambling Act (No. 34 of 2014) and was sentenced to 15 months’ imprisonment and a fine of $20,000.

Prosecution’s address on sentence

6       The Prosecution submitted for a term of 18 months’ imprisonment and a fine of $20,000. The Prosecution relied on the following arguments:

(a)     The applicable sentencing principles are found in Koo Kah Yee v Public Prosecutor [2021] 3 SLR 1440 (“Koo Kah Yee”). The five-step sentencing framework set down in Koo Kah Yee by Chief Justice Sundaresh Menon (“CJ Menon ”) ought to be applied to the present case.

(b)     Based on the offence-specific factors set out in para 55 of Koo Kah Yee, the level of harm should be regarded as Moderate and the level of culpability should be regarded as Medium for the following reasons:

i.       The number of players under the Accused and the number of bets placed on the platform were significant. There were 175 players under the Accused and a sum of S$407,373.86 was placed as bets.

ii.       While the duration of offending was for about a week (1 – 7 September 2018), the Accused had created the club in 2017 and had recruited David sometime in April 2018, which was about five months before the offending period. This shows that the club had been in existence for a substantial period of time.

iii.       The Accused had earned a sum of $8,000 from the club.

iv.       The Accused played a substantial role in running the club. He renewed the validity of the club every month. He also opened tables within the club, kicked out players, tagged players to various agents and bestowed titles on agents.

(c)     The starting point sentence should therefore be in the lower end of the Moderate Harm-Medium Culpability range at 24 months’ imprisonment. Taking into account his plea of guilt, as well as the fact that there is a similar offence taken into consideration, the starting point sentence should be adjusted downwards to 18 months’ imprisonment.

(d)     As a starting point, at para 52 of Koo Kah Yee, it was held that an offender who facilitates online gambling as a principal under s 11(1) RGA would generally be regarded more seriously than a case in which an agent facilitates one or more aspects of running an online game on behalf of a principal under s 9(1) RGA. The sentence imposed on the Accused must therefore be higher than the sentence imposed on David, who pleaded guilty to an offence under s 9(1)(d) RGA and was sentenced to 15 months’ imprisonment and a fine of $20,000.

(e)     With reference to the case precedents, the sentence of 18 months’ imprisonment is also appropriate. In PP v Elger Kua Meng Tern [2019] SGMC 5 (“Elger Kua”), the offender similarly created a club on “PPPOKER”. He had a total of 296 players under him and facilitated remote gambling between 8 and 14 January 2017. The aggregate value of the bets amounted to around $159,000. The offender was sentenced to 14 months’ imprisonment.

(f)     While the number of players under the Accused is slightly less than those under the offender in Elger Kua, the amount of bets in the present case is much higher. There should therefore be a significant uplift to take into account the amount of bets placed in the present case. The sentence of 18 months’ imprisonment is therefore appropriate.

(g)     With respect to the imposition of a fine, it was held in Koo Kah Yee that in the context of an offence under s 11(1) RGA, a fine would generally be imposed in addition to any custodial sentence to disgorge any profits made by the offender. The mandatory minimum fine of $20,000 is therefore warranted in this case.

Mitigation and Defence’s Submissions on Sentence

7       The Defence submitted for a non-custodial sentence.

8       The Mitigation Plea sets out the Accused’s personal circumstances. The Accused is an only child and lives with his parents. The Accused completed his National Service and commenced his university education at the Singapore Institute of Management in February 2021. He is pursuing a bachelor’s degree in Economics and Business. In February 2022, the Accused obtained approval to disrupt his course of study and suspend the remaining eighteen months’ of his course until the criminal charges against him were finally dealt with.

9       The Defence also submitted that the applicable sentencing framework for offences under s 11(1) RGA is set out in Koo Kah Yee. Applying the First Step, the Defence argued that the harm caused in the present case falls at the higher end of the Low Harm band as this case bears material similarities to the case of Chan Li Shan Serene & ors [2021] SGMC 30 (“Serene Chan”).

a)     The amount involved here is S$407,373.86, which is far lower than the amounts in Koo Kah Yee (S$18million) and PP v Choo Yi Neng Costner [2021] SGMC 50 (“Costner Choo”) (S$2.17million) but is closer to the amount in Serene Chan’s case, albeit higher than the amount in Serene Chan’s case.

b)     There was no syndicate involvement.

c)     There was no transnational element.

d)     There was the use of the internet but there were no attempt to avoid detection. This is also similar to Serene Chan and Costner Choo.

e)     The Accused earned S$8,000 which is significantly less than the salary received by the offender in Koo Kah Yee.

f)     The timeframe of the offence was seven days. This is closer to the time frame in Serene Chan’s case, than it is to Koo Kah Yee’s case or Costner Choo’s case.

10     The Defence further argued that save for the quantum of bets, which is admittedly higher in the present case, and the difference in the time frame, one day in Serene Chan’s case versus seven days in the instant case, there are several similarities between Serene Chan’s case and the present case. The Accused should therefore be similarly placed in the Low Harm grouping as was the offender in Serene Chan’s case. The Defence further stated that applying the First Step from Koo Kah Yee’s case, the Accused’s culpability may be placed at the higher end of Low Culpability or at the cusp of Moderate Culpability as it sits between Serene Chan’s case and Costner Choo’s case.

11     The Defence further submitted that the Accused originally started the club when he was a minor, aged 20 years, to play poker with his co-workers and friends during his leisure time. This suggests a lower level of planning and premeditation compared to Serene Chan (where the three adults with “respectable day jobs” agreed to set up their own poker club after incurring losses) and Costner Choo (where the two co-accused persons agreed to start the club as a business venture). Having started the club, the Accused renewed the club periodically, opened tables, kicked out players, and tagged players to the agents. This suggests a level of culpability equal to that in Serene Chan and Costner Choo, but much lower than that in Koo Kah Yee.

12     Having regard to the Second Step, the Defence submitted that the starting point of the sentence ought to be on the higher end of Low Harm-Low Culpability grouping, or at the very most on the cusp of Low Harm-Moderate Culpability. This would translate to a sentencing starting point of a high fine and or an imprisonment term of up to 10 months.

13     Having regard to the Third Step, the material similarities between the current case and Serene Chan (save for the quantum of bets and the time frame) are undeniable. Even taking into account the quantum of bets and the time frame, the current case bears far more similarities to Serene Chan than to Costner Choo. This would suggest a position within the Low Harm-Low Culpability grouping, or at the cusp of Low Harm-Moderate Culpability.

14     Having regard to the Fourth Step, the Defence submitted that there are very real offender-specific mitigating factors applicable in the present case. It is pertinent that the Accused was only 20 years old, (a minor as defined in the Women’s Charter) when he formed the Club on the PPPoker application. At the time of the offence, the Accused had just turned 21 years of age.

15     Further to the above, Dr Munidasa Winslow stated in his medical report dated 7 January 2022, that the Accused was suffering from the following conditions which contributed to his vulnerability to addictions at the time of the offence:

a)      Gambling Disorder, in early remission (DSM-5; F63.0) – as defined by “persistent and recurrent problematic gambling behaviour leading to clinically significant impairment or distress”; and

b)      Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, Combined presentation (ADHD; DSM-5; 314.01) – as defined by “a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development”.

16     The Defence submitted that while the Court routinely applies the principle of “general deterrence” when passing a sentence for cases under the RGA, this is wholly inappropriate in the present matter. The Accused falls squarely within the specific category of young AND vulnerable persons (by reason of his psychiatric conditions) which Parliament sought to protect when it enacted the RGA, and the Court ought to give due regard to the intentions of Parliament by adopting the principle of “rehabilitation” for the present case.

17     The Defence referred to the second reading of the Remote Gambling Bill on 7 October 2016 by Minister Mr S Iswaran where he stated,

The Bill has two key objectives: first, to tackle the law and order issues associated with remote gambling; second, to protect young persons and other vulnerable persons from being harmed or exploited by remote gambling.

18     The Defence strenuously argued that the Accused fell within the category of persons whom the Minister said ought to be protected under the RGA. That would be the purpose of the Act and that is principally why the Act was enacted. The Accused just wasn’t someone who woke up one day and decided to set up a club. He was playing these games as well, and then he thought that perhaps he could play with his friends too. That was why he set up the club. He was only 20 years old when he set up the club. He was a young person who was exposed to remote gambling at a young age and was therefore vulnerable. He was exploited by the gaming companies who were allowed to spread or create and spread such gambling games on mobile platforms and make them readily available to the general public. The Accused is exactly the type of young person which the RGA seeks to protect.

19     The Defence also cited Ng So Kuen Connie v PP [2003] 3 SLR(R) 178 for the following proposition at [58]:

… the element of general deterrence can and should be given considerably less weight if the offender was suffering from a mental disorder at the time of the commission of the offence. This is particularly so if there is a causal link between the mental disorder and the commission of the offence. In addition to the need for a causal link, other factors such as the seriousness of the mental condition, the likelihood of the appellant repeating the offence and the severity of the crime, are factors which have to be taken into account by the sentencing judge. In my view, general deterrence will not be enhanced by meting out an imprisonment term to a patient suffering from a serious mental disorder which led to the commission of the offence.

20     The Defence acknowledged that the Court has to bear in mind the principle of parity. The co-accused, David Ee, was sentenced to 15 months’ imprisonment but it was likely that the Court had adopted the principle of deterrence. However, this should never eclipse the specific and unique circumstances of each case. The Court has the discretion to revise a sentence upwards or downwards depending on the countervailing circumstances.

21     As stated by Chao Hick Tin JA in PP v Ng Sae Kiat and others [2015] 5 SLR 167 at [87]:

Given that we consider that the parity principle applies as between the Respondents and Vincent Tan and further that they are equally placed in terms of moral culpability, there ought to be parity in the sentences passed on them as compared to that imposed on Vincent Tan. However we accept there is a countervailing consideration in this case: it is trite law that this court has the discretion to enhance a sentence which it considers manifestly inadequate (PP v UI [2008] 4 SLR(R) 500 at [12(d)]) and the court’s discretion in this respect will not be fettered by an erroneously lenient sentence given to a co-offender or imposed in past cases involving identical offences. This is because although parity and consistency in sentencing are desirable goals, they are not necessarily overriding considerations (Yong Siew Soon v PP [1992] 2 SLR(R) 261 at [11] and Meeran bin Mydin v PP [1998] 1 SLR(R) 522 at [14]). We bear these countervailing considerations in mind but it is also important for us to note that ultimately, the circumstances of each case are of paramount importance in determining the appropriate sentence (Dinesh Singh Bhatia s/o Amarjeet Singh v PP [2005] 3 SLR(R) 1 at [24]).

22     The Defence reiterated that the Court ought to adopt the principle of “rehabilitation” in deciding the sentence here, given the Accused’s young age and vulnerability. The Defence cited the case of Siow Kai Yuan Terence (“Terence Siow”) [2020] 4 SLR 1412 which sets out a three-limb framework to determine whether an offender has the propensity for reform. Based on the framework, the Defence argued that the Accused had demonstrated extremely strong propensity for reform.

23     For the first limb, the Accused has shown evidence of remorse by pleading guilty. He has given full and frank disclosure of his involvement in the offence. He has also taken active steps post-offence to leave his errant ways behind. On the second limb, he is shown to have strong familial support and also the availability of positive outlets. On the last limb, he has completely stopped gambling for the past three years. Unlike in Terence Siow where the Court said that the fact that the offender has not recommitted any other offence has little weigh because it has only been 18 months, in this case, it has been three years. As noted by Dr Winslow, the Accused is currently on the path of rehabilitation. He takes full responsibility for his involvement in the offence and deeply regrets the anguish he has caused his parents as a result of his arrest.

24     Following his arrest, the Accused completed his National Service, followed by one year of university education with the Singapore Institute of Management. He hopes to resume and complete his studies after closing this chapter of his life. The Accused has cooperated with the authorities from the very start of the investigations. He had voluntarily returned to Singapore on 26 September 2018 and was arrested by the police.

25     The Defence argued that the sentencing matrix in Koo Kah Yee allows the Court to impose a wide range of fines and the Court has the discretion of imposing non-custodial sentences. The High Court had done so by imposing a fine of S$40,000 in the case of Serene Chan, which is materially similar to the present case save for the quantum of bets and the timeframe of the offences. Taking into account the factors listed above and given the additional mitigating factor of the Accused’s age and vulnerability, a high fine of S$100,000 would be appropriate in the circumstances. The Defence concluded by stating that the Accused is an only child and his parents are prepared to make payment of the fine imposed.

Prosecution’s reply

26     The Prosecution disagreed that the Accused was exploited and that rehabilitation is the primary sentencing consideration in this case. It was argued that the Accused was not exploited at all but had instead orchestrated the entire enterprise. He had graduated from playing the game to organising it, and he went on to profit from it as well, earning at least $8,000 from the enterprise. It was not simply “playing with his friends”, as submitted by the Defence. It was a business enterprise and the Accused was clearly profiting from it.

27     The Prosecution argued that the Defence’s submission that the ambit of the RGA is to protect accused persons such as this Accused is flawed. There is no evidence to support this in either the parliamentary debates or in Koo Kah Yee where CJ Menon had laid out the objectives of the RGA.

28     The Prosecution also countered the submission that rehabilitation should be the primary sentencing consideration because the Accused was slightly over the age of 21 at the time of the offence. The Prosecution pointed out that in Terrence Siow, the key question which CJ Menon dealt with was as follows:

… whether the respondent, as an adult offender both at the time of the offence and of sentencing, has demonstrated an extremely strong propensity for reform such that rehabilitation comes to the fore.

29     The DPP argued that there is no evidence apart from the psychiatric report that suggests that rehabilitation should come to the fore in this case. The Prosecution further submitted that Dr Winslow’s report should be given limited weight for three reasons.

30     First, the initial interview with the Accused was on 15 October 2021, which is more than three years after the offence was committed in September 2018 and about two and a half years after he was charged in Court in April 2019. The facts in the report are all self-reported. There is no objective evidence to support any of the facts that the Accused had related to Dr Winslow.

31     Second, Dr Winslow’s report is inconsistent with the facts contained in the SOF. It is also inconsistent with the facts set out in the Defence’s submissions. Dr Winslow stated in his report that the Accused had joined a club known as “Wicked G” which was part of the US and Europe-based union, “AAA King King”. He had hoped to win back the money which he had lost in gambling. However, he continued to lose money, and also incurred a debt owed to the “AAA King King” union. When the union began chasing the Accused for money, he was introduced to illegal loan sharks by the host of the common gaming houses. He had good credit initially, and the loan sharks agreed to lend him a significant amount of money on condition that he paid them back. The “AAA King King” union then offered to give the Accused US$250 for each person that he referred to the union. The Accused accepted the offer and introduced several acquaintances to the union. These facts are not in either the SOF or in the Defence’s submissions. This inconsistency shows that the account given to Dr Winslow is unreliable and it also calls into question Dr Winslow’s diagnosis of the Accused.

32     Third, the offence here is not one of gambling. It is an offence of organising a remote gambling service. It is not clear from the report how a diagnosis of attention deficit and hyperactivity disorder or a diagnosis of pathological gambling can be linked to an offence under s 11(1) RGA. Dr Winslow does not make it clear and as such, the Prosecution submitted that the Court should not place excessive weight on Dr Winslow’s report.

33     The Prosecution added that the amount of bets in Serene Chan’s case was $11,682. This pales in comparison to the amount of bets in the present case which is over $400,000. The charge in Serene Chan’s case relates to one single day of gambling, on 9 November 2018. The duration of the offending in the present case is longer, for about a week. Furthermore, the facts in Serene Chan’s case show that the offenders had actually stopped the offending long before police investigations had begun. Whereas in the present case, the enterprise was still continuing at the commencement of investigations, and the Accused was arrested only as a result of police investigations.

34     The Prosecution pointed out that the present case is closer in terms of its factual matrix to Elger Kua’s case. In Elger Kua, the number of players was about 296 and the aggregate value of the bets was about $159,000. There were three offenders facilitating 296 players. There were 175 players under the Accused alone, and the amount of bets was also higher at over $400,000.

Decision on sentence

35     The prescribed punishment for offences under s 11(1) RGA is a term of imprisonment for up to seven years, and also be liable to a fine of not less than $20,000 or not more than $500,000. The central question in this case is whether the custody threshold is crossed.

Sentencing Approach

36     Parties were agreed that the applicable sentencing framework for the offence under s 11(1) RGA is set out in CJ Menon’s decision in Koo Kah Yee. It is a five-step sentencing framework:

a)     First Step: Identify (a) the level of harm caused by the offence (slight, moderate or severe); and (b) the offender’s culpability (low, medium or high) having regard to the relevant offence-specific factors.

i.       The factors that go towards harm include, (a) the aggregate value of the bets involved; (b) the involvement of a syndicate; (c) the involvement of a transnational element; and (d) the difficulty of detection.

ii.       The factors that go towards culpability include, (a) the degree of planning and premeditation; (b) the level of sophistication; (c) the offender’s role; (d) personal gain; and (e) the duration of the offending.

b)     Second Step: After ascertaining the levels of harm and culpability, identify the indicative sentencing range using the following matrix:

c)     Third Step: Identify the appropriate starting point within the indicative sentencing range by assessing the harm and culpability on a more granular level.

d)     Fourth Step: Make adjustments for offender specific factors.

e)     Fifth Step: Make further adjustments taking into account the totality principle where an offender faces multiple charges.

37     The Court stated that, whilst s 11(1) RGA permits the issuance of a fine only, the general rule should be that aside from cases where both harm and culpability fall on the lowest end of the spectrum, a combination of a fine and custodial sentence would be warranted. Deterrence similarly calls for the imposition of fines to disgorge the profits of offenders who may also be sentenced to imprisonment. This is essential to dispel the notion that the pecuniary rewards reaped from unlawful remote gambling activities can be enjoyed without consequence.

Case precedents cited

38     I turn now to examine the case precedents cited by parties.

(1)   Koo Kah Yee v Public Prosecutor

39     In Koo Kah Yee v Public Prosecutor, the offender worked for an organisation which was operating as a remote gambling syndicate. The offender was aware of the nature of the organisation’s activities and worked as an administrative staff for the organisation between February 2012 and November 2016. She even recruited one other administrative staff into the syndicate. The offender drew a salary of $55,000. The syndicate operated two remote gambling four-digit (“4D”) websites from Singapore. It established three shell companies for the purpose of carrying out illegal 4D and horse betting activities through the two websites.

40     A total of 49 persons were arrested for activities associated with the syndicate. Police investigations revealed that the total betting revenue received through one of the two websites for the period 22 November 2015 to 14 August 2016 (more than eight months) was $18,207,212.62.

41     The offender pleaded guilty to one charge under s 11(1) RGA read with s 109 Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) for abetting, by intentionally aiding in, the provision of Singapore based remote gambling services from 2 February 2015 to 27 November 2016. Three other related charges were taken into consideration for sentencing. She was sentenced to 12 months’ imprisonment on the proceeded charge. Her appeal against the sentence was dismissed.

42     CJ Menon commented that the level of harm caused in this case was at the low end of the severe range while the offender’s culpability fell within the low end of the medium band. He determined that the appropriate starting sentence would be two and a half years’ imprisonment with a fine. After making adjustments for an early plea of guilt, a clean record, but noting the TIC charges, he concluded that the appropriate sentence for the offence would be 24 months’ imprisonment and a fine to disgorge the offender’s profits. However, there was no cross-appeal filed and he declined to disturb the District Judge’s sentence.

(2)   Public Prosecutor v Elger Kua Meng Tern

43     In Elger Kua, which is a case that pre-dates Koo Kah Yee, the offender pleaded guilty to one charge under s 4(1) CGHA, and one charge under s 11(1) RGA. A third charge under s 8(1) RGA was TIC-ed.

44     Sometime in August 2016, the offender created an account on the mobile application “PPPOKER”. Similar to the present case, the application allowed its users to play online poker games, which involved the placing of bets. After creating the account, the offender created a club on the PPPoker application, which allowed him to invite and grant approval to other account holders to join his club and play online poker games with other club members. Thereafter, the offender facilitated participation by recruiting club members who would play within this application. He asked acquaintances, including the players who went to his “Texas Hold ’Em” tournaments, to create an account on the PPPoker application. They would then search for his Club ID 1117, send a request to join the club, whereupon he would approve the application.

45     There were around 296 players in the club, although not all members played regularly. Between 8 January 2017 to 14 January 2017, the offender facilitated remote gambling by the club members on the PPPoker application with a total bet value of $159,101. The offender would settle accounts with the club members by meeting them in person to hand over monies or by way of bank transfers. The offender earned about 5% of the value of the bets from the club members. His monthly gross profit was around $40,000, but he claimed that his monthly nett profit was much lower at around $10,000 because he gave discounts to club members who had lost too much to encourage them to continue playing in his club.

46     The offender was sentenced to 14 months’ imprisonment and a fine of $20,000 in default one months’ imprisonment for this offence. The offender’s appeal against sentence lapsed.

47     This case was referred to by CJ Menon in Koo Kah Yee. CJ Menon agreed with the District Judge that facilitating online gambling as a principal under s 11(1) RGA would generally be regarded more seriously than a case where an agent facilitates one or more of the aspects of running an online game on behalf of a principal under s 9(1) RGA. He also agreed with the District Judge that the imposition of a fine in addition to a period of imprisonment under the RGA was necessary to negate the profit motive of remote gambling activities.

(3)   PP v Choo Yi Neng Costner

48     In Costner Choo’s case, three offenders were involved in providing a Singapore-based remote gambling service by organising online poker games. They utilised an existing online poker application, invited players, and organised online poker games. The offenders decided to jointly host a poker club on the online poker application as a business venture. Over a period of 105 days, from 23 September 2017 to 5 January 2018, players placed a total of not less than $2,166,741.20 worth of bets via 293 unique accounts.

49     The offenders procured agents who would source for players. Costner Choo’s co-offender, Dominic Tay Chun Long (“Tay”), also invited players to gamble in the club. Tay served as the central management and control of the remote gambling service. He managed the remote gambling by keeping track of their players’ bets. Every week, Tay would generate a report summarising the tables that were opened and the games that were played that week. Tay would send these reports to Costner Choo to “tidy up” the report. Costner Choo would calculate the final profit or loss incurred by each individual player. This would form the basis for that week’s settlement of monies. Tay would then distribute the money or the money’s worth through the agents or directly to the players.

50      Costner Choo pleaded guilty to one charge of providing a Singapore-based remote gambling service under s 11(1) RGA. Another charge under s 8 RGA was TIC-ed.

51     Based on these facts, the Court pegged this case at Moderate Harm – lower end of High Culpability with a starting sentence of 30 months’ imprisonment. The Court then gave a ⅓ discount after taking into account Costner Choo’s clean record, early plea of guilt and cooperation with the police during investigations. He was sentenced to 20 months’ imprisonment. The sentence was upheld on appeal.

(4)   Public Prosecutor v Chan Li Shan Serene and others

52     In Serene Chan’s case, the offender and two of her friends started as poker players in an online club on the “Pokerrrr2” mobile application. After suffering betting losses, they decided to set up their own online club on the same application to host their own poker games. Each of them invited friends to join their club to play. Friends of those friends were also accepted. A WhatsApp chat group was set up and used to notify club members of upcoming games, and to also update on the settlements after games were concluded. Games were run regularly in the evenings every two to three days. The offender and her friends took turns to host. The offender’s role was to update the chat group and attend to the win/loss settlements using her POSB account. Her friends supervised the games to prevent huge losses. As hosts, the offender and her friends raked in 5% as tips from all bets placed at their club. This was regardless of the win/loss outcomes of the games. The club proceeds were shared equally amongst them. The club operated for six months from June to December 2018, when they finally decided to close the club down. In that time, club membership had grown to 60-70 active and non-active players. Subsequent police investigations into gambling rings led to their arrest.

53     The District Judge determined that the level of harm fell squarely within the slight range as the amount of bets for the charge was $11,682 involving one evening’s operations with 29 club members playing that night. After considering the role played by the offender, the Court determined that her culpability fell within the high band. This led to an indicative sentencing range as laid down in Koo Kah Yee of one to two and a half years’ imprisonment with a disgorgement fine.

54     The Court accepted that the offender did not embark on this venture as part of a criminal syndicate, or to fund some other illicit activities. The offender and her friends took the initiative to cease the club’s operations on their own after becoming concerned over its legality. She had also pleaded guilty to the offence under s 11(1) RGA at an early stage. The offender was sentenced to three months’ imprisonment and a fine of $20,000 in default two months’ imprisonment. On appeal, the sentence was reduced to a fine of $40,000 in default four months’ imprisonment.

55     The appellate court, upon applying the sentencing framework, determined that the level of harm caused was low and the level of culpability was low as well. The court was of view that the indicative sentencing range was a fine of at least $20,000 and/or a short term of imprisonment. However, given the wide range prescribed for fines, this seemed to be an appropriate case for fines to be imposed. The circumstances did not point towards custodial sentences for the offender. The appellate court took into account the offender’s plea of guilt and was of the view that a fine of $40,000 in default four months’ imprisonment would be appropriate. This would include the disgorgement element.

Sentencing framework applied

56     I turn now to apply the five-step sentencing framework prescribed in Koo Kah Yee to the present case.

First Step: Level of harm caused/Level of culpability

57     The facts revealed that the aggregate value of the bets in this case was substantial - S$407,373.86. There were 175 players under the Accused. The duration of offending was for about a week (1 – 7 September 2018), but the Accused had created the club in 2017 and had recruited David sometime in April 2018, which was about five months before the offending period. The Accused earned a sum of $8,000 from the commission of the offence. There was no evidence of a transnational element or of syndicate involvement.

58     The Accused played a substantial role in running the club. He managed David and renewed the validity of the club every month. He also opened tables within the club, kicked out players, tagged players to various agents and bestowed titles on agents.

59     Based on these facts, I agreed with the Prosecution that the level of harm is at the lower end of the Moderate range and the level of culpability is within the Medium band. My reasons are as follows.

60     The aggregate value of the bets, S$407,373.86, was significant. While the amount does not run into the millions as seen in Koo Kah Yee or Costner Choo, it is far higher than the bet value in Serene Chan’s case - $11,682. The high bet value in the present case indicates a much higher level of harm than in Serene Chan’s case.

61     It was highlighted by the Prosecution that the Accused had created the club in 2017 and had recruited the agent, David, in April 2018. This suggests that more bets may have been placed prior to the offending period which points to a higher aggregate bet value. I should make it clear that I did not attach too much significance to these facts - that the club was created much earlier or that David was recruited some five months prior to the period specified in the charge. It is unclear on the facts whether the Accused had provided remote gambling services during this earlier period or what bets if any, were placed during this time. It would be unhelpful to speculate. I confined myself largely to the period specified in the charge in determining the level of harm caused as well as the Accused’s level of culpability.

62     The Accused hosted the poker punters on his virtual club, Wicked G, through the mobile application PPPoker. As noted by the Court in Koo Kah Yee,[note: 2] such a platform affords anonymity, especially for the operators of the club. This holds true for the present case as well. The police had initially picked up a punter named Leonard who identified David. David was questioned and he in turn identified the Accused. The police clearly had to carry out extensive investigations and multiple levels of inquiry before the Accused was discovered and apprehended. These facts point to the difficulty in detection which merits an enhanced assessment of the level of harm.

63     In Costner Choo’s case, which bore a similar factual matrix, the Court held that the level of harm fell squarely within the moderate range. The value of bets and the period of offending in Costner Choo was higher but similar to the present case, there was no syndicate involvement or a transnational element. In the present case, unlike Serene Chan’s case, the bet value was substantial and the offending had continued up until police investigations caught up with the Accused. Accordingly, I found that the level of harm here was lesser than in Costner Choo’s case but still within Moderate harm band, albeit at the lower end of the range.

64     I turn now to assess the Accused’s level of culpability.

65     The Accused set up the club, Wicked G, in 2017. I accepted the Defence’s submissions that the Accused had originally created the club to play poker with his co-workers and friends during his leisure time. In other words, the club had started innocently enough. The planning and premeditation to provide remote gambling services for profit only came later. The facts show that the Accused took active steps to execute that plan.

66     He began by recruiting an agent, David, to source for players for the club. As noted in Koo Kah Yee,[note: 3] where offenders recruit others into a criminal operation, this would usually be an aggravating factor. At the time of his arrest, the Accused was hosting 175 poker players via his club. This is far more than the 29 players involved in the charge in Serene Chan’s case. In any case, Serene Chan and her friends did not actively recruit players. They simply invited their friends and friends of those friends to join the club.

67     As the sole host of the club, the Accused single-handedly managed the functions of the club. He renewed the validity of the club every month, opened tables within the club, kicked out players, tagged players to various agents and bestowed titles on agents. David would assist only when the Accused was not available. The Accused’s role was therefore central to the functioning of the club.

68     In comparison, in Serene Chan’s case, the offender’s role was limited to updating the chat group and attending to the win/loss settlements using her POSB account. It was not critical to the functioning of the club. Similarly, in Costner Choo’s case, his role was secondary to that of his co-offender Tay. Costner Choo’s role was to study the reports generated by Tay and to calculate the final profit or loss incurred by each individual player. Yet Costner Choo’s level of culpability was assessed to be at the lower end of the High culpability range.

69     The Accused and David received a commission or a ‘rake’ for each round of play. The Accused alone earned a substantial profit of $8,000 from operating the club. On the other hand, Serene Chan earned only about $194 (5% of $11,682 shared equally amongst the three of them) for her efforts.

70     In the final assessment, the Accused’s culpability is higher than that of the offender in Serene Chan’s case. He may not have executed a more elaborate plan or set up a more sophisticated club to provide the remote gaming services, but the extent and his involvement, the duration of the offending, and the personal gain from the operations of the club was far greater. I therefore agreed with the Prosecution that the Accused’s culpability fell within the Medium culpability band.

Second Step: Indicative sentencing range

71     It would follow then that the indicative sentencing range for Moderate Harm-Medium Culpability would be one to two and a half years imprisonment.

Third Step: Appropriate starting point

72     I next embarked on a more granular assessment of the harm and the culpability factors. Based on the value of the bets and the ambit of the remote gambling services provided, and also noting the absence of a syndicate involvement or a transnational element, I concluded that the level of harm fell within the lower end of the Moderate Harm range. Given the Accused’s substantial role in the club’s operations, I found that the level of culpability fell squarely within the Medium Culpability band. The appropriate starting point would be 24 months’ imprisonment.

Fourth Step: Offender specific factors

73     In the Accused’s favour, I acknowledged his plea of guilt as clear evidence of his remorse and gave it its due weight. I also noted that he had cooperated with the police following his arrest and has not reoffended since. The two aggravating factors present in this case are the TIC charge for gambling under s 7 CGHA and his criminal antecedents for unrelated offences. I did not view them as significant aggravating factors and did not attach much weight to them.

74     It was argued by the Defence that the principle of rehabilitation takes precedence in this case and displaces the principle of deterrence. They relied on two sources in support of this submission. The first was the parliamentary reports for the second reading of the Remote Gambling Bill. The second was the medical report prepared by his psychiatrist, Dr Winslow. I will first deal with the ministerial statements made in Parliament.

75     The Defence submitted that the purpose of the RGA as stated by Minister Mr S Iswaran, during the parliamentary reading is: first, to tackle the law and order issues associated with remote gambling; second, to protect young persons and other vulnerable persons from being harmed or exploited by remote gambling. The Defence argued that the phrase ‘young persons and other vulnerable persons’ would include offenders like the Accused. The Prosecution responded that the Defence’s arguments were misconceived. The purpose of the RGA is not to protect offenders like the Accused who had graduated from playing the game to organising it and profiting from it as well. The Prosecution also submitted that the distinction must be made between the offence of providing remote gambling services and the offence of gambling.

76     I agreed with the Prosecution that from a plain reading of the ministerial statement there is no support for the proposition that a young adult offender who had in the past participated in remote gambling but is now charged with providing remote gambling services, ought to be considered for rehabilitation rather than deterrence. Neither was such an intent articulated or alluded to by CJ Menon in Koo Kah Yee when he considered the purpose of the RGA. The twin purposes expressed by the Minister in Parliament is also found in s 7 of the RGA. The provision itself does not go beyond what was already stated by the Minister in Parliament. The Defence’s arguments are unsupported.

77     Nevertheless, I did go on to assess if on the facts of this case, the key sentencing consideration ought to be rehabilitation. The Accused may have been exposed to remote gambling at a young age and thereafter become addicted to it. However, the present charge is not for illegal gambling. It is for the provision of the remote gambling services. The Accused is exactly the type of person which the RGA seeks to deter in order prevent law and order issues associated with remote gambling. He can hardly be termed as a ‘victim’ who is harmed or exploited by remote gambling. The argument for rehabilitation as the principal sentencing consideration must fail.

78     The Defence also relied on Dr Winslow’s medical report. The Prosecution had correctly highlighted that the facts found in the medical report are not found in the SOF and not relied on either by the Defence during their submissions. I agreed with the Prosecution that the differing accounts were troubling. The account provided to Dr Winslow was entirely self-reported. It is not found in the Defence’s submissions or in the SOF. It does raise concerns about the veracity of the account given to Dr Winslow, and consequently the diagnosis which was made by Dr Winslow.

79     In any case, I agree with the Prosecution that it is unclear how a diagnosis of Gambling Disorder, in early remission and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, combined presentation, is relevant to the charge for providing unauthorised remote gambling services. Consequently, I ascribed little weight to the medical report.

80     At the end of the assessment, I found that the principle of deterrence remained as the key sentencing consideration here and that this was not an appropriate case for a sentence of a fine only.

81     In the final analysis, after balancing the aggravating factors against the mitigating factors, I concluded that a downward adjustment of six months from the starting sentence of 24 months would be appropriate. Accordingly, I imposed a sentence of 18 months’ imprisonment with a disgorgement fine of $20,000 in default one month’s imprisonment.

Conclusion

82     The total sentence of 18 months imprisonment with a fine of $20,000 in default one month’s imprisonment is neither crushing nor manifestly excessive. It is just and appropriate given the facts of this case.
