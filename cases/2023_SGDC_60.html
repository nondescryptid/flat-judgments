Principal District Judge Victor Yeo Khee Eng:

Introduction

1       This is an appeal against conviction and sentence by Mr Mohamad Shalahuddin Bin Sahid (the “Accused”). The Accused had claimed trial to two charges, one charge of outrage of modesty under s 354(2) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) (“PC”) and one charge of attempted outrage of modesty under s 354(2) read with s 511(1) of the PC.

2       At the commencement of trial, there was a subsisting gag order that no person shall publish the name, address, and photograph of the victim or any evidence which is likely to lead to her identification. The order remains to date.

The Charges

3       The proceeded charges read as follows: -

1st Charge DAC-906677-2020 (Re-amended) [C1B] [“ First Charge ”]

You, [Accused], are charged that you, sometime in 2016, in a bedroom at Blk 770 Pasir Ris St 71 [unit number redacted], Singapore, did use criminal force to one [victim’s name redacted], a female then under 14 years old age (a female then aged 8 to 9 years old, DOB: DD.MM.2007), knowing it to be likely that you would thereby outrage her modesty, to wit, by using your finger to touch the anus area of the victim in a circular motion, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under s 354(2) Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

2nd Charge DAC-906678-2020 (Re-amended) [C2C] [“ Second Charge ”]

You, [Accused], are charged that you, sometime between 9 and 13 September 2019, at Blk 770 Pasir Ris St 71 [unit number redacted], Singapore, did attempt to use criminal force to one [victim’s name redacted], a female then under 14 years old age (a female then aged 12 years old, DOB: DD.MM.2007) with intent to outrage her modesty, to wit, by attempting to insert your hand into the back of her pants, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under s354(2) read with s 511(1) Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

4       The trial lasted for 11 days, over three separate tranches of hearing dates in September 2021, January 2022, and February 2022. The Prosecution led evidence from a total of 10 witnesses, including the victim, and the Defence led evidence from two witnesses, including the Accused. At the conclusion of the trial, I found that the Prosecution had proven its case against the Accused beyond reasonable doubt, and accordingly, I convicted the Accused on both charges.

5       After hearing the Prosecution’s submission on sentence and the Defence’s mitigation plea and submission on sentence, I imposed the following sentences on the Accused.

6       In respect of the first charge, I sentenced the Accused to 18 months’ imprisonment and six (6) weeks’ imprisonment in lieu of three (3) strokes of the cane, given that the Accused, who is above 50 years of age, could not be caned pursuant to s325 Criminal Procedure Code 2010. In respect of the second charge, I sentenced the Accused to four (4) weeks’ imprisonment. I further ordered that the sentences run consecutively, making an aggregate sentence of 18 months and 10 weeks’ imprisonment.

7       The Accused, being dissatisfied with my decision, has filed an appeal against both conviction and sentence. I hereby set out the reasons for the conviction and sentence imposed.

The Law

27     It is trite that in any criminal proceedings, at the end of the trial, the court must decide whether the evidence adduced by the Prosecution was sufficient to prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt.

28     In this respect, I am mindful that in cases involving sexual offences, it was often the case of the words of the victim against the words of the accused. The general approach to be taken in sexual offences has been laid down in the cases of Khoo Kwoon Hain v PP [1995] 2 SLR 767, Teo Keng Pong v PP [1996] 3 SLR 329 and subsequently approved in Tang Kin Seng v PP [1997] 1 SLR 46. The Honourable Chief Justice Yong Pung How stated the following at [39] and [44]:

“In a trial for sexual offences, what is important is that the evidence is weighed carefully, always bearing in mind the relevant aspects of human nature and behaviour. It would be wrong to be bogged down by technicalities especially when it has no logical problem at hand…

In my view, the right approach is to analyse the evidence for the Prosecution and for the Defence, and decide whether the complainant's evidence is so reliable that a conviction based solely on it is not unsafe. If it is not, it is necessary to identify which aspect of it is not so convincing and for which supporting evidence is required or desired. In assessing the supporting evidence, the question then is whether this supporting evidence makes up for the weakness in the complainant's evidence. All these would, of course, have to be done in light of all the circumstances of each case and all the evidence, including the defence evidence, as well as accumulated knowledge of human behaviour and common sense.”

29     With these guiding principles in mind, I will now proceed to analyse the evidence presented by the Prosecution and the Defence at trial. I do not propose to rehash all the evidence led from each individual witness, but I will however, in the course of my Grounds of Decision, deal with the evidence of the material witnesses in this trial.

The Prosecution’s Case

30     The Prosecution’s case is that the Accused, a trusted family friend whom the young victim felt closer to than her own parents, took advantage of his position and her ignorance to violate her on multiple occasions.

Evidence of the Victim (PW1)

31     In respect of the first charge, which occurred sometime in 2016, the Prosecution led evidence from the victim, who testified that herself and her sister, PW8, were having tuition in the dining area at the Accused’s house when the victim had a breakout of hives on her arm [note: 15]. This occurred sometime nearer the end of the tuition session [note: 16]. Mdm Siti offered to apply powder on the hives to soothe it, and they went into the master bedroom for Mdm Siti to do so [note: 17].

32     The Accused was in the master bedroom at the time, and he told Mdm Siti to go back out to the dining area to continue teaching PW8, while he offered to apply powder on the victim [note: 18]. Mdm Siti then left the victim with the Accused in the said bedroom and the Accused closed the bedroom door [note: 19].

33     Following that, the Accused told the victim to pull her pants down and she did so, together with her underwear. She laid down on the bed with her back facing up and her feet dangling from the edge of the bed [note: 20]. Next, the victim felt the Accused’s hand touching one of her butt cheeks and slowly moving onto the other butt cheek [note: 21]. She then felt one of the Accused’s fingers slowly going in a circular motion on her anus [note: 22] around five to eight times [note: 23]. After this, the Accused then told her that he was done, and she left the bedroom to return to the dining area to study [note: 24].

34     The victim testified that, at the time, she did not realise what the Accused did was wrong, and it was only when she attended sex education class in Primary Five that she realised that what the Accused did was a “bad touch” [note: 25]. This led to her breaking down in class, but she did not tell anyone about what had happened because a part of her did not want to believe that it happened [note: 26].

35     In respect of the second charge which occurred sometime between 9 and 13 September 2019, the victim testified that she went to the Accused’s house for tuition with Mdm Siti. She had resumed tuition with Mdm Siti in the later part of 2019 despite what had happened to her earlier because her parents wanted her to take up tuition with Mdm Siti again, given that her PSLE was approaching, and her Mathematics grades were falling behind [note: 27].

36     The victim gave evidence that during one of her breaks, she was sitting on the edge of the couch in the living room when the Accused came to sit next to her [note: 28]. At the time, she was leaning forward as she sat, and the length of her shirt was such that her back would be slightly exposed when she did so. The Accused then tried to insert his hand into the back of her pants and when she felt his fingers on her waist (just above where her pants were), she swept or slapped his hand away [note: 29]. After that, the Accused got up and left [note: 30].

37     The victim further testified that after one of the victim’s tutoring sessions with Mdm Siti between 9 and 13 September 2019, the Accused had sent her home via car. During this journey, the Accused asked the victim if PW8 had already grown pubic hair, which greatly alarmed the victim [note: 31]. As the victim feared that her sister would also fall prey to the Accused, she decided to talk to her friend about this, and then eventually disclosed what happened between the Accused and her to the school counsellor, PW6 Deon Tan-Goh Siew Hui (“Ms Deon”) on 18 September 2019 [note: 32].

38     Under rigorous cross-examination, the victim’s evidence remained materially consistent and unshaken. In respect of the first charge, when it was put to her that there was no logical explanation for the Accused to have asked her to pull her pants down and lay down on the bed if the hives were on her arm, especially when he was allegedly meant to assist her in applying powder on her hives, she had agreed to the same. She was also candid in expressing that the Accused had not in fact asked her to pull down her underwear, but she had done it of her own accord when she was instructed to pull her pants down as she had thought it meant the same thing. This was also consistent with the evidence she had given in her Evidence-in-Chief [note: 33].

39     When it was further put to the victim that the Accused had only touched her buttocks once before when he was assisting his wife to powder her on one of the occasions that she had taken a shower at their house, the victim maintained that it did not happen and that on that occasion he had only touched her back. She was unequivocally clear and consistent in her evidence that the incident where the Accused had touched her butt cheeks and anus was a separate occasion and did not seem to conflate the two occasions [note: 34].

40     In respect of the second charge, it was put to the victim during cross-examination that the Accused had not used his fingers to touch her at the lower back of her waist just above her pants whilst they were on the couch as alleged, but that he had actually tapped her on the back of her shoulder whilst she was having tuition at the dining table [note: 35]. The victim confirmed that the Accused had indeed tapped her on the back of her shoulder and reminded her to study but maintained that was separate from the incident that happened on the couch [note: 36]. Whilst she could not recall when exactly the incident at the dining table happened, she was clear that it had happened on a separate occasion prior to the incident on the couch [note: 37]. She was, however, sure in her evidence that the incident on the couch happened at least a week before she made the police report [note: 38].

41     When questioned on why she never told anyone prior to the police report being lodged in 2019, despite knowing that the incidences in 2016 and 2019 was considered molest, the victim testified that she did not tell her family about what happened all this while because she was afraid that they would not believe her and would choose to believe the Accused instead [note: 39]. Prior to the matter being reported to the Police, the victim had a good relationship with everyone in the Accused’s family, and was close to the Accused, his wife, their daughter and youngest son. In fact, at the material times, the victim was not close to any of her family members, and instead had acknowledged the Accused as one of the persons whom she trusted the most [note: 40].

Evidence of the Victim’s mother (PW2)

42     In her evidence, PW2 largely corroborated the evidence given by the victim on how the latter ended up having tuition with Mdm Siti at the Accused’s house. She also testified that the victim was an introvert and largely kept to herself and would not share much with her parents or family. PW2 stated that the victim only started to open herself up more after the police report was lodged and the incidents of molest came to light [note: 41]. She also confirmed that prior to this, both the victim and her family did not have any kind of trouble or disputes with the Accused and his family [note: 42].

43     PW2 further testified to the fact that throughout the time both families had known each other, the Accused had treated the victim differently from her other two daughters. She stated that whenever the Accused went on family trips where the victim’s family did not join, he would always only get the victim a present and not the other daughters. When questioned, the Accused had stated that it was because the victim ‘is his princess and she’s [his] special’. She also affirmed that the Accused and his wife would only take the victim out for her birthday and not the other daughters [note: 43]. In fact, when both families planned to go to Malacca together in 2019, the Accused was the one who instructed his wife to call PW2 to request that they take the victim with them in their car. However, the victim’s father was not comfortable with the victim going alone with the Accused’s family and insisted that the victim’s youngest sister, went with her. As the victim’s father was unable to take leave on the same day, the victim’s family only headed up to Malacca separately the following day [note: 44].

44     Under cross-examination, PW2 also testified that the victim and her sisters stopped going to the Accused’s house for tuition in 2017 and 2018 as she was busy running a business [note: 45]. They only resumed going to the Accused’s house for tuition in 2019 because that was the year the victim was taking her PSLE [note: 46]. PW2 explained that this had happened because Mdm Siti had reached out to her sometime in early 2019. According to PW2, Mdm Siti called because the Accused had instructed the latter to do so as ‘[his] princess is taking PSLE [that] year’. As such, Mdm Siti proposed to give the victim a tuition crash course [note: 47].

Evidence of Dr Parvathy Pathy (PW4)

45     The Prosecution led evidence from Dr Parvathy Pathy, who was the Senior Consultant, Child Guidance Clinic at the Institute of Mental Health who interviewed the victim and her father on 14 November 2019. Dr Pathy concluded that the victim was able to give an account of the alleged matter and reported some symptoms suggestive of post-traumatic stress and is fit to give evidence in court. Arrangements were also made for the victim to receive counselling [note: 48].

46     Dr Pathy testified that during her consultation with the victim, she observed that the victim was quiet, timid and nervous looking, and was rather hesitant and needed more time to process questions and give an account. After she built a rapport with the victim, she told her that the Accused did inappropriate things to her when she was in Primary 3. Recounting the incident, the victim told Dr Pathy that on that occasion when she had rashes, the Accused offered to apply powder and when the Accused applied powder on the buttocks and he circled her anus with his finger [note: 49].

47     When Dr Pathy asked what made her eventually disclose the matter, the victim shared that she eventually disclosed the matter to her school counsellor as the Accused began to ask her if her younger sister had pubic hair and she began to worry that the Accused might do the same things to her sister. During cross-examination, Dr Pathy explained that from her personal experience in this field and in the literature on child sexual abuse, she is not surprised that the reason why the child does not tell anyone about the incident earlier is that they are afraid that nobody would believe them. She also made sure to ask the victim if she was telling the truth and that she could be punished if she lied, and the victim stated that she was telling the truth regarding the Accused [note: 50].

Evidence of Dr Geetha Visvalingam (PW5)

48     The Prosecution led evidence from Dr Geetha, who was the Associate Consultant from the Division of Obstetrics and Gynaecology at KK Women’s and Children’s Hospital who had interviewed the victim after the police report was lodged. Her main role and the purpose of her interview with the victim was to ascertain the brief background facts or context which the sexual assault took place as well as the mode of sexual assault. This would then guide Dr Geetha in assessing the various tests and physical examinations that would need to be conducted accordingly [note: 51].

49     In Dr Geetha’s evidence given at trial, she testified that during her consultation with the victim, the victim told her that when the Accused applied powder on the buttocks and then circled her anus with his finger, he did not put his finger inside her anus in relation to the alleged molest in 2016 i.e. the first charge as recorded in her medical report [note: 52]. She further testified that the tear at 5 o’clock of the hymenal ring and as well as a partial tear at around 10 o’clock of the hymenal ring was an old hymenal tear but whether the said tear is correlated to the sexual assault is inconclusive as there are many causes for hymenal tears. However, Dr Geetha stated that having hymenal tears is not common for a person like the victim who was not sexually active, unless the victim was doing some horseback riding or gymnastics or had a fall or where there is clear cut trauma, for example, from bicycle rides. In the absence of some history suggestive of these things, it is very unlikely that the victim just happened to have a hymenal tear [note: 53].

Evidence of Ms Deon Tan-Goh Siew Hui (PW6)

50     The Prosecution also led evidence from Ms Deon, who was the school counsellor at the primary school the victim was attending at the time. She testified that she had been having sessions with the victim even prior to the victim divulging the incidents of sexual assault to her. She first started seeing the victim when the latter approached her expressing thoughts of self-harm sometime in August 2019 [note: 54]. The victim had told her that she felt neglected by her parents and felt lousy about herself [note: 55].

51     Thereafter, Ms Deon continued to follow up with her for a few more sessions. Sometime after the 2019 September school holidays, the victim approached Ms Deon urgently to inform her about the incidences of sexual assault that she had experienced from the Accused. That was the first time the victim had divulged to Ms Deon that she was being touched inappropriately. When Ms Deon questioned the victim on why she felt the need to share it with her then, the victim replied that she was worried that the same thing would happen to her younger sister, PW8, as the Accused had started to ask if the latter had grown pubic hairs [note: 56].

52     Under cross-examination, Ms Deon testified that when the victim had first divulged this information to her, she told her that it was her tutor’s husband who is a family friend that had touched her inappropriately since she was in Primary 3 [note: 57]. The victim also shared with Ms Deon that there was a break for about a period of two years before she resumed tuition with Mdm Siti [note: 58]. According to what the victim had told her, the reason why they had stopped going for tuition at the Accused’s house was because the family had gone through some financial difficulties when her mother’s business partner ran away. As the family was struggling to cope, they had stopped contacting the Accused and his family for a period of about two years [note: 59].

53     Although Ms Deon was not told by the victim specific details like exact locations and dates of the occurrence of each of the incidences [note: 60], she maintained in her evidence that the victim was coherent in recounting the incidents and was consistent in doing so [note: 61]. This was even so when Ms Deon conceded to the learned Defence Counsel’s suggestion that there might have been some loss in translation between what was actually shared to her by the victim and what Ms Deon might have interpreted or understood from what she was being told.

54     Ms Deon further testified that according to protocol, before the case could be reported to the Child Protection Services, she would first need to seek concurrence from her school leaders i.e. the school principal or vice-principal. Before that, she would first need to inform her reporting officer, who would first conduct another interview with the victim before ascertaining whether the matter should be escalated to the school leaders. As her reporting officer was on leave the day the victim first divulged this information i.e. 18 September 2019, Ms Deon had no choice but to let the victim head home that day whilst she waited for further instructions. After ensuring that she was able to keep herself safe upon returning home, the victim was allowed to go home after school [note: 62]. The next morning, Ms Deon’s reporting officer sat down with the victim and asked her some further questions, before making the decision to inform the school leaders. The decision was then made to report the matter to the Child Protective Services and to bring the victim to make the police report against the Accused [note: 63].

55     At the close of Prosecution’s case, I was of the view that the Prosecution had made out a prima facie case against the Accused in respect of the two proceeded charges and accordingly, I called upon the Accused to enter his defence.

The Defence’s Case

56     The Defence’s case is one of total denial of the two charges. The Accused maintained that he did not outrage the modesty of the victim in the manner as described but had only applied powder on the victim after her bath when his wife was attending to the victim’s sister PW8. There was no intention to or knowledge that his act of powdering the victim would outrage the victim’s modesty.

57     As the Prosecution’s main witness is the victim and their case is based solely on the victim’s account and version of events, the Defence submitted that the testimony given by the victim is not so “unusually convincing” to meet the Prosecution’s standard burden of proof beyond reasonable doubt to warrant a conviction.

58     The Defence also submitted that the testimonies given by the Prosecution witnesses should be treated with caution as they are not independent evidence but merely repeated what the victim had told them. Moreover, certain witnesses are biased as they trusted what the victim had told them.

59     The Defence further submitted that even if there were discrepancies in the Accused’ evidence, these are minor or not material. He was in a state of confusion when he gave his statement to the police and was not thinking straight.

Evidence of the Accused (DW1)

60     The Accused testified that he got to know the victim sometime in 2013 as the latter’s aunt’s husband was a close friend of his [note: 64]. Thereafter, the victim started having tuition with his wife at his home sometime in January 2016. The victim and her younger sister, PW8, would go over to the Accused’s home every Friday for tuition except during the school holidays, Hari Raya Puasa or during the fasting month [note: 65]. In his evidence, the Accused also corroborated the victim’s and her parents’ testimony that amongst the victim and her two sisters, he was closest to the victim. He testified that he treated her like the youngest child in his family and would usually only buy presents for her on her birthday or when she did well for examinations [note: 66].

61     In respect of the first charge, the Accused denied offering to powder the victim when she had a breakout of hives and in doing so, told her to pull down her pants and lie down on the bed. It was also his evidence that he never used his finger to touch her anus in a circular motion [note: 67]. The Accused testified that he had only known about the victim’s hives breakouts because she had approached him to apply powder for her and he would do so on her instructions. According to the Accused, he would put the powder on his hands and apply it to the parts of the victim’s body where she tells him it was itching. The Accused testified that it was usually on her joint areas like the elbows, knees and neck and the top of her back [note: 68]. This happened about two to three times in 2016. The Accused further testified that the victim will not only ask him to apply powder on her but also approach Mdm Siti and his other children to do so [note: 69].

62     The Accused also testified that whilst on all three occasions he had applied powder on the victim, it had indeed happened in the master bedroom with the door somewhat closed, he claimed that the door was never fully shut but was always slightly ajar [note: 70]. It was also his evidence that he had never laid on the bed beside the victim and would only apply powder on her whilst she was seated on his bed fully clothed [note: 71].

63     Further, the Accused testified that the only occasion where he had applied powder to the victim whilst she was naked was sometime in 2016 after the victim had showered in the toilet of his master bedroom. Following her shower, Mdm Siti had dried the victim, and after the victim complained about feeling itchy to Mdm Siti, Mdm Siti instructed the Accused to apply powder on the victim because she was busy with PW8. He then did so in Mdm Siti’s presence by putting powder on his hands and applied the powder from the victim’s back down to her legs [note: 72].

The Main Issues at Trial

76     The main issue before the court is whether the victim’s account is sufficiently credible (as opposed to that of the Accused) for this Court to accept it as being “unusually convincing” given that there were no independent eye-witnesses to the alleged incidents other than the victim’s account of the incidents relating to the two charges.

77     As observed by the Court of Appeal in AOF v Public Prosecutor [2012] 3 SLR 34 (“AOF”) at [111], the “unusually convincing” standard “applies to the uncorroborated evidence of a witness in all offences (and not just sexual offences), where such evidence forms the sole basis for a conviction” [emphasis added]: Public Prosecutor v GCK and another matter [2020] 1 SLR 486 (“GCK”) at [104].

78     The “unusually convincing” standard is thus applicable in all offences where the sole basis relied upon for conviction is the evidence of a witness whose evidence is uncorroborated. In the context of a sexual offence, it is not uncommon that the offending acts are often perpetrated behind closed doors, where the offender is alone with the victim and without the presence of other persons. Thus, in assessing whether the witness’ testimony is “unusually convincing”, the court weighs the demeanour of the witness alongside the internal and external consistencies in the witness’ testimony: AOF at [115]. In this regard, “reliance on the demeanour of witnesses alone will often be insufficient to establish an Accused’s guilt beyond reasonable doubt” [emphasis in original]: Jagatheesan s/o Krishnasamy v Public Prosecutor [2006] 4 SLR(R) 45 at [42].

79     The court’s task must therefore be on “an assessment of the internal consistency within the content of a witness’s testimony” and “an assessment of the external consistency between a witness’s evidence and the extrinsic evidence, which includes testing the former against the inherent probabilities and uncontroverted facts” [emphasis in original]: GCK at [137].

80     Furthermore, in the present case, I was mindful that the material witness and victim of the alleged sexual assault is a child. In assessing the credibility of her evidence and her behaviour in relation to the matter, I find the guidance provided in the decision of the High Court in PP v Yue Roger Jr [2019] 3 SLR 749 (“Yue Roger Jr (HC)”) at [30] – [34] (which was affirmed on appeal by the Court of Appeal in Yue Roger Jr v PP [2019] 1 SLR 829) to be most instructive:

30     I accepted that victims of sexual offences may not behave in a stereotypical way. Many victims report their sexual abuse early to a family member, friend, the police, or other persons in authority. However, there is no general rule requiring victims of sexual offences to report the offences immediately or in a timely fashion. Instead, the explanation for any such delay in reporting is to be considered and assessed by the court on a case-by-case basis (see DT v PP [2001] 2 SLR(R) 583 at [62]; Tang Kin Seng v PP [1996] 3 SLR(R) 444 at [79]). While I accept that an omission to report the offence in a timely fashion, in the absence of other evidence, may in certain circumstances make it difficult to establish a case against the accused beyond reasonable doubt, I emphasise that the effect of any delay in reporting always falls to be assessed on the specific facts of each individual case.

31    The Victim’s behaviour in the present case in continuing with her rope skipping training with the Accused until 2010 may at first blush seem odd given that she was the victim of repeated sexual abuse. So was the fact that after one instance of sexual intercourse, she went on to go for training as per normal. However, all this must be seen in the light of the fact that the Victim was at the material time a child of just 13 years, for whom training and competing with the Accused’s private rope skipping team was the centrepiece of her life and, indeed, her aspirations for the future. While the average adult may be expected to react in a particular way – for example, to resist, report or complain about an assault as soon as possible – a child or juvenile cannot be expected to always react similarly. The thinking process, assumptions and viewpoint of a child or juvenile victim may lead to a course of action that may on its face appear unreasonable or improbable to an adult. However, the court must always be mindful of the reasons behind what may seem like unexpected conduct on the part of a child or juvenile victim, and should not measure a child or juvenile by adult standards.

32    What the court should do is to assess, given the evidence in respect of the specific complainant and the allegations made, whether what is put forward is consonant with the likely probabilities. A child or juvenile complainant may not be expected to complain if he or she feels vulnerable, or is otherwise focused on matters other than protecting his or her modesty. A child or juvenile is by definition immature, and should not, in the absence of evidence showing otherwise, be held to the measure of an adult. The thought processes and concerns of a child or juvenile may also continue to evolve and permutate as he or she matures, such that it may be some time before he or she is in a position to complain.

33    Thus, in the present case, the fact that the Victim did not complain in a timely manner and remained in contact with the Accused over the extended duration of the abuse did not rob her of credibility; I accepted that the Victim was focused on her continued participation in the private rope skipping team, and did not know what to do about the Accused’s sexual advances. I accepted her explanation that she did not resist or report the offences to a teacher or family member as rope skipping was her priority at that time and she feared that her place on the private rope skipping team would be jeopardized were she to do so. I also accepted that she had not reported the offences to her family or the police as she felt ashamed.

34    Similarly, the fact that the Victim was not driven to despair or helplessness was not by itself a ground for disbelief of her evidence. People react in different ways to sexual abuse and may compartmentalize or rationalize their reactions. A calm, undisturbed disposition may generally incline the court to conclude that no wrong was committed, but it is not necessary for a complainant to be distraught for her to be believed.

[Emphasis added]

Findings

83     In terms of internal consistency, I found that the victim’s description of events to be sufficiently detailed, logical and coherent. Despite the rigorous cross-examination by the Defence, I note that her testimony remained largely unshaken.

[note: 98]

93     Having observed the victim gave her evidence in court, and having seen her demeanour, I found that the victim was a truthful and forthright witness. [note: 99] She also honestly disclosed that she could not remember if the Accused had told her to lie down on the bed after she took off her pants, or if she did that herself, even though there did not seem to be any ostensible reason for her to do so. But during her testimony, the victim did not embellish her evidence and was adamant and clear that his hand did not go into her pants. [note: 101]

96     Thus, having carefully considered the evidence of the victim, I was satisfied that on the totality of the evidence, the victim’s evidence was internally consistent, logical and coherent, and externally consistent with the other witnesses’ testimonies. She had also given her evidence in an honest and forthright manner, and I found her evidence to be unusually convincing. Accordingly, I accepted the victim’s evidence.

97     As for the Accused, I did not find him to be a credible witness, and I disbelieved his evidence and his denial.

98     In my view, I found the Accused’s account of the alleged incidents to be inconsistent and not credible. The Accused testified that the only time he had applied powder to the victim’s buttocks was after her bath in the master bedroom when his wife told him to do so. 100    Furthermore, the Defence elected not to cross-examine the recorder of the statement, IO Jamil during the Prosecution’s case. I did not find his explanation that he was sleep-deprived prior to statement recording and being stressed out throughout the statement recording process to be credible or convincing. [note: 102] This bare allegation of the Accused was clearly contradicted by the rebuttal evidence of Superintendent Burhanudeen Bin Haji (PW9) who testified that at the time of the statement recording, the Accused was not in any distressed state and he was able to articulate and tell PW9 his account of events in relation to the allegation. [note: 103]

101    Likewise, IO Jamil in the rebuttal evidence also testified that the Accused did not raise any complaints that he was sleep deprived or other complaints before or during the statement recording. He had asked the Accused to give his account and at no time did he try to make the Accused agree to what he wanted the Accused to say. The Accused also did not show any signs of stress before the recording of his statement and spotted errors and made amendments in his statements. [note: 104]

102    I also found it difficult to believe the Accused’s explanation that when the statement was read back to him by IO Jamil to confirm its contents, his intention was just to agree to whatever was stated regardless of its truth or correctness, [note: 105] given that the Accused must have known the seriousness of the allegations and the consequences that flow from his answers. In other words, I did not accept that the Accused was pressured into accepting parts of his statement, and I disbelieved his bare denial.

103    In short, I found the Accused’s testimony to be inconsistent and not believable. He has clearly given different accounts in his testimony and in his police statement P9 of the incidents of him applying powder on the victim, for which he has not provided any sound explanation for their difference. More significantly, the Accused went so far as to disavow his own pleaded Case for the Defence by claiming that he had “never seen [it] before”, [note: 106] even though they clearly contain his instructions prior to trial, the material portion which the Accused instructed that he had applied powder on the buttocks of the victim when she complained to the Accused that her buttocks were itchy. In short, I did not find the Accused to be a credible witness.

104    For completeness, I should add that I did not think that the evidence of DW2 was of any assistance to the defence of the Accused. She only remembered an occasion when the victim and her sister had taken a shower and the Accused was asked to help dry the victim and apply powder on her back and to the part of her buttocks. This was clearly not the incident that the victim was referring to. The witness also could not remember if there were other occasions where the Accused assisted to put powder on the victim.

105    In respect of the incident relating to the second charge, DW2 testified that she did not notice anything strange that happened between the Accused and the victim when they were seated on the couch. It was clear from the cross-examination that she could not see where the Accused’s arm and hands were placed when he was seated beside the victim.

106    Thus, having carefully considered all the evidence adduced at the trial, and having considered the respective submissions of the parties, and bearing in mind the established principles in assessing the credibility of the witnesses, I was satisfied that the Prosecution has proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt. Accordingly, I found the Accused guilty of the offences and I convicted him on both charges.

111    Prosecution relied on the sentencing framework for Outrage of Modesty offences under s 354(2) of the Penal Code was set out in GBR v Public Prosecutor and another appeal [2018] 3 SLR 1048 (“GBR”), and endorsed by the Court of Appeal in BRJ v Public Prosecutor [2020] SGCA 21 (“BRJ”). This “two-step sentencing bands” approach is as follows:

(a)     The court would first identify which band the offence in question falls within and determine precisely where within that range the present offence falls in order to derive an indicative starting point, which reflects the intrinsic seriousness of the offending act.

(b)     The court would then consider the aggravating and mitigating factors which are personal to the offender to calibrate the appropriate sentence for that offender.

112    In GBR at [27]-[30], the High Court set out three main categories of offence specific factors:

(a)     Degree of sexual exploitation: These includes the part of the victim’s body the accused touched, how the accused touched the victim, and the duration of the outrage of modesty. The offence is more aggravated if the victim’s private parts are touched, there is skin-to-skin contact, and the sexual exploitation continued for a sustained period rather than a fleeting moment.

(b)     Circumstances of the offence: These includes factors such as the presence of premeditation, the use of force or violence, the abuse of a position of trust, the use of deception, the exploitation of a vulnerable victim, and other aggravating acts accompanying the outrage of modesty.

(c)     Harm caused to the victim: This includes physical or psychological harm.

113    Having regard to the offence-specific factors, the court would then place the offences within an appropriate band. The sentencing bands for the offences under s 354(2) of the Penal Code are as follows (GBR at [31]):

Band

S354(2) Penal Code

Band 1

Less than one year’s imprisonment

Band 2

One to three years’ imprisonment

Band 3

Three to five years’ imprisonment

Caning should also be imposed if the facts and circumstances of the case warrant this as an additional deterrent. The starting point is that caning will be imposed where a victim’s private parts or sexual organs are intruded upon.



114    Applying the sentencing framework in GBR, the Prosecution submitted that there was an abuse of trust in this case as the Accused was a trusted family friend whom the victim felt close to, and an intrusion to the private parts of the victim for the first charge with skin-to-skin contact for a sustained period of time. There was also an element of exploitation of vulnerable victim vis-à-vis the age-ceiling of 14 years. Furthermore, there was psychological harm caused to the victim as she exhibited symptoms suggestive of post-traumatic stress such as flashbacks of the events and nightmares of being molested by people she trusted.

115    Prosecution submitted that given these offence-specific aggravating factors, the incident relating to the first charge fell in the middle-upper end of Band 2 of the sentencing ranges set out under GBR.

116    Furthermore, the Accused has claimed trial and he should not be entitled to any sentencing discount unlike an offender who elected to plead guilty and spared the victim from testifying and saved the resources of the State.

117    The Prosecution also highlighted two precedent cases that I found useful in calibrating the appropriate sentence in the present case. The first was the case of PP v GDX [2021] SGDC 142 (“GDX”). In GDX, the accused abused his 3 stepdaughters, resulting in two counts under s 354(1) and one count under s 354(2) of the Penal Code being preferred against him. He was convicted after trial. For the s 354(2) charge, he used his hand to touch the victim’s left breast over her clothes by grabbing her breast and using his thumb to rub it in an up and down motion lasting for about a minute. The judge pitched the starting point as 16 months’ imprisonment (which was eventually reduced to 14 months’ imprisonment due to totality because the sentences for all 3 charges ran consecutively) and imposed 18 weeks’ imprisonment in lieu of nine strokes of the cane.

118    The second case was PP v BNO [2018] SGHC 243 (“BNO”). In BNO, the accused was the parent of the nine-year-old victim’s friend. He took advantage of the trust reposed in him by the victim’s parents and the victim when he violated the victim when the victim was sleeping over at his place. For the s 354(2) charge involved, the victim was pretending to sleep when the offender touched the victim’s penis for a few seconds from outside the victim’s shorts before pulling the victim’s shorts down and touching the victim’s exposed penis. He then fellated the victim. He was convicted after trial in the High Court. Justice See Kee Oon found that this was a case that fell in the middle-upper of Band 2 and imposed a sentence of two (2) years’ imprisonment and three (3) strokes of the cane. This was upheld on appeal by the Court of Appeal.

In GCY, the offender was charged with 21 counts of aggravated outrage of modesty under s 354(2) Penal Code and two other charges under the Films Act (Cap. 107, 1998 Rev. Ed.) The three charges faced by the offender involved the following acts:

(a)     Putting his hand into one of the victims’ (“A1”) shorts and rubbing her vulva back and forth (skin-on-skin);

(b)     Using four fingers, which were placed together, to rub A1’s vulva in a forward and backward motion (skin-on-skin):

(c)     Putting his hand into another victim’s (“A2”) shorts and rubbing her vulva back and forth (skin-on-skin).

121    The Defence also submitted that the Accused in the present case exhibited a lower level of premeditation for the first charge and with respect to the second charge, which was an attempt charge, the level of premeditation was even lower. In comparison, the learned Defence Counsel submitted that the accused in GCY displayed a significantly higher level of premeditation by using games on his mobile devices or drawings to distract them whilst he molested them. Further, the fact that he recorded them while doing so was an aggravating factor as it displayed a presence of mind to relive the incidents for his own pleasure and gratification.

122    With respect to the element of abuse of trust, the Defence submitted that there are primarily two types of such abuse – the first being a situation where the accused is in a position of responsibility towards the victim e.g. parent and step-parents and their children, medical practitioners and patients, teachers and their pupils. The second being a situation where the victim places her trust in the accused by virtue of his office of employment e.g. a bus driver and a school child (GBR at [29(c)]). The learned Defence Counsel further submitted that deterrence is an important consideration where there is an abuse of trust in an inter-familial context, due to the challenge of detection of the offences and the consideration barriers faced by the victim in reporting them (GBR at [29(c)]). In this regard, the Defence conceded that the victim was treated as a family member and that the Accused had a responsibility towards her. The Defence also conceded that the Accused did abuse his position of trust placed onto him not only by the victim but also the victim’s parents. However, the learned counsel sought to argue that the level of abuse is not as significant as it is in GCY. In doing so, it was submitted that in GCY¸ it involved three young victims (aged 4 to 9 years old) – one of whom was the child of the accused’s parent’s neighbour (with whom the parents shared such a close neighbourly relationship that the victim was allowed to be with the accused and his children unsupervised), and another was the daughter of the accused’s sister. The Defence contended that the abuse of position from an inter-familial context is more significant than it is in a teacher-student relationship as compared to the present case. It was their submission that there is a greater inherent trust placed on a family member than would a teacher, who ultimately is a stranger to the family.

123    In addition, the Defence submitted that the harm caused in the present case is on the low to medium end of the spectrum. To buttress this point, the Defence argued that the degree of sexual exploitation and low level of abuse of the Accused’s position of trust only caused minor psychological harm and that there is no evidence that the victim’s future has been affected.

124    Further, the learned Defence Counsel sought to rely on PP v BLV [2017] SGHC 154 wherein Aedit Abdullah JC (as he then was), noted that s 354(2) Penal Code represents a general position that offences against minors warrant more severe punishment. However, s 354(2) is not intended to treat all offences committed against minors equally. Specifically, the Defence highlighted that Aedit Abdullah JC held that with respect to enhanced offences, “the aggravating factor of young age would apply if the victim was materially younger than the stipulated age ceiling, and in a graduated manner depending on how much young the victim was”. Furthermore, it would further aggravate the offence if the victim possesses further vulnerabilities, e.g. the victim suffered from any mental or physical infirmities which would warrant a harsher sentence. The Defence submitted that in the present case, the victim was not particularly vulnerable, save for her young age and there were no other aggravating factors.

125    Given the above considerations, the learned Defence Counsel submitted that a sentence of 12 months’ imprisonment for the first charge and three (3) months’ imprisonment for the second charge would thus be appropriate. The learned Defence Counsel further submitted that caning should not be imposed in the present case as there are no substantial aggravating factors.

126    Finally, the learned Defence Counsel urged this court to order the sentences for the two charges to run concurrently, making a global sentence of 12 months’ imprisonment.

Sentencing Considerations

127    In deciding on the appropriate sentence to impose, I have considered the applicable sentencing framework for outrage of modesty cases as set out in the case of GBR, and the two-step sentencing bands approach as set out above.

128    In this regard, both parties agreed that the present case falls within Band 2 of the GBR sentencing framework. However, the parties disagreed on the calibration of the imprisonment term within the sentencing range as set out in the said framework; which is between one to three years’ imprisonment. I should note that it was also established in GBR that caning is generally imposed for offences that fell within Band 2, with a starting point of three strokes of the cane.

129    According to the sentencing guidelines in GBR, Band 2 encompasses cases where two or more of the aggravating factors are present. At the lower end of the band are cases where there may be an absence of skin contact with the private parts of the victim, but with other presenting aggravating features; such as the abuse of a position of trust or showing a pornographic film.

130    At the higher end of the spectrum are cases where there was skin contact of the victim's private parts, or the use of deception. Sentences of between 2 years and 2½ years, with three strokes of the cane have been imposed on such cases.

131    In the present case, I considered the Accused’s culpability to be high as there was skin-to-skin contact with a private part of the victim. Further, as conceded by the Defence, there was an element of an abuse of trust in this instance as the Accused was a close family friend and a father-like figure to the victim, who was also a vulnerable victim given her young age.

132    Having carefully considered the respective submissions of the parties, I could not agree with the Defence’s submission that the present case falls into the low end of Band 2 of the sentencing framework. In particular, I could not agree with the Defence that the only harm caused to the victim is “minor psychological harm” and that there is no evidence that the victim’s future has been affected. Quite clearly, the medical report tendered in the course of the trial revealed that the victim has reported some symptoms (i.e. thoughts of self-harm and nightmares) suggestive of post-traumatic stress disorder.

133    I agreed with the Prosecution’s submission that the present case falls within the higher end of Band 2, and that the sentencing considerations of deterrence and retribution ought to feature more prominently in this case. Applying the graduated approach as highlighted by Aedit Abdullah JC in PP v BLV at [132], it would seem that there is a fairly wide range of sentencing outcomes from precedent cases falling within Band 2 anywhere from 15 months to two years and six months’ imprisonment[note: 107]. The considerations that calibrates the sentencing range accordingly turns on the specific aggravating details of the case e.g. age of the minor, abuse of position of trust, whether there was criminal force or deception used, and whether there was skin-on-skin contact with the private parts.

134    In the present case, I could not ignore the relatively young age of the victim in relation to the stipulated age ceiling of 14 years, and at the material time of the offence, the victim did not even realise that what the Accused did to her was wrong until she learned during a sex education class that what had happened was a ‘bad touch’. It would seem that the accused had capitalised on her innocence due to her young age to commit the said acts on her. There was also such an abuse of the position of trust that the victim felt that she could not talk to anyone about what had happened as she did not think that anyone would believe her. The protracted length of abuse due to the difficulty in detecting such acts that has happened in the inter-familial context should be taken as an aggravating factor. However, there was no criminal force or deception used in the present case. I also considered the other aggravating factors of skin-on-skin contact and intrusion of a private part for a sustained period of time.

135    In my assessment, the Prosecution’s sentencing submission of at least 18 months’ imprisonment for the first charge, having taken reference from the case of GDX, would be an appropriate sentence.

Imprisonment term in lieu of caning

136    I next turn to the question of imposition of caning. The case of PP v Chow Yee Sze [2011] 1 SLR 481 at [9] highlights that the well-established sentencing benchmark is nine months’ imprisonment with caning as a starting point for molest or outrage of modesty under s 354 of the Penal Code where “a victim’s private parts or sexual organs [were] intruded” (Chandresh Patel v Public Prosecutor [1995] 1 CLAS News 323; MA 357/1993 (“Chandresh Patel”), per Yong Pung How CJ). The District Judge specifically referred to this sentencing benchmark at [7] of his GD. Since Chandresh Patel, the nine months’ imprisonment plus caning benchmark has consistently been considered by the courts to be the correct benchmark for s 354 offences over the past 15 years. It was also established in GBR that for offences that fell under Band 2 of the sentencing framework, caning will nearly always be imposed, and the suggested starting point would be at least three strokes of the cane.

137    Considering that both general deterrence and retribution take centre-stage in the sentencing considerations, there is clearly a need to compensate for the deterrent effect and retributive effect of caning that was lost by reason of the exception as the Accused is above the age of 50 and is exempted from caning under s 325(1)(b) CPC.

138    It was established in the seminal case of Amin bin Abdullah v PP [2017] 5 SLR (“Amin”) that the correct starting point was that an offender’s term of imprisonment should not be enhanced, unless there were grounds to justify doing so. The court should not proceed on the presumptive basis that once an offender was exempted from caning, his sentence of imprisonment should be enhanced. The relevant provisions were worded as open-ended discretion-conferring provisions and the sections were not framed in terms that suggested that the imprisonment sentence should be enhanced in circumstances where there is exemption from caning. This also cohered better with the fact that the courts should not, in general, exercise punitive powers absent sufficient justification.[note: 108]

139    The relevant factors that may warrant an enhancement of the sentence of an exempted offender include: (a) the need to compensate for the deterrent effect of caning that was lost by reason of the exemption; (b) the need to compensate for the retributive effect of caning that was lost by reason of the exemption; and (c) the need to maintain parity among co-offenders.

140    The court would generally find it helpful to first identify the principal sentencing objective(s) that underlay the imposition of caning for the offence in question. The court should consider whether the objective(s) in question would be furthered by the imposition of an additional term of imprisonment in lieu of caning [note: 109].

141    In relation to factor (a), I considered that the sentencing objectives for caning to be imposed for the offence in question must be deterrence and retribution. In my view, the additional term of imprisonment was required to replace the lost deterrent and retributive effect of caning. I was mindful of the Accused’s age at the time of sentencing and there was nothing to indicate to the court that the Accused would suffer disproportionately from the imprisonment term imposed.

142    In considering the duration of the enhancement, Amin at [89] sets out the indicative ranges of sentences which would better allow sentencing judges to calibrate the extent of the enhancement to fit the circumstances of each case. The indicative guidelines are as follows:

(a)     One to six strokes avoided: up to three months’ imprisonment;

(b)     Seven to 12 strokes avoided: three to six months’ imprisonment;

(c)     13 to 18 strokes avoided: six to nine months’ imprisonment; and

(d)     More than 19 strokes avoided: nine to 12 months’ imprisonment.

143    Applying the indicative guidelines established in Amin in relation to the duration of enhancement of the imprisonment term in lieu of caning, I was of the view that it would be appropriate to impose an imprisonment term of six weeks’ in lieu of three strokes of the cane that would otherwise be imposed on the Accused. This was also in line with what the court had imposed in the case of GDX. In this respect, the duration of enhancement in addition to the 18 months’ imprisonment imposed for this charge would not exceed the sentencing range for an offence under Band 2 of the GBR sentencing framework. As such, an imposition of an enhanced sentence in lieu of caning would not be considered to be manifestly excessive.

144    Accordingly, for the first charge, I imposed a sentence of 18 months’ imprisonment and 6 weeks’ imprisonment in lieu of caning.

145    As for the second charge, the Prosecution submitted for a short custodial sentence, whereas the Defence submitted for a three months’ imprisonment term. There seem to be no dispute between parties that the offence in question falls within Band 1 of the sentencing framework. According to the framework, offences under Band 1 should generally attract a sentence of less than one year’s imprisonment and generally no caning will be imposed.

146    Considering that this was a charge of attempt to commit the offence of outrage of modesty, where the Accused attempted to insert his hand into the back of the victim’s pants, and there was no intrusion of any of the victim’s private parts, and the offence was more opportunistic rather than premediated, and in the absence of any other aggravating factors, I was of the view that a sentence of 4 week’s imprisonment would be appropriate.

Sentences to run consecutively

147    I next turn to the question of whether the imprisonment terms should be ordered to run consecutively or concurrently.

148    In the case of PP v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 799 (“Raveen”), it was held that as a general rule, a multiple offender who has committed unrelated offences should be separately punished for each offence, and this should be achieved by an order that the individual sentences run consecutively (Raveen at [41]). As the Prosecution had highlighted in that case, if the sentences were not to be ordered to run consecutively, the effect would be to grant such an offender an unwarranted discount since by having the sentences run concurrently, the offender would escape at least in part, if not entirely, punishment for the corresponding offences.  In that judgment, the Honourable Chief Justice Sundaresh Menon stated that the general rule of consecutive sentences for unrelated offences is well justified as a matter of principle and policy.

149    In the present case, the convicted charges were of the same nature committed to the same victim, albeit one was an attempt at the commission of the offence. They were clearly unrelated and had happened on two separate occasions under materially different circumstances. The one-transaction rule did not apply here as there was no unity of time or place and the facts that form the subject matter of each charge were materially different. As the offences are distinct and the charges relate to separate incidents that happened on separate occasions, I agreed with the Prosecution that the sentences ought to be ordered to run consecutively. To not order a consecutive sentence would be to give unwarranted discount to the Accused for his multiple offending behaviour.

150    I was also mindful of the totality principle – as was noted in Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v PP [2014] 2 SLR 998 (at [2]) (“Shouffee”), that in circumstances where the court is inclined to order sentences to run consecutively, it is necessary for the court to run a final check to ensure that the aggregate sentence is proportionate to the overall criminality presented and not excessive.

151    In this respect, I did not think that the aggregate sentence would violate the totality principle, in that it would not be disproportionate to the overall criminality and not crushing. Even with a consecutive sentence, the aggregate sentence of 18 months’ and 10 weeks’ imprisonment fell squarely within the Band 2 range of sentence of one to three years’ imprisonment.

152    In my view, the global sentence would commensurate with the gravity of the offences and reflect the culpability of the Accused, and also serve as a sufficiently strong deterrent message to would-be offenders in a similar position as the Accused.
