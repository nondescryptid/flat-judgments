Decision Date:14 April 2023

District Judge Tan May Tee:

Introduction

1       This is a medical negligence claim arising from orthognathic or jaw surgeries performed by two oral maxillofacial surgeons, the first and second defendants, on the plaintiff, a 39-year-old woman.

2       In July 2015 when the plaintiff was 32 years old, she consulted an orthodontist, one Dr Alfred Cheng (“Dr Cheng”), who informed her that she had a dental malocclusion with bimaxillary jaw protrusion[note: 1]. He referred her to the first defendant who was then practising at the fourth defendant’s clinic at Mount Elizabeth Novena Specialist Centre.

3       The first defendant assessed her to have significant functional dentofacial deformities with a protrusive mouth and lips and recommended a bimaxillary jaw surgery to treat her condition, ie a surgery on both upper and lower jaws. This surgery involved cutting the jaw bones and repositioning them so as to address the protrusion and correct the misalignment of the teeth and jaw. The bones would then be secured in place using bone plates, screws and wires.

4       The plaintiff agreed and consented to the procedure which was carried out in December 2015 at the Mount Elizabeth Novena Hospital (“MNH”) by both the first and second defendants. The plaintiff’s dentofacial deformities were significantly corrected through the surgery but there was a resultant minor facial asymmetry which caused her to seek a second orthognathic surgery about six months after the first surgery, and subsequently a third surgery eleven months later.

5       The plaintiff instituted this action claiming that the first and second defendants were negligent in the medical care, treatment and advice given to her. She alleges that their mistakes had caused her to have to undergo jaw surgery so many times. She alleges that she suffered, and continues to suffer, pain and discomfort from the surgeries. She claims damages from the defendants including her loss of income as well as the cost of any future treatments and surgeries she may require.

6       The defendants deny the plaintiff’s claim in its entirety. They say that the first surgery had successfully corrected the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformities and the resultant minor residual asymmetry was well within expectations of surgical outcomes. The defendants had repeatedly emphasised to the plaintiff that the aesthetic outcome of orthognathic surgeries can never be predicted with complete certainty and it was unrealistic to expect perfect symmetry when dealing with a complex structure such as the human face. However, after her jaw deformities were corrected and her looks improved, the plaintiff had insisted on proceeding with the second and third surgeries, seemingly due to her quest for aesthetic perfection.

7       It is the defendant’s case that the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformity has been corrected and her overall aesthetic appearance has improved significantly as a result of the surgeries. They say that the plaintiff had been appropriately advised on all relevant and material risks, complications, and alternative treatments in respect of each of the three surgeries carried out. Their diagnosis, care, treatment and management of the plaintiff was reasonable, appropriate, and consistent with acceptable standards of care.

8       Accordingly, the defendants ask that the plaintiff’s claim against them be dismissed with costs as it is totally without merit.

Chronological background

Pre-surgery consultation

9       The plaintiff first consulted Dr Cheng on 6 July 2015. She was recommended orthodontic treatment (ie braces) together with surgical jaw correction. He referred her to the first defendant for the surgical procedure[note: 2]. According to the plaintiff, she had agreed to see the first defendant because of his substantial experience in jaw surgery and had good reviews from his patients[note: 3].

10     On 20 July 2015, the plaintiff met the first defendant who was then practising at the fourth defendant’s clinic at Mount Elizabeth Novena Specialist Centre. At that consultation, she complained of difficulty biting with her front teeth, speech dysfunction, lips incompetence and a gummy or crooked smile. The first defendant assessed her to have significant functional dentofacial deformity with protrusive mouth and lips.

11     Based on his clinical findings, the first defendant discussed with the plaintiff the option of orthognathic surgery to address her dentofacial deformity. He explained what the procedure would entail and that the entire treatment plan would take approximately two years to execute so this would be a significant undertaking. According to the first defendant, he had, as per his routine practice, explained to the plaintiff the risks and complications associated with such a surgery including pain, discomfort, swelling, nerve injury and numbness. The plaintiff indicated that she would start her pre-surgical orthodontic treatment before commencing any surgical planning and was given an open date to return for a follow-up appointment[note: 4].

12     On 20 November 2015[note: 5], the plaintiff returned to the fourth defendant’s clinic. She was attended to by both the first defendant and the second defendant. A detailed review of her face, jaw and teeth was performed. This entailed taking facial measurements, facebow recordings, upper and lower jaw impressions, wax bites and photographs to document her condition. Her dental x-rays taken at Dr Cheng’s clinic on 19 November 2015 were also reviewed.

13     Upon consideration of the clinical investigations and findings, the first and second defendants assessed that the plaintiff had a Class I malocclusion with skeletal Class I relationship, severe upper and lower anterior alveolar protrusion resulting in severely proclined anterior teeth and lips, as well as the inability to bite with her front teeth. She also had vertical maxillary excess with gummy smile, jaw canting causing a crooked smile, a retrusive chin and four non-functional wisdom teeth. They informed the plaintiff of their findings and their diagnosis.

14     To treat the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformity, they offered her the option of a bimaxillary jaw surgery with a LeFort I osteotomy in the upper jaw for impaction, set back and levelling, a bilateral sagittal split osteotomy in the lower jaw for setback and levelling and a chin osteotomy for advancement, with the removal of the plaintiff’s wisdom teeth as a necessary adjunctive procedure to prevent them from becoming inaccessible and trapped by soft tissue as a result of the surgery to setback the jaws (“the 1st Surgery”).

15     According to the defendants, they had explained to the plaintiff that the 1st Surgery was a treatment option to correct her dentofacial condition. They informed her of the risks associated with the surgery which included the risks of wisdom teeth removal such as pain, discomfort, swelling, inferior alveolar nerve injury and permanent numbness to the lips and chin. They also explained to the plaintiff that the 1st Surgery might not produce ‘perfect’ results – there might still be residual discrepancies after the surgery as it was not possible to predict the aesthetic outcome of orthognathic surgeries with complete certainty.

16     The plaintiff was also advised of the alternative treatment option of orthodontic treatment, ie braces with premolar extractions; however, this would only result in partial compensation and would not fully correct her dentofacial deformity. Only orthognathic surgery would provide her the chance of a full correction of the skeletal disharmony she presented with.

17     The plaintiff was keen to undergo the 1st Surgery as she wanted a full correction of her dentofacial condition. She was aware of the alternative option as she had previously discussed it with Dr Cheng.

18     On 4 December 2015[note: 6], the plaintiff attended a final pre-surgical discussion and treatment planning session with both the first and second defendants. They reiterated what had been discussed during the previous consultations with regard to the indications, alternatives, risks and benefits of the 1st Surgery. The plaintiff remained keen to proceed. She signed a consent form entitled “Consent for Operation or Procedure”[note: 7], indicating her consent to undergo the stated procedure under general anaesthesia. The 1st Surgery was scheduled for 10 December 2015.

The 1st Surgery

19     The first and second defendants carried out the 1st Surgery on the plaintiff on 10 December 2015 in the Mount Elizabeth Novena Hospital (“MNH”). In the course of the procedure which lasted from 4.55pm to 7.50pm, a minor fracture occurred at the right ramus or lower jaw of the plaintiff. Medically, this was described as an “unfavourable fracture” in an otherwise uneventful surgery. The fracture was rectified immediately with a fixation plate and did not compromise the functional or aesthetic results of the 1st Surgery. According to the defendants, such minor fractures are not uncommon nor unexpected during a surgery like the 1st Surgery as maxillary, mandibular and chin osteotomies involve cutting, reshaping and repositioning of the jaw bones. The surgical manipulation that has to be performed can cause minor fragmentations of bone within the operative area[note: 8].

20     Following the 1st Surgery, the plaintiff was seen by the first defendant on 12 December 2015 who noted that she was healing normally, there were no signs and symptoms of infection, and the occlusion was satisfactory. X-rays of the plaintiff’s post-surgical condition were also taken. The plaintiff was discharged well from MNH on 13 December 2015[note: 9].

21     The plaintiff had follow-up consultations with the first defendant on 14 December 2015, and with the second defendant on 19 December 2015, 24 December 2015, and 31 December 2015. She was assessed to be healing well during all these consultations[note: 10].

22     At a further consultation with the second defendant on 7 January 2016, the plaintiff informed him that she did not have any pain. As she was healing well with no signs of infection and had satisfactory occlusion, the plaintiff was referred back to Dr Cheng for post-surgical orthodontic management[note: 11].

23     Post-operatively, both the first and second defendants assessed that the plaintiff’s lip protrusion in both upper and lower lips, the gummy smile, maxillary excess, and the inability to bite with her front teeth had been adequately corrected. There was, however, a mild residual canting (or tilting) of the maxilla due to her lips being asymmetrical, but the result was considered to be medically acceptable. The 1st Surgery was successful in achieving its aim of correcting the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformity[note: 12].

24     On 18 January 2016, the plaintiff saw the first defendant for a further review. She complained of pain and numbness in the anterior mandible as well as a “hollowing” of her left cheek. On examination, he noted the operation site to be healing well, there were no signs or symptoms of infection, and occlusion was satisfactory. He prescribed the plaintiff a painkiller and arranged a follow-up consultation with him and the second defendant three days later on 22 January 2016[note: 13].

25     On 22 January 2016, the plaintiff was seen by the first and second defendants. She complained of pain at her right mandible and “hollowing” at her left mandible / temporomandibular joint area. On examination, she was noted to be healing well. The facial hollowing was assessed to be likely due to a lack of facial fat which was pre-existing before the 1st Surgery and could be seen in her preoperative photographs. Since it was less than two months after the 1st Surgery, it was not unexpected for the plaintiff to still have some pain or muscle soreness as the healing process takes time and differs from patient to patient. The plaintiff was advised to allow herself some time to heal, and to wait for her facial features to settle down before further assessment. The plaintiff agreed[note: 14].

Events post 1st Surgery and leading to 2nd Surgery

26     On 4 March 2016, the plaintiff had a further follow-up review. The first and second defendants examined her and noted that the operation site was healing well, there were no signs or symptoms of infection, and occlusion was satisfactory. At this consultation, she was initially seen by both the first and second defendants, but after she expressed her preference to be treated only by the first defendant, the second defendant no longer participated in her treatment until sometime in April 2017 when he was asked to assist in a subsequent surgery[note: 15].

27     During the 4 March 2016 consultation, despite having previously agreed to wait for her facial features to settle down after the 1st Surgery, the plaintiff voiced her dissatisfaction with the outcome of the surgery and complained that she had a crooked smile. The first defendant ordered further investigations for her including x-rays, photographs of her teeth and face as well as a computed tomography (“CT”) scan of her face. All these investigations showed good skeletal and dental occlusion[note: 16].

28     On 12 March 2016, the investigation results were shared with the plaintiff. The first defendant reiterated to the plaintiff that the 1st Surgery had been successful in functionally correcting her dentofacial deformity. As for the aesthetic outcome, while it was noted that the plaintiff had a canted eye level and zygomatic prominence on the left side, these were not features that were correctible by orthognathic surgery. The occlusal cant of 1.5mm where the left side of the jaw was slightly lower than the right was already present before the 1st Surgery, with a rotation of the lower jaw to the right side and chin point lowering on the right side. The first defendant explained that the minor residual asymmetry was well within expectations of surgical outcomes, and that objectively, the aesthetic outcome was very acceptable[note: 17].

29     The plaintiff, however, insisted that the aesthetic outcome was not good enough for her, and she wanted to correct any residual facial asymmetry, even though considered minor. The first defendant then explained that if the plaintiff was not keen to continue with only orthodontic treatment and accept the minor residual asymmetry, then further bimaxillary jaw surgery with a rotation of the upper and lower jaws was a possible treatment option. This would involve a Lefort 1 osteotomy, bilateral sagittal split osteotomy and genioplasty (“2nd Surgery”)[note: 18].

30     While the plaintiff was keen to proceed with the 2nd Surgery, the first defendant felt that the plaintiff’s reaction to the minor facial asymmetry was disproportionate to the actual degree of asymmetry present and referred her to see a psychiatrist, Dr Calvin Fones. He had also observed that the plaintiff appeared to have lost a significant amount of weight[note: 19].

31     The plaintiff was next seen by the first defendant on 19 March 2016. She informed him that she had seen Dr Fones that day and had a further appointment with him on 2 April 2016. She was keen to proceed with the 2nd Surgery[note: 20].

32     She had a further consultation with the first defendant on 26 March 2016 to discuss the 2nd Surgery. At this consultation, the first defendant recommended that she see a second psychiatrist, Dr Lee Ee Lian, for consideration of psychological treatment for her low body weight. On 28 March 2016, the plaintiff called to inform the clinic that she was currently seeing her own psychologist. The first defendant informed the clinic staff to let the plaintiff know that he would require a report from her psychologist. On 2 April 2016, the clinic staff conveyed the first defendant’s requirement to the plaintiff on the phone. The fourth defendant’s records documented that the plaintiff told the clinic staff that she did not need to see a psychiatrist[note: 21].

33     The plaintiff attended further pre-surgical consultations with the first defendant on 14 April 2016, 7 May 2016 and 23 May 2016. Over the course of these consultations, the first defendant explained to the plaintiff the planned surgical movements to be made during the 2nd Surgery and that the objective of the surgery was to show less gum and teeth on the left side and to rotate the right side out slightly as well as to increase jaw height on the right side. The possible risks and complications associated with the 2nd Surgery were discussed, which included the risk of pain, nerve injury, numbness, residual facial asymmetry after surgery due to the pre-existing soft tissue asymmetry (corner of lip, canted eye level and zygomatic prominence) which could not be fully corrected by skeletal surgery. The first defendant said that he had exercised special care to manage her expectations regarding the surgical outcome due to the fact that the plaintiff had previously indicated that she wanted perfect facial symmetry which he pointed out to her was not a realistic goal[note: 22] and encouraged the plaintiff to take her time to decide if she wanted to proceed with the 2nd Surgery.

34     The 2nd Surgery was scheduled for 1 June 2016 and the plaintiff signed the requisite Consent Form[note: 23].

35     Prior to the surgery, the plaintiff signed a “Separation Agreement” dated 26 May 2016 with her then employer, World First Asia Pte Ltd, by which she agreed to a voluntary termination of her employment with waiver of the notice period of one month and payment of a lump sum ex gratia amount. According to the plaintiff, this was because her employer was not keen that she take a further two months of medical leave for the 2nd Surgery[note: 24].

The 2nd Surgery and post-operative consultations

36     On 1 June 2016, the first defendant performed the 2nd Surgery on the plaintiff at MNH. He was assisted by another colleague, one Dr Clement Lye Poh Wah (“Dr Clement Lye”)[note: 25].

37     The 2nd Surgery was uneventful. The first defendant assessed the plaintiff on 3 June 2016 and noted that the operation site was granulating well, there were no signs or symptoms of infection, and occlusion was satisfactory. The post-operative x-rays taken on 3 June 2016 showed satisfactory results and the plaintiff was discharged well from MNH later that day[note: 26].

38     The plaintiff attended follow-up reviews with the first defendant on 6 June 2016, 7 June 2016, 10 June 2016 and 16 June 2016. During these reviews, the plaintiff was found to be healing well. During the 10 June 2016 consultation, a minor residual upper midline deviation to the left side was noted. The first defendant recommended that a skeletal anchorage plate be placed to allow elastic traction of the jaw and the plaintiff was agreeable. On 30 June 2016, the first defendant performed the placement of the skeletal anchorage plate and the procedure proceeded uneventfully[note: 27].

39     Then, from 1 July 2016 to 11 July 2016, elastic traction was carried out. The plaintiff had post-operative reviews on 1 July 2016, 2 July 2016, 4 July 2016, 7 July 2016, 9 July 2016 and 11 July 2016 and to change the elastics for the elastic traction. She was assessed to be healing well, with satisfactory occlusion and no signs or symptoms of infection. The traction treatment resulted in significant improvement of the midline deviation, with only a slight / minor degree of midline deviation still present after traction. The first defendant explained this to the plaintiff, and also informed her that the 2nd Surgery had substantially corrected the canting of the upper jaw[note: 28].

40     At the follow-up consultation on 14 July 2016, the plaintiff complained of a residual midline deviation to the left by 1.5mm, an asymmetrical bite and protrusion of the right angle of the jaw. Some mild prominence of the angle of the mandible on the right side was noted, which had not been observed during or immediately after the 2nd Surgery. The protrusion was minor and did not affect the plaintiff functionally and was within surgical expectations. The first defendant explained to the plaintiff that the 2nd Surgery had corrected the canting of the upper jaw and a repeat surgery was not recommended. He advised the plaintiff to see Dr Cheng to consider further traction of the upper midline using the skeletal anchorage plate. He informed her that if she was really keen on improving the aesthetic outcome, she could consider undergoing a minor facial recontouring surgery to pare down the right protruding angle of the mandible. However, this surgery should only take place after complete healing from the 2nd Surgery which would take about six months[note: 29].

41     On 29 July 2016, the first defendant removed the plaintiff’s skeletal anchorage plates. The plaintiff was again noted to be healing well, with no numbness, bleeding or swelling[note: 30].

42     On 5 August 2016, when the first defendant next saw the plaintiff, she complained that her maxilla was canted. The first defendant advised her that any residual canting should be corrected with orthodontics. On examination, she had no pain at the site where the skeletal anchorage plates were removed and she was told that her smile should become more balanced with time. To further assist her and her recovery, regular 6-weekly appointments to manage her gingival health were offered[note: 31].

43     At a further review on 19 September 2016, the plaintiff updated the first defendant that Dr Cheng had decided on non-extraction therapy, as this would eliminate the potential for dark buccal corridors ie dark spaces between teeth and corner of the mouth when smiling. She also informed the first defendant that Dr Cheng was adjusting her midlines through orthodontic treatment and might do more cant correction. On examination, there were no signs or symptoms of infection or numbness noted. As the plaintiff was still on braces, the first defendant advised her that any facial recontouring surgery should only be considered after the anterior teeth had moved to the correct midline position and occlusion was stable[note: 32].

44     On 23 September 2016, the plaintiff was seen again by the first defendant. They discussed the original need for the surgery as well as her job history. According to the first defendant, the plaintiff was unhappy with the slight midline deviation to the left, canting of the jaw, and prominence of the right angle of the mandible and felt very strongly that she should have further surgery. The first defendant explained that a further orthognathic surgery was not recommended. The purpose of the original surgery was to achieve functional correction of the dentoskeletal deformity, and this had already been achieved. It was reiterated to her that the minor aesthetic discrepancies were within acceptable limits, and it was not realistic to expect total symmetry or a ‘perfect’ outcome as aesthetics and appearances are subjective. After a lengthy discussion, the plaintiff agreed to complete her orthodontic treatment first and to consider undergoing mandibular recontouring later[note: 33].

45     On 1 November 2016, at a follow-up consultation, the plaintiff complained of pain in her right jaw. It was explained to her that the pain was most likely due to traction from her braces, and the first defendant reassured her that the pain would likely subside over time. He prescribed her Lyrica and Arcoxia and advised her to monitor the pain. The first defendant also told her that if the pain persisted after the orthodontic treatment, a referral to a pain specialist for consultation could be done if necessary[note: 34].

46     On 23 March 2017, the plaintiff went back to the fourth defendant’s clinic as she wanted to discuss the possibility of a further orthognathic surgery. Photographs of the plaintiff’s teeth and face were taken and the plaintiff also had a full-face CT scan done as well as further x-rays. To assist the plaintiff’s understanding, the first defendant drew a diagram of a skull and jaw to illustrate to the plaintiff what undergoing further surgery would entail, ie how the bones would be rotated or pushed back[note: 35].

47     On 26 April 2017, the first and second defendants saw the plaintiff together with Dr Clement Lye. According to the plaintiff, although she had explicitly mentioned that she only wanted the first defendant to handle her case after the 1st Surgery, he said he had to loop in the second defendant again as he was not able to do it himself. At this lengthy consultation with the three doctors, the plaintiff complained of an unbalanced bite with upper and lower teeth midline deviation to the left side, protrusion of her teeth and lips causing strain and tightness, lateral protrusion of the right angle of the mandible and increased prominence of the right chin. They assessed that she had a slight residual facial asymmetry and malocclusion, and offered the following treatment options[note: 36]:

(a)     Orthodontic correction of the malocclusion with braces and optional removal of two upper premolars to correct the midline deviation;

(b)     Minor surgical recontouring of the right protruding angle of the mandible; or

(c)     Further bimaxillary surgery with the removal of upper and lower left and right premolars. This would include a LeFort osteotomy in the maxilla, both upper and lower anterior segmental surgery with extractions of the four first premolars and a bilateral sagittal split osteotomy for the mandible and chin recontouring (the “3rd Surgery”).

48     The first and second defendants emphasised to the plaintiff the need to have realistic expectations of the surgical outcomes since it is not possible to achieve a perfect correction, even in the best of hands, and especially when aesthetic results are subjective. They also advised her of the standard orthognathic surgical risks including pain, discomfort and swelling, inferior alveolar nerve injury and permanent numbness to the lips and chin and reiterated the need to have realistic aesthetic expectations. The plaintiff indicated that she understood but remained keen to proceed with the 3rd Surgery[note: 37].

49     On 4 May 2017, the first and second defendants had a further review with the plaintiff during which the surgical plan and the potential risks and complications of the 3rd Surgery were again discussed. The plaintiff indicated her understanding and signed the consent form to undergo the stated procedure under general anaesthesia[note: 38].

The 3rd Surgery and post-operative consultations

50     The first and second defendants performed the 3rd Surgery on 8 May 2017 at MNH. The records indicate that both the surgery and the plaintiff’s post-operative recovery were uneventful.

51     On 11 May 2017 (post-operative day 3), the second defendant assessed the plaintiff and noted that the operation site was granulating well with no signs or symptoms of infection and occlusion was satisfactory. Post-operative x-rays were also taken which showed satisfactory findings. The plaintiff was discharged well from MNH later that day. She was reviewed again on 15 May 2017 and 25 May 2017 by the second defendant, and by the first defendant on 19 May 2017[note: 39].

52     On 1 June 2017, she was seen by both the first and second defendants. Subsequently, on 8 June 2017 and 12 June 2017, the plaintiff attended further follow-up reviews with the second defendant. At the 12 June 2017 consultation, the temporary arch bars from the 3rd Surgery were removed uneventfully. On 23 June 2017, she was reviewed again by both the first and second defendants[note: 40]. During all these reviews, the plaintiff was noted to be healing well with no signs of infection, and the occlusion was satisfactory. Both the first and second defendants assessed that the residual asymmetry had been corrected with a good functional and aesthetic outcome.

53     On 6 July 2017, at a further consultation with the second defendant, the plaintiff complained of some pain at the right infraorbital area but had no numbness. The second defendant noted again that the operation site was healing well and the occlusion was satisfactory. The second defendant prescribed Lyrica and Arcoxia to the plaintiff for the pain. While it was noted that the plaintiff had previously indicated an allergy to Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (“NSAIDs”) which includes Arcoxia, the plaintiff did not report any allergic reaction to Arcoxia or Lyrica after they had been prescribed to her on 1 November 2016 by the first defendant. As she reported that she had found them to be effective in treating her pain and did not report any issues with taking Lyrica and Arcoxia previously, the second defendant proceeded to prescribe her Lyrica and Arcoxia for her pain management[note: 41].

54     On 8 August 2017, when the plaintiff was next reviewed by the second defendant, she complained of occasional pain at the area of her upper lip and/or base of her nose and numbness in the lower lip, with sensation of two to three upon ten (2-3/10) on the Visual Analogue Scale (“VAS”). The second defendant prescribed low-level laser therapy (“LLLT”) for the plaintiff’s post-surgical neurosensory disturbance, and she underwent her first cycle of LLLT that day. The operation site was also assessed to have healed[note: 42].

55     On 8 September 2017, 10 October 2017, 21 November 2017 and 23 February 2018, the plaintiff underwent four further cycles of LLLT during her consultations. She did not have any further complaints of pain. The numbness that she had reported on 8 August 2017 had also improved with treatment. At the 21 November 2017 consultation, it was also documented that her slight numbness had improved[note: 43].

The plaintiff’s conduct after the 3rd Surgery and commencement of action

56     On 23 February 2018, the plaintiff was last seen by the second defendant. Her occlusion was checked and found to be satisfactory, the operation site was granulating well and had no signs or symptoms of infection. At this consultation, she scored her level of sensation on the VAS as 7/10. She was scheduled to have another cycle of LLLT in three months’ time, but she defaulted[note: 44].

57     After the removal of her braces by Dr Cheng on 1 March 2018, the plaintiff called the fourth defendant’s clinic to complain that she was unhappy with her appearance. According to the plaintiff, she noticed that her lips had thinned out or otherwise caved in. She also experienced persistent pain. She, however, refused to return to the clinic for further reviews with the first and/or second defendants. She requested a medical report on her treatment and the clinic provided her with two medical reports dated 15 March 2018 and 4 August 2018[note: 45]. The first and second defendants obtained a copy of the x-rays ordered by Dr Cheng on 1 March 2018 and photographs of the plaintiff taken on 3 April 2018, which showed that the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformity had been adequately corrected with a good outcome[note: 46].

58     Instead of responding to the fourth defendant’s requests for her to meet with the first and second defendants to discuss her concerns, the plaintiff exhibited aggressive and abusive behaviour. She sent vulgar and expletive-laden text messages to the clinic staff, demanding to meet with the first defendant only. When she was informed that since both the first and second defendants were involved in her treatment, both of them would meet her if she wished to discuss her condition and treatment, she refused. When she subsequently threatened to go to the clinic premises and “make a scene” and “scream and shout”, the defendants informed her that the matter would be referred to their lawyers[note: 47].

59     The plaintiff commenced this action in November 2018 through solicitors assigned to her by the Legal Aid Bureau. It appears that they had filed the writ of summons in November 2018 to foreclose any time bar issues from arising.

60     In order to support her claim, the plaintiff obtained a specialist report of her condition from one Associate Professor Asher Lim (“A/Prof Lim”), a senior consultant with the National University Centre for Oral Health, National University Hospital, Singapore (“NUHS”) dated 19 April 2019[note: 48]. However, the plaintiff was informed by the Legal Aid Bureau that neither the photographic evidence she had supplied nor the specialist report that she had procured from NUHS was useful in supporting her claim. She was therefore denied legal aid and advised to engage a private solicitor[note: 49]. The plaintiff chose instead to prosecute her action as a self-represented litigant.

The plaintiff’s pleaded case

61     In her statement of claim filed on 20 November 2019, the plaintiff pleaded that the first and second defendants owed her a duty to provide appropriate, reasonable and sound medical care, advice and treatment to her.

62     In 2015, when she first sought medical care, treatment and advice from the first defendant, she had presented with bimaxillary protrusion, excessive gingival display and an occlusal cant. The first defendant had advised her to undergo surgery which she agreed. The surgery was carried out by the first and second defendants on 11 December 2015.

63     Following the surgery, the plaintiff found out that her chin deviation and her occlusal cant were not corrected and/or adequately corrected. The first defendant then advised her to undergo a further surgery.

64     The plaintiff underwent the recommended second surgery on 3 June 2016 which was carried out by the first defendant. Following the second surgery, the plaintiff continued to suffer from poor occlusion and the first defendant further advised her to undergo a third revision surgery.

65     The plaintiff underwent the recommended third surgery which was carried out by the first and second defendants on 8 May 2017. During this third surgery, the plaintiff underwent upper and lower segmental osteotomies.

66     Notwithstanding the surgeries, the plaintiff did not receive the full benefit of the surgical treatment. In particular, her symptoms worsened.

67     She is unable to open her mouth completely and suffers from myalgia of her right masseter, lower lip and chin paraesthesia/hyperesthesia, hyperesthesia of her upper frenum region and periodontitis at her lower second molars. She continues to suffer from chronic pain and from severe periodontitis.

68     The plaintiff asserts that the first and second defendants were negligent in the medical care, treatment and advice rendered her, in that they:

(a)     failed to advise her on the risks and complications associated with each of the three surgeries they carried out on her;

(b)     failed to obtain her informed consent in respect of each of the three surgeries;

(c)     failed to offer her other alternative treatments to the surgeries they carried out;

(d)     failed to properly carry out each of the three surgeries;

(e)     failed to ensure adequate steps were carried out to rectify jaw deformity during the surgery; and

(f)     carried out the first surgery in 2015 which led to the plaintiff undergoing two additional revision surgeries in 2016 and 2017.

69     In more particularity, the plaintiff had also alleged that:

(a)     Prior to the 1st Surgery, the defendants did not inform her:

(i)       That there would be a need to do a jaw operation more than once including having to incorporate “segmentals” where four premolars had to be removed;

(ii)       That there would be residual deformities not corrected like canted upper jaw, a misaligned bite where the teeth only meshes on the left and not on the right, a slanted chin post-operation and misalignment of the facial midlines;

(iii)       of potential risks like Temporomandibular Joint Disorder (TMD) and chronic pain when she is allergic to painkillers; and

(iv)       complications associated with wisdom teeth removal.

(b)     Prior to the 2nd Surgery, the first defendant did not inform her:

(i)       That there would be an unexpected procedure where the right mandible had to be hinged causing an unbalanced facial contour which could only be shaved down at least six months later when the bones had healed.

(ii)       That there would be such a complication in the operation and she would have to endure chronic pain on the right masseter.

(iii)       That due to the post-operative placement of the upper jaw being parallel to the eyes, the bite would be asymmetrical and could only be corrected with “segmentals” due to the injury on the right mandible.

(c)     Prior to the 3rd Surgery, the defendants did not inform her that by undergoing “segmentals”, the lips would cave in and there would be major changes to the pout.

70     In relation to diagnosis and treatment, the plaintiff has alleged that:

(a)     The defendants should have carried out a CT scan prior to the 1st Surgery and 2nd Surgery;

(b)     After the 1st Surgery, her “chin deviation and her occlusal cant were not corrected and/or adequately corrected”;

(c)     She suffered from trismus ie the inability to open the mouth fully due to the “unfavourable fracture” which occurred during the 1st Surgery; and

(d)     They should not have prescribed her Arcoxia when she reportedly has an allergy to NSAIDs.

As a result of the first and second defendants’ breach and/or breaches of their duty of care to her, the plaintiff says that she suffered, and continues to suffer injuries, pain and sufferings. She relies on the specialist report of A/Prof Asher Lim of NUHS for the various heads of alleged injuries.

71     The plaintiff further claims that on account of the jaw and mouth injuries which she suffered during the three surgeries performed, she continues to suffer discomfort as well as the risk of developing further pain and discomfort. Further, the plaintiff says she continues to require medical monitoring, care and treatment and should her condition worsen, she might have to undertake more intense medical investigations, treatment and care for chronic pain. She claims general and special damages, as well as an order for provisional damages.

The defendants’ defence

72     The defendants admit that at all material times they owed a duty of care to the plaintiff to provide medical advice, care and treatment to her in accordance with proper standards of care. They, however, deny that they had breached that duty.

73     The defendants plead that they had duly discharged their duty of care owed to the plaintiff, and with regard to each of the plaintiff’s specific allegations of breach of duty, they say that:

(a)     The plaintiff was appropriately advised of the relevant and material risks, complications, and alternative treatments of each surgery, and she had provided her informed consent to undergo each surgery. In particular, the plaintiff was specifically advised of the risk of pain, nerve injury, numbness, and the fact that there might be residual discrepancies/minor asymmetry after the surgeries. She was also counselled on the need to have realistic aesthetic expectations since the outcome of the surgeries cannot be predicted with complete certainty.

(b)     When the plaintiff was being advised in relation to the 1st Surgery, the initial treatment plan did not include further dentofacial surgeries. The 2nd and 3rd Surgeries were only subsequently offered as treatment options because the plaintiff was dissatisfied with her aesthetic appearance despite the fact that her dentofacial deformity had been functionally corrected, and the very minor asymmetry was considered well within reasonable expectations of surgical outcomes.

(c)     The plaintiff was appropriately advised of the reasonable alternative treatments.

(d)     During the time the plaintiff was under the care of the defendants, she did not have any temporomandibular joint disorders or periodontitis, and there was no clinical indication to offer treatments for the same. The plaintiff’s condition was assessed based on the examinations and clinical findings at the material time, and the appropriate treatment options were offered accordingly.

(e)     At all material times, the first and/or second defendants performed the surgeries with reasonable skill and care in accordance with the proper standard of care. After the 2nd Surgery, some mild prominence of the angle of the mandible on the right side was noted during the healing process. The protrusion was not observed during or immediately after the 2nd Surgery. This was an unexpected aesthetic outcome which could occur despite reasonable care and skill being taken in the conduct of the surgery. The protrusion was minor and did not affect the plaintiff functionally. The mild residual discrepancies after the surgeries were considered medically acceptable and well within surgical expectations and were not due to any lack of care or skill in the way the surgeries were performed.

(f)     After the 1st Surgery, the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformity was assessed to have been corrected with good functional outcome and minor aesthetic discrepancies which were within acceptable limits. However, as the plaintiff remained dissatisfied, further treatment options were discussed with her and she made the decision to undergo the 2nd Surgery and the 3rd Surgery. It was explained to the plaintiff that even in the best of hands, a perfect correction of all deformities can never be assured as aesthetic results are very subjective, particularly when dealing with a complex structure such as the human face.

(g)     The fact that the plaintiff’s presenting dentofacial deformity had been corrected and she was assessed to have a good functional outcome had been confirmed by A/Prof Lim in his medical report dated 19 April 2019.

(h)     The risk of some degree of pain, nerve injury and permanent numbness are known complications of the surgeries which can occur in spite of all appropriate care being taken. The plaintiff had been well advised of the risks of these complications and had consented to undergo the surgeries on that basis. The defendants deny there was any lack of care or skill in the manner the surgeries were performed. They aver that any symptoms the plaintiff might have been experiencing were not caused and/or contributed by any negligence on their part.

Applicable legal principles

74     It is settled law since the Court of Appeal judgment in Hii Chii Kok v Ooi Peng Jin London Lucien and another [2017] 2 SLR 492 (“Hii Chii Kok”) that there are two different standards of care applicable to medical negligence cases, one relating to the provision of medical advice and the other to diagnosis and treatment.

75     In relation to the provision of advice by a doctor to his patient, ie whether there was informed consent by the patient, a three-stage test applies:

(a)     First, the patient is required to identify the exact nature of the information that he alleges was not given to him and establish why it would be regarded as relevant and material. Information which a doctor ought to disclose is (a) information that would be relevant and material to a reasonable patient situated in the particular patient’s position, or (b) information that a doctor knows is important to the particular patient in question. This stage of the inquiry is undertaken from the perspective of the patient.

(b)     Second, the court determines whether the information (as found relevant and material pursuant to the first stage) was in the possession of the doctor at the relevant time. This stage is a factual inquiry.

(c)     Third, if the court finds that the doctor did possess the information, it then has to determine whether the doctor was justified in withholding the information from the patient. This stage of the inquiry is to be undertaken from the doctor’s perspective, having regard to the standards of a reasonable and competent doctor.

76     In relation to diagnosis and treatment, the Bolam test (with the Bolitho addendum) applies (see Hii Chii Kok at [100] to [102]). Under the Bolam test (laid down in Bolam v Friern Hospital Management Committee [1957] 1 WLR 582), a doctor will not be found negligent if he has acted in accordance with a practice accepted at the time as proper by a responsible body of medical opinion, even if other doctors might have adopted a different practice. The Bolitho addendum refers to the qualification to the Bolam test that in assessing whether a body of opinion is responsible, reasonable or respectable, the court has to be satisfied that the experts, in forming their views, had directed their minds to the question of comparative risks and benefits and have reached a defensible conclusion on the matter (see Bolitho v City and Hackney Health Authority [1998] AC 232).

Issues

77     Based on the pleaded allegations of the plaintiff, the court is required to decide on the following issues:

(a)     Whether the plaintiff had been appropriately advised of the relevant and material risks, complications, and alternative treatments in relation to the 1st Surgery, the 2nd Surgery and the 3rd Surgery;

(b)     Whether the diagnosis, treatment and management of the plaintiff by the first defendant and/or second defendants had been carried out in accordance with the proper standard of care for each of the three surgeries;

(c)     Whether any of the plaintiff’s alleged injuries were caused by the defendants’ breaches of duty; and

(d)     Whether the plaintiff is entitled to her claim of general and special damages.

78     Issue (a) deals with the question of informed consent. Issue (b) deals with the plaintiff’s allegations of the defendants’ specific breaches including whether the post-surgical care and management of the plaintiff was carried out in accordance with the proper standard of care for each of the three surgeries and whether the functional and aesthetic outcomes were within expectations of surgical outcomes for each of the three surgeries. Issue (c) touches on the aspect of causation while issue (d) will deal with the plaintiff’s claim for damages.

Whether plaintiff was appropriately advised on risks, complications, and alternative treatments in relation to each of the three surgeries

79     As per the three-stage test formulated in Hii Chii Kok to determine if informed consent had been given, it is incumbent on the plaintiff to first identify the exact nature of the information that she alleges was not given to her and establish why it would be regarded as relevant and material. From the Further and Better Particulars of the Statement of Claim[note: 50] furnished by the plaintiff, she had made specific allegations as to the risks and complications associated with each of the three surgeries that were not informed to her.

1st Surgery

80     The plaintiff claims that she was not informed of[note: 51]:

(a)     the need for repeat surgery;

(b)     residual deformities not corrected like a canted upper jaw, misaligned bite, a slanted chin and facial midline misalignment;

(c)     the potential risks of temporomandibular joint disorder (“TMD”) and chronic pain; and

(d)     the complications associated with wisdom teeth removal.

81     The plaintiff’s assertions that she had not been properly advised nor given her informed consent prior to the 1st Surgery were vigorously denied by the defendants. Both the first and second defendants had in their respective AEICs described what had transpired in the various consultation sessions that they had with the plaintiff which are supported by the contemporaneous clinical notes. They had explained the clinical indications, alternative treatment options, and potential risks and complications of the surgery to the plaintiff in detail including the possibility of pain, nerve injury and numbness. They testified that they had emphasised the fact that she should not expect the Surgery to produce “perfect” aesthetic results as minor residual discrepancies are to be expected post-surgically. At all times, the plaintiff showed that she understood the information given to her and was keen to proceed. Neither the first nor the second defendant had actively encouraged the plaintiff to undergo the surgeries in favour of other alternative treatment options.

82     Notably, the plaintiff did not challenge the accounts given by either of the defendants (as recounted in the Chronology at [11] to [18] above) nor the content of the clinical notes at the trial.

83     It is not disputed that the plaintiff had seen the first defendant on 20 July 2015, and both the first and second defendants on 20 November 2015 and 4 December 2015 prior to the 1st Surgery. The clinical notes indicate that the session on 20 July 2015 lasted from 12.58pm to 1.20pm; the session on 20 November 2015 from 11.18am to 1.34pm and the session on 4 December 2015 from 5.56pm to 6.17pm[note: 52]. The notes also recorded in brief the plaintiff’s complaints, the investigations done in the orthognathic work-up including all requisite measurements[note: 53] and the surgical plan. A note was included stating that risks and complications were explained to the patient. The plaintiff had understood and accepted that the alternative treatment through orthodontics alone would not be able to fully correct her dentofacial deformity. Hence, she was keen to proceed.

84     Given the fact that the plaintiff had three pre-surgery consultations which took up significant time, I find no basis to disbelieve the defendants’ account of the “consent-taking process” as articulated by DW1 during his cross-examination as follows[note: 54]:

Now, in terms of our consent-taking process, it is also very rigorous. So, what we do is we had---before all three operations, with an hour-long consultation with the patient to find out what she wanted, everything that she did. We drew diagrams, we drew pictures. We used photographs of previous surgeries to show her and not only that, after that we did not make her sign a consent form, because I understand that taking of consent is actually a process not just a single consent, just a single signing of the consent form, we made it a special appointment separate from that initial long consultation to allow her to sign the consent form, so that if she had any things that she would be---any questions or whatever, we could address it to her.

85     The process was completed when the plaintiff signed the Consent Form on 4 December 2015[note: 55] which expressly stated that the plaintiff as:

“the undersigned patient consent to undergo the operation / procedure of maxillary and mandibular and chin osteotomies and extraction of all wisdom teeth having understood the nature, purpose, risks, benefits and alternatives which were explained to me”

(underlined portion being handwritten)

86     Given the fact that the Consent Form had also specifically mentioned the extraction of all wisdom teeth, the plaintiff’s allegation that she was not informed of the complications of wisdom teeth removal is unfounded.

87     In the light of the defendants’ evidence, the plaintiff’s allegation that she was not informed of the need for repeat surgeries is incomprehensible – there was no need for any repeat surgery if the plaintiff had accepted the minor residual asymmetry and aesthetic imperfections which were within surgical expectations. The plaintiff has not adduced any evidence to counter the defendants’ clear position that the repeat surgeries were not required to correct any functional deformities.

88     As for advising her on the risk of TMD, the defendants deny that TMD is a known risk associated with bimaxillary orthognathic surgeries. No evidence has been adduced by the plaintiff to challenge the defendants’ position.

89     Accordingly, I find that the plaintiff had been advised as to the nature, purpose, risks and benefits of the 1st Surgery and had given her informed consent as confirmed by her signature on the Consent Form on 4 December 2015.

2nd Surgery

90     The plaintiff’s allegations on the lack of informed consent as regards the 2nd Surgery are made only against the first defendant, in that he failed to inform her that[note: 56]:

(a)     there would be an unexpected procedure where the right mandible had to be hinged causing an unbalanced facial contour; the right angle of the mandible could only be shaved down six months later; this anomaly resulted in an unbalanced jaw line;

(b)     there would be such a complication in this operation and she would have to endure chronic pain on the right masseter; and

(c)     due to the post-op placement of the upper jaw, where the upper jaw was placed parallel to the eyes, the bite would be asymmetrical and could only be corrected with segmentals due to the injury on the right mandible.

91     The plaintiff’s reference to the “unexpected procedure” leading to an unbalanced facial contour at the right mandible appears to refer to the right mandibular angle prominence which came about only sometime after the 2nd Surgery. According to the clinical records, it was noted during the follow-up consultation on 14 July 2016. It had not been observed during or immediately after the Surgery. The first defendant’s evidence is that this was a minor protrusion which did not affect the plaintiff functionally and was within surgical expectations.

92     The first defendant had also testified that after the 1st Surgery, he had assessed the plaintiff to have minor facial asymmetry which could be corrected through orthodontics with orthognathic surgery being a possible treatment option. The plaintiff had, however, insisted on the surgery to correct the residual facial asymmetry. Concerned at the disproportionate reaction from the plaintiff to the minor facial asymmetry, the first defendant had gone to the extent of referring her to see two psychiatrists, Dr Fones and Dr Lee, before he was prepared to go ahead with the 2nd Surgery.

93     From the numerous consultations that he had with her (as recounted at [26] to [33] above) and documented in the clinical records[note: 57], viz on 4 March 2016 (from 3.45pm to 4.30pm), 12 March 2016 (from 11.35am to 12.23pm), 19 March 2016 (from 10.30am to 11.20am), 26 March 2016 (from 11.00am to 11.15am), 14 April 2016 (from 1.57pm to 2.20pm), 7 May 2016 (from 12.55pm to 1.38pm) and 23 May 2016 (from 1.15pm to 1.26pm), there is ample evidence to support the first defendant’s account that he had advised the plaintiff on the risks and complications associated with the 2nd Surgery, the alternative treatment of orthodontics only and emphasised the need for realistic aesthetic expectations.

94     The plaintiff has no basis to deny that she had provided her informed consent for the 2nd Surgery after all the consultation sessions with the first defendant which culminated in her signing the Consent Form dated 1 June 2016[note: 58] which expressly stated that she as:

“the undersigned patient consent to undergo the operation / procedure of MAXILLARY AND MANDIBULAR OSTEOTOMIES UNDER GENERAL ANAESTHESIA having understood the nature, purpose, risks, benefits and alternatives which were explained to me by Dr Winston Tan”

(underlined portion being handwritten)

95     Accordingly, I find that the plaintiff had been advised as to the nature, purpose, risks and benefits as well as of the alternative treatment available to her. She had given her informed consent for the 2nd Surgery as confirmed by her signature on the Consent Form on 1 June 2016.

3rd Surgery

96     With regard to the 3rd Surgery which was performed on 8 May 2017, the plaintiff’s pleaded complaint is that the defendants did not inform her that by undergoing “segmentals”, her lips would “cave in and there would be major changes to the pout”[note: 59].

97     The plaintiff has not explained nor provided photographs of what she means by her allegation that she should have been informed that the 3rd Surgery would cause her lips to “cave in” or “major changes to the pout”.

98     The defendants’ clinical records[note: 60] show that the plaintiff was attended to by both the first and/or second defendants (as outlined in [43] to [49] above) at three separate consultations on 23 March 2017 (from 12.00pm to 12.32pm), 26 April 2017 (5.50pm to 7.00pm) and 4 May 2017 (9.20am to 10.20am). The first and second defendants had explained to the plaintiff that the purpose of the segmental surgery was to address her complaint of protrusion of her teeth and lips causing strain and tightness at her lips. She was informed of the alternative treatments but chose to proceed with a third round of orthognathic surgery. The clinical notes for 26 April 2017 clearly document that this was a consultation where six identified concerns of the plaintiff were addressed together with her expectations of the surgery.

99     A final discussion was held on 4 May 2015 where she signed the Consent Form indicating that she,

“the undersigned patient consent to undergo the operation / procedure of maxillary and mandibular osteotomies and extraction of all four premolars having understood the nature, purpose, risks, benefits and alternatives which were explained to (her) by Dr Winston Tan”

(underlined portion being handwritten)

100    From my assessment of the evidence, I find that the plaintiff had given her informed consent to proceed with the 3rd Surgery.

Whether defendants’ diagnosis, treatment and management of the plaintiff was in accordance with the proper standard of care

101    The plaintiff’s case is that the defendants had failed to properly carry out the 1st Surgery in 2015 such that she had to undergo two revision surgeries in 2016 and 2017. In particular, the plaintiff made the following specific allegations on the diagnosis and treatment provided by the defendants[note: 61]:

(a)     failure to carry out a CT scan before the 1st Surgery which would have reduced the risks of unfavourable fractures occurring during the surgery and complications from wisdom tooth removal;

(b)     wrong positioning of the jaws;

(c)     displacement of the right jawbone post-1st operation that had to be hinged out of emergency in the 2nd operation;

(d)     dispensing Arcoxia as a painkiller to the plaintiff when she reportedly has allergy to NSAIDs;

(e)     failure to offer treatments relating to TMD, periodontitis, remedies to reduce the “air bubble” on her right cheek, treatments for the nerve pain around the gumline from the right second molar to the first molar; and

(f)     failure to offer chronic pain management.

102    In order to prove that the defendants had breached their duty of care as alleged, it was incumbent on the plaintiff as per the Bolam test, to procure an expert opinion to show that the defendants’ acts and/or omissions as particularised by her were not in accordance with practices accepted by other competent members of the same profession, namely in the field of oral maxillofacial surgery. Without such expert evidence, the plaintiff’s case simply cannot get off the ground. Her reliance on materials sourced from the internet does not assist her case given their doubtful provenance[note: 62].

The defendants’ expert report

103    By contrast, in order to avoid liability, the defendants had obtained the opinion of one Dr Andrew Ow Tjin-Chiew (“DW3”), as their expert witness.

104    DW3 is an oral maxillofacial surgeon in private practice, with extensive experience and qualifications as described in his curriculum vitae. He had served as the immediate past President of the College of Dental Surgeons, Singapore, and is presently still a Visiting Senior Consultant at the KK Women’s and Children’s Hospital. He had previously also served in academia as an Assistant Professor at the National University of Singapore Faculty of Dentistry, Department of Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery. Thereafter, he was the Director of Service for Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery and a Senior Consultant at the Ng Teng Fong General Hospital, Department of Dentistry. He further informed the court that he has done close to 300 jaw surgeries in the past 10 to 15 years.

105    Notwithstanding the impressive credentials presented by DW3, pursuant to the Bolitho addendum to the Bolam test, the court still has to be satisfied that the opinion rendered satisfies the threshold test of logic in that the expert has directed his mind to the comparative risks and benefits relating to the matter and reached a defensible conclusion.

106    DW3’s opinion was based on his review of the medical records, the operative notes, radiographs and clinical pictures as well as the court documents provided to him (including the NUHS report of A/Prof Lim obtained by the plaintiff). He had comprehensively dealt with the diagnosis of the plaintiff’s condition, the recommended treatment by the first and second defendants and how the treatment had been carried out. Reference was also made to relevant medical literature to support his conclusions. DW3’s expert opinion clearly satisfies the Bolam-Bolitho test, and I find no reason to reject it.

107    Notably, DW3 had opined that the diagnosis of the plaintiff was of a severe and complex dentofacial deformity which is one of the more difficult dentofacial conditions to treat. Specifically, on his assessment of the procedures carried out, his opinion was as follows:

(a)     The 1st Surgery prescribed which included a Lefort 1 osteotomy, bilateral sagittal split osteotomies and a genioplasty and the surgical movements decided upon were appropriate to address the plaintiff’s medical diagnosis. There has been a significant correction and improvement of the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformity from the 1st Surgery. Taking into account the initial severity of the dentofacial deformity, the postoperative functional and aesthetic result for the 1st Surgery was clinically acceptable and well within the expectations of surgical outcomes.

(b)     The 2nd Surgery prescribed to the plaintiff included a Lefort 1 osteotomy, bilateral sagittal split osteotomies and chin parring. The planned surgical movements were aimed at correcting the 1.5mm show of gum / teeth on the left side, rotating the maxilla outwards on the right side (yaw movement) to support the lips; and increasing the ramus height on the right side. As the 1st Surgery had already achieved significant functional and aesthetic improvements for the plaintiff, the 2nd Surgery was prescribed to address the plaintiff’s minor aesthetic concerns. The post-surgery clinical photographs showed that these aesthetic concerns had been addressed appropriately. However, the surgery had also resulted in an unexpected right mandibular angle prominence. This was difficult to anticipate pre-operatively and might have resulted from the effect of the yaw and cant correction of the maxilla on the mandible. As the prominence was mild, it might not have been readily apparent during the surgery, especially if the bone segments of the mandible were seated passively.

(c)     The 3rd Surgery was a segmental Lefort 1 osteotomy, extraction of upper and lower first premolars, lower anterior segmental osteotomy, bilateral sagittal split osteotomies, chin parring and right-angle reduction. The surgical plan and movements were performed to address the complaints presented (teeth do not fit well, upper and lower dental mid-lines deviated to left by 3mm and 1mm, bimaxillary protrusion, lips feel strained/tight, right angle of jaw obvious, and chin in middle of face but right lower chin border more bulbous / lower than left). The post-surgery clinical photographs showed that there was a further correction of the plaintiff’s dentofacial deformity including a correction of the right mandibular angle prominence and the lower right chin border. The post-operative functional and aesthetic result for the 3rd Surgery was clinically acceptable and well within the expectations of surgical outcomes.

108    Upon his review of the medical records, radiographs and photographs provided, DW3 had also opined that the post-surgical care and management of the plaintiff after each of the Surgeries was reasonable, appropriate and in accordance with the proper standard of care for each of the three surgeries. This included the monitoring of the plaintiff’s vital signs and pain control throughout her hospital stays, the regular post-operative reviews following her discharge after each of the surgeries, the drugs prescribed to her, further procedures carried out in conjunction with her orthodontic treatment, treatments to address her complaints of numbness and pain as well as the provision of dental services (scaling and cleaning) to maintain her gingival health. The plaintiff has not disputed that these treatments were provided to her.

109    DW3 had also addressed the plaintiff’s specific complaints/concerns in his AEIC:

(a)     “failure to carry out a CT scan before the 1st Surgery”:

(i)       The defendants had done a pre-operative workup prior to the 1st Surgery, which included an orthognathic assessment, x-rays (orthopantomogram and lateral cephalograph), dental models and photographs. This was in accordance with appropriate and well-established practice.

(ii)       The use of two-dimensional x-rays as the sole imaging modality as part of the pre-operative assessment prior to orthognathic surgery is routine practice.

(iii)       A CT scan would have exposed the plaintiff to unnecessary radiation and should therefore only be performed if there were proper clinical indications to do so. In the plaintiff’s case, there were no such clinical indications. Pre-operative x-rays showed that the plaintiff’s lower left wisdom tooth was fully erupted while the lower right wisdom tooth was very superficially impacted. Accordingly, these teeth would not have been a significant contributing factor to an unfavourable fracture, and hence taking a pre-operative CT scan to assess their position and risk contribution was unnecessary.

(b)     “The “unfavourable fracture” which occurred during the 1st Surgery had caused the plaintiff to suffer from trismus”:

(i)       On whether the occurrence of an “unfavourable fracture” would be informed to a patient, this varies from practice to practice, and DW3 would have informed his patient of the same. However, the occurrence of the “unfavourable fracture” in the 1st Surgery did not affect the plaintiff’s surgical result in any way.

(ii)       From DW3’s review of the medical records, there was no indication that the plaintiff suffered from any trismus between the 1st Surgery when the “unfavourable fracture” occurred and the 2nd Surgery.

(iii)       The medical records showed that the plaintiff experienced some temporomandibular joint and masseter muscle discomfort after the 2nd Surgery and the 3rd Surgery. There was no specific finding of trismus documented. The plaintiff did not attend her follow-up consultations with the defendants after 23 February 2018.

(iv)       From the NUHS medical report, the plaintiff’s maximal mouth opening was noted to be 24mm, with assisted maximal mouth opening 29mm and pain at her left and right masseteric region. She was diagnosed with myalgia of the right masseter muscle. From this report, the slight limitation in mouth opening or guarding due to pain was caused by myalgia of the right masseter muscle which is a treatable condition.

(c)     “dispensing Arcoxia as a painkiller to the plaintiff when she reportedly has allergy to NSAIDs”:

(i)       The plaintiff appears to have made contradictory allegations. It was noted that the plaintiff had also complained that the defendants had not dispensed Arcoxia and prevented her access to it.

(ii)       It was noted that the plaintiff had requested for Arcoxia as it was the only painkiller prescribed by her family doctor that did not result in facial swelling. As such, if the defendants were aware that she had taken Arcoxia without any side effects, it was appropriate for them to prescribe Arcoxia.

110    From his review of the medical records, the operative notes, radiographs and clinical pictures as well as the court documents provided to him (including the NUHS report of A/Prof Lim obtained by the plaintiff), DW3 had concluded as follows:

(a)     The plaintiff was appropriately advised of the relevant and material risks, complications, and alternative treatments of each of the three surgeries.

(b)     The surgical management of the plaintiff was reasonable / appropriate and in accordance with the proper standard of care, for each of the three surgeries.

(c)     The plaintiff’s functional and aesthetic outcomes were within expectations of surgical outcomes for each of the three surgeries.

(d)     The post-surgical care and management of the plaintiff was reasonable / appropriate and in accordance with the proper standard of care for each of the three surgeries.

111    As the plaintiff did not challenge the competence of DW3 as an expert and provided no contrary opinion from another expert, I accept his conclusion and find that the diagnosis, care, treatment and management of the plaintiff by the first and second defendants was reasonable and appropriate at all material times.

112    As a result of the orthognathic surgeries performed by the first and second defendants, the plaintiff’s previous dentofacial deformity had been corrected. This had been confirmed by the “second opinion” that the plaintiff had sought from NUHS. A/Prof Lim’s report states that from his clinical examination, the plaintiff’s frontal view and side view appear aesthetically normal in proper ratio, her lips are competent, there is no gross asymmetry, the chin point is on with her facial midline, occlusion is satisfactory and there is no joint sound on jaw movements.

113    There has clearly been a marked improvement in the plaintiff’s dentofacial condition after the surgeries. The photographs of the plaintiff after the 3rd Surgery when compared with those taken prior to the 1st Surgery also show, objectively, a distinct improvement in her overall aesthetic appearance[note: 63].

Whether any of the plaintiff’s alleged injuries were caused by the defendants’ breaches of duty

114    Even if the defendants were found to have breached their duty of care to the plaintiff, whether in their diagnosis, advice and treatment of the plaintiff while she was their patient, no recovery is allowed unless the plaintiff is also able to prove that the injuries she allegedly suffered were caused by the defendants’ acts or omissions.

115    The plaintiff pleaded a total of eight particulars of injuries in her statement of claim as follows:

(a)     Chronic pain in her jaw and mouth area;

(b)     Myalgia of her right masseter;

(c)     Lower lip and chin parathesia (sic) and hyperparathesia (sic);

(d)     Hyperparathesia (sic) of her upper frenum region;

(e)     Severe peridonditis (sic);

(f)     Perdontitis (sic) of her lower second molars;

(g)     Inability to open her mouth completely;

(h)     Difficulties speaking properly.

116    They appear to have been derived from her presenting complaints as well as the diagnosis set out in A/Prof Lim’s specialist report of 19 April 2019[note: 64]. As seen in the report, upon completion of his investigations and clinical examination of the plaintiff, A/Prof Lim’s diagnosis was that she had:

(a)     myalgia of her right masseter,

(b)     lower lip and chin paraesthesia / hyperesthesia,

(c)     hyperesthesia of her upper frenum region; and

(d)     periodontitis at her lower second molars.

117    As the defendants had not agreed to dispense with the cross-examination of A/Prof Lim, the plaintiff ought to have called him as a witness. Without his oral testimony, and taken at face value, there is nothing in the report which supports the plaintiff’s case that the injuries diagnosed were caused by the surgeries performed by the defendants. A/Prof Lim had in fact proposed that the plaintiff see a chronic pain specialist to manage her chronic pain, undergo further radiographic examination to confirm the severity of her periodontitis and consult a periodontist for further management but she had declined.

118    With regard to the other four “injuries” not mentioned in A/Prof Lim’s report, they appear to have some reference to the contents of a letter from one Dr Jamie Lee, a general dentist who had given the plaintiff dental treatment from July 2015 to October 2021. He had affirmed an AEIC exhibiting the letter but had declined to attend court as the plaintiff’s witness. As such, the court can place no weight on the letter.

119    As neither A/Prof Lim nor Dr Jamie Lee was willing to testify as her witness[note: 65], the plaintiff has adduced no evidence to discharge her burden of proof as to causation of her injuries.

120    On the part of the defendants, they dispute that the injuries / symptoms complained of by the plaintiff are causally connected with the three surgeries for the following reasons:

(a)     Chronic pain, myalgia, paraesthesia, and hyperesthesia are subjective complaints, with many possible causes unrelated to the three surgeries, such as tension headaches, stress-related jaw pain or muscle soreness. There is nothing in A/Prof Lim’s report to indicate that these conditions were caused, or contributed to, by the three surgeries.

(b)     Periodontitis is a gum infection caused by bacteria accumulating on the teeth and gums and is typically caused by poor dental hygiene. It is unrelated to the three surgeries.

(c)     The plaintiff’s alleged inability to open her mouth completely is unrelated to the three surgeries. As opined by DW3, the plaintiff’s slight limitation in mouth opening or guarding due to pain as at 26 March 2019 when she was assessed by A/Prof Lim was caused by the myalgia of the right masseter muscle.

(d)     The plaintiff’s alleged difficulties with speaking properly are not supported by any medical report.

121    I agree with the defendants:

(a)     Complaints of pain, myalgia, paraesthesia, and hyperesthesia cannot be objectively ascertained. In any case, the plaintiff had been counselled repeatedly that pain, discomfort, numbness and nerve injury were risks associated with the surgeries. She had nonetheless consented to undergo the procedures and cannot now hold them responsible for her alleged suffering. Considering that she had also declined A/Prof Lim’s proposal to see a chronic pain specialist for further management, the veracity of her complaint is in doubt.

(b)     As for her periodontitis, the first defendant had testified that based on the panoramic x-ray taken by Dr Cheng on 1 March 2018, there was no sign of any periodontal disease. Hence, the periodontal pocketing subsequently found could not be caused by the defendants. The plaintiff had asserted during her cross-examination of the defence witnesses that she had developed periodontitis due to the extended time that she had to wear braces because of the three operations. DW3 was specifically asked by the court for his explanation to which he answered as follows[note: 66]:

From what I gather from the X-rays and her notes and Prof Asher Lim’s reports, it doesn’t seem in any way that orthognathic surgery would result in periodontal disease… Prof Lim only 2 reports two areas where she had what we call pocketing: a 5mm 3 pocket on one side and a 7mm pocket on the other side. These were associated with two second lower molars… this condition actually is not a very serious condition.. It is an entirely treatable condition as well. Orthognathic surgery would not result in periodontal pocketing in these areas, for sure.

… 90 per cent of patients in Singapore will have some form of gum disease or periodontal disease. This is taken from the NDC, National Dental Centre, website. So, the patient can develop periodontal disease for any sort of reasons. In this case, I do not think that it was related to orthognathic surgery. Poor oral hygiene, but unlikely because she had undergone cleaning, regular cleaning, with the dental hygienist at the practice. So, it could have been developed after she stopped seeing Dr Lye, which was in February 2018, and throughout the year where she saw doctor Prof Asher Lim in 2019. So, by then, almost 11 months had passed and she could have developed this disease after. So, there are many factors that could have contributed to that, but I do not think orthognathic surgery would have resulted in that.

From the evidence adduced, I would have to find that the plaintiff’s periodontitis was caused by her lack of dental hygiene, and not by the three surgeries performed by the defendants.

(c)     As for the plaintiff’s complaint of inability to open her mouth completely, the reported limitation was a mere 5mm only as documented by A/Prof Lim. This was due to the pain at the right masseteric region which is a treatable condition. As she had rejected his offer for chronic pain management by a specialist, the condition may not be as serious as alleged.

(d)     Lastly, on the plaintiff’s allegation of difficulties with her speech, aside from having no supporting medical evidence, she did not exhibit any such impediment at the trial. She was articulate and her voice was clearly audible despite being masked even though this was no longer a requirement at the time.

122    In summary, the plaintiff has no basis whatsoever to blame the defendants for her alleged injuries, and her case on negligence fails on this point alone.

Whether the plaintiff is entitled to her claim of general and special damages.

123    The issue of damages does not arise for consideration since the plaintiff has failed to prove the necessary elements of breach of duty as well as causation.

124    Even if the plaintiff had succeeded in establishing the defendants’ breach/breaches of duty, her claims for the various heads of damages are spurious:

(a)     In her statement of claim, she had pleaded an item for future medical care expenses, but no particulars have been furnished as required by O 18 r 12(1C) of the Rules of Court 2014 (“ROC 2014”). All we have on record is a bare assertion in her statement of claim that she continues to require medical monitoring, care and treatment on account of her jaw and mouth injuries. There is no evidence whatsoever that she has continued to seek medical treatment since she unilaterally stopped her follow-up appointments at the fourth defendant’s clinic. Further, she had declined to follow up with referrals by A/Prof Lim to manage her alleged chronic pain and periodontitis.

(b)     In her opening statement filed for the trial, she had made a claim for $2,599.60 for the cost of periodontitis treatment. Even if causation had been proven, aside from the fact that this claim was not particularised as required by ROC 2014, no evidence by way of any invoice for such treatment was tendered to the court.

(c)     In her opening statement filed for the trial, she had also made a claim for loss of wages of an estimated $1,600,000 for the period June 2016 to August 2022 without any evidence in support. She did not call her previous employer, World First Asia Pte Ltd, to give evidence, and from the documents exhibited in her AEIC, it appears that she had voluntarily resigned her employment in May 2016 and accepted an ex gratia lump sum payment. Hence, any loss of income would have been self-induced. The belated inclusion of further income documents in her closing submissions is in breach of civil procedural rules, and the court must disregard them. Further, as pointed out in the 4th Defendant’s Closing Submissions, such a claim is in excess of this court’s jurisdiction.

(d)     She had also further claimed $99,722.59 for her debt to the bank for her medical expenses, and a further $60,000 as interest paid to the banks for the multiple surgeries. No evidence was adduced as to how these sums are attributable to the defendants’ alleged breaches of duty. At the trial, the plaintiff had also disclosed that her medical bills were in fact covered by insurance. So, her entire claim appears suspect.

Conclusion

125    While the plaintiff as a self-represented litigant might be accorded some measure of indulgence, she is nonetheless subject to the same rules and procedures of the court. As the plaintiff, she was fully cognisant that she bears the burden of proof of every aspect of her claim. She persisted in prosecuting her claim despite being advised on her lack of supporting evidence. She has also named the third and fourth defendants in the action without pleading any cause of action against them.

126    In the premises, the plaintiff’s claim is hereby dismissed with costs. On the issue of costs, the parties are directed to file their respective written submissions within 14 days from the release of this judgment for the court’s consideration.
