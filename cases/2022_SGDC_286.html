13 December 2022

District Judge Ng Tee Tze Allen:

1       This action arose out of Mr Choon Cher Siong Ronnie’s (the “plaintiff”) attempt to leave a joint venture.

2       The fact of the joint venture is undisputed. The plaintiff entered it with Ms. Florensia Leovanny Liong (the “1st defendant”), Mr Tan Tor Teng Jerome (the “2nd defendant”), and Mr Patrick Chong Tah Lick (the “3rd defendant”). The joint venture was for the purchase (and subsequent sale) of a property at B30-2 Tower B, South Bay Plaza, Penang, Malaysia (the “Property”). In furtherance to the joint venture, the plaintiff and the 1st defendant took a loan from HSBC Bank Malaysia Berhad (“HSBC”) to finance the purchase of the Property (the “HSBC Loan”).

3       In 2016, disagreements emerged between the plaintiff and the 2nd defendant. The plaintiff claimed that this eventually led to all four parties agreeing on 4 December 2017 for him to exit the joint venture (the “alleged Exit Agreement”) and for, amongst other things, the three defendants to continue servicing the mortgage of the Property and to reimburse him for his initial outlay for the Property.

4       The plaintiff also claimed that the defendants breached the alleged Exit Agreement by failing to service the HSBC Loan. This led to HSBC auctioning the Property, applying the sales proceeds to the HSBC Loan, and suing the plaintiff and the 1st defendant for the balance.

5       The plaintiff sued for an indemnity and/or damages to be assessed.

6       I heard the trial on 23 June 2022 and 5 July 2022. Only the plaintiff and the 1st defendant attended. They exchanged written closing submissions on 26 August 2022 and made oral reply submissions on 23 September 2022. After considering the evidence and their submissions, I dismissed the plaintiff’s claim against the 1st defendant. I also fixed costs at S$15,000 plus GST and reasonable disbursements to be paid by the plaintiff to the 1st defendant.

7       As for the 2nd and 3rd defendants,

(a)     The plaintiff sought no order against the 2nd defendant. He submitted that a bankruptcy order had been made against the 2nd defendant and as such the proceedings against him were stayed.[note: 1] No order was made against the 2nd defendant.

(b)     The plaintiff sought final judgment against the 3rd defendant in his absence. In this respect, the plaintiff’s counsel submitted that he had already obtained judgment against the 3rd defendant for the reimbursement of his initial outlay for the Property, and as such, he was only seeking judgment for the outstanding sum under the HSBC Loan.[note: 2] I acceded to the plaintiff’s request. Pursuant to ROC 2014, O 35, r 1(2), I gave final judgment to the plaintiff for the sum of RM637,227.15 with interest fixed at 5.33% to run from the date of writ to judgment. Costs was fixed at S$8,000 (all in) plus GST to be paid by the 3rd defendant to the plaintiff.

8       The plaintiff has appealed against my decision to dismiss his claim against the 1st defendant. As such, I set out the full grounds of my decision.

Facts

The joint venture

9       Unless otherwise stated, the following facts are undisputed as between the plaintiff and the 1st defendant.

10     The parties entered the joint venture on or around 1 September 2012. This was by way of two agreements:

(a)     The first was a written agreement (the “written JV Agreement”). The parties to the written JV agreement were the plaintiff, the 1st defendant, and 2nd defendant. They agreed to invest in the Property pursuant to the written JV Agreement.[note: 3]

(b)     The second agreement was an oral agreement (the “oral JV Agreement”). The parties to the oral JV Agreement were the plaintiff and all three defendants. The net effect of the oral JV Agreement was that the 3rd defendant joined the joint venture and undertook the obligations under the written JV Agreement.[note: 4]

11     Broadly speaking, the net effect of the written JV Agreement and the oral JV Agreement (collectively, the “JV Agreement”) was that all four parties joined the joint venture to invest in the Property. The terms of the joint venture were largely found in the written JV Agreement save for the parties’ respective share in the joint venture. Under the oral JV Agreement, it was agreed that the 2nd defendant had a 50% share in the investment and the other parties had a 16.667% share in the investment.[note: 5] I will refer to the terms of the JV Agreement later in my judgment, if necessary.

12     On 1 March 2013, the plaintiff and the 1st defendant took the HSBC Loan for the purchase of the Property.[note: 6] The parties serviced the HSBC Loan by making payments into a HSBC Account No XXX (the “HSBC Account”). HSBC would deduct the mortgage payments from the HSBC Account.[note: 7]

The plaintiff’s attempt to leave the joint venture

13     In 2016, a dispute arose between the plaintiff and the 2nd defendant.[note: 8] I do not need to go into the details of this dispute. Suffice to state, the plaintiff sought to leave the joint venture because of this dispute. To that end, the following correspondence, amongst others, was exchanged:

(a)     On or around 3 October 2016, the plaintiff sent a letter to the 1st defendant titled “HANDING OVER OF ITEMS AND DOCUMENTS FOR SOUTHBAY PLAZA B-30-2, TOWER B”. The letter listed various items which the plaintiff wanted to handover to the 1st defendant. The letter also purported to set out the following “T&Cs in relation to the three [defendants] taking over [of the plaintiff’s] shares of the property”:[note: 9]

This letter also serves to inform you that we had agreed on 27 Oct on the following T&Cs in relation to the three of you taking over my shares of the property:

1.    All legal and finance costs related to this will be shared equally by all 4 investors

2.    I shall be refunded for whatever money I had paid up for this investment till 3 Oct 2016.

3.    The amount to refund me is $17,145.1. Please see the worksheet attached.

4.    This is not a property sales. It is just a withdrawal of my investment.

5.    I will not be responsible for any more finance matter from 27 Oct 2016.

(b)     On 29 October 2016, the plaintiff followed up with an email to the 1st defendant on the handover. He copied the 2nd defendant. The email stated:[note: 10]

Vanny

My wife will contact you next week to collect the whole bag from Mah Seng we took during the Penang trip.

All the keys, access cards, remote controls and documents are inside as original.

I do not [sic] need to keep anything since I am no more a partner.

I will let you know soon the fund to return to me being my share paid up so far.

My lawyer can only take action to contact HSBC and his Malaysia associate in my exit from this investment after we have received your letter confirming what we agreed in the sms on 27 Oct.

So please expedite this once you collect the bag next week.

Thanks

Choong.

(c)     On or around 11 November 2016, the 1st defendant acknowledged receipt of the 3 October 2016 letter and the items listed therein. She also wrote “KIV” next to the terms reproduced at [13(a)] above and wrote the following note at the end of the 3 October 2016 letter:[note: 11]

Note: Can send me the working sheet for the payment so I can check through the payment done by our side & [the 2nd defendant]?

14     The plaintiff testified that he stopped paying into the HSBC Account around this point.[note: 12] The 1st defendant did not contest this.

15     That said, the plaintiff’s case was not that the parties agreed for him to leave the joint venture in 2016 or that the above correspondence evidenced such an agreement. Instead, his case was that the above correspondence showed that the 1st defendant knew that he intended to leave the joint venture.[note: 13] Accordingly, the correspondence was “the lead-up to the agreement expressed in [the 2nd defendant’s] email of 4.12.2017 to [the plaintiff], copied to [the 1st and 3rd defendants].”[note: 14]

16     This brings us to the emails which the plaintiff premised the alleged Exit Agreement on. My analysis together with the full text of these emails are set out at [52] below. For now, it suffices to summarise these emails:

(a)     On 27 November 2017, the plaintiff sent an email to the 1st defendant, copying the other two defendants. In that email, the plaintiff referred to an SMS from the 1st defendant on the topping up of the HSBC Account. He refused to do so. In support, the plaintiff referred to “the series of events that had taken place over the past years”, his agreement with the 2nd defendant, and asserted that he was “already out of this partnership from that point”.[note: 15]

(b)     On 4 December 2017, the 2nd defendant replied, copying the 1st and 3rd defendants. He confirmed the contents of the plaintiff’s 27 November 2017 email and stated that the plaintiff “will not be liable for any losses or have any share if there is a profit from the sale of this property. However, [the plaintiff] will receive whatever money [he had] invested in this property when the property is sold.”[note: 16]

(c)     The next day, on 5 December 2017, the plaintiff responded to thank the 2nd defendant and to “agree to [the 2nd defendant’s] confirmation on the latest state of the partnership.”[note: 17]

17     The 1st defendant did not dispute receiving the above emails. However, she claimed that she did not see the 4 December 2017 email at the material time. She also denied agreeing to the plaintiff’s withdrawal from the joint venture and asserted that she was not privy to any agreement between the plaintiff and the 2nd defendant.[note: 18]

State of the joint venture after the plaintiff’s attempt to leave

18     The plaintiff remained a borrower under the HSBC Loan despite the alleged Exit Agreement.[note: 19] For this reason, he received late payment notices from HSBC which he would forward to the defendants.[note: 20] He was omitted from a number of correspondence exchanged by the three defendants relating to the joint venture.[note: 21]

19     The 1st defendant remained a party to the joint venture and continued to receive emails on the same. One instance of this is Mr Jeremiah Kwan’s email of 26 July 2019. Mr Kwan had sent this email on the 2nd defendant’s behalf. In this email, he wrote:[note: 22]

Hi all,

This is to keep everyone in the loop from the meeting on 6-July-2019.

[The 2nd defendant] has invest a total of 239,819 RM till date.

Therefore, [the 1st defendant and the 3rd defendant] both have to invest a total of 119,909.5 RM each till date to match up with [the 2nd defendant].

…

Please refer to the attachment.

Choong left the investment on December 2017.

…

Mr Kwan’s email attached a pdf document which purported to set out the defendants’ respective share in the investment. According to the pdf document, the 2nd defendant held 50% of the investment, with remaining 50% being split equally between the 1st and 3rd defendants. This represented an increase of the 1st and 3rd defendants’ share in the investment from 16.667% to 25%.

20     The 1st defendant did not object to the increase of her share in the investment. Instead, she ignored the email[note: 23] and continued to make payments into the HSBC Account as and when it was needed.

Default of the HSBC Loan and the plaintiff’s demands

21     A second dispute arose. This time, it was between the 1st and 2nd defendants. Again, I do not need to go into the details of this dispute. Suffice to state, the 1st defendant stopped making payments into the HSBC Account because of this dispute. The 1st defendant’s final payment was made on 23 September 2019.[note: 24]

22     After the HSBC Account ran out of funds in 2020, HSBC started writing to the plaintiff to demand payment.[note: 25]

23     The plaintiff forwarded these demands to the defendants by way of email. In these emails, the plaintiff would propose options that the defendants could take.[note: 26] However, the plaintiff’s emails went unanswered. This resulted in the plaintiff threatening to enforce his rights under the JV Agreement.[note: 27] However, these threats were ignored also.

24     On 29 December 2020[note: 28] and 6 January 2021,[note: 29] the plaintiff’s solicitors, Lawrence Chua Practice LLC (“LCP”) wrote to the defendants. The substance of both letters was the same. Broadly, both letters referred to the JV Agreement and “a subsequent agreement in 2017”. Both asserted that each of the defendants were obligated to:

(a)     service and pay the mortgage loan to HSBC for the Property; and

(b)     reimburse the plaintiff for “his total outlay for the Property as an initial investor, agreed to be discharged and exit from his obligations under the JV Agreement”.

Both letters also asserted that the defendants had defaulted on the above obligations, that the plaintiff had given them the opportunity to remedy their default within 14 days (per clause 11 of the JV Agreement), and that the defendants had failed to do so. Accordingly, LCP purported to serve notice “to terminate the JV Agreement on behalf of [the plaintiff] against each one of [the defendants] pursuant to clause 11.1.”

25     On 13 January 2021, the plaintiff commenced this action to seek inter alia a reimbursement of his initial outlay as well as “an indemnity for all damages, interests, costs and expenses payable to HSBC”.[note: 30]

26     In August 2021 (i.e. about 10 months before trial) HSBC auctioned the Property. It applied the auction proceeds to the outstanding HSBC Loan and sued the plaintiff and the 1st defendant for the balance RM 637,277.15.[note: 31]

27     This brings us to the trial before me.

The parties’ cases

28     The plaintiff’s case was that the 1st defendant breached the alleged Exit Agreement. He alleged that on or about 4 December 2017, the 1st defendant entered the alleged Exit Agreement either orally[note: 32] or through a written agreement which was confirmed by conduct.[note: 33] The plaintiff said that the alleged Exit Agreement was one of two things:

(a)     First, the alleged Exit Agreement either was a standalone agreement with the following terms:

(i)       The plaintiff was permitted to exit and/or be discharged from his obligations under the JV Agreement as an investor;

(ii)       The three defendants would continue to service the HSBC Loan; and

(iii)       The plaintiff would not benefit from any share of the profits nor be liable for any losses but would be reimbursed in full for all his outlay (contributions and expenses) in the investment in the Property upon its sale.

(b)     Alternatively, the alleged Exit Agreement varied the JV Agreement to give effect to the aforementioned terms.

29     The plaintiff alleged that the 1st defendant breached the alleged Exit Agreement when she refused to continue servicing the HSBC Loan. On this basis, he claimed: (a) S$27,309.25 (being his initial outlay in the joint venture); and (b) RM637,277.15 (being the sum HSBC sued him for).[note: 34]

30     The 1st defendant did not deny that she stopped servicing the HSBC Loan or the plaintiff’s quantification of his alleged losses. But she denied being a party to the alleged Exit Agreement. Her pleaded case was that she was not aware of the alleged Exit Agreement and that she never agreed for the plaintiff to exit or be discharged from the JV Agreement.[note: 35]

Issues

31     The only cause of action which the plaintiff advanced was breach of the alleged Exit Agreement. This was confirmed by plaintiff’s counsel during oral reply submissions who stated that the plaintiff was not pursuing any other cause(s) of action.

32     As such, I did not consider the viability of any other potential causes of action (e.g. unjust enrichment or breach of the original JV Agreement). Instead, I focused purely on whether there was a breach of the alleged Exit Agreement. In this respect, given the parties’ positions, the sole issue I had to consider was whether the 1st defendant was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement.

Whether the 1st defendant entered an oral Exit Agreement

33     I start with the issue of whether the 1st defendant was part of an oral Exit Agreement. Here, it is important to remember that the burden of proof was on the plaintiff to prove his positive case. This is especially so where the plaintiff alleged the existence of an oral contract and there are gaps in the evidence: Cham Tam Hoi v Wang Jian [2022] SGHC 192 (“Cham Tam Hoi”) at [38].

34     In the present case, the plaintiff did not discharge his burden of proof.

35     First, the plaintiff changed the position he took in his pleadings and his AEIC that the alleged Exit Agreement was reached orally in December 2017.[note: 36] During cross examination, he testified that the 4 December 2017 alleged Exit Agreement was between himself and the 2nd defendant and that the others agreed for him to leave approximately one year earlier in end 2016:

NE (5 Jul 2022) p.16 ln 11-20

Q    Now, you are saying that Jerome agreed on 4th December 2017, correct?

A    Yes.

Q    By way of this email. Now, when did Vanny agree?

A     Before this happened, there was already discussion, right, already discussion on my exit sometime in 2016, end 2016. Then in 2017, Vanny text---SMS me to ask me to---actually 2016 an oral agreement, till then now, Jerome’s office has never approached me for any payment. So that’s already the first indication they consider me out of this already…

NE (5 Jul 2022) p.17 ln 24 to p.18 ln 7

Q    5(1). You are saying that, actually, before 4th December 2017, in end 2016, they had all already agreed orally to (a), (b) and (c)?

A     Yes, because since the beginning of 2016, Jerome start having problem to top up the fund. And I almost receive every month a reminder letter from HSBC on insufficient fund. The bank call me. So I got very stressed up over this and start to email Jerome. And Jerome get frustrated with me and I think he share this with the other two defendant who, even though they had done nothing at all to help in this, sided with him to express displeasure over my action. So I said, “Okay, I’m doing this free of---free will. And since you guy find me not suitable partner, I will go out”, you know. So they agreed that I will go out. Only thing that we didn’t follow up with black and white until I was jolted to the memory when I received the SMS in 2017 to top up the fund. But between then and there, I have never paid anything. You can see from the payment record, you know.

(emphasis mine)

36     This was a material difference that I could not overlook. In Chan Tam Hoi, the High Court dismissed a respondent-claimant’s application to amend his pleading that an oral agreement was reached in January 2019 instead of November 2018. The High Court observed that permitting the application would result in “changing the narrative completely by saying that the oral agreement materialized several months later than the original pleaded date.” This would prejudice the appellant-defendant because he had always ran his defence based on an oral agreement in November 2018:

51      I dismiss this application. To my mind, there is a material difference between the pleaded case that Alleged Oral Agreement was concluded in November 2018, against a case that it was only concluded in January 2019. There must be a single point in time when the necessary consensus ad idem is reached. Even leaving aside this trite principle that there must be a definite point of formation for every contract (see the High Court decisions of Day, Ashley Francis v Yeo Chin Huat Anthony and others [2020] 5 SLR 514 (“Day, Ashley Francis”) at [53]; Independent State of Papua New Guinea v PNG Sustainable Development Program Ltd [2019] SGHC 68 at [149]), Mr Tan’s belated application is a bridge too far to cross. While I will allow for some latitude in relation to the date of consensus ad idem for oral agreements, such as pleading a range of dates within a specified period, that latitude cannot extend to changing the narrative completely by saying that the oral agreement materialised several months later from the original pleaded date. This will prejudice the appellant as he has always run his defence based on an oral agreement in November 2018, not an agreement at a completely different point in time.

(emphasis mine)

37     Similarly, in the present case, permitting the plaintiff to rely on an oral agreement reached in end 2016 (i.e. 1 year earlier than his pleaded case) would change the nature of the plaintiff’s case completely. It would also unduly prejudice the 1st defendant whose defence was based on the allegation that the alleged oral Exit Agreement was entered in December 2017.

38     Second, it was not clear from the plaintiff’s evidence that the 1st defendant was even part of the alleged 2016 agreement. Despite his oral testimony at [35] above, the plaintiff also gave evidence which indicated that the alleged Exit Agreement was reached between the 2nd defendant and himself (as opposed to all three defendants and him). To take one example:[note: 37]

Q:    So, where in the JV agreement, Mr Choong, is your right to the refund of your full principal and any other losses?

A:     This has been agreed by Jerome when I---when we talk about we opting out 2016. I say okay, refund me my---whatever I’ve paid But he said, “No, you’ll have to---you’ll only get it after the property is sold”, you know.

(emphasis mine)

Similarly, the plaintiff’s email of 27 November 2017 and the 2nd defendant’s email of 4 December 2017 also indicated that the two of them were the only parties to the alleged Exit Agreement (see [52] below).

39     Due to the confusing state of the plaintiff’s evidence, I sought to clarify with him what his case was. In the course of answering my questions, the plaintiff confirmed that was no written or oral Exit Agreement with the 1st defendant. Instead, he was relying on the 1st defendant’s subsequent conduct to show that the 1st defendant agreed to the alleged Exit Agreement:[note: 38]

Court:

No. So no oral agreement, no written agreement. What you’re relying on is the subsequent conduct to show that---

Witness:

Yah.

Court:

---she had agreed.

Witness:

Yes.



40     Now, I accept that the contemporaneous documentary evidence and conduct of the parties are indicative of whether an oral contract was reached: Cham Tam Hoi at [67]. However, all of these became moot when the plaintiff admitted that no such oral agreement existed. Given his admission, I dismissed the plaintiff’s claim insofar as it was premised on the existence of an oral Exit Agreement.

41     I turn to the plaintiff’s allegation that the alleged Exit Agreement was a written agreement which was confirmed by conduct.

Whether the 1st defendant entered a written Exit Agreement which was confirmed by her conduct

Genesis of the plaintiff’s alternative case

42     This aspect of the plaintiff's case did not start optimistically. It was conceived after the plaintiff admitted that there was no oral Exit Agreement between him and the 1st defendant and closed his case. It was only then that the plaintiff applied to amend his Statement of Claim (Amendment No.1) to include his alternative case that the Exit Agreement was in “such writing confirmed by the [defendants’] conduct”.

43     Furthermore, the plaintiff’s proposed amendment was poorly phrased. On its plain reading, the plaintiff was only still alleging breach of an oral Exit Agreement. On this basis, the plaintiff’s case would fail for the reasons at [33]-[40] above. The relevant paragraph of the plaintiff’s Statement of Claim (Amendment No.2) is reproduced below:

5.     On or about 4.12.2017, arising from a dispute, the Plaintiff and all 3 Defendants orally agreed as evidenced by writing from the 2nd Defendant to the Plaintiff, copied to the 1st and 3rd Defendants, and/or such writing confirmed by the Defendants’ conduct, whereby parties agreed to enter into a compromise and/or vary the [JV Agreement] on the terms and conditions that: (1) the Plaintiff was permitted to exit and/or be discharged from his obligations under the [JV Agreement] as an investor; (2) the 3 Defendants would continue to service the mortgage loan on the Property; and (3) the Plaintiff would not benefit from any share nor be liable for any losses but would be reimbursed in full for all his outlays (contributions and expenses) in the investment in the Property upon its sale.

(emphasis in bold)

The black underlined words were included following the plaintiff’s first round of amendments on 10 March 2021. The red underlined words were included following the plaintiff’s oral amendment application before me.

44     Notwithstanding, I did not think that allowing the amendment and proceeding on the plaintiff’s proposed interpretation would prejudice the 1st defendant. During his oral application, the plaintiff’s counsel explained that the amendments were intended to mean that the alleged Exit Agreement was “by writing that’s confirmed by all the three defendants’ [sic] subsequent conduct”.[note: 39] And having heard this explanation, the 1st defendant did not object to the amendment. It was on this basis that I allowed the proposed amendment.

45     I turn to consider the plaintiff’s alternative case.

Preliminary point: Distinction between accepting that the plaintiff exited the joint venture and entering into the alleged Exit Agreement for him to do so

46     I start with a preliminary point. As I discussed with counsel during oral reply submissions, there was to my mind a distinction between:

(a)     the 1st defendant accepting that the plaintiff had left the joint venture; and

(b)     the 1st defendant entering into the alleged Exit Agreement with the plaintiff for him to do so.

47     The former results in a simple cutting of ties.

48     The latter, however, results in the 1st defendant incurring additional contractual obligations. This is illustrated from the reliefs the plaintiff sought. Thus, while it might be possible for the plaintiff to sue for damages and an indemnity pursuant to clause 11.1 of the written JV Agreement (a point that the plaintiff did not advance and which I did not decide), he could not for the S$27,309.25 refund. The JV Agreement has no provision to support this claim. Thus, this claim is possible only if the 1st defendant was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement. And indeed, as the plaintiff’s counsel confirmed, the plaintiff’s entire case was premised on the 1st defendant being a party to the alleged Exit Agreement (see [31] above).

49     In my judgment, however, the most that the plaintiff could establish was that the 1st defendant accepted that the plaintiff had left the joint venture. The evidence he relied on did not prove that the 1st defendant entered the alleged Exit Agreement. Under such circumstances, I was not prepared to hold her liable for contractual obligations which I did not think she agreed to. I explain.

Merits of the plaintiff’s alternative case

50     Broadly, the plaintiff submitted that the 1st defendant was party to the alleged Exit Agreement because: (1) she did not object to the 2nd defendant’s email of 4 December 2017,[note: 40] (2) she agreed during her cross examination that she had allowed the plaintiff to leave the joint venture,[note: 41] and (3) her subsequent conduct and correspondence were consistent with the plaintiff having left the joint venture.[note: 42]

51     I was unable to accept these submissions.

The 4 December 2017 email was neither an agreement nor an offer capable of acceptance

52     I start with the 4 December 2017 email. This email was sent by the 2nd defendant to confirm the contents of the plaintiff’s 27 November 2017 email. The plaintiff’s email, in turn, was sent to the 1st defendant to explain why he would not be topping up the HSBC Account. What is notable about both emails is that, firstly, neither could be construed as an offer capable of acceptance. And secondly, neither purported to create a fresh agreement. Instead, both emails appeared to simply confirm the terms of a pre-existing agreement between the plaintiff and the 2nd defendant for the plaintiff to leave the joint venture. I reproduce these emails below:

Plaintiff’s 27 November 2017 email [note: 43]

Hi Vanny

I refer to your SMS on topping up the account.

Let us recollect the series of events that had taken place over the past years.

I had agreed with Jerome [i.e. the 2nd defendant] that the best option is to let the 3 years pass and then to look for a buyer. Not to add any more costs to the project.

So I told him I will not pursue the issue of removing my name from the partnership nor to get a POA letter done. This is to save legal costs for everyone as pointed out by Jerome [i.e. the 2nd defendant].

I also agreed I will not get my investment sum back till the unit is sold.

But I am already out of this partnership from that point. I will get back whatever I had put in until that point of time.

So whatever fresh fund top-up will be only from three of you.

I hope this clarifies the matter.

Regards

Choong

(emphasis mine)

2nd defendant’s 4 December 2017 email [note: 44]

Dear Choong

Thank you for the email.

This is to confirmed that you will not be liable for any losses or have any share if there is a profit from the sale of this property. However, you will receive whatever money you have invested in this property when the property is sold.

We have already put the property up for sale, we just need to wait for a suitable buyer.

In the meantime, we will continue to service the loan

Cheers

Jerome Tan

Plaintiff’s 5 December 2017 email [note: 45]

Hi Jerome

Thank you for your reply on this.

I agree to your confirmation on the latest state of the partnership.

Best wishes

Regards Choong

53     This is crucial. Assuming my read of the emails is correct, it was difficult to see why the 1st defendant would become a party to a pre-existing agreement between the plaintiff and the 2nd defendant simply because she did not voice her objections when told about it subsequently.

54     Now, I accept that there are situations where silence may be considered as acceptance of an offer. However, these situations are the exception rather than the norm. The general rule is that silence per se is equivocal and does not amount to consent: Midlink Development Pte Ltd v The Stansfield Group Pte Ltd [2004] 4 SLR(R) 258 at [50]-[52]. In the present case, however, the plaintiff was seeking to go even further. He was, in effect, submitting that the 1st defendant should be considered a counterparty to a pre-existing contract simply because she did not object to it after being told about it. This was a proposition for which I needed authority to accept. However, none was furnished.

55     In any event, even if the 2nd defendant’s 4 December 2017 email could be construed as an offer, it appeared from the face of the plaintiff’s response on 5 December 2017 that any agreement was reached between himself and 2nd defendant only (see [52]). Indeed, as noted, this was also the plaintiff’s evidence also (see [35] above).

56     This brings me to the plaintiff’s submission during oral reply submissions that the 2nd defendant had, through the 4 December 2017 email, agreed to the alleged Exit Agreement on behalf of the 1st defendant. This submission was completely unmeritorious. First, nowhere in the pleadings or evidence was it even suggested that the 2nd defendant had the authority (whether actual or ostensible) to enter into agreements for the 1st defendant. Second, nowhere on the face of the 4 December 2017 email was it stated that the 2nd defendant was doing so. Indeed, the point was not even advanced in the plaintiff’s written closing submissions. Instead, it was a response to my pointing out that the plaintiff had admitted that he did not enter into a written agreement with the 1st defendant (see [39] above). Quite clearly, it was an afterthought.

The 1st defendant did not admit being party to the alleged Exit Agreement

57     I turn to the admissions by the 1st defendant which the plaintiff relied on.

58     I accept that the 1st defendant had admitted during cross examination that the plaintiff had left the joint venture. However, that is quite different from admitting that she was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement.

59     In fact, the 1st defendant’s “admissions” were to the contrary. Her evidence was that the plaintiff had left pursuant to his agreement with the 2nd defendant. This is evident from the portions of the transcript which the plaintiff reproduced in his closing submissions.[note: 46] I highlight two in particular:[note: 47]

NE (5 Jul 2022) p.121, ln 17-24

Q:    Okay. You agree with my earlier question that this also signify---indicates that P is already out of the JV? I’m not saying out of the bank loan, right? Bank loan is, can never get out. But he’s out of the JV. With all this proposal---

A:    He’s out from the JV---

Q:    ---(indistinct) the POA, correct?

The 1st defendant’s conduct did not show that she agreed the alleged Exit Agreement

60     I turn to the 1st defendant’s conduct which the plaintiff relied on. The plaintiff’s main point here was that the 1st defendant had acted as if the plaintiff had left the joint venture. In this respect, the plaintiff emphasized that the 1st defendant did not object to the 4 December 2017 email or demand/require the plaintiff to make any further contributions until 27 May 2019. In his closing submissions, the plaintiff highlighted three sets of correspondence:

(a)     Mr Kwan’s email of 26 July 2019 where he: (a) stated that the plaintiff “left the investment on December 2017” and (b) attached a pdf document which provided that the 1st and 3rd defendants were each responsible for 25% of the investment (as opposed to the initial 16.67%) (see [19] above).[note: 48]

(b)     The 1st defendant’s email of 20 January 2020 wherein she asked the plaintiff “Can u want to take up [the 2nd defendant’s] share? It is quite heavier for me and [the 3rd defendant] for 2 shares.”[note: 49] This, the plaintiff submitted could only have been an invitation for the plaintiff to return to the joint venture to help.[note: 50]

(c)     The 1st defendant’s WhatsApp message of 11 July 2020 to the plaintiff and the 3rd defendant. In that message, the 1st defendant referred to HSBC’s demand for payment of RM1,699.00, and stated “our half will [be] RM850”.[note: 51] In this respect, the plaintiff submitted that “our” referred to the 1st and 3rd defendant.[note: 52]

61     Perhaps.

62     However, at the end of the day, it was insufficient for the plaintiff to prove that the 1st defendant knew of the alleged Exit Agreement. It was also insufficient for him to prove that the 1st defendant knew of, consented to, or acquiesced to his departure. As stated at [46]-[48], what the plaintiff needed to prove was that the 1st defendant was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement.

63     And this is where his case fell short. In the final analysis, the evidence did not permit me to make such a finding. Quite apart the matters at [52] above, the evidence (at the most) only permitted me to accept the plaintiff’s case that the alleged Exit Agreement existed insofar as the 2nd defendant was concerned. It did not permit me to go further to find that the 1st defendant was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement also. I explain.

64     First, it was not the plaintiff’s case that the above correspondence and conduct constituted acceptance of an offer. His point was that these correspondence supported his position that the 1st defendant had agreed to his departure from the joint venture.

(a)     In his AEIC, he relied on Mr Kwan’s email ([60(a)) above]) and the 1st defendant’s 11 July 2020 WhatsApp message ([60(c)] above) as proof that the 1st defendant “had agreed to or should absorb my share of the [JV Agreement] after my exit”.[note: 53]

(b)     In a similar vein, the plaintiff stated in his AEIC that the 1st defendant’s 20 January 2020 WhatsApp message ([60(b)] above) indicated that the 1st defendant’s “knowledge of, consent and/or acquiescence to [his] previous exit from the [joint venture] under the [alleged Exit Agreement].”

65     However, while the above correspondence was indicative of 1st defendant accepting the plaintiff’s departure from the joint venture, none of them stated that this was because the 1st defendant was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement.

66     Next, the plaintiff’s reliance on the 1st defendant’s silence and failure to chase for payment did not assist him. Such conduct was equally consistent with the 1st defendant simply accepting that the plaintiff was no longer part of the joint venture, and perhaps, that she was prepared to absorb the plaintiff’s share of the joint venture. However, it does not prove that she was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement.

67     In fact, the possibility that the alleged Exit Agreement was entered only with the 2nd defendant was more consistent with text the 4 December 2017 email (see [52] above). This possibility also appeared to be the plaintiff’s own understanding of the alleged Exit Agreement :[note: 54]

68     Under these circumstances, I could not find on a balance of probabilities that the 1st defendant was a party to the alleged Exit Agreement. Indeed, the plaintiff’s own testimony indicated that he had left the joint venture having secured only the 2nd defendant’s consent to the terms of the alleged Exit Agreement. If so, there was no reason find that the 1st defendant to be a party to the alleged Exit Agreement or to foist contractual obligations onto her just because she did not object to that agreement.

69     In coming to this decision, I am aware that the 1st defendant’s pleaded defence was that she did not accept the plaintiff’s departure from the joint venture. I am also aware that the contemporaneous documents and the 1st defendant’s own testimony contradicted this. However, at the end of the day, the plaintiff must prove his case on a balance of probabilities. And to discharge this burden, it is not sufficient for him to debunk the 1st defendant’s pleaded defence or prove that his case is a “better explanation” for certain events than the 1st defendant’s. The relevant principles are set out in Tan Chin Hock v Teo Cher Koon and another and another appeal [2022] 2 SLR 314 at [31]:

31    … A plaintiff in a civil suit must prove his case on the balance of probabilities. A plaintiff proves his case ‘on the balance of probabilities’ when he shows that his case is more probably true than not true: Clarke Beryl Claire (personal representative of the estate of Eugene Francis Clarke, deceased) and others v SilkAir (Singapore) Pte Ltd [2002] 1 SLR(R) 1136 at [58]. He does not meet this burden by showing his case is a “better explanation” for certain events than the defendant’s. This means that a trier of fact is not bound to prefer one of the parties’ assertions. A third alternative is available where the state of the evidence is unsatisfactory: the judge may simply find that the plaintiff has failed to discharge his burden: Wee Yue Chew v Su Sh-Hsyu [2008] 3 SLR(R) 212 at [8] citing Popi M [1985] 1 WLR 948.

(emphasis mine)

Conclusion

70     For the reasons above, I find that the plaintiff failed to prove his case on a balance of probabilities. The only cause of action he advanced against the 1st defendant was breach of an alleged Exit Agreement. However, he could not prove that the 1st defendant was party to it.

71     His original case—that he had reached an oral Exit Agreement with the 1st defendant—was undermined by his own testimony that he did not reach any such oral agreement with the 1st defendant.

72     His alternative case—that the 1st defendant and him had entered a written Exit Agreement which was confirmed by conduct—was not supported by the evidence. The email which he relied on to establish the written contract did not appear to be an agreement to begin with. On its face and in the relevant context, it was simply an email which reiterated the terms of a prior agreement reached with the 2nd defendant. And even if I accepted that the email was an offer which could be accepted through conduct, the conduct and the supposed admissions which the plaintiff relied on did not go far enough. At the most, they showed that the 1st defendant accepted the plaintiff’s departure from the joint venture. But they did not prove that the 1st defendant had agreed to the alleged Exit Agreement. And this was crucial to the plaintiff’s claim.

73     Accordingly, the plaintiff’s claim against the 1st defendant is dismissed.

74     Having heard the parties’ submissions on costs and taken into account the wasted trial day caused by the 1st defendant, the quantum of the plaintiff’s claim, the complexity of this matter, as well as the documents placed before me, I fixed costs at S$15,000 plus reasonable disbursements and GST to be paid by the plaintiff to the 1st defendant.
