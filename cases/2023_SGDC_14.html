Introduction

1       The accused (the “Accused”) was convicted of 169 charges after a trial (the “Trial”) and sentenced to a global term of 27 months’ imprisonment (the “Sentence”). Being dissatisfied with the decision of the court, the Accused had filed an appeal against her conviction and sentence (the “Appeal”).

2       At the hearing of the Appeal, the High Court directed the limited remittal of this case for the purpose of admitting two statements of the 77-year-old[note: 1] victim, Mr Tan Soy Kiang (the “Victim”), dated 5 February 2015[note: 2] and 23 February 2016[note: 3] (the “2015 Statement” and the “2016 Statement”, respectively, and, collectively, the “Statements”) and for the Victim to be cross-examined on the inconsistencies between the Statements and his oral testimony at the Trial.[note: 4]

3       The purported inconsistent parts of the Statements were marked up by the Defence on the Statements:

Statement

The 2015 Statement[note: 5]

Q What do you know about the facts of this case?

A … For my CPF account, I did not pass any amount to Ah Hiang [ie. Mdm Boo Sok Hiang], but got lend S$300/- to Ah Tan [ie. the Accused] to help her. The other amount from my CPF account that was withdrawn I all spend or lend to other friends already.

(Hereinafter, referred to as the “2015 Underlined Text”.)

The 2016 Statement[note: 6]

Q13 Did anyone mention to you that the money you had given to Mdm Tan [ie. the Accused] or Ah Hiang is lawyer fees you were paying to the late Mr Lee Kuan Yew or Mr Lee Hsien Loong?

A13 No. I only know the money I was giving to Mdm Tan or Ah Hiang is for lawyer fees to help Mdm Tan settle her joint account with her husband. I do not know if she engaged a lawyer or not. I just believed them.

(Hereinafter, referred to as the “2016 Underlined Text”.)

6       The Victim did not appear to be malingering. He came across as an earnest and genuine witness although the challenges that he faced in recollection and reasoning were apparent through his weathered face. I was mindful that a medical report dated 18 May 2015 (the “2015 IMH Report”)[note: 8] by Dr San San Thinn, Associate Consultant from the Institute of Mental Health (the “IMH”), had earlier diagnosed the Victim with mild intellectual disability:[note: 9]

An IQ assessment done by our senior clinical psychologist Ms. Chermain Wong on 8 May 2015 by using Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale-IV (WAIS-IV). [The Victim’s] current cognitive profile indicates that his domains of intellectual functioning are in the extremely low to borderline range. Assessment of his adaptive behaviours further confirms that in terms of his conceptual, social and overall adaptive skills, [the Victim] is currently performing at an extremely low level. [The Victim’s] current presentation is consistent with Mild Intellectual Disability. (Emphasis in original text.)

7       The Prosecution applied for the oral examination of the Victim to be held in abeyance pending assessment by a medical professional as to his fitness to give evidence. The Defence did not object to the application.

8       In support of the application, the Prosecution tendered a memorandum dated 14 September 2021 (the “2021 TTSH Memo”) by Dr Faisal Johandi (“Dr Faisal”),[note: 10] a medical officer from Tan Tock Seng Hospital, which stated the following diagnosis of the Victim’s mental condition:[note: 11]

…

This admission, patient has been Newly diagnosed with mixed dementia (alzehimer’s with stroke disease)

-    8/9/21: AMT 4/10, MMSE 11/30 (limitations in orientation, attention, recall and language)

…

(Emphasis in original text.)

9       Given the new diagnosis of mixed dementia by Dr Faisal, which was not disputed by the Defence, I decided that it would be prudent for an appropriate medical practitioner to ascertain the extent to which the Victim’s fitness to give evidence had been impaired, if at all. As such, I stood down the oral examination of the Victim and ordered the remittal hearing to continue with other witnesses who were involved in the recording of the Statements.

10     On 13 June 2022, the Prosecution tendered a detailed medical report on the Victim dated 14 April 2022 (the “2022 IMH Report”) by Dr Daw San San Thinn (“Dr Daw”), Consultant Psychiatrist from the IMH,[note: 12] which stated the following:

Purpose of report:

1 a.[The Victim] has been referred to the Institute of Mental Health (IMH) on 16 Dec 2021 for a forensic psychiatric evaluation to assess:

i.Whether [the Victim] is suffering from any mental condition, and if so, the severity of the condition and its symptoms. In particular, whether IMH agrees with TTSH’s diagnosis of mixed dementia as stated in the attached TTSH medical report.

ii.Whether [the Victim] is medically fit to give reliable evidence in Court as a witness, and if yes, whether there are any limitations to the extent to which he can give evidence. In this connection, [the Victim] is required at the remittal hearing to explain in Court certain inconsistencies in two of his witness statements and his Court testimony.

…

To answer your queries:

26 a.     Whether [the Victim] is suffering from any mental condition, and if so, the severity of the condition and its symptoms. In particular, whether IMH agrees with TTSH’s diagnosis of mixed dementia as stated in the attached TTSH medical report.

From corroborative history from his helper and his niece revealed that his cognitive function including memory, attention, language, perceptual-motor and executive functioning has declined since 2020. His cognitive function has deteriorated after two falls in Jul and Sep 2021 and it interfered his independence in daily activities. He requires assistance in toileting and Instrumental Activities of Daily Livings.

MRI Brain imaging on 8 Sep 2021 showed multiple old lacunar infarcts, multiple scattered old micro-hemorrhages (sic) and severe chronic microvascular ischemic changes.

He was diagnosed with Major Vascular Neurocognitive Disorder (Vascular Dementia) with Behaviour and Psychological Symptoms with Dementia (BPSD). He is suffering from moderate severity, as he needs assistance in toileting (one of bADL). …

26 b.     Whether [the Victim] is medically fit to give reliable evidence in Court as a witness, and if yes, whether there are any limitations to the extent to which he can give evidence. In this connection, [the Victim] is required at the remittal hearing to explain in Court certain inconsistencies in two of his witness statements and his Court testimony.

Regarding his mental capacity to give reliable evidence in Court as a witness, he was not able to give details about court hearing and he did not remember the details about the case, including his witness statements and his Court testimony. Hence, he is mentally incapable to explain in Court certain inconsistencies in his witness statements and his Court testimony.

Opinions and Recommendations

27     I am of the opinion that:

a)[The Victim] is suffering from Major Vascular Neurocognitive Disorder (Vascular Dementia) with Behavior and Psychological Symptoms with Dementia (BPSD), moderate severity.

b)He is currently unfit to give reliable evidence in Court as a witness.

(Emphasis in original text.)

11     I was mindful that Dr Daw’s examination of the Victim was not based solely on the information provided by the Victim’s helper and niece. Dr Daw had also studied an “MRI Brain imaging” conducted on 8 September 2021[note: 13] and had independently examined the victim. Dr Daw had observed:[note: 14]

[The Victim] was not orientated to time, place and person. He had poor attention and significant poor short term memory. He replied “I don’t remember” to most of the questions.

He scored 11 our of 29 in Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE). His recall memory on 3 items was zero.

12     The 2022 IMH Report was explained to the Accused, who was unrepresented on 13 June 2022, and the Accused did not dispute the contents.

13     Given that the Victim was medically certified to be unfit to give reliable evidence in court as a witness, I allowed the Prosecution’s application for the Victim to be released as a witness for the remittal hearing.

THE RECORDING OF THE 2015 STATEMENT

14     Mr Chin was not directly involved in the investigations into the Accused but had been called upon to help with the recording of the 2015 Statement. Mr Chin had enlisted the assistance of Mr Ang as Mandarin, Hokkien and Teochew interpreter since the Victim spoke in a mixture of these three languages, [note: 15] and had started recording the 2015 Statement in open-ended format and finished in more specific question-and-answer format.[note: 16]

15     After the 2015 Statement was recorded, it was read back to the Victim in a mixture of Mandarin, Hokkien and Teochew, and the Victim was invited to make amendments which he declined to do so.[note: 17] Mr Chin testified that the Victim, Mr Chin and Mr Ang signed on the 2015 Statement after it was recorded.[note: 18]

16     During examination-in-chief, the Prosecution pointed out certain non-material amendments made to the 2015 Statement. Mr Chin testified that these were clerical corrections made by him during the recording process and not as a result of the Victim’s request.[note: 19]

17     Mr Chin observed that the Victim was slow to respond to questions during the recording of the 2015 Statement but came across as earnest albeit somewhat incoherent and inconsistent in some of his responses. Nonetheless, Mr Chin could tell that the Victim understood the questions which were posed to him.[note: 20]

18     The Defence’s cross-examination of Mr Chin was largely confined to the clarification of Mr Chin’s evidence during examination-in-chief.[note: 21]

19     Mr Ang’s evidence during examination-in-chief was consistent with Mr Chin’s evidence. According to Mr Ang, the Victim appeared fit to give his statement and understood the question asked of him even though he looked tired. The Victim did not appear unwell.[note: 22]

20     The Defence’s brief cross-examination of Mr Ang was confined to ascertaining where the 2015 Statement was recorded, which was not in dispute.[note: 23]

THE RECORDING OF THE 2016 STATEMENT

21     Mr Manoj had recorded the 2016 Statement in question-and-answer format, with the assistance of Mr Loh as Hokkien interpreter and appropriate adult:[note: 24]

Mr Manoj

So, my process of recording a statement from [the Victim] was, uh, based on a question and answer format. I would pose him the ques---I would, uh, say the question and then, uh, Mr David Loh would, uh, assist to interpret my question to him in Hokkien. Once he understood it---to---to ensure that he understands the question, then [the Victim] would, uh, respond and give his answer in Hokkien. And thereafter, um, Mr David would, uh, translate [the Victim’s] answer to me in English, then I would type it out, and then after that, read that same answer back to Mr David, then he would, uh, reconfirm if it was accurately captured, uh, with a---he would reconfirm it with [the Victim]. And then I would move on to the next question.



22     Mr Manoj had also allowed the Victim the opportunity to make amendments to the 2016 Statement but the Victim had declined to do so. According to Mr Manoj, the Victim did not raise any concerns about what was recorded in the 2016 Statement and appeared to understand what was asked of him.[note: 25] After the 2016 Statement was recorded, the Victim, Mr Manoj and Mr Loh signed on it.[note: 26]

23     Mr Loh’s testimony during examination-in-chief was consistent with Mr Manoj’s evidence. Mr Loh also testified that the Victim came across as physically and mentally normal during the recording of the 2016 Statement.[note: 27]

24     The Defence had no cross-examination for Mr Manoj and Mr Loh.

THE DEFENCE’S SUBMISSIONS

25     In the Defence’s submissions for the remittal hearing (the “Defence Submissions 2”),[note: 28] the Defence averred that the 2015 Underlined Text was inconsistent with the following oral testimony of the Victim:[note: 29]

Q

And, Mr. Tan, if you knew that Mdm. Tan didn’t actually need to pay or to give money to Lee Kuan Yew, would you have withdrawn your CPF savings?

A

No need to.



26     In the Defence Submissions 2,[note: 30] the Defence further raised issues with other parts of the 2015 Statement which were not marked-up or drawn to the Court’s or Prosecution’s attention during the remittal hearing (the “2015 Additional Text”):[note: 31]

Question: What do you know about the facts of this case?

…

Q1) Why are you giving money to Ah Tan through Ah Hiang?

(Emphasis in original text.)



27     The following sets out in extenso the Defence’s elaborate submissions in relation to the 2015 Underlined Text and the 2015 Additional Text:[note: 32]

5.    … the false representations that payments were needed for LKY were a far cry; the manifestations of such representations were probably a figment or a contrived summary of [the Victim’s] Statements as to the response to the question, “Q2) What does this have to do with Lee Kuan Yew or Lee Hsien Loong?” clarified so far as material that “A2) [The Accused] said that she went to Lee Kuan Yew’s office to get help. She paying legal fees, Out of pity, I help as she(sic) no money to draw from her account …” further underscoring that the false representations – that monies were needed for LKY – which formed the gravamen of the charge was never forthcoming; what had transpired between the parties – PW16, Appellant & the Deceased – at all material times before the descend of the others – niece, husband, reporters and even police statements of the Appellant (P22) and Deceased (P9 & P10) – were clear; the desire to help was voluntary, not due, cause or arose out of misrepresentations from the Appellant or even the Deceased.

6.    It may be said that since PW16’s 1st Statement was recorded on 5th February 2015 rightly or wrongly, “IO Peh” had thought that this was a case where PW16 had “been paying a lot of money to two ladies who had claimed that the Victim owed money to Mr Lee Kuan Yew (“Mr Lee”).

7.    If so, it appears that the Police have put the Cart before the Horse, that is before getting the Prosecution’s case or House in Order they had decided to apprehend the Accused and the Deceased on 5th February 2015 to record statements which the Trial Court perceptively observed that the Prosecution’s case revealed the background as one where “…The Victim had been paying a lot of money to two ladies who had claimed that the Victim owed money to Mr. Lee Kuan Yew. (Mr. Lee)” when the actual case was on an entirely different footing.

(Emphasis in original text.)

28     It could be discerned that the Defence’s arguments in relation to the 2015 Statement concerned MAC-904775-2017 (the “CPF Charge”) and comprised two main prongs:

(a)     that the Victim’s desire to help the Accused and Mdm Boo Sok Hiang (“Mdm Boo”) was voluntary and not a result of any misrepresentations; and

(b)     that the police had proceeded against the Accused with a pre-conceived notion of her complicity in the deception involving the use of Mr Lee Kuan Yew’s (“Mr Lee”) name.

29     The 2016 Underlined Text concerned the remaining 168 charges, DAC-918286-2017 to DAC-918453-2017 (the “Non-CPF Charges”). In the Defence Submissions 2, the Defence had also referred to the following parts of the 2016 Statement which were not marked-up or drawn to the Court’s or Prosecution’s attention during the remittal hearing (the “2016 Additional Text”):[note: 33]

Q2)

Are you able to tell me who cheated you?

A2)

Mdm Tan cheated me.

Q3)

… how Mdm Tan cheated you…

A3)

Mdm Tan told me that she had opened a joint account with her husband. She was not able to withdraw the money from the bank because her husband did not turn up at the bank to sign the documents for withdrawal of the money. Mdm Tan then told me she needed money to engage lawyer. Ah Hiang then told me to help Mdm Tan and give her some money first to engage a lawyer. I decided to help and so I passed my salary to Ah Hiang. I think Ah Hiang passed the money to Mdm Tan. The reason why I passed the money to Ah Hiang is because I am busy working and she is able to collect from me to pass to Mdm Tan.

…

Q4)

So how were you cheated by Mdm Tan?

A4)

I feel that I was cheated because Mdm Tan told me that once she gets a lawyer and can withdraw the money from her joint account with her husband, she will pass me S$100,000/-.



30     The gravamen of the Defence’s submissions in relation to the 2016 Statement and the Non-CPF Charges was that “representations were also made that payments were needed for LKY but nowhere did [the Victim] despite being questioned twice when his metal faculty were intact mention, complaint and or protest that he parted monies because he was told that payments were needed for LKY”.[note: 34] (Emphasis in original text.)

31     The Defence also claimed that, “[o]n the whole, [the Accused’s] statements, recorded as early as 5th February 2015 & 23rd February 2016, have afforded a completely different perspective or reason from the Prosecution’s case theory that [the Victim] was deceived by a lie that payments were needed for MM Lee”.[note: 35]

THE PROSECUTION’S SUBMISSIONS

32     The gist of the Prosecution’s submissions was that the Statements should not affect the court’s decision to convict the Accused for the following reasons:

(a)     The inconsistencies might have arisen from the Victim’s mental disability.

(b)     There was a wealth of objective evidence that corroborated the Victim’s oral testimony.

(c)     The Accused’s defences at the Trial were illogical and she was a disingenuous witness.

(d)     The Statements must be considered in totality as parts of the Statements supported the conviction.

ASSESSMENT OF THE EVIDENCE

33     Based on the testimonies of Mr Chin, Mr Manoj and their respective interpreters, the Victim clearly understood the purpose of the Statements and the questions asked of him. There was no evidence of any impropriety in the manner in which the Statements were recorded.

34     However, Mr Chin testified that, even though the Victim came across as earnest during the recording of the 2015 Statement, he was slow to respond to questions and his responses were somewhat incoherent and inconsistent. Mr Manoj had similarly observed that the Victim was “a bit slow” and “require[d] some assistance” during the recording of the 2016 Statement.[note: 36]

35     Additionally, the Statements must be viewed in the context of the Victim’s advanced age and the 2015 IMH Report that was prepared around the time that the 2015 Statement was recorded and before the 2016 Statement were recorded, and that diagnosed the Victim with mild intellectual disability.

EFFECT OF THE STATEMENTS ON THE COURT’S ORIGINAL FINDINGS AND DECISION

36     To prove the CPF Charge, the Prosecution had to establish the following elements:

(a)     The Accused had deceived the Victim into believing payments were needed for Mr Lee;

(b)     By such manner of deception, the Accused had induced the Victim to withdraw the CPF Monies, which the Victim would not have done if he had not been so deceived; and

(c)     The withdrawal of the CPF Monies caused or was likely to cause damage to the Victim’s property.

37     At the end of the Trial, the court found that the Prosecution had proved these elements beyond reasonable doubt. In this vein, the Defence’s submission that the Victim’s desire to help the Accused and Mdm Boo was voluntary was disingenuous since the court had found that false representations had been made to the Victim.

38     The Defence’s further allegation that the police had proceeded against the Accused based on a pre-conceived notion of her complicity was not supported by the evidence or put to any of the police officers who testified at the remittal hearing.

39     More pertinently, the 2015 Underlined Text related to a tangential issue, ie. who received the CPF Monies, rather than the elements of the CPF Charge. The purport of the 2015 Underlined Text, ie. that the Victim did not pass any amount to Mdm Boo, was not inconsistent with the Victim’s evidence at the Trial that the CPF Monies were not paid to anyone directly but deposited into his bank account:[note: 37]

Q

Okay. And a few lines above that, you see that your CPF savings were being credited into a personal bank account number, there is an account number XXX, it’s a POSB bank account.

A

Yes.

Q

Whose bank account is this?

A

Mine.

Q

Now, was there an ATM card that tied to this POSB bank account?

A

Yes.

Q

And at this time, May 1999, when you went to fill up this form, who was holding on to this ATM card?

A

Ah Hiang. Ah Hiang.

Q

Did Ah Hiang have a password to make---the PIN---the password or PIN number to make withdrawals using the ATM card?

A

I---I have no time, so I will get her to withdraw money to me. Because I need to go to work, I don’t have time. She knows about it. I gave it to her.



40     Even though Mdm Boo had access to the Victim’s bank account since she was holding on to the Victim’s automated teller machine (“ATM”) card, the court found during the Trial that the evidence was inconclusive as to the amount of the CPF Monies that were paid to the Accused eventually.[note: 38]

41     In sentencing the Accused for the CPF Charge, the Court had also agreed with the Defence that there was no evidence that the Accused had benefitted, in part or in full, from the Victim’s withdrawal of the CPF Monies.[note: 39]

42     Next, the court found that the Defence’s assertion of inconsistencies in the 2016 Underlined Text in relation to the Non-CPF Charges was misconceived.

43     First, Mr Lee had clearly featured in the deception suffered upon the Victim, who stated this in the 2015 Statement:

…

So over 10 years ago, I got to know that [the Accused] has a POSB joint bank account with her husband. Their joint account requires joint signatory. Approximately 10 years ago, [the Accused] had relationship problems with her husband who refused to sign his portion of the signatory whenever [the Accused] wishes to make a withdrawal. As such, Ah Tan engaged legal help. Ah Tan even went to MP who advised her to seek legal help from Lee Hsien Loong and Lee Kuan Yew. As such, [the Accused] needed to pay legal fees to help fight her case. I volunteered to help Ah Tan out of sympathy. …

…

Q1)    Why are you giving money to [the Accused] through [Mdm Boo]?

A2)    It is just to help [the Accused] for the legal letter. She need how much I pay how much.

Q2)    What does this have to do with Lee Kuan Yew or Lee Hsien Loong?

A2)     Because MP recommended to [the Accused] to go to Lee Kuan Yew. [The Accused] said that she went to Lee Kuan Yew office to get help. She paying legal fees. Out of pity, I help as she no money to draw money from her account. Since Lee Kuan Yew step down, Lee Hsien Loong take over.

…

Q5)    Why would [the Accused] go to Lee Kuan Yew to get legal help when he is not a lawyer?

A5)     As she was recommended by MP as he is higher authority.

…

(Emphasis added.)

44     Second, the Victim had testified at the Trial on many occasions that he had given money to the Accused so that she could make payments to Mr Lee.[note: 40]

45     Third, even though the precise details of Mr Lee’s purported involvement appeared to be lost on the Victim, this was unsurprising given that the deception started as far back as the year 2000, about 15 years before the Statements were recorded. In fact, the Accused herself had forgotten what she had said exactly about Mr Lee’s involvement when she gave her statement to the police on 5 February 2015[note: 41]:

Q10)  What did you tell [Mdm Boo] about paying money to Mr Lee Kwan Yew?

A10)   I cannot remember what I exactly told her, but I did told her I need to pay Mr Lee Kwan Yew money so he will give me money at a later stage.

(Emphasis added.)

46     What was unequivocal was the Victim’s belief that the Accused needed the money to pay to Mr Lee.

47     Therefore, the Defence’s assertion that “nowhere did [the Victim] despite being questioned twice when his mental faculty were intact mention, complaint and or protest that he parted monies because he was told that payments were needed for LKY”[note: 42] was not supported by the evidence.

CONCLUSION

48     Having considered the fresh evidence in the form of the Statements and after hearing further submissions by the Prosecution and the Defence, I was satisfied that the Defence’s assertions of inconsistencies were unfounded.

49     Furthermore, the conviction of the Accused on all 169 charges was not founded solely on the evidence of the Victim but also on the preponderance of objective evidence that pointed to the Accused’s guilt, for instance:[note: 43]

(a)     The Accused’s 1st Statement;[note: 44]

(b)     Mdm Boo’s 1st Statement and Mdm Boo’s 2nd Statement;[note: 45]

(c)     The TNP New Report and Mr Godwin’s Evidence;[note: 46]

(d)     The evidence of the Victim’s former employers;[note: 47]

(e)     The recordings of the confrontation at the void-deck of a block of HDB flats between the Victim’s niece and Mdm Boo;[note: 48] and

(f)     The recordings of the confrontation at MOS Burger and the Agreement signed by the Accused.[note: 49]

50     Therefore, the court found that the admission of the Statements did not affect the court’s original findings and decisions at Trial, and decided that the conviction of the Accused on all 169 charges, as well as the individual and global sentences of 27 months’ imprisonment, would stand.
