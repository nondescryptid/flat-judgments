Introduction

1       Of the many offences prosecuted in the District Court, there are few which are more heinous or repulsive than those involving child abuse. This category of offences includes the production or distribution of materials depicting the sexual abuse of a child. Beyond the horrific physical and psychological harm suffered by the child in the production of such materials, the child‚Äôs suffering can be indefinitely prolonged when such materials are distributed over the Internet; with many child victims experiencing ‚Äúfear, guilt, shame and betrayal‚Äù whilst ‚Äúconstantly worrying about being recognised by someone who had viewed‚Äù the materials showing their abuse (Coen Teunissen and Sarah Napier, Australian Institute of Criminology, ‚ÄúChild sexual abuse material and end-to-end encryption on social media platforms: An overview‚Äù (No 653, July 2022), at p 1).

2       In 2019, Parliament updated the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) (‚ÄúPenal Code‚Äù) by enacting specific laws against child abuse material (‚ÄúCAM‚Äù), a term defined in section 377C(1) of the Penal Code. Recognising that ‚Äúterrible harm is caused to these children who are used in the production of [child abuse] material‚Äù, Parliament criminalised ‚Äúthe spectrum of offences to deal with every person involved, from consumers to the producers of such material‚Äù (Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (6 May 2019) vol 94 (‚ÄúParliamentary Debates‚Äù) (K Shanmugam, Minister for Home Affairs)). This led to the enactment of provisions such as section 377BI of the Penal Code, which prescribes that an offender who distributes CAM, and who knows or has reason to believe that the material is CAM, shall on conviction be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to 7 years, and shall also be liable to fine or caning.

3       Hindakumbure Charindu Dilshan Rajapaksha (‚Äúthe Accused‚Äù) is the second person prosecuted under section 377BI of the Penal Code. From 16 March 2020 to 24 July 2020, the Accused distributed 17 films containing CAM in a WhatsApp chat group he was one of the administrators of, which had a total of 256 participants when these films were disseminated. As described by the Prosecution, the content of these films ‚Äúscrape the very pits of depravity - they involve children being raped, possibly being trafficked into sex, and in one clip a prepubescent child filmed having sex with a farmyard animal‚Äù.[note: 1] The Accused is also charged for a separate offence under section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code, for having transmitted 943 obscene films (which do not include the 17 films containing CAM) in the same WhatsApp chat group. He pleaded guilty to both charges on 2 March 2023. Having carefully considered parties‚Äô submissions, I now set out my decision on sentence.

9       In relation to the s 292(1)(a) PC offence, the Prosecution assessed the harm to be high given the content of the materials circulated, the extremely wide audience the films were distributed to, and the sheer number of films distributed by the Accused. However, the Prosecution assessed the culpability of the Accused to be lower than the offender in two precedent cases ‚Äì Public Prosecutor v Luo Zhiang [2016] SGDC 115 (‚ÄúLuo Zhiang‚Äù) and Public Prosecutor v Chan Chun Hong [2015] SGDC 125 (‚ÄúChan Chun Hong‚Äù) ‚Äì given that he was not involved in the production of the films nor was he personally seeking out CAM. 10     In relation to the s 377BI PC offence, the Prosecution highlighted that section 377BI of the Penal Code carries 28 times the statutory maximum punishment (7 years‚Äô imprisonment) compared to section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code (3 months‚Äô imprisonment), the provision which such offences were previously prosecuted under.

12     The Prosecution submitted that in the present case, almost every single harm factor is engaged to a significant degree, given that, amongst other things, the Accused had distributed films involving the most ‚Äúrepulsive of [CAM]‚Äù, many of the children in the films were prepubescent and the scale and nature of distribution was extensive.[note: 2] In relation to the Accused‚Äôs culpability, the Prosecution submitted that the facts disclose a ‚Äúsignificant level of consciousness and culpability by the Accused‚Äù, as the Accused ‚Äúknew that these [films] he forwarded involve child pornography given that he had downloaded [them] from WhatsApp groups where such material was rife‚Äù, and the Accused was an administrator of the group he had circulated these materials to.[note: 3]

13     The Prosecution then proceeded to explain that its sentencing position of 9 to 12 months‚Äô imprisonment ‚Äútakes reference from‚Äù[note: 4] the single, unreported case of Public Prosecutor v Tay Yi Wen, Alvin (SC-902766-2022) (‚ÄúAlvin Tay‚Äù), which appears to be the first case where an offender was convicted and sentenced for a section 377BI Penal Code offence. In Alvin Tay, the offender distributed 20 child abuse recordings to a Tumblr user, with the aim of obtaining nude photos from the recipient. He was sentenced to 8 months‚Äô imprisonment.

14     During oral submissions, I requested for the Prosecution to focus on how the appropriate sentence should be calibrated by simply using the sentencing factors highlighted and the full sentencing range prescribed under section 377BI of the Penal Code (ie, from an imprisonment sentence of 1 day to 7 years), as the Prosecution had agreed that Alvin Tay, being an unreported decision, has limited precedential value. The Prosecution reasoned that the harm involved in the present case can be assessed to be between medium to high, while the Accused‚Äôs culpability between low to medium. The Prosecution then submitted that on this approach, an imprisonment sentence of at least 2 years would in fact be justifiable for the s 377BI PC offence.

15     Finally, the Prosecution urged the Court to run both sentences consecutively, as both offences target different legally protected interests. The Prosecution submitted that while section 377BI of the Penal Code is targeted at protecting children from sexual abuse, section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code is targeted at maintaining public decency or public morality.

(a)     The Accused did not make any request to receive and/or trade CAM in exchange for his distribution of the 17 films.

(b)     The Accused did not make any monetary gain from the distribution of the CAM.

(c)     The Accused did not purchase or intentionally procure the CAM for the purposes of distribution. He had simply forwarded them when he received them in other WhatsApp group chats.

(d)     The proportion of CAM the Accused distributed to the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù was only a tiny fraction (ie, 1.7%) of the entire batch of pornographic materials he had forwarded. Each of the 17 films containing CAM had been forwarded in bulk with various other pornographic materials. This shows, in the Defence‚Äôs view, the Accused‚Äôs ‚Äúlow level of proclivity‚Äù towards CAM.[note: 5]

20     Similar to the Prosecution, the Defence relied significantly on Alvin Tay when calibrating the sentence to be imposed in the present case. The Defence submitted that as the severity of the offence in the present case is, in the round, similar to that in Alvin Tay, the Court should impose a similar sentence of 8 months‚Äô imprisonment for the s 377BI PC offence.

21     The Defence then highlighted the personal circumstances of the Accused, submitting that the lengthy period of investigations and the entire court process has been punishing enough on the Accused, as he has not been able to work or continue with his studies while on a student pass. The Defence emphasised that the Accused is deeply remorseful for his actions, and that as a father with a young son of his own, the gravity of the offences has not been lost on the Accused.

22     Finally, the Defence submitted that both sentences should run concurrently in light of the one-transaction rule that offences which form part of a single transaction should generally run concurrently (Public Prosecutor v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 799 at [98(b)]). This is because both offences had taken place in the course of the same transaction, given that:

(a)     They are of the same genre, involving the distribution of obscene materials.

(b)     They were committed within the same time period.

(c)     They were committed in the same manner (ie, by the Accused forwarding the materials in bulk to the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù).

23     The Defence submitted that the exceptions to the application of the one-transaction rule (in Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998 (‚ÄúMohamed Shouffee‚Äù) at [41] and [45]) do not apply. In the Defence‚Äôs view, the s 377BI PC offence ‚Äúfactually constitutes a part of‚Äù the s 292(1)(a) PC offence, given that the 17 films containing CAM are part of the other obscene materials forwarded by the Accused.[note: 6] Furthermore, the gravity of the offences as a whole would be adequately reflected in the imposition of the sentence for the s 377BI PC offence, as that is the more serious offence and can be considered ‚Äúan aggravated form‚Äù of the s 292(1)(a) PC offence.[note: 7] Finally, the Defence also highlighted that in Alvin Tay, the sentence for the s 377BI offence (ie, 8 months‚Äô imprisonment) was ordered to run concurrently with a separate offence under section 377BC of the Penal Code (ie, the distribution of 320 voyeuristic images and recordings), even though the two offences involve ‚Äúdifferent types of obscene material‚Äù (ie, voyeuristic images and recordings on one hand, and CAM on the other).

The Court‚Äôs Decision on Sentence

The sentence for the s 292(1)(a) PC offence

24     Section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code states that whoever transmits by electronic means any obscene object shall be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to 3 months, or with fine, or with both. It has been explained that the purpose of a provision like section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code is to protect or shield the public from moral corruption, in particular those who are ‚Äúmost clearly at risk [such as the] very young or the psychologically disturbed‚Äù (Sentencing Practice in the Subordinate Courts (LexisNexis, 3rd Ed, 2013) (‚ÄúSentencing Practice‚Äù) at p 619, which was cited with approval in Chan Chun Hong at [29]). Both the Prosecution and the Defence calibrated their proposed sentences for the s 292(1)(a) PC offence by relying on the reported decisions in Luo Zhiang and Chan Chun Hong, which I shall now address.

25     In Luo Zhiang, the offender pleaded guilty to one charge under section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code for uploading, to a pornographic website, a video he had taken of his female colleague changing. The victim‚Äôs face was visible and it showed her clad in her undergarments. The offender was sentenced to 2 months‚Äô imprisonment for the offence under section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code, on top of the separate sentences he had received for the act of taking photographs and videos of his colleagues changing. When calibrating the sentence for the section 292(1)(a) Penal Code offence, the Court reasoned as follows (Luo Zhiang at [32]):

The factors which influence the sentence include the degree and type of obscenity together with the form in which it is presented; the scale; and to whom the publication was made to. I noted that the video clip was uploaded onto a pornographic site and the victim‚Äôs face was visible. However, there was no extreme obscenity to warrant a sentence that was two weeks‚Äô short of the maximum punishment. Accordingly I determined a sentence of eight weeks‚Äô imprisonment to be appropriate.

26     In Chan Chun Hong, the offender had registered an account on various online platforms for the purpose of transmitting CAM to and receiving CAM from other users. He then sent seven different emails (to seven individual email addresses) transmitting between 12 to 50 photos/videos containing CAM in each email, for the purposes of trading pictures and videos of girls below 10 or 12 years old. The Accused pleaded guilty to seven charges under section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code. In reaching the decision to impose a sentence of 2 months‚Äô imprisonment for each of the section 292(1)(a) Penal Code charge, the Court considered the egregious content of the photos/videos transmitted, the fact that the offender had made no commercial gain, the images were not distributed to the public at large, and that the bulk of the offences were committed when the offender was suffering from depression (Chan Chun Hong at [31]).

27     In the present case, the Accused had transmitted, to an extremely large group of 256 individuals each time, highly obscene materials such as films which depict rape, ‚Äúbondage, discipline, sadism and masochism‚Äù (‚ÄúBDSM‚Äù) sexual activity, and bestiality.[note: 8] Crucially, the quantity of obscene films transmitted (943 films) as stated in the single section 292(1)(a) Penal Code charge far exceeds the number of obscene materials the offenders in Luo Zhiang and Chan Chun Hong had transmitted. While the Defence recognised that these are highly aggravating factors, it submitted that these factors are counter-balanced by the following differences between the Accused‚Äôs case and the reported precedents:

(a)     Unlike in Luo Zhiang, the Accused was not in anyway involved in the making or producing of the obscene films. He had merely forwarded what he had received in bulk to the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù.

(b)     Unlike in Chan Chun Hong, the 943 obscene films the Accused is charged for transmitting do not contain CAM, and he also had not actively requested for obscene materials in exchange.

28     In my view, an imprisonment sentence of 2 months for the Accused‚Äôs s 292(1)(a) PC offence similar to that imposed in Luo Zhiang and Chan Chun Hong will not adequately reflect the severity of the Accused‚Äôs offence, given the following distinguishing factors:

(a)     In Luo Zhiang, the offender was separately punished for his act of recording his victims, and the 2 months‚Äô imprisonment he had received for his section 292(1)(a) Penal Code offence was for the uploading of one film to a pornographic website. An uplift of the sentence to be imposed in the present case is warranted given the sheer number (943) of obscene films transmitted by the Accused, many of which contain materials which are far more objectionable than what was transmitted by the offender in Luo Zhiang.

(b)     While the Accused did not expressly request for obscene materials or CAM in exchange unlike the offender in Chan Chun Hong, I do not think a clear distinction in culpability exists in this regard when one considers that the Accused had committed the s 292(1)(a) PC offence as the administrator of the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù, which by 6 August 2020, had ‚Äúover 6,000 obscene videos and files‚Äù being circulated.[note: 9] While the Accused might not have expressly requested for obscene materials in exchange, the factual context suggests that there was no need for him to do so. He was an ‚Äúactive participant‚Äù[note: 10] in the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù which was essentially a platform where obscene materials were actively traded in large volumes. By his actions, the Accused had facilitated and encouraged the exchange of obscene materials far beyond the scale and reach of that obtained by the offender in Chan Chun Hong.

29     For these reasons, I agree with the Prosecution that the sentence to be imposed for the s 292(1)(a) PC offence should be near the maximum prescribed. As the Accused did not make any monetary gain from the transmission of these obscene materials, I am of the view that an imprisonment sentence of 2 months and 2 weeks for the s 292(1)(a) PC offence is sufficient and justified.

The sentence for the s 377BI PC offence

The background to section 377BI of the Penal Code

30     Section 377BI of the Penal Code came into force on 1 January 2020, and its genesis can be traced to the Penal Code Review Committee (‚ÄúPCRC‚Äù) August 2018 report (‚ÄúPCRC Report‚Äù). In the PCRC Report, the PCRC noted that there were ‚Äúno specific offences involving child pornography, and a current patchwork of laws exist to address offences involving pornography‚Äù (PCRC Report at p 124). The PCRC observed that (PCRC Report at p 125):

(a)     technological developments have ‚Äúnot just facilitated the distribution of child pornography, but the demand for it as well‚Äù;

(b)     there has been a rise in the ‚Äúcommercial sexual exploitation of minors‚Äù; and

(c)     even in cases where there is no sexual penetration of the child in the production of CAM, ‚Äúthe psychological damage [to the child] is serious‚Ä¶.most children who engage in sexual acts for the production of pornography report dysfunctional sexualized behaviour, feelings of worthlessness and shame‚Äù.

31     The PCRC concluded that our laws as of August 2018 were ‚Äúinadequate to address the serious problem that the rise of the Internet has created for offences such as child pornography‚Äù (PCRC Report at p 125). It recommended that new offences be enacted to ‚Äúdeal with the entire ecosystem of offences perpetrated in the production, distribution, and consumption of [CAM], taking into account the rapid development of technology and the Internet‚Äù (PCRC Report at p 126).

32     In relation of the proposed punishments for the new offences targeting CAM, the PCRC observed that the penal provisions which previously used to cover offences involving child pornography (such as section 292 of the Penal Code) are ‚Äúinappropriate ‚Ä¶. points of reference [as they] do not have the objective of protecting children from abuse, and are unsuitable to meet the modern issues presented by the Internet‚Äù (PCRC Report at p 131). The PCRC reasoned that the punishments prescribed for the new offences should be ‚Äúsufficient to act as a denunciation of [CAM] and a general deterrent to protect children from abuse‚Äù (PRCR Report at p 131). For the proposed offence of distributing CAM, the PCRC recommended that the punishment be a mandatory imprisonment term for up to 7 years, with discretionary caning, and discretionary fine.

33     The PCRC recommendation for a standalone offence targeting the distribution of CAM (with a mandatory imprisonment term for up to 7 years, with the possibility of fine and caning) was adopted by Parliament on 6 May 2019 (see above at [2]). This led to the enactment of section 377BI of the Penal Code in its current form. The dual, principal concerns of the harm caused to children and the scale in which CAM is being disseminated today were similarly voiced during the parliamentary debates:

(a)     ‚ÄúThe global market for [CAM] is growing at an alarming speed, "aided" by advances in technology and the World Wide Web. Children used in the production and distribution of [CAM] suffer horrific immediate physical injuries as well as emotional, mental and psychological disorders with long-lasting repercussions‚Äù (Parliamentary Debates (Mr Patrick Tay Teck Guan)).

(b)     ‚ÄúWe must protect our children. They are innocent and must be shielded from depraved and corrupt humans who seek offensive pleasure from such lewd acts‚Äù (Parliamentary Debates (Mr Christopher de Souza)).

(c)     ‚Äú[This] law seeks to recognise the special harm and depravity inherent in [CAM]‚Äù (Parliamentary Debates (Mr Alex Yam)).

The sentencing approach adopted

34     It is clear from the foregoing that the predominant sentencing considerations with regards to section 377BI of the Penal Code are those of specific deterrence, general deterrence, and retribution. In determining the appropriate sentence to be imposed for a s 377BI PC offence, the Court should keep in mind the extent of harm caused to children in the distribution of the CAM and the scale at which the CAM in question was distributed, given that these were the primary concerns highlighted by both the PCRC and Parliament.

35     Being a relatively new offence on the books which was introduced partly because the previous punishment provisions were deemed inadequate, the Court in sentencing an offender for a section 377BI Penal Code offence must also be especially attuned to whether it is utilising the full sentencing range prescribed (see Wong Tian Jun De Beers v Public Prosecutor [2022] 4 SLR 805 at [56] and [58]). This is to avoid, amongst other things, sentences being ‚Äúclustered around the lower end of the statutorily prescribed range‚Äù (Public Prosecutor v GED and other appeals [2022] SGHC 301 (‚ÄúGED‚Äù) at [2]), which can gradually result in ‚Äúa sentencing trend [that is] inconsistent with Parliament‚Äôs stance with respect to‚Äù the purpose behind why section 377BI of the Penal Code had to be enacted in the first place (Tan Song Cheng v Public Prosecutor and another appeal [2021] 5 SLR 789 (‚ÄúTan Song Cheng‚Äù) at [26]).

36     In the present case, both the Prosecution and Defence did not ‚Äì rightfully, in my view ‚Äì request for a sentencing framework or sentencing benchmarks to be laid down, given that this is a court of first instance and no prior case law on section 377BI of the Penal Code exists (see Kwan Weiguang v Public Prosecutor [2022] 5 SLR 766 at [44] and Public Prosecutor v Sindok Trading Pte Ltd (now known as BSS Global Pte Ltd) and other appeals [2022] 5 SLR 336 at [29]). Instead, both parties proposed a set of sentencing factors the Court should consider when calibrating the sentence, namely, factors going towards the harm caused by the offence and factors going towards the offender‚Äôs culpability.

37     Having submitted on the extent in which these factors are engaged on the present facts, both parties then proceeded to calibrate the proposed sentence by relying significantly on the facts and the sentence imposed in Alvin Tay (see above at [13] and [20]). During oral submissions, I expressed my concern that such an approach may be problematic because Alvin Tay is an unreported decision. The High Court has cautioned, on a number of occasions, that ‚Äúsentencing precedents without grounds or explanations should bear little, if any, weight [as] it will not be possible in such cases to discern what had weighed on the mind of the sentencing judge or why the sentencing judge had approached the matter in a particular way‚Äù (Janardana Jayasankarr v Public Prosecutor [2016] 4 SLR 1288 at [13(b)]; see also Keeping Mark John v Public Prosecutor [2017] 5 SLR 627 at [18] and Tan Song Cheng at [25]). This common refrain rings even louder in the present situation, when all that is available to the Court to take reference from is a single, unreported decision, and not even a body of unreported precedents.

38     In these circumstances, I am of the view that the better approach would be for the Court ‚Äì and parties assisting the Court as well ‚Äì to simply calibrate the appropriate sentence in the present case based on the sentencing factors it considers relevant and the full sentencing range prescribed by Parliament for the offence. As mentioned earlier at [14], when the Prosecution took up the Court‚Äôs suggestion during oral submissions to do so, the proposed sentence which it came to accept is justifiable on the facts (ie, 2 years‚Äô imprisonment) differs materially from the sentence it had originally proposed in its written submissions (ie, 9 to 12 months‚Äô imprisonment), the latter being calibrated using Alvin Tay as a reference point. In my view, this anomaly concretely illustrates the need for caution when relying on unreported decisions so as to avoid ‚Äúthe dangers of the ‚Äúanchoring effect‚Äù‚Äù (GED at [128]), both by the Court and the Prosecution alike.

39     The approach I will adopt in determining the sentence to be imposed for the s 377BI PC offence will be as follows: I will first set out what I consider to be relevant offence-specific factors to come to a view of whether, on the facts, the harm caused is best described as slight, moderate or severe; and whether the culpability of the Accused is best described as low, medium or high. With this information, I will then identify an indicative sentence based on the full sentencing range statutorily prescribed for the s 377BI PC offence (ie, 7 years‚Äô imprisonment). Finally, I will make adjustments to derive the eventual sentence to be imposed by taking into account relevant offender-specific aggravating and mitigating factors. For the avoidance of doubt, I am not prescribing a sentencing framework to be applied to all section 377BI Penal Code offences or other related offences. I am simply describing ‚Äì and thereafter applying ‚Äì what I consider to be an analytically and juristically sound approach to determine the just and appropriate sentence for the s 377BI PC offence in the present case.

40     I shall now turn to the list of non-exhaustive offence-specific factors which I find relevant in the calibration of the appropriate sentence for the s 377BI PC offence.

The relevant sentencing factors

(1)   Factors going towards harm

41     Before setting out the factors going towards harm which I consider relevant, I wish to address an important point which the Defence highlighted in its oral submissions, which is that the Court must take care to ensure that the Accused is being punished for distributing CAM, and not for producing it, the latter being a more serious offence prescribed under section 377BH of the Penal Code (with a maximum imprisonment term of up to 10 years).

42     The argument which the Defence alluded to appears to be this: At the point when the CAM is distributed, the child who was filmed would already have been physically and/or psychologically harmed by whoever was responsible in the production of the film. It is therefore legally and morally questionable for the aforementioned harm suffered by the child to be pinned on the person distributing the film unthinkingly as if he was the one responsible in producing it, when in most cases ‚Äì like the present one ‚Äì the offender would not have been involved at all in the production or initial distribution of the film.

43     This argument serves as a reminder that in order to justly punish an offender for a section 377BI Penal Code offence, the Court ought first have a clear grasp of the harm which is caused in the distribution of CAM. What exactly is the harm caused when an offender (who is uninvolved in the production or initial distribution of the CAM) distributes CAM? In my view, there are at least three types of harm which can be said to be engaged:

(a)     First, the distribution of CAM is harmful because it can lead to the re-victimisation of the child involved in the film. This has been alluded to above at [1], in that when such materials are disseminated over the Internet, and this is known to the child, it can prolong or deepen his or her psychological suffering. The manner in which the child is being sexually objectified ‚Äì not just by the initial perpetrator but countless viewers worldwide ‚Äì can also cause him or her to internalise this dehumanising perspective, resulting in ‚Äúfeelings of worthlessness and shame‚Äù (the PCRC Report at p 125).

(b)     Second, the distribution of CAM is harmful because it can lead to the normalisation of, or desensitisation towards, child abuse, which in turn can lead to more children being abused. It has been reported that ‚Äúthe majority of those possessing and distributing [CAM] also commit hands-on sexual offenses against minors‚Äù (RAINN, ‚ÄúWhat is Child Sexual Abuse Material‚Äù <www.rainn.org/news/what-child-sexual-abuse-material-csam#> (25 August 2022)). Even for those who do not end up committing ‚Äúhands-on‚Äù offences against children, once they are desensitised and habitualised to seek a greater variety of CAM, they inevitably perpetuate the ‚Äúvicious cycle of demand that allows for horrific acts to occur to new children‚Äù.[note: 11] This is the basis in which the PCRC concluded that ‚Äúusers [of CAM] are complicit in the abuse involved in the production of the image [containing CAM]‚Äù (the PCRC Report at p 129). The same is true for those who distribute CAM as well.

(c)     Third, the distribution of CAM is harmful because facilitating the viewing of CAM for morally illegitimate reasons violates the dignity or inherent value of the child. On this view, the distribution of CAM is intrinsically harmful. It is an act which dehumanises and objectifies a child, thereby violating his or her dignity, regardless of whether the child is aware of or consciously affected by the act of distribution, or whether other children may be subsequently abused in the process. By contrast, when the same CAM is distributed or possessed for morally legitimate reasons (such as for the reasons listed under section 377BN(2)(a) of the Penal Code), the dignity of the child is not violated, and neither harm nor crime is established.

44     I have briefly sketched out the various ways one can understand how the distribution of CAM ‚Äì independent of its production ‚Äì is harmful. For all their differences, these explanations do converge in the practical recognition that the extent of harm suffered by the child in the production of a particular CAM is a good proxy for the extent of harm caused in the distribution of the same. A film where the child is greatly harmed in the production (for example, where she suffers sexual abuse of a violent nature) is likely to cause greater harm as well when distributed because the act of distribution can result in the child being re-victimised more severely, lead to a more extreme form of desensitisation towards child abuse, and/or more egregiously violates the dignity of the child.

45     With this point clarified, I shall now set out a list of non-exhaustive factors going towards harm in a section 377BI Penal Code offence which I am of the view are relevant.

46      The egregiousness of the nature of the sexual act on display in the CAM. This follows plainly from the discussion above. The ‚Äúmore intrusive and the more overtly sexual the nature of the acts ‚Ä¶ shown[,] the higher the degree of objective harm that will generally be caused, all else being equal‚Äù (GED at [51]). Less egregious content might include still images of children photographed naked. Egregious content might include films depicting children involved in sexual activities with another person, while even more egregious content might include films depicting children being subject to penetrative sexual activity (which, by definition, is rape), BDSM, gratuitous violence or acts of bestiality.

47      The extent in which the child appears to be in pain or in distress in the CAM. While it is self-evident that greater harm is caused when the CAM involves a child being subject to greater pain or distress, I agree with the Prosecution that the court must also take care to note that the fact that a child does not express that he or she is in pain or in distress in the CAM does not mean he or she is not traumatised, as the child could be habituated or abused to an extent where he or she does not express these emotions.

48      The age or vulnerability of the child in the CAM. If the child in question appears to be much younger than the stipulated age ceiling of 16 years old (ie, the child is prepubescent, or even worse, a toddler), greater harm is caused (GBR v Public Prosecutor and another appeal [2018] 3 SLR 1048 at [29(f)]). Similarly, greater harm is caused if the child in question appears to be suffering from a mental or physical disability, or if it is obvious that he or she ‚Äúhas a relationship with the [person producing the CAM] that renders the [child] susceptible to being manipulated or taken advantage of‚Äù (GED at [61]).

49      The degree of identifiability of the child in the CAM. The more identifiable the child is, the higher the degree of harm that will generally be caused when such films are distributed. Besides how clearly the face of the child is shown in the CAM, the court may also consider whether the child‚Äôs name ‚Äúor other forms of identification are disclosed‚Äù in the CAM (GED at [52]).

50      Evidence of commercialisation or child trafficking within the CAM. A commercialised set-up or the presence of indicators that there is child trafficking involved would suggest that the CAM in question is produced or distributed for profit. Distribution of such material increases the harm caused to the child in question (as he or she may be repeatedly abused) and other children in general, as it is likely to incentivise the production of more of such materials. Indicators of commercialisation may include the filming quality of the CAM and the presence of ‚Äústudios‚Äù used, while indicators of child trafficking may include the existence of multiple children in the film and/or evidence of the children filmed being addicted to drugs.

51      The nature and extent of the distribution of the CAM. The more persons the CAM has been distributed to by the offender, the greater is the harm caused to the child or children in general. As mentioned above at [34], this is one of the key concerns expressed by the PCRC and Parliament. Significant weight, in my view, should be placed on this factor when calibrating the harm caused.

52      The profile of the recipients the CAM is distributed to. If the CAM is distributed to individuals seeking CAM, or in a forum or platform set up to exchange CAM, this increases the harm caused as such distribution would likely fuel the demand for and increase the risk of further child sexual abuse.

(2)   Factors going towards culpability

53     I shall now set out a list of non-exhaustive factors going towards culpability which are, in my view, relevant to a section 377BI Penal Code offence.

54      The motive of the offender. An offender who commits the offence for ‚Äúpersonal gain, which need not be limited to monetary gain‚Äù (GED at [65]) should generally be regarded as an offender exhibiting higher culpability.

55      The duration of distribution and/or possession of the CAM. All things being equal, an offender who possesses or distributes CAM for a longer period of time should be deemed more culpable than one who has committed the offence in a one-off instance (GED at [78]).

56      The manner or method of distribution of the CAM. An offender who makes it harder to detect or eradicate the CAM distributed because of the manner or method used should be considered more culpable. The online dissemination of CAM, especially in a medium where the CAM can be easily forwarded, would make it harder for the CAM to be eradicated. The use of the Dark Web, or VPN blockers, to distribute CAM also aggravates the offender‚Äôs culpability as these can be considered to be intentional steps taken by the offender to avoid detection.

57      The degree of knowledge of the offender of the content of the CAM. An offender who has greater awareness of the precise content of the CAM in question, and yet continues to distribute it, is more culpable than one who has ‚Äúreason to believe‚Äù that the material he is distributing contains CAM, but is not fully aware of its entire or exact content.

58      The role or position of the offender in the forum or platform where the CAM was distributed. An offender who abuses his position of power or influence in the forum or platform he is a moderator or administrator of to distribute and facilitate the exchange of CAM is, all things being equal, more culpable than a regular member or participant of the same forum or platform who distributes CAM.

59      The accompanying behaviour of the offender when distributing the CAM. An offender‚Äôs culpability is enhanced if, besides distributing the CAM, he actively promotes or encourages the viewing or further distribution of CAM, by, for example, discussing its content.

60      The manner in which the offender had obtained the CAM. An offender is less culpable if he had unknowingly obtained CAM in an innocuous setting, before going on to distribute it. In comparison, an offender who received CAM from an illicit source or a setting in which CAM is regularly distributed has a higher culpability. On this scale, an offender has the highest culpability if he had intentionally sought out CAM with a view to distributing it.

Calibrating the appropriate sentence on the present facts

(1)   Harm caused by the offence

61     I shall now analyse the offence-specific factors going towards harm on the present facts. In my view, the Prosecution is correct to state that almost every single harm factor is engaged to a significant degree in the present case.

62     First, of the 17 films containing CAM (of which details are set out in Annex C of the SOF), I consider 13 of them to contain content which are highly egregious in nature, given the sexual acts being recorded and the apparent age of the child victims. Of these 13 films:

(a)     4 of them show an adult male and a girl engaging in penile-vaginal and/or anal sex.[note: 12] Of these 4 films, 3 of the victims appear to be prepubescent, while the other victim appears to be slightly older (though she still appears to be below 16 years of age). The film involving the last victim is 6 minutes and 49 seconds long.

(b)     3 of them show an adult female and a boy engaging in penile-vaginal and/or anal sex.[note: 13]

(c)     1 of them shows 2 boys (one of whom is prepubescent) taking turns to insert their penises into the anus of a donkey.[note: 14]

(d)     1 of them shows a prepubescent girl engaging in fellatio with a prepubescent boy.[note: 15]

(e)     1 of them shows a girl engaging in fellatio with an adult male.[note: 16]

(f)     1 of them shows an adult female engaging in fellatio with a boy.[note: 17]

(g)     2 of them show an adult male groping and molesting a girl.[note: 18]

63     The egregiousness of the content of these films is aggravated by the fact that where there was penetrative sexual activity involved, no protection could be seen being used. One of the rapists in the films had even ejaculated on the child victim‚Äôs vagina.

64     Second, almost all of the faces of the children in these 17 films are identifiable. Many of them also appear to be self-conscious at being filmed whilst they were being sexually abused, with two of the girls trying ‚Äì but eventually failing ‚Äì to cover their faces in embarrassment,[note: 19] and another clearly in discomfort.[note: 20]

65     Third, there is some evidence of commercialisation in at least 2 of the films,[note: 21] given the presence of a background which resembles a studio, with occasional camera flashes.

66     Fourth, the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù in which the Accused had distributed these 17 films to was one where some participants had expressly requested for CAM.[note: 22] It is therefore very likely that these films had fallen into the possession of those with paedophilic tendencies.

67     Last but certainly not least, the nature and extent of the distribution of these 17 films was extensive. These 17 films were distributed by the Accused to at least 256 other persons in the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù. In this regard, I do not give much weight to the Defence‚Äôs submission that the films were ‚Äúnot distributed to the public at large‚Äù.[note: 23] While the Defence‚Äôs submission is technically correct, it is unrealistic, in my view, to think that these films (which were made for easy sharing and forwarding) would only circulate amongst the 256 participants in the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù and not end up on other chat groups or even publicly accessible websites, given the profile of the participants in the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù and the sheer number of people the films had been distributed to.

68     In the light of all of these aggravating factors, I am of the view that the harm in question in the present case should be pegged at the higher end of moderate harm. While the content of most of these 17 films is extremely vile, I do not consider the harm to have crossed into the category of severe harm because there was no gratuitous violence or brutal behaviour on display in any of the films, none of the victims appears to be younger than the age of 8 or 9, and most of the films were less than a minute long. In my view, pegging the overall harm in the present case at the higher end of moderate harm accurately reflects the harm caused by the egregious content of the films being distributed and the number and profile of persons they have been distributed to.

(2)   The Accused‚Äôs culpability

69     In relation to the Accused‚Äôs culpability, I consider the following factors to be culpability-enhancing:

(a)     The Accused had distributed the 17 films containing CAM over a period of more than 4 months. It was not disputed by the Defence that the offences only stopped because of the arrest of the Accused and another participant in the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù.[note: 24]

(b)     The Accused had a significant level of awareness that he was distributing CAM, even though he is charged under the ‚Äúreason to believe‚Äù limb under section 377BI of the Penal Code. This is borne out by the following facts admitted by the Accused without qualification:

(i)       Forensic investigations reveal that the Accused would at the very least had ‚Äúseen the thumbnails of the films‚Äù he had downloaded and distributed, and it would have been apparent from these thumbnails that some of these films contained ‚Äúpornographic films [involving] children or young persons‚Äù.[note: 25]

(ii)       The Accused had ‚Äúadmitted [to the Police] that he had viewed some of these 17 [films] and knew that it [sic] contained pornography of children‚Äù.[note: 26]

(iii)       The Accused was ‚Äúspecifically chided by other participants in the Wela Group Chat for forwarding [CAM]‚Äù but he ‚Äúdid not stop forwarding videos containing [CAM]‚Äù.[note: 27]

(c)     The Accused had obtained many of these films from other WhatsApp chat groups which contained links to CAM, and where participants had requested for CAM.[note: 28] These were definitely not innocuous sources.

(d)     The Accused had distributed CAM whilst he was an administrator of the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù. Instead of using his power and position to stop the distribution of CAM, he was an active contributor of CAM and other obscene materials, and persisted in continuing to do so even though some members had voiced concerns.

70     I consider the following factors to be culpability-reducing:

(a)     The Accused did not distribute CAM for monetary gain. I am not persuaded however by the Defence‚Äôs submission that he had not distributed the materials in exchange for ‚Äúother materials of a similar nature or otherwise‚Äù.[note: 29] Given the amount of obscene materials which were being exchanged on the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù, and similarly in the other WhatsApp chat groups the Accused was a member of, the irresistible inference is that the Accused‚Äôs ‚Äúgain‚Äù in distributing CAM was so that there would be a ‚Äústeady stream of [other] illicit content being circulated to the Wela Group Chat‚Äù,[note: 30] possibly for his own consumption or possession.

(b)     There is no evidence that the Accused had actively sought out CAM for the purpose of distribution. As the Defence highlighted, the Accused had simply forwarded pornographic materials he received in other WhatsApp chat groups in bulk, and the films he had forwarded in bulk would occasionally include a single film involving CAM.[note: 31]

(c)     There is no evidence that the Accused had requested or discussed CAM in any of the WhatsApp chat groups he was in. Even though other members of the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù had occasionally requested for CAM, there is also no evidence that the Accused had replied to them, or specifically forwarded CAM to these members.[note: 32]

71     The Defence submitted that the Court should also consider the fact that the 17 films constituting CAM only comprise a tiny fraction (ie, 1.7%) of the entire batch of pornographic materials forwarded by the Accused to the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù, which suggests that his ‚Äúlevel of proclivity towards [CAM]‚Äù is low, and that this should be culpability-reducing.[note: 33] With respect, I do not find this argument persuasive. In my view, this point is essentially a mathematical description of the factual reality that the Accused had not intentionally sought out CAM for the purpose of distribution, which I have already taken into account as being culpability-reducing. The mere fact that the actual number of films containing CAM constitutes a very small percentage of all of the obscene films distributed by the Accused should not be treated as an independent culpability-reducing factor. In my view, an offender guilty of section 377BI of the Penal Code who happens to have also distributed a vast amount of other types of obscene materials to the detriment of society (and which also constitutes an offence) should not be deemed less culpable and punished less severely for the s 377BI PC offence compared to another offender who had not distributed other obscene materials or had distributed a smaller quantity of the same. If anything, the fact that the Accused had distributed other obscene materials in such large quantities should be considered an aggravating factor had a separate charge relating to these other obscene materials not been preferred. It does not and should not reduce his culpability for the s 377BI PC offence.

72     Having considered and weighed all of these culpability-enhancing and culpability-reducing factors, I am of the view that that the Accused‚Äôs culpability should be pegged at the higher end of low culpability. The Accused‚Äôs culpability is low because he had not distributed CAM for profit, and there is no evidence of him actively seeking out CAM for distribution or discussing the content of CAM with any person. However, I assess his culpability to be at the higher end of low culpability because of his significant level of awareness that he was distributing CAM and the fact that he had distributed the CAM in his position as an administrator of the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù.

(3)   The indicative sentence and eventual sentence imposed

73     Having assessed this case to be one under the higher end of moderate harm, and the higher end of low culpability, I am of the view that for an offence in which the maximum imprisonment sentence is 7 years, the indicative sentence based on the offence-specific factors to be imposed on the Accused should be 19 months‚Äô imprisonment. The sentence of 19 months‚Äô imprisonment is above the one-fifth mark but below the one-quarter mark of the maximum sentence prescribed by Parliament. Given that the harm involved in this offence is bordering on severe harm, and the Accused‚Äôs culpability bordering on medium culpability, I am of the view that the indicative sentence I have derived (ie, 19 months‚Äô imprisonment) is justified and cannot be considered excessive.

74     I now turn to consider whether there are any offender-specific aggravating or mitigating factors which should further calibrate the sentence upwards or downwards.

75     I give no mitigating weight to the Defence‚Äôs submission that the investigations for this matter had ‚Äúspanned one and a half years and has taken a huge toll‚Äù on the Accused as he has not been able to work or continue with his studies while on a student pass.[note: 34] In my view, the personal hardship faced by the Accused from the point he was investigated is the natural consequence of his own wrongful actions and it is trite that such factors should ordinarily not be given mitigating weight (Kow Keng Siong, Sentencing Principles in Singapore (Academy Publishing, 2nd Ed, 2019) at pp 906 ‚Äì 907). Furthermore, in the present case, the amount of time taken for investigations to be completed is in my view wholly reasonable given: (a) the sheer number of obscene materials found in the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù, (b) the time and resources the police had to expend to correctly trace a significant number of these obscene materials to the Accused (as the Accused had, prior to his arrest, ‚Äúdeleted the videos and accompanying messages from his WhatsApp‚Äù[note: 35]); and (c) the time and resources in which the Info-Communications Media Development Authority had to expend watching and classifying the obscene materials.

76     In my view, the only relevant offender-specific factor the Court should give weight to is the Accused‚Äôs plea of guilt. However, I am of the view that the mitigatory value of the Accused‚Äôs plea of guilty is reduced, for the following reasons:

(a)     First, the Accused did not plead guilty at the earliest opportunity. In fact, he had decided to plead guilty to the s 377BI PC offence only after the criminal case disclosure conference (‚ÄúCCDC‚Äù) process was completed and after trial dates were fixed. The Defence tried to explain that this was because the Accused‚Äôs phone had been seized by the police and he had no access to the relevant materials earlier. I do not find this a convincing reason as to why the Accused chose to plead guilty at such a late stage. The Accused had already admitted, during his interview by the police, that he had viewed some of the 17 films and knew that they contained CAM.[note: 36] This constitutes the gravamen of the s 377BI PC offence. It also bears mention that the Accused had already ‚Äúdeleted the videos and the accompanying messages from his WhatsApp‚Äù[note: 37] at the point his phone was seized, so whether or not the Accused was in possession of his phone once investigations commenced should have little or no bearing on whether he should plead guilty to the s 377BI PC offence. In any case, under the CCDC regime, the Defence would already have had access to all relevant materials which the Prosecution is under an obligation to disclose before the 3rd CCDC hearing on 29 December 2022. Yet, the Accused continued to elect to claim trial which led to trial dates being fixed, and only decided to plead guilty on 3 February 2023, more than a month after the 3rd CCDC hearing. In the circumstances, I am of the view that the mitigatory value of the Accused‚Äôs plea of guilt should not be given as much weight as in a case where an offender had pleaded guilty at the earliest opportunity.

(b)     Second, unlike in other types of sexual offences, the Accused‚Äôs decision to plead guilty for the s 377BI PC offence cannot be said to have the merit of sparing a sexual victim ‚Äúthe trauma of having to testify in court and relieve the humiliation she suffered as a result of the offence‚Äù (GED at [132]), which is generally given significant mitigating weight. This is because the child victims in the present case are not known to either the Prosecution or the Defence and they would not have been called as witnesses for the trial.

77     In the circumstances, the only mitigatory value to be placed on the Accused‚Äôs decision to plead guilty is the fact that he had saved some of the Court‚Äôs time and some state resources which would have been expended had he proceeded to claim trial. Given the reduced mitigatory value of his plea of guilt, I am of the view that the indicative sentence for the s 377BI PC offence should be reduced by only 3 months. Therefore, the eventual sentence which I find just and appropriate for the s 377BI PC offence is 16 months‚Äô imprisonment.

78     Given the significant harm involved in the Accused‚Äôs offence, the considerable extent of the Accused‚Äôs culpability, and the reduced mitigatory value of his plea of guilt, the original sentences proposed by both the Prosecution (9 to 12 months‚Äô imprisonment) and the Defence (8 months‚Äô imprisonment) in their written submissions are, in my respectful view, clearly inadequate. The imposition of either an 8 months‚Äô or 12 months‚Äô imprisonment sentence in the present case will not do justice to the full sentencing range Parliament has prescribed for this serious offence, and will not be ‚Äúproportionate to the severity of the [s 377BI PC offence] and the offender in question‚Äù.[note: 38]

Distinguishing Alvin Tay

79     For completeness, I will also address Alvin Tay even though the decision is of limited precedential value, given that parties have focused much attention on it.

80     The facts in Alvin Tay are summarised as follows:

(a)     Between August to November 2020, the offender bought and sold sex videos by communicating with interested buyers via the messaging function on Tumblr, and accepting payment via PayLah before sending a Mega.NZ link containing obscene images or recordings. He earned a total of about $4,000 from these transactions. At the time of arrest, he was in possession of 99,872 intimate images and recordings.

(b)     There were two proceeded charges which the offender pleaded guilty to: first, for intentionally distributing 320 voyeuristic recordings of other persons without their consent, via Tumblr, an offence under section 377BC of the Penal Code. Second, for knowingly distributing 20 recordings involving CAM to one Tumblr user in exchange for nude photos from the recipient, which constitutes the relevant section 377BI Penal Code charge. It is unclear from the facts how the offender had obtained these 20 recordings involving CAM, whether the recipient he had distributed these recordings to had requested or shown an interest in CAM, and whether the offender had actually received nude photos in return.

(c)     These 20 recordings showed young persons below 16 years old in a sexual pose or being involved in a sexual activity such as fellatio, girls touching their exposed breasts, girls removing their clothing or repeatedly touching their bare private parts. The most egregious video (which lasted 2 minutes and 15 seconds long) depicted a prepubescent girl and boy engaging in various sexual acts together in a toilet, including fellatio performed by the girl. Most of the videos captured the subject‚Äôs face entirely and were at least about one minute long.

(d)     An imprisonment sentence of 8 months was imposed on the offender in Alvin Tay for the section 377BI Penal Code offence.

81     Having carefully compared the facts of both cases, I am of the view that the harm involved in the present case is significantly more severe that that present in Alvin Tay, such that it should not be surprising to find the sentence imposed in the present case to be far lengthier than that imposed in Alvin Tay.

(a)     First, the most egregious recording distributed in Alvin Tay is one involving a boy and a prepubescent girl engaging in sexual acts. In the present case, there are at least 11 films distributed by the Accused of which content is as egregious or more egregious than that one recording in Alvin Tay (see above at [62]). The terrible harm caused to the children whose sexual assault was filmed and distributed in the present case far exceeds that caused in Alvin Tay.

(b)     Second, the offender in Alvin Tay had circulated the recordings involving CAM to only one person. By contrast, the Accused in the present case had distributed the films to 256 people each time, some of whom were individuals who had expressly requested for CAM.

82     In terms of the culpability of the offender in Alvin Tay compared to the Accused‚Äôs culpability, I observe the following:

(a)     First, the offender in Alvin Tay had committed the offence when he was 21 years‚Äô old. By contrast, the Accused was 27 years‚Äô old when he committed the s 377BI PC offence.

(b)     Second, the offender in Alvin Tay was charged to have known that the recordings he had distributed contained CAM, whereas the Accused was charged under the ‚Äúreason to believe‚Äù limb. However, the difference in culpability on this factor is not stark, given the significant level of awareness the Accused possessed that what he was distributing contained CAM (see above at [69(b)]).

(c)     Third, the offender in Alvin Tay had expressly sought to exchange CAM for his own personal gain, ie, to obtain nude photos from the recipient in return. I accept that this aggravating factor is not present here, but I am also of the view that this distinguishing factor is offset by the culpability-enhancing fact of the Accused having distributed the 17 films in his position as an administrator of the ‚ÄúWela Group Chat‚Äù.

83     It also bears mention that the offender in Alvin Tay had elected to plead guilty at the earliest opportunity (in fact, at the very first court mention), while the Accused had done so only after the CCDC process was completed and trial dates fixed.

84     Given all of the foregoing differences, it is in my view neither surprising nor unfair for the Accused to receive a sentence which is twice the length of that imposed in Alvin Tay. In any case, this Court‚Äôs reasoned decision as to how it had calibrated the sentence in the present case to be that of 16 months‚Äô imprisonment has been set out in full above.

The imposition of consecutive sentences

85     Having determined the individual sentences to be imposed for both offences, I now turn to the issue of whether the sentences should be imposed consecutively (as submitted by the Prosecution) or concurrently (as submitted by the Defence).

86     The Defence‚Äôs submission for the sentences to run concurrently is premised on the applicability of the one-transaction rule, which states that ‚Äúwhere two or more offences are committed in the course of a single transaction, all sentences in respect of those offences should be concurrent rather than consecutive‚Äù (Public Prosecutor v Law Aik Meng [2007] 2 SLR(R) 814 at [52]). In the present case, it is not disputed that both the Accused‚Äôs offences were committed via the same conduct and largely within the same time period, such that they are indeed proximate as a matter of fact. However, this does not conclusively determine whether the sentences should run concurrently. In Mohamed Shouffee (at [30] ‚Äì [31]), the High Court clarified that:

30    [t]he better articulation of the rationale for the [one-transaction] rule is found in the principle that consecutive sentences are not appropriate if the various offences involve a ‚Äúsingle invasion of the same legally protected interest‚Äù‚Ä¶

31    On this formulation, the real basis of the one-transaction rule is unity of the violated interest that underlies the various offences. Where multiple offences are found to be proximate as a matter of fact but violate different legally protected interests, then they would not, at least as a general rule, be regarded as forming a single transaction.

87     Therefore, to determine whether consecutive or concurrent sentences should be imposed in the present case, the Court has to first consider what are the legally protected interests underlying the s 292(1)(a) PC offence and the s 377BI PC offence.

88     Under section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code, the wrongful act in question is the transmission of ‚Äúobscene‚Äù objects. The word ‚Äúobscene‚Äù is defined under s 42 of the Penal Code as follows:

The word ‚Äúobscene‚Äù, in relation to any thing or matter, means any thing or matter the effect of which is, if taken as a whole, such as to tend to deprave and corrupt persons who are likely, having regard to all relevant circumstances, to read, see or hear the matter contained or embodied in it.

[emphasis added]

89     Reading section 292(1)(a) together with section 42 of the Penal Code, I agree with the Prosecution that the relevant legally protected interest which is targeted by section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code is the maintenance of public morality or decency in society. The purpose of section 292(1)(a) of the Penal Code is to prevent the transmission of materials which are prone to ‚Äúdeprave and corrupt‚Äù persons receiving such materials. The same legally protected interest is also targeted by equivalent provisions such as sections 29 to 32 of the Films Act, of which ‚Äúprimary concern in sentencing is the protection of the public from moral corruption‚Äù (Sentencing Practice at p 627).

90     On the other hand, the relevant legally protected interest targeted by section 377BI of the Penal Code is the protection of children from being sexually abused or exploited. This is evident from the relevant extracts from the PCRC Report and the parliamentary speeches I have cited above (at [30] ‚Äì [33]); in particular, the observation of the PCRC that provisions like section 292 of the Penal Code do not ‚Äúhave the objective of protecting children from abuse‚Äù (PCRC Report at p 131), thus necessitating the enactment of provisions such as section 377BI of the Penal Code. Put simply, Parliament did not enact section 377BI of the Penal Code to better protect recipients of materials containing CAM from moral corruption, however important or positive that outcome may be. Section 377BI of the Penal Code is fundamentally about the protection of children. When an offence under section 377BI of the Penal Code is committed, it is the dignity and bodily integrity of a child which is being violated, and the interest in preventing this particular form of violation is conceptually and morally distinct from the interest in upholding the moral character or decency of the general public.

91     For these reasons, I am of the view that the s 292(1)(a) PC offence and the s 377BI PC offence committed by the Accused target different legally protected interests, and the Accused‚Äôs commission of both offences in the present case should therefore not ‚Äúbe regarded as forming a single transaction‚Äù (Mohamed Shouffee at [31]). The one-transaction rule thus does not apply in the Accused‚Äôs favour. Having committed two offences and in the process violated two different legally protected interests, it is only just and appropriate for the sentences of both offences to run consecutively, resulting in an aggregate sentence of 18 months and 2 weeks‚Äô imprisonment.

92     Alternatively, even if the s 292(1)(a) PC offence and the s 377BI PC offence should be viewed or understood as forming part of a single transaction, I am of the view that the imposition of consecutive sentences in the present case remains nonetheless justified in order to ‚Äúgive sufficient weight to the interest of deterrence so as to discourage behaviour of the sort‚Äù exhibited by the Accused (Mohamed Shouffee at [45(a)]), which consists in him misusing his role as an administrator of a popular WhatsApp chat group to distribute, and encourage the exchange of, child abuse and other obscene materials. In this day and age where child abuse and other obscene materials can spread so quickly and easily through such messaging platforms, the imposition of consecutive sentences is necessary to send a clear signal that users of such platforms will be held accountable for all of the illegal materials they are charged and convicted for unlawfully distributing. The imposition of consecutive sentences in the present case is also ‚Äúin keeping with the gravity of the offences‚Äù (Mohamed Shouffee at [45(b)]). Having been charged and convicted for unlawfully distributing a total of 960 films, the true gravity of the Accused‚Äôs offences will not, in my view, be accurately reflected if he were to be effectively punished just for his distribution of 17 of these 960 films, notwithstanding the fact that the punishment for the s 377BI PC offence is much more severe than the punishment for the s 292(1)(a) PC offence.

93     Finally, I also consider the principle of totality and am of the view that the imposition of consecutive sentences in the present case cannot be said to be crushing or disproportionate, considering the Accused‚Äôs age and the fact that the aggregate sentence imposed (ie, 18 months and 2 weeks‚Äô imprisonment) is in fact lower than the indicative sentence I have earlier derived for the s 377BI PC offence (ie, 19 months‚Äô imprisonment).

Conclusion

94     In the concluding paragraphs of its written submissions, the Prosecution put forth the following, impassioned plea for justice:

26.    Every single instance of sexual activity that is present in these clips is an instance of a child being sexually abused. It is hard to overstate what the Court is really looking at. Each clip is a child in the midst of being groped, raped, and violated. Some of these might be children being trafficked into sex, others being raped by family members, sometimes both.

27.    Any recording of child sexual abuse is a reminder of a living nightmare. Each time a clip is distributed, a fresh trauma and harm is being done again to that child as paedophiles savour the horrific abuse that has happened (and might well still be happening) to these children. Each distribution further ensures that the worst moments of their lives will percolate through the internet, making it more and more difficult to erase. It is also a living nightmare because the acts of distribution, possession, and consumption create the vicious cycle of demand that allows for horrific acts to occur to new children.

28.    The Accused‚Äôs actions feed into that very cycle of evil. At a time where such child sexual abuse continues to grow unabated, we urge the Courts to do all it can to combat such evils. No child should ever have to suffer the sort of abuse seen in these clips. We owe it to them to stop it however we can.

95     With sorrowful indignation ‚Äì at the depravity of those who produced, distributed or consumed the materials in question; but even more, at the plight of each and every precious child so inhumanely abused and violated in them ‚Äì I agree. In the face of this monstrous evil, the Court has a duty to impose a sentence which sufficiently reflects society‚Äôs justified abhorrence of acts which encourage or facilitate the sexual abuse of children; and one that is properly situated within the full sentencing range prescribed by Parliament given the circumstances of the offence and the offender in question.

96     For all the foregoing reasons, I have come to the view that the sentences originally proposed by both the Prosecution and the Defence in their written submissions are inadequate, and that the just and appropriate aggregate sentence the Accused deserves for his offences is 18 months and 2 weeks‚Äô imprisonment. It should now be clear that with section 377BI of the Penal Code in force, those who choose to be complicit in the sexual abuse of children by knowingly distributing CAM will be severely dealt with. The harm which they so callously inflict and prolong on an abused, suffering child shall reap for these offenders the full weight of justice which the Court will, in accordance with law, unflinchingly mete out.

97     It remains for me in closing to express my gratitude to the Prosecution and the Defence for their detailed and helpful submissions.
