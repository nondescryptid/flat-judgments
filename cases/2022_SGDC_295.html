District Judge Salina Bte Ishak:

Background

1       The accused, Mr Tan Keng Hoon, a 66 years-old male Singapore citizen had faced the following charge:

Charge

are charged that on 25 February 2020 at about 9.25 p.m., at the designated zebra crossing of Changi Village Road towards End, nearest lamppost 35, Singapore, did drive a motorcar SDL4360Z, on a road without reasonable consideration for other persons using the road, to wit, by failing to keep a proper lookout for other road users at the zebra crossing, resulting in a collision with a pedestrian namely one Tay Mian Cheo (male /59 years old), who was walking across the zebra crossing from your left to right, and hurt was caused to the said pedestrian, by such driving, you have thereby committed an offence under Section 65(1)(b) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”).

And further, that you, before the commission of the said offence, has been convicted on the following earlier occasion: a) On 11 January 1997 in the Subordinate Court No. 25N, Singapore, of an offence of Speeding (exceeding speed limit of 70kmph road) under Section 63(4) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276), which conviction has not been set aside, and you shall thereby be liable for punishment under Section 65(4)(b) of the RTA.

Procedural history

2       The case was first mentioned in Court 7B on 9 June 2021. The case was adjourned several times for the Prosecution to obtain directions, for the Defence Counsel Ms Audrey Liaw Shu Juan (PY Legal LLC) to make representations, further representations as well as for the corresponding replies to be made. In the course of these adjournments, the accused discharged his Defence Counsel on 22 February 2022 and had been acting in person since.

3       The accused was initially scheduled to plead guilty to the present charge on 20 June 2022 in Court 7B. On 20 June 2022 as the accused disputed the Statement of Facts and qualified his plea, his matter was fixed for a pre-trial conference in PTC Chambers 8-40. On 11 October 2022, the accused was again scheduled to plead guilty in Court 7B but the accused disagreed with the Statement of Facts. At the pre-trial conference in Chambers 8-40 on 9 November 2022, as the accused indicated that he had intended to plead guilty, his case was once again fixed for a plea guilty mention on 23 November 2022 in Court 7B.

4       On 23 November 2022, the accused finally pleaded guilty to the charge and admitted to the Statement of Facts without any qualification. After carefully considering the Prosecution’s submissions on sentence and the accused’s plea in mitigation, I sentenced the accused to fine of $2,000 in default seven days’ imprisonment and disqualified him from holding or obtaining all classes of driving licences for a period of 15 months with effect from 23 November 2022.

5       The accused being dissatisfied with my decision had filed his Notice of Appeal against his sentence and the order of disqualification on 1 December 2022. He has paid the fine in full.

6       Having set out the background for the present case, I now provide the reasons for my decision.

Salient Facts

7       The accused was the driver of the motorcar bearing vehicle registration number SDL4360Z (the “motorcar”) at the time of the accident. The victim is Tay Mian Cheo, a 59-year-old male Singaporean. He was a pedestrian at the time of the accident.

First information report

8       On 25 February 2020 at about 9.25 p.m., the police received a call with text “Changi Village Rd 99131/B02 (BUS STOP) SINGAPORE”. The location reported was Changi Village Road towards End, near lamppost 35.

Facts pertaining to the charge

9       Investigations revealed that on 25 February 2020 at about 9.25pm, the accused was driving the motorcar at the designated zebra crossing of Changi Village Road towards End, near lamppost 35, Singapore. LTA enforcement cameras showed that the accused was travelling along the right side of 2-lane road whilst going straight. However, as he was approaching the said zebra crossing, the accused failed to bring the motorcar to a halt and also failed to lookout for the victim who was walking across from accused’s left to right at the designated zebra crossing. As a result of the accused’s failure to stop the motorcar, the motorcar then collided with the victim’s right side.

Victim’s injuries

10     The victim remained conscious after the collision but sustained head and rib pain. He refused conveyance from the scene by ambulance but admitted himself to Tan Tock Seng Hospital (“TTSH”) later that same day on 26/02/2020. At TTSH, the victim was given outpatient care and granted 3 days of hospitalization leave from 26 to 28 February 2020. The victim also reported sustaining minor head and rib pain.

11     The victim’s medical report, dated 12 October 2021 from TTSH, indicated that he sustained abrasion over the left occipital region of the head, tenderness over the left 8th and 9th ribs laterally and limited left shoulder range of motion due to pain.

12     The accused was not injured and there was no damage to his motorcar. There were no mechanical defects in the motorcar.

13     At the time of accident, the weather was fine, the road surface was dry and visibility was good with street lighting. The traffic flow was light.

14     As the accused was convicted on 11 January 1997 in the Subordinate Court No. 25N, Singapore of an offence of speeding (exceed speed limit of 70kmph road) under Section 63(4) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276) and the conviction has not been set aside, he is therefore liable for enhanced punishment.

Sentencing

Prescribed Penalty

15     The prescribed penalty for an offence of dangerous driving by a repeat offender under s 65(1)(b) punishable under s 65(4)(b) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”) is fine not exceeding $5,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both.

16     Pursuant to s 65(8) of the RTA, a person is regarded as repeat offender under subsections 2(b), (3)(b), (4)(b) and (5)(b) respectively if the person has been convicted for an offence under ss 65(1), 63, 64, 66 or 116 of the RTA or for an offence under ss 279, 304A, 336, 337 or 338 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) where the act constituting the offences involve the use of a motor vehicle.

17     In addition, s 42(1) of the RTA provides that a court before which person convicted of an offence in connection with the driving of a motor vehicle, may where so required by this Act, order him to be disqualified from holding or obtaining a driving licence for life or for such period as the court may think fit.

Antecedents

18     On 11 January 1997 in Court 25N, the accused was convicted for an offence of speeding under s 63(4) of the RTA where a fine of $300 was imposed. On 21 December 2019, the accused had paid composition of $200 for an offence of speeding under s 63(4) of the RTA.

Prosecution’s Submissions on Sentence

19     The Prosecution had submitted that both the culpability and harm were low in the present case. Nevertheless, it was the Prosecution’s position that in respect of the potential harm, it could not be said to be insignificant. The accused had driven his car into a walking pedestrian.

20     It was further submitted that the accused is a repeat offender for speeding and two months prior to the accident, he paid a composition fine for speeding as well. In the premises, the Prosecution had sought a fine of at least $2,000 and disqualification of least 15 months.

Mitigation

21     In his written in mitigation, the accused had stated that he is a divorcee who lives with his family and has one adult son. He is a businessman managing his food and beverage outlets island wide. He apologised for his serious error of judgment that led to the commission of the offence. He accepted that he did drive his car which had collide into the victim but maintained that he was not reckless nor a danger to other road users.

22     On the day in question at about 9:45pm, he was driving his motorcar along Changi Village Road to one of his outlets at Lorong Bekukong. According to the accused he had driven below the speed limit because it was not a main road. At the zebra crossing he collided into the victim who had just started crossing the zebra crossing from his left to right. It was only when he had driven forward that he collided into the victim. He stated that he was not in a rush as he traversed this stretch of road regularly and knows the place rather intimately. He immediately stopped his car and attended to the victim. Some other passers-by also came to the victim’s assistance. The accused requested one of them to call for the ambulance.

23     According to the accused he genuinely did not have sight of the victim. He submitted that as his in-car camera footage had not shown the victim crossing the pedestrian crossing, this attested to the fact that he had not seen the victim moments before the collision. As he is aware of the pedestrian crossing, he had slowed down before traversing the pedestrian crossing. It was further submitted that as there were motorcars parked on the left, it may have contributed to the victim emerging from a blind spot.

Extenuating circumstances and mitigation plea

24     The accused submitted that he had pleaded guilty without going through a trial. By pleading guilty he had demonstrated his genuine remorse and avoided wasting the Court’s time by claiming trial. He urged the Court to accept his early plea of guilt as a mitigating factor.

25     The accused further submitted that he is a first offender for an offence of this description and did not have any previous antecedents. He had maintained a clean driving record since obtaining his driving licence save for his speeding offence which was almost 25 years ago.

26     According to the accused, he had offered to send the victim to the hospital but the latter refused his offer. He noticed that the victim reeked strongly of alcohol. The accused made reference to the victim’s medical report as well as his discharge summary to highlight that his injuries were minor.

Submissions on sentence

27     The accused referred to PP v Mills Simon Nicholas [2021] SGDC 187 to submit that his culpability was low to moderate and the harm caused was also low. He urged the Court to take note of this when calibrating the fine and disqualification. He further referred to several unreported decisions (as set out at Part E iii to vi at pages 8 to 9 of his mitigation) as well as the High Court decision in Kwan Weiguang v PP [2022] SGHC 121 (“Kwan Weiguang”) to seek a disqualification order of less than 12 months notwithstanding the fact that he had a dated previous conviction for speeding.

28     He urged the Court to impose a disqualification period of ten to eleven months as the sooner his disqualification ends, the sooner he would be able to resume his normal work and grassroots activities. Without the benefit of his driving licence, he would have to rely on public transport for his normal day to day activities. He also urged the Court to impose a light sentence as he needs to supervise his six restaurants on a daily basis.

Prosecution’s Reply

29     In response to the plea in mitigation, the Prosecution highlighted that although the accused had indicated that he had pleaded guilty at the earliest stage, the present case has been in the court system for one and half years. It was fixed for a plead guilty mention several times. It was also highlighted that the accused had stated that he has no previous conviction. That is not correct as he has acknowledged his antecedent.

30     With regards to the cases cited in his plea in mitigation, it was submitted that none of them pertain to cases without antecedents. It was the Prosecution’s case that the accused was being sentenced as a repeat offender. With respect to the case of Kwan Weiguang, this related to a charge of dangerous driving under s 64 and not s 65 of the RTA where no hurt was caused to the victim. The High Court in that case had stated that the 12 months disqualification was confined where no hurt was caused. It was further submitted that the offence in this case involves causing of hurt and a disqualification of 15 months was more than fair.

31     With regard to the lighting condition, it can be seen that victim was crossing at slow speed and the accused’s vehicle was within his visibility. The accused should have kept a proper lookout for the victim who was crossing the zebra crossing. the lighting was perhaps not the best at that time it would have been especially incumbent on the accused to look out for any pedestrians who was crossing the zebra crossing.

Decision on sentence

Sentencing approach

Irresponsible driving under the Road Traffic Act

32     The prescribed penalty for the present offence under s 65(1)(b) of the RTA was enhanced on 1 November 2019 following the passing of the Road Traffic (Amendment) Act 2019 (Act 19 of 2019). The Amendment Act arose out of a review of the RTA and it aimed to provide stronger deterrence against irresponsible driving.

33     As at the date of my decision, there is no existing sentencing framework enunciated by the High Court for offences under s 65(1) of the Road Traffic Act and punishable under s 65(4)(b) of the RTA. There is however a sentencing framework for an offence under s 65(1) for causing grievous hurt punishable under s 65(3)(a) of the RTA prescribed by Vincent Hoong J (“Hoong J”) in Sue Chang (Xu Zheng) v Public Prosecutor [2022] SGHC 176 (“Sue Chang”). In that case, Hoong J had dismissed the appeal and prescribed a sentencing framework for offences punishable under section 65(3)(a) of the RTA where the offender has claimed trial.

34     In Sue Chang, Hoong J made to two key observations at [38] to [40] in relation to the RTA amendments in the context of careless or inconsiderate driving. Firstly, as observed by Sundaresh Menon CJ (“Menon CJ”) in Wu Zhi Yong v Public Prosecutor [2022] 2 SLR 587 (“Wu Zhi Yong”) at [15] (albeit in the related context of s 64 of the RTA), the Amendment Act envisaged a new scheme of penalties for careless or inconsiderate driving in a tiered structure calibrated according to the degree of hurt caused. This is codified in the RTA as ss 65(2) to 65(5). The maximum punishments which may be imposed for each category of harm increase concomitantly with the seriousness of the harm caused; this translates into wider ranges of punishments where more serious harm is occasioned. For instance, where death is caused, s 65(2)(a) provides that a first-time offender is liable to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or to both. In contrast, the residual category (where no actual physical harm is caused, but which includes cases of non-personal injury or potential harm) captured in s 65(5)(a) provides that a first-time offender is liable to a fine not exceeding $1,500 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months or to both. By tiering the punishment provisions in accordance with the type of harm suffered, Parliament has given clear expression to the need to give explicit consideration to the outcomes that result from instances of careless or inconsiderate driving. This is a stark departure from the structure of s 65 of the pre-2019 RTA, where there was a single range of punishment with no differentiation based on the type and/or degree of harm caused.

35     Secondly, while Parliament has retained the distinction between reckless or dangerous driving under s 64 of the RTA and careless or inconsiderate driving under s 65 of the RTA, this distinction has been made more pronounced through the refining of the punishment provisions. These two provisions reflect the differing circumstances under which an irresponsible driving offence can occur. Accordingly, the current architecture of the RTA concerning irresponsible driving offences tiers the punishment provisions according to both harm and culpability, not dissimilar to the Penal Code.

36     At [65] of his judgment, Hoong J opined that the most suitable sentencing approach to adopt in respect of offences under s 65(3)(a) of the RTA is the two-stage, five-step sentencing framework laid down by Menon CJ in Logachev Vladislav v PP [2018] 4 SLR 609 at [75](“Logachev-hybrid approach”). At [84] to [108] of his judgment, Hoong J had set out the five steps to be taken namely:

(a)     The first step is to identify the level of harm caused by the offence and the level of culpability of the offender. The harm caused by the offence may be categorised into three levels of increasing severity: low, moderate and serious. Likewise, the offender’s culpability may be categorised into three levels: low, moderate and high. The non-exhaustive list of factors within the broad categories of harm and culpability which fashion the two axes of the matrix are:

(i)        Factors going to harm

The degree of harm caused would generally refer to the nature and degree of the grievous bodily injury caused to the victim(s): see Tang Ling Lee v PP [2018] 4 SLR 813 at [25] (“Tang Ling Lee”). The levels of harm can be divided based on the severity of the grievous hurt suffered as follows:

(i)        Low: The hurt caused can be managed with conservative treatment, with no or short periods of hospitalisation and/or medical leave. The harm occasioned would typically involve minor fractures/dislocation at less vulnerable parts of the body.

(ii)        Moderate: The injuries are of a more permanent nature and/or involve more complex fractures/dislocation which necessitate some surgical procedures with a moderate period of hospitalisation and/or medical leave.

(iii)        Serious: The injuries are of a very serious or permanent nature and/or necessitate significant surgical procedures. The victim’s daily living is usually permanently and severely affected. This includes injuries resulting in loss of limb, sight, hearing, member or other major bodily functions or paralysis.

In addition, there are two other factors which may contribute to the severity of the harm caused, namely: (a) property damage; and (b) potential harm. These factors are to be considered in tandem with the above descriptions of each level of harm.

(ii)        Factors going to culpability

This can be measured based on the following factors:

(a)       Circumstances which required the offender to exercise extra care or consideration. Some examples of these circumstances include where the offender drives: (i) within a school or residential zone; (ii) a heavy vehicle that is more difficult to control and requires a quicker reaction time; or (iii) in poor road conditions (eg, heavy rain or heavy traffic);

(b)       The manner of driving. Under this factor, it is apposite to consider any dangerous driving behaviour exhibited by the offender. This would include, for example, driving against the flow of traffic, speeding, sleepy driving, drink-driving, driving while under the influence of drugs, driving while using a mobile phone, flouting traffic rules, or “hell-riding” situations: see Tang Ling Lee at [28]. In addition, considerations pertaining to the duration of the offender’s inattention (eg, momentary or prolonged/sustained), the avoidability of the offender’s distraction or the reasonableness of the offender’s misjudgement are also relevant;

(c)       The offender’s conduct following the offence. An offender’s conduct that is “belligerent or violent” upon arrest would constitute an aggravating factor: see Edwin s/o Suse Nathen v Public Prosecutor [2013] 4 SLR 1139 (“Suse Nathen”) at [32]. Where the offender fails to stop in an attempt to evade arrest or to avoid apprehension by the authorities, this should also weigh against him: see Public Prosecutor v Lee Meng Soon [2007] 4 SLR(R) 240 at [33].

(b)     The second step is to identify the applicable indicative sentencing range in the sentencing matrix. Any term of imprisonment imposed may be accompanied by a fine of up to $5,000, if appropriate as this is explicitly provided for in s 65(3)(a) of the RTA. The sentencing judge should be alive to possibility of imposing such a combination of punishments in order to properly take into account the full sentencing range prescribed by Parliament. Hoong J opined that Parliament has stressed the importance of both harm and culpability and did not suggest that one factor should be given more weight in the sentencing analysis than the other. He emphasized that the proposed sentencing framework and the corresponding indicative ranges set out above are concerned solely with section 65(3)(a) of the RTA.

(c)     The third step is to identify the appropriate starting point within the indicative sentencing range. This is to be informed by the level of harm caused by the offence and the level of the offender’s culpability.

(d)     The fourth step is to make adjustments to the starting point where necessary to take into account the offender-specific aggravating and mitigating factors: see Ng Kean Meng Terence v Public Prosecutor [2017] 2 SLR 449 (“Terence Ng”) at [62]-[71].

(e)     The fifth step is to make further adjustments to take into account the totality principle. This step is relevant only where an offender is faced with multiple charges. It requires the court to consider the need to make further adjustments to the individual sentences for each charge to take into account the totality principle. The totality principle has been expressed in Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998 at [54] and [57] as comprising two limbs. The first limb examines whether the aggregate sentence is substantially above the normal level of sentences for the most serious of the individual offences committed. The second limb considers whether the effect of the sentence on the offender is crushing and not in keeping with his past record and his future prospects.

37     Although the present case concerns a charge of a repeat offender who had driven without reasonable consideration for others using the road and caused hurt, as both statutory provisions relate to the prescribed penalties for irresponsible driving under s 65(1), I was of the view the observations as well as the sentencing approach set out by Hoong J provides some useful guidance on how an offence punishable under s 65(4)(b) of the RTA should be dealt with.

The sentence

38     I now turn to the facts of the present case and the application of the above sentencing framework.

39     The first step is to identify the level of harm caused by the offence and the level of culpability of the offender. In the present case, I agreed with the Prosecution that the level of harm was low as the victim sustained an abrasion over the left occipital region of the head as well as tenderness over the left 8th and 9th ribs laterally. The victim was given outpatient care and granted three days of hospitalization leave from 26 to 28 February 2020.

40     As for the accused’s level of culpability, I was of the view that it was at the higher end of low as the accused failed to slow down and stop as well as to keep a proper lookout for the victim who was already walking across from accused’s left to right at the designated zebra crossing. In the present case, as the victim was crossing at a designated zebra crossing, he clearly had the right of way.

41     Paragraphs 77 to 79 of the Highway Code sets out the duties of motorist in relation to pedestrians, pedal cyclists, riders of power-assisted bicycles or personal mobility devices and drivers of mobility scooters or motorised wheelchairs who have the right of way on pedestrian crossings.

42     In addition, rule 5 of the Road Traffic (Pedestrian Crossings) Rules provides that a vehicle approaching a pedestrian crossing must proceed at such a speed that would enable him to stop before reaching the crossing.

43     I did not accept the accused’s assertion that as his in-car camera footage had not shown the victim crossing the pedestrian crossing, this attested to the fact that he had not seen the victim moments before the collision. It was clear from the LTA enforcement camera footage that the victim had almost traversed one lane of Changi Village Road carrying an orange plastic bag as the accused approached the zebra crossing. As such, the victim would have been within the accused’s line of sight and not possibly emerging from a blind spot had he kept a proper lookout. I further disagreed with his assertion he had slowed down before traversing the pedestrian crossing as it was clear from the LTA enforcement camera footage that he had not slowed down. Even if the victim had been under the influence of alcohol, the fact remains that he was crossing at a designated zebra crossing and had the right of way.

44     The second step is to identify the applicable indicative sentencing range in the sentencing matrix. Any term of imprisonment of up to two years imposed may be accompanied by a fine of up to $5,000, if appropriate as this is explicitly provided for in s 65(4)(b) of the RTA. The sentencing judge should be alive to possibility of imposing such a combination of punishments in order to properly take into account the full sentencing range prescribed by Parliament. Based on the Sue Chang sentencing framework where the level of culpability is at the higher end of low and the harm low, the appropriate sentence would be a fine.

45     The third step is to identify the appropriate starting point within the indicative sentencing range. This is to be informed by the level of harm caused by the offence and the level of the offender’s culpability. In the present case, in view of the low degree of harm caused and the culpability at the higher end of low, I was of the view that the starting point should be a fine of at least $3,000.

46     The fourth step is to make adjustments to the starting point where necessary to take into account the offender-specific aggravating and mitigating factors. In the present case, I considered the fact that the accused had pleaded guilty to the charge, he had rendered immediate assistance to the victim, he had cooperated with the authorities and had a dated previous conviction. This antecedent had already been taken into account under the “repeat offender” provision for the purpose of this charge. I also considered that two months prior to the present charge on 21 December 2019, the accused had paid composition of $200 for an offence of speeding. Nevertheless, I was mindful that there were no culpability enhancing factors such as speeding in the present case. Accordingly, I was of the view that a downward calibration to a fine of $2,000 was warranted in view of these mitigating factors.

47     The fifth step was not applicable in the present case as the accused faced a single charge.

Disqualification order

48     In deciding on the appropriate period of disqualification, I was mindful that the disqualification order and the sentence imposed are not mutually compensatory; an increase in the quantum of the fine imposed should not be taken to mandate the imposition of a reduced period of disqualification than would otherwise have been ordered: Edwin s/o Suse Nathen v Public Prosecutor [2013] 4 SLR 1139 at [13].

49     In determining the appropriate length of the disqualification order, I was guided by the approach in the recent case of Kwan Weiguang v Public Prosecutor [2022] SGHC 121 (“Kwan”). Although Aedit Abdullah J dealt with an appeal against a disqualification order of 15 months that was imposed on the appellant for an offence of dangerous driving under s 64(1) and punishable under s 64(2C)(a) of the RTA, the factors considered are relevant to the present case. He held at [59] and [61] that the objective of the disqualification order is as follow:

A driving disqualification order combines three sentencing objectives: punishment, protection of the public and deterrence (Edwin s/o Suse Nathen v Public Prosecutor [2013] 4 SLR 1139 (“Edwin Nathen”) at [13]). Often, due to the limited range of fines in the punishment provision, the impact of a disqualification order is likely to be felt much more acutely than any marginal increase in the quantum of the fine (see Edwin Nathen at [13])…

61    A disqualification order also serves to remove the privilege to drive from those who have abused it, and this also reinforces the notion of shared usage of the roads. It must be remembered that driving is an inherently dangerous activity. The licence to drive is a privilege accorded to persons who, through a series of properly administered tests, have demonstrated that they are capable of meeting the standards expected of a reasonably competent driver (Jali bin Mohd Yunos v Public Prosecutor [2014] 4 SLR 1059 at [36]). The former Minister for Home Affairs, Prof S Jayakumar, had expressed the following view which reinforces this notion (see Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (28 March 1990) vol 55 at col 960): “… we must get every driver in Singapore to note that when he is issued a licence, it is in fact a very special privilege and it is granted on condition that he drives in a responsible manner bearing in mind the interest of others.”

50     In Kwan at [65] to [71], the relevant and material factors when determining the period of disqualification are:

(a)     Circumstances of the commission of the offence;

(b)     Continued ability to drive and attitude towards other road users.

51     In the present case, the accused had failed to slow down and stop when approaching a zebra crossing. As he had failed to keep a proper lookout, he had collided into the right side of the victim who was already walking across the designated zebra crossing and hurt was caused. I agreed with the Prosecution that the High Court decision in Kwan related to a charge of dangerous driving under s 64 and not s 65 of the RTA where no hurt was caused to the victim. The High Court in that case had stated that the 12 months disqualification was confined where no hurt was caused. As there is no prescribed minimum disqualification period for the present charge, s 42(1) of the RTA applies and the court may order him to be disqualified from holding or obtaining a driving licence for life or for such period as the court may think fit. In my view, as the present case involves causing of hurt to a vulnerable victim crossing a designated pedestrian crossing who had the right of way, a disqualification for a period of 15 months for all classes of vehicles was necessary as a form of deterrence to address the accused’s poor and irresponsible driving.

52     All things considered, I was of the view that a sentence of $2,000 in default seven days’ imprisonment and a disqualification from holding or obtaining all classes of driving licences for a period of 15 months with effect from 23 November 2022 to 22 February 2024 to be just and appropriate in the circumstances.
