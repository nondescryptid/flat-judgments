Introduction

1       The accused, Valdez Ana Rubirosa Manalo, is a 50-year-old female Filipino/Singapore PR. She was charged with three counts of employing a foreign employee without a valid work permit in contravention of section 5(1) of the Employment of Foreign Manpower Act (Chapter 91A, 2009 Rev Ed) (“EFMA”). Each of the three charges attract enhanced punishment under section 5(6)(b)(i) of the EFMA because of her previous conviction under section 5(1) of the same Act.

2       On 30 November 2022, I delivered judgment on the present case where I read out my brief oral remarks to explain why I was not calling for the accused to enter her defence on the three charges. I also stated that my brief oral remarks were delivered subject to full grounds being issued in due course, if necessary. The Prosecution has since applied for my written grounds of decision. Pursuant to section 298 of the Criminal Procedure Code 2010 (“CPC”), I am issuing my written grounds of decision to facilitate parties on their next course of action.

Charges

3       The three charges proceeded with by the Prosecution were largely similar in that they alleged that on at least one occasion during various periods of time, the accused did employ a particular foreigner, “…as a food preparation assistant for [her] business FVP Trading and Services…without a valid work pass…”, in contravention of section 5(1) of the EFMA. With the exception of the second foreigner (B2), each of the other two foreigners, were in fact, also employed with a valid work permit to work as a foreign domestic worker (“FDW”) for the same period of time. For B2, the period set out in the charge (i.e. her alleged employment as a “food preparation assistant”) largely overlapped with her employment period as a FDW.

4       For easy reference, I have reproduced the text of the first charge, and also set out the differentiating details of the other charges in a table below. I will refer to the three foreigners collectively as “the Foreigners”.

5       The first charge (MAC-906570-2021) reads:

You… are charged that you, on at least 1 occasion between July 2019 and September 2019, at [residential address], did employ a foreign employee, one Braga Marites Bagasbas (FIN: XXXXXXX), as a food preparation assistant for your business FVP Trading and Services (UEN: 53101532K) located at [business address], without a valid work pass, and you have thereby committed an offence under section 5(1) of the Employment of Foreign Manpower Act (Chapter 91A, 2009 Rev Ed) (“EFMA”).

And further,

That you, prior to the commission of the said offence, were on 17 April 2012 in Court 23 of the State Courts convicted on two charges under section 5(1) of the EFMA (vide MAC-001621-2012 and MAC-001622-2012) and sentenced to $1,800 fine per charge, which convictions and sentences have not been set aside to-date, and you are therefore liable to be punished with enhanced punishment under section 5(6)(b)(i) of the EFMA.

Test at the end of the Prosecution’s Case

9       The procedure for trial is set out at section 230 of the CPC. In the present case, the Prosecution, having set out its evidence in the ASOF, submitted that there was a case to answer for all three of the charges, while the Defence argued that the defence of the accused should not be called.

10     As provided in section 230 (1)(j) of the CPC, if the court after considering the evidence provided by the Prosecution is of the view that “there is some evidence which is not inherently incredible and which satisfies each and every element of the charge as framed by the prosecutor or as altered or framed by the court, the court must call on the accused to give his defence”. Section 230(1)(k) of the CPC also makes clear that “the court must order a discharge amounting to an acquittal if it is of the view that there is no such evidence…”.

11     The Defence referred to the case of PP v Wong Wee Keong & Anor [2016] 3 SLR 965 (“Wong Wee Keong”), where See Kee Oon JC (as he then was) discussed the test that has to be applied at this stage of the proceedings. At [33], his Honour stated:

As explained by Chan Sek Keong CJ in Re Nalpon Zero Geraldo Mario [20121 3 SLR 440 ("Re Nalpon") at [261, this is a statutory codification of the test which was set out in the seminal decision of the Privy Council in Haw Tua Tau v PP [1981-19821 SLR@ 133 ("Haw Tua Tau"). The question, Chan CJ explained, was not whether the evidence as it presently stood had already established the guilt of the accused beyond a reasonable doubt but whether the evidence — if it were all accepted as accurate — would do so (at [261). At a minimum the evidence put forward has to cover every constituent element of the offence in question; if it did not, then it would plainly be impossible for a conviction to be lawfully sustained. In deciding whether to call on the accused to enter his defence, regard should be had to the following guiding propositions:

(a)    All evidence of primary fact should be accepted as being true, unless it is so inherently incredible that no reasonable person would be able to accept it as being true or if it has been discredited or shown to be wholly unreliable. This may happen, for example, during the course of cross-examination (see Haw Tua Tau at [151; Re Nalpon at [251).

(b)    Inferences may be drawn, but only if they are reasonable — it is not enough that the inference may be credible or not inherently incredible. In this regard, there is a different standard which applies to primary facts and inferences insofar as the former should be accepted as true unless it is inherently incredible whereas inferences can only be accepted if they can reasonably be drawn (see Re Nalpon at [251). It is not necessary that the inference be irresistible or that it must be only possible inference that may be drawn from the facts.

(c)    The totality of the evidence has to be considered when determining whether evidence is so inherently incredible that it can be accepted or if the inferences sought to be drawn are reasonable enough to pass muster. The court cannot only look to those parts of the evidence which are favourable to the prosecution's case and ignore those which are detrimental: i.e., it cannot pick out only the plums and leave the duff behind (see Public Prosecutor v IC Automation (S) Pte Ltd [1996] 2 SLR(R) 799 at [171).

[emphasis added]

Elements of the Offences

12     In line with the words of See JC (as he then was), it is important to consider each and every constituent element of the offences in question to determine if there was evidence put forward to satisfy that element.

13     Based on the wording of the charges, it appeared that the elements that the Prosecution has to prove for each charge was as follows:

(a)     On at least one occasion at the accused’s home, during the duration of one or three months specified in the particular charge, the accused did employ each of the said foreigner (B1-B3) as a “food preparation assistant” for the accused’s business FVP Trading which was located at a different address;

(b)     B1-B3 do not have a valid work pass for such employment; and

(c)     (For purposes of attracting the enhanced punishment only) that the accused has a previous conviction under s 5(1) of the EFMA.

14     However, the Prosecution seemed to have a somewhat different take on what it needed to prove as regards the first element. As submitted by the prosecutor,[note: 1] even though the Foreigners were only asked by the accused to work on at least one occasion for a certain number of days and for a certain number of hours, the Prosecution’s case was that the Foreigners were still employed by the accused as “food preparation assistants” to work for her business for the entire period of one to three months stipulated in the charges. This was essentially also the entire time they were working in the accused’s home as FDWs. This was what the Prosecution said that it had to prove.

Prosecution’s Arguments

15     The MOM prosecutor set out the Prosecution’s case as follows[note: 2]:

The crux of our case, Your Honour, at the outset is not really a question of whether or not the foreigners were employed. And the reason we say this is because the EFMA regulates work passes. Not the question of employment and not the question of when the foreigner is employed.

Now, we accept the word, “Employ” was used in Section 5(1), but it is used in the context of whether or not there is a valid work pass…

…Section 12 of the EFMA…sets out the extent of the validity of the work pass. And so when we say that the validity of the work pass is circumscribed to only domestic duties, we are also saying that any act that falls outside the scope of domestic duties must mean that the accused needs a separate work pass that is valid for these acts to be carried out legally, Your Honour. And if she or---rather if the foreigners do not have this valid work pass any acts that fall outside the scope of domestic duties must be illegal employment.

[emphasis mine]

16     The Prosecution argued that based on the ASOF, the three Foreigners, B1-B3, named in the three charges did work for the accused’s business during the respective periods. Such work included peeling the bananas, wrapping the peeled bananas with spring roll skins, cutting vegetables and grating sweet potatoes, wrapping the vegetables and sweet potatoes with the spring roll skin, and placing food into plastic containers for the accused’s business. The accused would then bring the prepared food items to her business.

17     The Prosecution further argued that the work done by the Foreigners fell within the definition of “employ” in s2 of the EFMA as the accused had on at least 1 occasion during the period in Table 2, “instructed” the Foreigners to do work for the food stall (and they duly did so) and she thereby “engage[d] or use[d] the service of [the Foreigners] for the purpose of any work”.

18     Based on the definition of a “valid work pass” at s 12 of the EFMA, as the Foreigners were employed as domestic helpers only during their respective periods of employment as FDWs, they were restricted to only the “trade, sector, occupation or type of employment” that their work passes were approved for.

19     In addition, the Employment of Foreign Manpower (Work Passes) Regulations 2012 (the “Regulations”) at reg 4(2), and regulatory condition 3 at Part II of the Fourth Schedule (“Regulatory Condition 3”) define the scope of work that a FDW is allowed to perform, and restricts a FDW to “only household and domestic duties at…the residential address stated in the work permit”.

20     The Prosecution submitted that the accused would have been aware of the work permit conditions and regulatory conditions applicable to the Foreigners and enshrined in the Regulations at the time she made the application in light of her declaration to this effect on each of the three times that she made the applications (one for each of the three Foreigners)

21     The MOM had also given a press release in reply to an opinion piece written in the Straits Times on 30 March 2017 (the “Opinion Piece”), whereby the MOM had explained that “Employers cannot use Foreigners to help out in businesses”. Further, the MOM had regularly released other publicly available documents in multiple languages concerning the scope of work that an Foreigner can perform. Thus, where an Foreigner is asked to do work for a business, that would be outside the scope of household and domestic duties, and consequently, outside the scope of allowable activities in her work pass. In any event, ignorance of the law is no defence.

22     In the present case, as the Foreigners were asked by the accused to carry out food preparation work for her business, this was accordingly, outside the scope of their household and domestic duties.

23     The Prosecution also cited two cases involving offenders who had pleaded guilty to charges under s 5(1) EFMA (the “PG cases”). Each of the two PG cases involved an employer of a FDW who instructed the said FDW to do work for the employer’s business, or to perform tasks (painting in respect of one of the PG cases) which was outside the scope of their domestic duties. These cases were:

(a)      PP v Lim Sheng Song (unreported) [SC-903482-2020] (“Lim Sheng Song”), where the offender pleaded guilty and was convicted of, inter alia, s 5(1) EFMA offences for instructing his FDW to prepare food for his food stall. The food preparation occurred at the accused’s residence. The FDW did not receive any additional compensation for assisting in the accused’s business. The offender was sentenced to a fine of $10,000.

(b)      PP v Tan Kim Hai (unreported) [SC-902551-2020] (“Tan Kim Hai”) where the offender pleaded guilty and was convicted of, inter alia, a s 5(1) EFMA offence for instructing his FDW to paint his daughter’s rooms, a dividing wall between his home and a neighbour’s home, and the rooftop of his home. The offender was sentenced to a fine of $5,000 for the s 5(1) EFMA offence.

Defence’s Arguments

24     The Defence identified the elements of each charge to be as follows:

(a)     That the accused employed the Foreigners as “food preparation assistants”;

(b)     That the said employment was for the accused's business, FVP Trading and Services;

(c)     That the alleged employment of the Foreigners as “food preparation assistants” was without valid work passes.

(d)     That she has thereby contravened section 5(1) of the EFMA.

25     The focus of the Defence’s submissions was that the first element of the charge was not made out, and hence, the other elements need not be considered. I have summarised the main aspects of the Defence’s arguments below.

26     First, the isolated instances of assistance on the part of the Foreigners, who were FDWs, cannot, by any stretch of imagination, be construed as evidence of employment by the accused as “food preparation assistants”, when they were in the main doing their duties as domestic helpers. Such assistance cannot be construed as "usage" within the meaning of the word "employ" as defined under s 2 EFMA.

27     In this regard, the charges state that on at least one occasion, the Foreigners spent between one and a half to 3 hours assisting the accused in food preparation. There was no other evidence suggesting any further involvement. In contrast, their regular working hours as domestic helpers, far exceeded the time that they were assisting the accused in food preparation. As such, their assistance in food assistance was de minimis, and cannot be construed to mean that the accused employed them as “food preparation assistants” for her business This could be seen from the Table below.

28     Second, the Foreigners were engaged as FDWs and had valid work permits as FDWs, and they performed their duties as FDWs. In this regard, the following aspects of the undisputed facts were pertinent:

(a)     At all material times, the accused was the legitimate employer of the three Foreigners who were FDWs, and they were respectively in the lawful employment of the accused[note: 3];

(b)     The Foreigners had valid work permits to work for the accused;

(c)     At all material times, the Foreigners only worked at the residential address specified on their work permits, which was the residence of the accused;[note: 4]

(d)     The main duties of the said Foreigners were to look after the household and the accused's four children,[note: 5]and included, inter-alia, "taking care of the accused's 8-year-old child which included sending at 7 am and fetching him from school at 1 pm, preparing food for him (breakfast before he leaves for school, and lunch upon his return) and the family (three meals), and performing household chores such as sweeping and mopping the floor, washing the two toilets, ironing the clothes and doing the laundry, etc. At the material time, the family also had a pet dog that needed to be attended to by BI-B3, such as the cleaning of its cage, feeding, bathing and taking the dog for walks every day."

29     Third, as regards the purported employment of the Foreigners as “food preparation assistants” as alleged in the charges, there was no evidence of any of the usual incidents of employment. The only evidence of employment of the Foreigners was evidence of their employment as fulltime domestic helpers:

(a)     For one, the three Foreigners never worked at the accused's shop at Lucky Plaza, and it was not alleged that they had assisted the accused in her business at the shop. The snacks sold at the shop were made by the accused at home, and as for the preparation of these snacks, as stated at [5] of the ASOF, "the accused would do most of the work for her business herself".

(b)     There were isolated occasions, as encapsulated at [5] of the ASOF, where the accused had asked the three Foreigners for assistance, in the preparation of the snacks. Even then, the accused would do most of the work, and the task was not delegated wholly to the Foreigners.

(c)     The occasions when the accused asked for assistance were few and far between, and the respective charges only specified "on at least one occasion" during the respective periods of employment. The Defence argued that such mere ad hoc and isolated request(s) for assistance on the part of the accused could not be sufficient to even draw any reasonable inference that the said Foreigners were employed as “food preparation assistants” by the accused.

(d)     The accused did not set down a specific roster or specific schedule or time for the Foreigners to render assistance. They helped when they were able to, when they were in the midst of their main duties as domestic housekeepers. As stated at [5] of the ASOF, "when they were able to in the midst of their above-mentioned domestic duties at the residential address, the accused, had asked BI-B3 to assist her in preparing some ingredients . . . for her business as she was very busy and needed help." (emphasis as underlined)

(e)     The ASOF also referred to the Foreigners as rendering "assistance", as opposed to stating that they "worked" for the accused. In addition, the ASOF referred to the accused “asking them for assistance”, as opposed to “ordering them to work” or similar derivatives, which was inconsistent with the said Foreigners being employed as “food preparation assistants” by the accused.

(f)     In any event, the Foreigners were also under no obligation, legal or otherwise, to render assistance. As stated at [6] of the ASOF, "(t)heir assistance was voluntary, and they only assisted when they are free and able to." They assisted only when they were able to, and "(s)ometimes, they would be too busy to assist", as stated at [5] of the ASOF.

(g)     The facts clearly showed that the Foreigners themselves "would voluntarily offer to help the accused since she was busy,” rather than being instructed by the accused to carry out the work as “food preparation assistants.”

(h)     The fact that their assistance was purely at their sole discretion and within their absolute control, was evidenced by the statement at [7] of the ASOF, that "they only assisted when they are free and able to".

(i)     Simply put, the Foreigners were not even under any obligation or compulsion to assist. This must remove all doubt that they were employed as “food preparation assistants” by the accused at any time.

(j)     It was also stated at [5] of the ASOF that, "(t)he assistance provided by BI-B3 was only incidental to their main duties which was to look after the household and the 4 children". (emphasis as underlined)

(k)     In addition, there was no regularity or any form of continuity in their rendering assistance to the accused. As stated in all the charges, they assistance the accused" on at least 1 occasion".

(l)     The said Foreigners were not paid for their assistance, nor given any incentives, and neither did they expect any form of payment or compensation "for such incidental assistance". As stated at [6] of the ASOF, "they also did not expect to be paid or compensated for such incidental assistance.”

30     Section 2 of the EFMA provides a very broad definition of the meaning of the word "employ". Even by that broad definition of “employ”, there was totally no evidence to prove the firstelement that the accused had employed the said Foreigners as “food preparation assistants”. Specifically, the fact that:

(a)     the rendering of assistance was voluntary, and at the discretion of the Foreigners, and

(b)     coupled by the gratuitous nature of such assistance,

absolutely disavowed the existence of any employment of the Foreigners as “food preparation assistants” by the accused. Logic and common sense must prevail.

31     The Defence also cited cases where the words “employ” or “employment” have been interpreted. The Defence pointed out that:

(a)     The manner of remuneration and the degree of control exercised by the alleged employer over the workers, are significant considerations in determining the existence of an employment relationship even though they are not the only factors (see PP v Han Meng Siew [2010] SGDC 480 (“Han Meng Siew”), which cited Tamilkodi s/o Pompayan v PP (1999)1 SLR 702 (“Tamilkodi”) and Soh Lip Hwa v PP (2001) 4 SLR 198 (“Soh Lip Hwa”). The latter were cases of employment of immigration offenders under the Immigration Act (IA), where the definitions of the term "employ" in the EFMA and the IA were similarly drafted.

(b)     Even where an alleged offender had "used the services" of the immigration offenders, and had also exercised control and supervision over them, this does not necessarily mean that he has employed them (see Lee Boon Leng Joseph v Public Prosecutor [1996] 3 SLR(R) 655 (“Lee Boon Leng Joseph”)).

32     In the present case, the Defence reiterated that the facts showed that there was no remuneration, nor control over the Foreigners.

33     In addition, the Defence argued that this was also not a case of an unscrupulous employer of a defenceless FDW who had sought to abuse and exploit her FDW. As stated at [7] of the ASOF : "At all material times, BI-B3 never complained about being requested to assist the accused to prepare the ingredients for her Business. Further, BI-B3 had adequate rest, sleep and food, as well as lodging and their salaries were paid on time. They also had 4 off-days a month on Sundays. Their welfare was safeguarded while working for the accused, and they did not feel exploited or overworked at any point in time by the accused."

34     Further, the Defence argued that:

(a)     The accused did not have the necessary mens rea to employ the said Foreigners as “food preparation assistants” for her business, as exemplified by the following statement at [5] of the ASOF, that "the accused was unaware that this innocent request for assistance would end up being against the rules and regulations set by the Ministry of Manpower."

(b)     As for the Foreigners themselves, the ASOF was silent as to whether any of them perceived themselves to be employed as “food preparation assistants” of the accused and/or her business.

Court’s Decision

35     It was not disputed that the accused did employ B1-B3 as FDWs to perform clearly domestic duties. It was also undisputed that each of the Foreigners had a valid work permit to perform their role as FDWs at the material time. In addition, it was not disputed that the accused has a previous conviction for employing a foreigner without a valid work permit, albeit that the facts of that case were highly dissimilar to those of the current case (see Annex E and F). In any event, the Prosecution, correctly in my view, made no suggestion that the accused’s previous conviction, in any way, meant that she was culpable in the present case, or that her antecedent was somehow indicative of her guilt on the present charges.

36     The main remaining issue was whether the Prosecution had adduced sufficient evidence to establish the essential element of each charge, namely that the accused did employ each of the Foreigners (B1, B2 and B3) as a “food preparation assistant” over essentially the same period that they were working as FDWs for the accused under a separate and valid work permit.

Meaning of “employ” under the EFMA

Relative dearth of cases on meaning of “employ” under the EFMA

37     I start off my analysis by considering the definition of “employ” as set out in s 2 of the EFMA. This provides that:

“employ” means to engage or use the service of any person for the purpose —

(a)    of any work; or

(b)    of providing any training for that person,

whether under a contract of service or otherwise, and with or without salary.

38     Based on what was put forward by parties, there has not been any High Court decision that has specifically explored or considered the meaning of the word “employ”, as it is currently defined in the EFMA, although in Han Meng Siew, the District Court had made reference to cases that had dealt with the word “employ” in the context of the IA to help construe the meaning of “employ” in the EFMA (see [61] - [62] below).

39     Further, while the Prosecution put forward the two PG cases (see [23] above), both of which were District Court decisions, as rightly acknowledged by the Prosecution, the courts in both of those cases did not actually have to consider the meaning or scope of the word “employ” in the EFMA,[note: 6] as this was obviously not a contested issue in those plead guilty cases. Thus, parties did not have to address either of the District Courts, nor did the judges in those cases have the opportunity, or the need, to consider arguments on this matter. This was certainly not the situation in the present case, where the question of whether the accused did “employ” any of the Foreigners, was a contested matter and was in fact the main issue in dispute.

40     In any event, there were significant dissimilarities between the facts of the present case with the two PG cases. As pointed out by the Defence,

(a)     In Lim Sheng Song, the offender Lim, employed a few foreigners to work at his food stall and only one of them had a valid work permit to work for him as a domestic helper.

(i)       The other two were not FDWs of the offender, and were not in his employ in any way. From the facts presented which pertained to one of these two foreigners, the bulk of the time of that foreigner (Mar) spent was at the food stall with only a small amount of time each day actually doing domestic chores as a FDW (the latter being a role that she was employed to do).

(ii)       Even the FDW of the offender spent most of her time working for his food stall business, amounting to about 9 hours per day, for a period of about 3 years.

(b)     In Tan Kim Hai , the offender instructed the domestic helper to undertake work as a painter essentially, which was not part of her domestic household chores. This work included painting the offender’s daughter’s rooms for six hours each day for four days, painting the dividing wall for one week, as well as painting the rooftop of the house.

41     Having evaluated the documents relating to the PG cases, and compared them with the ASOF, I would highlight the following features of the present case that were clearly different from the facts of the PG cases:

(i)       In the present case, it was not disputed that B1-B3 had volunteered to assist the accused;

(ii)       They would also only help the accused only when they were free to do so;

(iii)       The tasks that they helped the accused with (which the Prosecution alleged made them “food preparation assistants”) were only incidental to B1-B3’s domestic work; and

(iv)       The time that B1-B3 spent on such work of “food preparation”, which was stated in the ASOF to be “at least one occasion for 2-3 hours” (in the case of B1 and B3), or “at least one occasion for 1.5 hours” (in the case of B2) (see Table 2 of [5] of the ASOF), was miniscule as compared to the time spent on doing domestic duties.[note: 7]

42     In contrast, for the PG cases, there was no suggestion that the work the foreigners did at the food stall, or as a painter, was ‘incidental’ to their work as FDWs, or that they had merely ‘volunteered’ to help or to assist the offender, as opposed to being instructed or directed to do the work. Nor was there evidence that the foreigners in the PG cases would only do the work when they were willing and able to, or that they had a right of refusal to carry out the work at the food stall or to perform the painting work respectively.

43     I would add that in regards the PG cases, the MOM had also tabled additional charges against the offenders relating to the unsafe conditions that the offenders had put these workers through in the course of their illegal employment. In contrast, there was no suggestion that the FDWs in the present case were exposed to anything remotely resembling unsafe conditions when they cut vegetables, peeled bananas etc.

44     All in all, the PG cases were clearly not useful to the Prosecution in its attempt to establish its case, even on a prima facie basis. Accordingly, I say no more about the PG cases.

Parliamentary Reports on the EFMA

45     The Prosecution also tendered the reports of the Parliamentary Debates pertaining to the second reading of the EFMA Bill. However, the Prosecution appeared to take the position that the Parliamentary Debates were not very helpful, as they did not shed light on the interpretation of the word “employ” in the EFMA. With respect, I do not agree with the assessment of the Prosecution that the Debates were unhelpful.

46     For one, the Parliamentary Debates made clear that enhanced penalties in the EFMA, which relate to the employment of foreigners without a valid work permit, were introduced, inter alia, to protect the well-being of the foreign and local workers. [note: 8] In the present case, this identified mischief which the EFMA was meant to address, was clearly not a real concern.

47     In this regard, as noted at [7] of the ASOF:

“At all material times, B1-B3 never complained about being requested to assist the accused to prepare the ingredients for her Business. Further, B1-B3 had adequate rest, sleep and food, as well as lodging and their salaries were paid on time. They also had 4 off-days a month on Sundays. Their welfare was safeguarded while working for the accused, and they did not feel exploited or overworked at any point in time by the accused.”

48     I would discuss more about the Parliamentary Debates when I consider the Prosecution’s argument that the cases relied on by the Defence (as well as some cases tendered by the Prosecution), which pertained to situations of illegal employment under the IA, were not relevant when considering whether the Foreigners in the present case were employed in breach of the EFMA.

Cases that discuss factors indicative of employment

49     Neither the Prosecution nor the Defence was able to tender any High Court decision that dealt directly with the meaning of the word “employ” as it is currently defined in the EFMA. Nevertheless, both parties tendered cases which had interpreted the meaning of ‘employ’ in the context of the prosecution of offenders for employing illegal foreigners in contravention of section 57(1)(e) of the IA. In my view, these cases provided useful guidance, as the definition of “employ” in the IA was similar to the definition of “employ” in the EFMA. I illustrate this below, with the similarities underlined for emphasis.

Definition of “Employ”

EFMA

Immigration Act

“employ” means to engage or use the service of any person for the purpose —

(a) of any work; or

(b) of providing any training for that person,

whether under a contract of service or otherwise, and with or without salary.

“employ” means to engage or use the service of any person, whether under a contract of service or otherwise, and with or without salary.”



50     I discuss these cases in greater detail below

51     In Tamilkodi:

(a)     The offender faced four charges of employing illegal foreigners as cleaners in contravention of s 57(1)(e) of the IA. In construing the meaning of “employ” under the IA, Yong Pung How CJ made it clear at [20] and [21] that “…the manner of remuneration and the degree of control exercised by the alleged employer over the workers continue to be significant considerations in determining the existence of an employment relationship”, even though these were “…not the only considerations which the court will take cognisance of when determining the existence of an employment relationship.”

(b)     In finding that the offender did employ the illegal foreigners, the court there also considered the fact that the latter were the only people performing the cleaning work at the premises that the offender was contractually required to carry out. Further, the illegal foreigners were doing such work on a daily basis over the course of one to four months, during which period the offender was also present.

(c)     While the offender denied employing the foreigners, and even claimed that money that he passed to them were “loans”, the Court considered it “…highly suspicious that the appellant would loan such large amounts to four people whom, on the credible evidence, he had met only once or twice previously…”, and found that: “(t)he irresistible inference from this must be that the Nepalese witnesses were the appellant’s own employees.” (see [27]).

52     Next, I consider the case of Soh Lip Hwa:

(a)     Here, the offender faced one charge of employing a Chinese national, Zhou, as a general worker, in contravention of s 57(1)(e) of the IA. Referring to the same definition of “employ” in the IA that was considered in Tamilkodi, Yong CJ at [35] of Soh Lip Hwa affirmed the earlier position set out in Tamilkodi that the manner of remuneration and the degree of control exercised over the workers continue to be significant considerations in determining the existence of an employment relationship, though they were not the only considerations which the court will take cognisance of.

(b)     On the facts of Soh Lip Hwa, Yong CJ found that the offender had indeed employed Zhou, since there was evidence that the offender had control over Zhou (see [36]), and had given money and a handphone to Zhou (see [41]).

(c)     It should also be noted that in Soh Lip Hwa, the court made it clear at [43] that the offence under s 57(1)(e) was not one of strict liability and that the Prosecution has the burden of establishing that Soh has the necessary mens rea, that is, he knew or had reasonable grounds to believe that Zhou was an immigration offender.

53     In the earlier case of Lee Boon Leng Joseph:

(a)     The Court also had to consider whether the offender had employed the illegal foreigners as cleaners within the meaning of section 2 of the IA.

(b)     Here, despite finding that there was evidence of control by the offender of the foreign workers, Yong CJ nonetheless found that the Prosecution had failed to prove that the offender had employed the foreigners. His Honour stated at [35]:

…there was a reasonable possibility that the appellant had merely “used the services” of the workers and not “employed” them in the true sense of the word. The matter can be tested this way: what recourse would the appellant have if the Indian workers did not turn up for work one day? Would he be left to his own devices or could he demand from Samy that he supply substitute workers? The payment of $300 a month in respect of PW6 and Samy’s responsibility to pay PW7’s monthly salary raises the reasonable possibility that the appellant could look to Samy in such a situation. That weighs against the Prosecution and raises a reasonable doubt that the appellant did not stand in the position of an employer, viz the worker.

[emphasis added]

54      Loh Kim Lan v Public Prosecutor [2001] 1 SLR(R) 15 (“Loh Kim Lan”), a case tendered by the Prosecution, also dealt with the definition of “employ” in the context of illegal employment under of s 57(1)(e) of the IA:

(a)     Here, the offender was alleged to have employed Ling, a PRC national, as a hostess. At [28], Yong CJ reiterated the same considerations adopted in the earlier cases. His Honour stated:

Nevertheless, several factors will still be taken into consideration by the court when deciding whether or not the test of “employment” has been satisfied in each case. These factors, which I set out in Tamilkodi s/o Pompayan v PP, include the manner of remuneration and the degree of control exercised by the alleged employer over the workers. I made it clear in that case, however, that these factors are non-exhaustive and each case must still be looked at in entirety when determining the existence of an employment relationship.

(b)     In finding that the second appellant had indeed employed the foreign hostess, the court considered that the foreigner was subjected to the control or directions of the second appellant, and that in any event, the presumption laid down in s 57(8) of the IA applied as the foreigner was found in the premises of the second appellant, and hence the latter was presumed to have employed her knowing that she was an immigration offender (see [40] and [41]).

55      Swee Lay Ching v Public Prosecutor [2003] 3 SLR(R) 62 (“Swee Lay Ching”) was another case tendered by the Prosecution.

(a)     Here, an illegal foreigner, Hu, worked as a stall assistant at the offender’s chicken rice stall. There was no evidence that the offender was present or had control of Hu, when Hu was arrested at his stall.

(b)     The Court stated that presence and control were merely indicators of employment and were not necessary to prove employment. On the facts, as Hu was seen by the police to be serving customers at the stall, and she also admitted that she worked for the offender, the Court upheld the offender’s conviction for employing Hu.

56     From the above contested cases, the following principles can be distilled to help determine if a person can be said to have “employed” a foreigner:

(a)     The manner of remuneration and the degree of control exercised by the alleged employer over the foreigner (see Tamilkodi and Soh Lip Hwa);

(b)     Whether, and to the extent that the foreigner was subjected to the control and directions of the alleged employer in respect of performing the work (see Loh Kim Lan);

(c)     What recourse would the alleged employer have if the foreigner did not turn up or perform the work (see Lee Boon Leng Joseph);

(d)     Whether the foreigner was the only one doing the work which the alleged employer needed to be done (see Tamilkodi);

(e)     Whether there was any presumption that operated to show that the foreigner was employed by the alleged employer (see Loh Kim Lan); and

(f)     Whether there was any evidence or admission by the foreigner that she was working for the alleged employer (see Swee Lay Ching);

57     I would also add that from the above cases, it could be seen that where the alleged employer was found to have employed the foreigner:

(a)     The evidence was that the foreigner was working “full time” for the alleged employer in the work that they were employed to do i.e. as a cleaner, general worker, hostess etc. In this respect, the foreigner was not concurrently employed by the same alleged employer to perform some other role, or to carry out some other work for that same employer;

(b)     The foreigner received some salary or other form of ascertainable remuneration for the work that they did;

(c)     The foreigner appeared to work under fixed or determined hours or periods of work, rather than on an ad hoc basis;

(d)     The employment occurred at the site pertaining to the specific role/work he was performing, such as the premises being cleaned (Lee Boon Leng Joseph, Tamilkodi), the hawker stall (Swee Lay Ching), the worksite (Soh Lip Hwa), or the nightclub (Loh Kim Lan). The foreigner was also frequently found/arrested at these work premises;

(e)     There was no evidence that the foreigner could or did refuse the work directed/provided to them by their alleged employers; and

(f)     There was no evidence the foreigner did the work on a “voluntary” basis, or was merely “assisting” without pay.

No evidence accused “employed” the Foreigners as per the charges

58     As noted above, the definition of “employ” in the EFMA and the IA are similar. In fact, it was clear that from the words of Dr Ng Eng Hen, the then Minister for Manpower who spoke at the second reading of the EFMA, that the EFMA was intended to consolidate the enforcement regimes in the IA and the Employment of Workers Acts. [note: 9] What was particularly instructive is that Dr Ng did NOT say that it was the intention of the MOM, or of Parliament, that the definitions of similar terms and concepts, such as that of “employ” in the EFMA, was or should be construed or interpreted differently from their predecessor provisions in the legislation that the EFMA consolidated.

59     Thus, in my view, the factors and circumstances identified by case authorities to be relevant in explaining the scope of the word “employ” in the context of the IA, clearly remained useful and instructive in construing the meaning and scope of this same word ‘employ’ in the EFMA.

60     In this regard, I disagreed with the Prosecution when it took the position that there was no need to refer to cases such as Tamilkodi, Soh Lip Hwa and Lee Boon Leng Joseph, which were decided in the context of the IA.[note: 10] With, respect, the Prosecution’s position does not appear to have any legal basis, nor does it draw any support from the Parliamentary Debates which, incidentally, were helpfully provided by the Prosecution.

61     I would add that there was also caselaw which supported my view that the factors identified in cases like Tamilkodi, Soh Lip Hwa and Lee Boon Leng Joseph, that dealt with employment in the context of the IA, were also relevant when determining if an employment relationship exists in the context of the EFMA. This is the decision in Han Meng Siew, a case which concerned an accused person who was alleged to have committed three counts under s 5(1) of the EFMA for allegedly employing three foreigners to work at a workshop without valid work permits.

62     At [54] of Han Meng Siew, after noting that the definition of “employ” in the IA and the EFMA were similarly drafted, the court then cited Tamilkodi and Soh Lip Hwa, and accepted that when determining the existence of an employment relationship, the manner of remuneration and the degree of control exercised by the alleged employer over the workers continue to be significant considerations, even though these were not the only considerations which the court will take cognisance of. Thereafter based on these factors and the evidence adduced, the court in Han Meng Siew found that the Prosecution had not proven the charges beyond a reasonable doubt and acquitted the alleged employer.

(1)   No sign the Foreigners were employed as “food preparation assistants”

63     Thus, clearly the considerations identified at [56] and [57] above, were also relevant in determining if the accused had employed the Foreigners as “food preparation assistants”, as alleged in the charges.

64     Having carefully considered these factors, I was of the respectful view that there was in fact no evidence that the Foreigners were so employed as alleged by the Prosecution.

(a)     First, there was no evidence that the accused exercised any control over the Foreigners, or that the Foreigners acted under her directions in performing their alleged role as “food preparation assistants”.

(i)       Instead, rather than acting under compulsion or directions from the accused, as made clear at [5] of the ASOF, “B1-B3 would voluntarily offer to help the accused, since she was busy.” Further, at [6] of the ASOF, it was emphasised that Their [B1 – B3’s] assistance was voluntary, and they only assisted when they are free and able to.”

(ii)       In this regard, I agreed with the Defence that the accused asked for help for food preparation, as opposed to instructing or directing the Foreigners to carry out work as “food preparation assistants”.

(iii)       Further, because of the voluntary nature of the assistance that the Foreigners was rendering to the accused, the accused would have no recourse against them, should they decide not to continue to assist her in food preparation (contrast this with the situation in Lee Boon Leng Joseph).

(iv)       In such circumstances, the accused could hardly be said to have employed them in the capacity of “food preparation assistants”.

(b)     Second, there was also clearly no evidence that the Foreigners were paid, remunerated, or in any way rewarded or profited from their supposed “additional” employment as “food preparation assistants” (unlike the situations in the above cases put forward by both the Prosecution and the Defence). In this regard, it was made clear at [6] of the ASOF that: “B1-B3 were not paid for their assistance, nor given any incentives.” In fact, as made clear at [6], “They also did not expect to be paid or compensated for such incidental assistance.”

(c)     Third, unlike the situation in the cases discussed above, where the foreigners were illegally employed specifically to perform the work for which their employers were subsequently convicted of making them perform, in the case of B1 – B3, the “assistance provided by B1-B3 was only incidental to their main duties which was to look after the household and the 4 children” (see [5] of the ASOF).

(d)     Fourth, unlike the above situations where the illegal foreigners were performing their illegal work only (and no other type of work), and they were also performing the illegal work on a full-time basis and usually based on some fixed routine or hours,

(i)       B1-B3 only carried out the “food preparation work” on an occasional, ad hoc and sporadic basis, and were spending the vast majority of their time performing their work as domestic workers. In this regard, as stated at [5] of the ASOF, “the accused would ask B1-B3 to assist her, on at least one occasion, and for not more than 1.5 to 3 hours. Sometimes, they would be too busy to assist.” Further, at [5] of the ASOF, it was made clear that: “The assistance provided by B1-B3 was only incidental to their main duties which was to look after the household and the 4 children”.

(ii)       Indeed, as highlighted at [14] of the Defence’s submissions, the amount of time that B1-B3 spent assisting the accused in food preparation, was a mere fraction of the time that they performed their duty as domestic workers.

(iii)       Further, this evidence also showed, as counsel pointed out, that there was no “continuity of employment” in their alleged roles as “food preparation assistants”. [note: 11]

(e)     Fifth, B1 – B3, were not the only ones dealing with food preparation. In fact, this appeared to be the work of the accused herself, and the accused also had staff at the shop to assist her (see [5] of the ASOF). This was quite unlike the situation in the earlier cases discussed, in particular, in Tamilkodi.

(f)     Sixth, as stated in the ASOF, B-B3 also never helped out at the \shop, or worked outside the residential premises. Instead, even for the assistance they rendered to the accused in food preparation, this was carried out at the residence where they were working full time and employed as FDWs. These differed from most of the other cases cited by the parties, including the PG case of Lim Sheng Song cited by the Prosecution.

(g)     Seventh, there was no presumption that operated to show that B1-B3 were employed as “food preparation assistants” by the accused, unlike in the case of Loh Kim Lan.

(h)     Finally, there was no evidence or statement from B1-B3, or from the accused herself, that B1-B3 were employed, or intended to be employed by the accused, as “food preparation assistants”.

65     In short, there was no evidence whatsoever that the accused employed B1-B3 as “food preparation assistants” at all.

(2)   No evidence that FDWs performed “work” for the accused

66     I would also add that there was no clear evidence that B1-B3 could be said to be performing “work” (as opposed to merely helping out, or assisting the accused) as “food preparation assistants”, within the meaning of the word “employ” in the EFMA. Nor was there even any evidence, adduced by the Prosecution, that such “work” would have required a work permit in any event.

67     First, it was stated in the EFMA (but not specifically stated in the IA) that was an additional requirement in the definition of “employ” that the engagement or use of the service of the foreigner must be “for the purpose of any work or of providing any training for that person”. The term “work” itself is not defined in the EFMA.[note: 12] That notwithstanding, there was no evidence that the “tasks” that the FDWs performed constituted “work” for the accused.

68     In this particular case, the alleged employment of B1-B3 was “as a food preparation assistant” and hence the “work” that B1-B3 was engaged to do, or whose service the accused had allegedly used for, was presumably work in relation to assisting in food preparation. However, it was not shown that what the three Foreigners did, could be said to be doing “work” within the meaning of the EFMA. I elaborate below.

(a)     First, the evidence was that the foreigners “…voluntarily offered to help the accused since she was busy” (as opposed to “work” for her). Further, they were not paid, nor did they expect to be rewarded or remunerated for performing such tasks.

(b)     Next, the boundaries of such “work” were also not defined, in that it appears entirely ad hoc and arbitrary, and was carried out as and when the Foreigners were able to help.

(c)     Arguably, what the FDWs did for the accused in their home (when they were able to find time from their domestic duties and when they were willing to) appeared more akin to “doing a favour” for the accused. This would be aligned with the words of the ASOF at [6] where it was provided that “(t)heir assistance was voluntary, and they only assisted when they are free and able to.”

(d)     In short, the intent and output of the Foreigners was not “work” within the meaning set out in the EFMA, certainly not from what was described in the ASOF.

69     In contrast, one can look at the interaction between the accused and the Foreigners, as regards the role of the Foreigners as FDWs. In that context, in the performance of their work being employed as FDWs (for which they each had a work permit), it was specifically provided at [4] of the ASOF that the accused would give “work instructions” to the Foreigners to perform work duties such as caring for the children and the dog, and also performing household chores. In contrast, there was nothing stated in the ASOF that the accused gave “work instructions” to the Foreigners in their alleged role as “food preparation assistants”. Instead, as stated earlier, the words used in the ASOF were that what the Foreigners did was (only) to “help” or “assist” the accused, on a voluntary and ad hoc basis.

70     In short, it was highly doubtful that what the Foreigners did, by cutting vegetables etc., even amounted to work, within the meaning of s 2 of the EFMA.

(3)   No evidence any of the Foreigners was in the role of “food preparation assistant”, or that this role required an additional work permit

71     Separately, even if the Foreigners could be said to be providing their services for the purpose of “work” within the meaning of s 2 of the EFMA, it was not shown that this made them “food preparation assistants” as alleged in the charge, or that a separate work permit (beyond the work permit already granted to B1-B3 as FDWs) was required for each of them to assume this role.

72     In this regard, it would appear from the clarification made by the Prosecution at [3] of its supplementary submissions dated 5 October 2022, that the “…said term “food preparation assistants”, is not a term ordinary used to describe the work that the three FDWs in the present case were engaged in”.

73     In my respectful view, the Prosecution’s belated clarification above cast further doubt on whether the accused could have even applied for a work permit for the Foreigners to be “food preparation assistants”, when this phrase did not even properly describe what the Foreigners did at the accused’s home by cutting vegetables etc. But this was precisely what the Prosecution alleged that she should have done i.e. applied for such a work permit for each of the three Foreigners. It was the Prosecution’s case that because of her failure to do this, that she had committed the alleged offences.

74     With respect, if the accused could not even have applied for a work permit to employ any of the Foreigner as a “food preparation assistant”, she could not hardly be said to have committed an offence for her “failure” to do so.

75     For completeness, I would add that in the present case, there was also no evidence led as to what was the proper or correct type of work permit the accused should have applied for, even if it could be said that she had “employed” the Foreigners, when she asked them to cut vegetables etc, OR that a separate work permit was even needed for such “employment”. These appear to be issues that were never considered or addressed by the Prosecution.

(4)   Lack of evidence of the duration of alleged employment

76     Finally, I deal with one last issue pertaining to the element of employment. This is the issue concerning the alleged period of employment that the Prosecution had set out in the charges.

77     On this issue, the Prosecution had taken the position that the accused employed the Foreigners as “food preparation assistants” for the entire one to three months period set out in their respective charges. These periods essentially covered the entire time the Foreigners were also employed by her as FDWs (with a slight variation in the case of B2), for which the accused had already applied for the requisite work permit for them to be FDWs

78     However, even taking the Prosecution’s case at its highest, the only evidence adduced in the ASOF, was that on “at least one occasion”, B1-B3 rendered assistance in food preparation, and the duration involved was for 1.5 hours in the case of B2, and 2-3 hours in the case of B1 and B3 (Table 2 at [5] of the ASOF). There was no evidence that the accused engaged the services of B1-B3 for the entire period of 1 to 3 months as alleged in the respective charges.

79     While I agreed with the Prosecution that there was no need to show that the Foreigners were actually cutting vegetables etc. for the entire period of their alleged employment as “food preparation assistants”, or that the Foreigners were carrying out such tasks on a 24/7 basis, there should at least be some evidence led that the Foreigners were obliged to, or could be called upon by the accused, their alleged employer, to perform the role of “food preparation assistants” during the period set out in the charges. Even then, with respect, the Prosecution failed to adduce any evidence to support this position.

(a)     For one, since it was not even clear when the accused sought the assistance of B1-B3 to help her with the food preparation work, and specifically whether she had asked for their help before or at the start of their employment as FDWs, there was no basis for the Prosecution to allege that the accused employed them as “food preparation assistants” from the start of their employment as FDWs, even if I accepted that she had even employed them as “food preparation assistants” at all.

(b)     Neither was there evidence that these Foreigners would be obliged to, or could be directed/instructed by the accused to carry out these tasks (as “food preparation assistants”) for any part of the rest of the period set out in the respective charges. Instead, they would apparently only help as and when they could, since the facts made it clear that they only “voluntary offer to help the accused”, and they “only assisted when they are free and able to do so” (see [5] and [6] of the ASOF).

80     To summarise, for the purpose of deciding whether to call the accused to give her defence, I was not satisfied that there was some evidence adduced by the Prosecution that satisfied the element that the accused had employed B1-B3 as “food preparation assistants” without a valid work permit, in the manner alleged in the respective charges.

Additional failure to adduce evidence of the requisite mens rea

81     The Defence also argued that the accused lacked the necessary mens rea to employ the said FDWs as “food preparation assistants” for her business, as exemplified by the statement at [5] of the ASOF that “ the accused was unaware that this innocent request for assistance would end up being against the rules and regulations set by the Ministry of Manpower.”

82     In contrast, the Prosecution suggested that due to the widespread publicity that the MOM had put forward through press releases, handbook, or other publicly available documents released from time to time, as well as the work permit conditions and regulatory conditions applicable to the three Foreigners, the accused would have been aware that B1-B3 could not be employed for purposes other than work as an FDW. In any event, the Prosecution submitted that ignorance of the law is no defence.

83     In addition, in oral submissions, the prosecutor originally stated that “…we are not saying that there is a mens rea requirement attached to Section 5(1)”,[note: 13] before clarifying his position to be that “the mens rea... insofar as Section 5(1) is concerned, only concerns that the accused had intended or rather had wanted or did in fact engage the use of services of this foreigner.”[note: 14]

84     Looking again for guidance from past cases, I noted that in the context of an offence under the IA for illegal employment, it was made clear in Soh Lip Hwa, that the offence under s 57(1)(e) IA was not one of strict liability and that the Prosecution had the burden of establishing that the offender had the necessary mens rea, in that he knew or had reasonable grounds to believe that the foreigner was an immigration offender (see [43]).

85     The issue thus arose in the present case as to whether:

(a)     this was an offence of strict liability or whether there was any mens rea requirement for an offence under s 5(1) of the EFMA; and

(b)     if mens rea has to be established, what the Prosecution actually needs to prove as the mens rea requirement. For example, was it simply that the Prosecution needed to show that the accused intended (and did) use the services of the Foreigner, or was it necessary to prove that she intended to employ them as “food preparation assistants”, and/or that she knew that an additional work permit was required for them to be employed in such a role ?

86     On the issue of whether this was a strict liability offence, or whether the offence requires mens rea to be established, I was of the view that mens rea was an essential requirement that has to be proven.

(a)     In Tan Cheng Kwee v PP [2002] 2 SLR(R) 122 (“Tan Cheng Kwee”), Yong CJ made clear at [13] that there is a presumption of law that mens rea is a necessary ingredient of any statutory provision that creates an offence. His Honour added that:

This presumption, however, can be rebutted by the clear language of the statute, or by necessary implication, although it is not sufficient if the provision merely lacks terms that are commonly associated with mens rea. Where an examination of the language of the statute does not assist, the court will have to look at all the relevant circumstances to determine the true intention of Parliament. Such considerations include the nature of the crime, the punishment prescribed, the absence of social obloquy, the particular mischief and the field of activity in which the crime occurred.

(b)     In the subsequent decision in PP v Koh Peng Kiat [2016] 1 SLR 753 (“Koh Peng Kiat”), the Court of Appeal reiterated at [58] that there is a common law presumption that mens rea is an essential ingredient of an offence, albeit one that may be displaced expressly or by necessary implication.

87     In the present case, it would appear that consistent with the operation of the common law presumption, the offence under s 5(1) EFMA would be presumed to have a mens rea requirement. There was also nothing put forward to show that such a requirement had been displaced expressly or by necessary implication.

88     As to what constitutes the mens rea element for the present offence, I was of the view that – because of how the charges were drafted - the Prosecution would need to at least establish that the accused intended to employ B1-B3 as “food preparation assistants” for the business in relation to the respective charges, when she asked them to perform the tasks set out at Table 2 of [5] of the ASOF. In my view, it was not enough to show that she merely knew intended to use their services to perform those tasks. The latter “requirement”, which appeared to be what the Prosecution was suggesting, would have made the offence really no different from one of strict liability.

89     In this regard, working on the basis that the Prosecution needed at least to show that accused intended to employ B1-B3 as “food preparation assistants” for the business, I noted that in the ASOF at [5], it was stated as an accepted fact by the Prosecution and Defence that “ the accused was unaware that this innocent request for assistance would end up being against the rules and regulations set by the Ministry of Manpower”.

90     Taken together with the Prosecution’s submission that the accused would have been aware of the work permit conditions and regulatory conditions applicable to the Foreigners when she employed them as FDWs, the above fact stated at [5] of the ASOF (see [89]), could arguably suggest that it was never the accused’s intention to “employ” them otherwise as FDWs when she asked them to perform the tasks set out in Table 2 of [5] of the ASOF. In other words, she did not have the requisite mens rea to commit the offence.

91     On the further issue of whether the Prosecution also needed to prove that the accused knew that an additional work permit was required for such “employment” to be permitted, I note the Prosecutor’s argument that the MOM had come up with press releases, the handbook, or other publicly available documents, presumably to suggest that she would have such knowledge. However, quite aside from the fact that these documents were not evidence adduced in court or referred to in the ASOF, it was not actually shown that the accused was made aware of them, or that she had the requisite knowledge as a result, nor was there any deeming provision that operated relating to mens rea. Be that as it may, even the Prosecution, based on what it put forward in its written submissions, seemed to accept that there needs to be shown knowledge by the accused that a separate work permit was required.

92     In any event, as I have already found that the help or assistant provided voluntarily by the Foreigners to the accused did not constitute her employing them as “food preparation assistants” at all, let alone for the entire period alleged by the Prosecution, and that there has also not been shown that a work permit was required for such “employment” of the Foreigners as “food preparation assistants”, there was strictly no need for me to actually make a finding that the Prosecution has also failed to prove the specific mens rea requirement, although it was clear from the discussion above, that the evidence certainly pointed to that conclusion.

Considering alternative charges or course of action

93     At the end of the Prosecution’s case, aside from deciding whether there was evidence to call the defence of the accused on the original charges, it is also provided in s 230(1)(g) of the CPC that “…the court may alter the charge or frame a new charge before calling on the accused to give his defence”.

94     In considering whether the court should exercise its discretion to amend the charge, I was guided by the decision in Goh Chin Soon v Public Prosecutor [2020] SGHC 162 (“Goh Chin Soon”). There, the High Court had set out at [69] the following requirements that had to be met before a trial court could exercise its discretion to amend an existing charge on its own motion:

(a)     The existing charge must be legally or evidentially deficient;

(b)     the offence reflected in the intended amended charge must be readily apparent from the evidence already adduced before the trial court;

(c)     the Prosecution must have discharged its evidential burden of proof on the intended amended charge given the state of the evidence before the trial court and the stage of the trial; and

(d)     the amendment must not prejudice the accused person.

95     In the present case, both the Prosecution and the Defence had originally made clear to me that there was no alternative offence, triable before me, that I should amend the charges to, should I find that there was insufficient evidence to call her defence on the original charges. I agreed with their assessment.

96     However, on 30 November 2022, following the Prosecution’s supplementary submissions dated 5 October 2022, that the “…said term “food preparation assistants”, is not a term ordinary used to describe the work that the three FDWs in the present case were engaged in”, the Prosecution apparently suggested that I could consider amending the charges to remove or change the phrase “food preparation assistant” in the charges.

97     However, with respect, based on the requirements set out in Goh Chin Soon, there was no basis for me to alter or frame alternative charges, even if it was only to amend the term “food preparation assistant” in the charges to something apparently more suitable to describe the role of the person performing the tasks identified in Table 2 of [5] of the ASOF, and then to call on the accused to enter her defence on such amended charges.

98     In any event, I would add that there was no evidence led as to what should be the proper description or title to use in place of the phrase “food preparation assistant” in the charges, even if the requirements in Goh Chin Soon were satisfied.

99     Separately, the Prosecution also explained that by getting B1-B3 to assist the accused in the food preparation, she would have breached a regulatory condition that she, as an employer, was subjected to under at reg 4(2) of the Regulations and Regulatory Condition 3. This condition essentially restricts an FDW to perform “only household and domestic duties at…the residential address stated in the work permit”.

100    However, this breach or infringement of a regulatory condition was not per se an offence that was triable before this court, and would not constitute an offence under s 5(3) EFMA.[note: 15] Instead, as explained by the Prosecution, by virtue of s 25(2) of the EFMA, a breach of such regulatory conditions was only punishable by means of a financial penalty that the Controller could impose.

101    I broadly agreed with the Prosecution that what the accused did could possibly amount to an infringement or breach of Regulatory Condition 3. In fact, the submissions of the Prosecution set out in the “Prosecution’s Submissions At The Close Of Prosecution’s Case”, especially from [14] – [26], could possibly present a case for suggesting that such an infringement or breach of the regulatory condition had occurred. However, as alluded to above, these same submissions do not show that the alleged offence under s 5(1) of the EFMA had been made out on any of the charges.

102    In addition, I noted from the prosecutor’s oral submissions on 22 September 2022, that there were EFMA amendments made in 2012 to split offences that used to be under the former EFMA to those that attracted financial penalties, and those that remained as offences under the EFMA.[note: 16] The latter, I presume, refer to offences that remain triable by the courts, such as an offence under s 5(1) of the EFMA.

103    Based on this decision taken by Parliament to make legislative amendments to the EFMA to effectively ‘decriminalise’ certain offences, one could even argue that the legislature has, through enacting such amendments, has intended to make a distinction between acts that still constitute offences, for which a prosecution in court is necessary before criminal sanctions can be imposed, and acts which Parliament considers to be infringements or breaches of regulatory conditions that should not punishable by the courts, but which should now be dealt with by the Controller through the possible imposition of a financial penalty instead.

104    This also meant that as regards any alleged breach of EFMA provisions or conditions by an employer, the correct choice of enforcement mechanism, and the correct choice of forum, both decisions to be made by the Prosecution, would be vital in determining whether they can achieve the correct result actually intended by Parliament.

105    In my respectful view, by asking for the occasional help from a FDW to cut vegetables, peel bananas and wrap them with spring rolls etc. for her business, without the trappings or circumstances of “employment” for such “work”, and in situations where the said FDW then actually agrees to help on her own volitation, as and when she was free and able to do so, what the accused did in the present case, may (or may not) amount to an infringement or breach of a regulatory condition. However, this does not mean that the FDW was thereby employed as a “food preparation assistant” by the accused.

106    Separately, why the MOM did not attempt to seek a financial penalty under s 25(2) of the EFMA through the Controller for what the accused did in the present case, was not entirely clear to this court. Be that as it may, having decided to proceed by way of prosecution in the courts, I was of the respectful view that the rather unique facts and evidence of the present case, as agreed to by both the Prosecution and the Defence, would not be sufficient or appropriate to justify even calling for the accused’s defence on the present charges.

107    In any event, whether the MOM still can, or should, take action against the accused by means of seeking a financial penalty under s 25(2) of the EFMA is not something within the purview or control of this court. I thus make no further comment on the matter.

Conclusion

108    Having carefully assessed the evidence, the relevant caselaw, the relevant statutory provisions and Parliamentary reports, as well as the submissions of the parties, I was of the respectful view that the Prosecution has failed to satisfy the requirement in s 230(1)(j) of the CPC that there was “…some evidence which is not inherently incredible and which satisfies each and every element of the charge as framed by the prosecutor”.

109    Accordingly, in line with s 230(1)(k) of the CPC, “the court must order a discharge amounting to an acquittal”. I therefore acquit the accused of all three charges framed by the Prosecution against her.

110    I would like to end off by thanking parties for their useful submissions, and for the approach they have taken in the conduct of this trial, especially in coming up with an agreed statement of facts.

111    At the end of the day, this case was rather unique, with facts that parties recognised have hitherto not appeared in the other cases cited. It is also unclear if such facts will ever be replicated in the future.
