District Judge Soh Tze Bian:

(A)   CHARGES

1       On 23 March 2023, the accused person, Syed Fathuddin Putra Bin Syed A Rahman (AP), male/31 years and a Malaysian national, who was unrepresented had pleaded guilty before me to the following 2 charges:

(a)     DAC-917088-2022

“…you, on the 3rd of November 2022, at about 1.01 pm, at the Pick-up point of Block 364A Sembawang Crescent, Singapore, were concerned in the delivery of uncustomed goods, to wit, 1112 cartons x 160 sticks and 80 packets x 16 sticks Gudang Garam Surya brand of duty unpaid cigarettes, weighing 262.313 kilogrammes, on which excise duty of $153,036.80 was not paid, using a Van bearing plate number GBK9580B, and you have thereby committed an offence under section 128H of the Customs Act 1960 punishable under section 128L(4) of the same Act.”[note: 1]

(b)     DAC-917090-2022

“…you, on the 3rd of November 2022, at about 1.50 pm, in a Van bearing plate number GBM416S parked at Deck 4A of Block 365 Sembawang Crescent Multi Story Carpark, Singapore, did store uncustomed goods, to wit, 2500 cartons x 200 sticks of assorted brands of duty unpaid cigarettes, weighing 421.344 kilogrammes, on which excise duty of $213,500.00 was not paid, and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 128I(1)(a)(ii) of the Customs Act 1960 punishable under section 128L(4) of the same Act.” [note: 2]

2       The AP also consented to 4 other similar Customs Act charges involving the evasion of goods & services tax (GST) or excise duty in DAC-917089-2022, DAC-917091-2022, DAC-917092-2022 and DAC-917093-2022 to be taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing.

(B)   STATEMENT OF FACTS

3       The statement of facts which the AP admitted without qualification stated as follows (emphasis in bold added):

“    The accused is Syed Fathuddin Putra Bin Syed A Rahman, FIN No.: GXXXXXXXW, Male, 31 years old, D.O.B: 07/10/1991, Malaysia national.

2    The vehicles involved are:

a.    One van bearing plate number ‘GBK9580B’ (“V1”); and

b.    One van bearing plate number ‘GBM416S’ (“V2”).

3    On 3 November 2022, at about 1.50 pm, at Block 364A Sembawang Crescent, Singapore, the accused was arrest for being concerned in the delivery of uncustomed goods (i.e. duty unpaid cigarettes).

Facts pertaining to DAC-917088-2022

4    On 3 November 2022, at about 12.55 pm, acting on information received, Customs officers arrived at Block 364B Sembawang Crescent, Singapore to arrest persons concerned in activities involving duty unpaid cigarettes. At about 12.58 pm, the officers saw V1 parked at the Pick-up point of Block 364A Sembawang Crescent, Singapore (“the Pick-up Point”). Subsequently, the accused alighted and was seen behaving suspiciously beside the passenger side door of V1. Suspecting that there were duty unpaid cigarettes in V1, at about 1.01 pm, the officers identified themselves to the accused and conducted a check on V1. Upon checking, the officers found 1,112 cartons x 160 sticks and 80 packets x 16 sticks Gudang Garam Surya brand of duty unpaid cigarettes (“C1”) in the rear compartment of V1. The accused admitted knowledge and ownership of C1 and was arrested. C1, V1, 1 lot of brown carton boxes and 1 lot of clear plastic wraps found in V1 were seized as case exhibits.

Facts pertaining to DAC-917090-2022

5    During the search, the officers found another van key in the accused’s possession. Upon further questioning by the officers, the accused informed that the key was for V2 which was parked at Deck 4A of Block 365 Sembawang Crescent Multi Story Carpark, Singapore (“the Carpark”). At about 1.50 pm, the officers conducted a follow-up search on V2 at the Carpark and found a further 2,500 cartons x 200 sticks of assorted brand of duty-unpaid cigarettes ("C2") stored in various compartments of V2. The accused admitted knowledge and ownership of C2. C2, V2 and one lot of carton boxes which C2 were found in were seized as case exhibits.

Investigation findings

6    Investigations revealed that since October 2022, the accused was working on a part time delivery job for an unknown Malay male known to him as “Abang”. Sometime in the night of 31 October 2022, the accused met Abang, together with 2 other unknown Malay males known to the accused as “Ann” and “Kecik”. Abang passed V2’s keys to the accused and Ann instructed the accused to drive V2 to Pandan Loop with Kecik to transfer duty unpaid cigarettes from a lorry to V2. The accused agreed and, at about 11 pm, Kecik and the accused proceeded to Pandan loop in V2. At the same time, a group chat with Abang, Kecik and the accused was created on WhatsApp mobile application. Abang would provide the instructions about the collection of duty unpaid cigarettes to Kecik and the accused via the group chat, and Kecik would update the accused of the same while he was driving V2.

7    On 1 November 2022, at about 1.30 am, Kecik and the accused located the lorry at Pandan Loop. Kecik, together with the lorry driver, unloaded boxes of duty unpaid cigarettes from the lorry, while the accused loaded them into V2. Thereafter, Kecik and the accused proceeded to the Carpark in V2. Kecik then left while the accused received additional instructions from Abang to transfer some of the duty unpaid cigarettes from V2 into V1 which was already parked at the Carpark. The accused did so and informed Abang after he was done.

8    Abang then instructed the accused to drive and deliver the duty unpaid cigarettes in V1 according to a delivery list provided by Abang via WhatsApp. This list included information on the location, quantity to deliver and amount of cash to collect from the customers of the cigarettes. The accused was told to store the remaining duty unpaid cigarettes, namely, C2, in V2 and to park V2 at the Carpark. The accused was promised $1,000 for each day of delivery. The accused agreed to do so. At the end of each day’s delivery, the accused would meet and pass the cash collected to Abang.

9    On the night of 2 November 2022, Abang instructed the accused to pick up one unknown Malay male using V1 and to proceed to Pandan Loop to collect C1 from a lorry. On 3 November 2022, at about 2 am, the accused and the unknown male arrived at Pandan Loop in V1 and transferred C1 from the lorry to V1. Thereafter, the accused sent the unknown male home and parked V1 containing C1 at the Carpark. At about 11 am, Abang informed the accused that he would have to deliver C1 to others. The accused then drove V1 containing C1 to the Pick-up Point at 1.01pm pending further delivery instructions from Abang, when he was arrested by the officers.

Excise duties leviable

10    The accused knew that the excise duties leviable on C1 and C2 were unpaid at the material time the offences were committed.

11    The excise duty leviable on C1 weighing a total of 262.313 kilogrammes is $153,036.80.

12    The excise duty leviable on C2 weighing a total of 421.344 kilogrammes is $213,500.

13    By virtue of the foregoing, the accused, on 3 November 2022, at about 1.01 pm, was concerned in the delivery of uncustomed goods, that is C1. On the same day, at about 1.50 pm, the accused also stored uncustomed goods, that is C2, in V2 at the Carpark. The accused thereby stands charged on the 2 proceeded charges.”

5       My reasons for the above sentences are as follows:

(a)     The prescribed punishment for the 2 proceeded charges are as follows:

(i)       1st charge (DAC-917088-2022) – Fine between $2,295,552 and $3,060,736 (15 to 20 times the excise duty evaded), or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 3 years, or to both; and

(ii)       3rd charge (DAC-917090-2022) – Fine between $3,202,500 to $4,270,000 (15 to 20 times the GST evaded), or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 3 years, or to both.

(b)     In Yap Ah Lai v PP [2014] SGHC 70, the High Court held (at [23] to [25]) that the offence of importation of uncustomed goods is directed at two evils: loss of revenue to the Government and the offence against the public policy and interest in reducing the consumption of harmful goods by raising their cost to the user, as there is also a strong public policy interest in keeping the prices of certain items high enough to discourage their consumption, but the public policy interest in discouraging consumption of the targeted goods is the stronger interest at play than the raising of revenue. The following factors were held by the High Court held (at [29] to [32]) to be relevant to sentencing for customs offences (emphasis in bold added):

“28    In Moey Keng Kong v PP  (“Moey Keng Kong”), Yong Pung How CJ (at [10]) identified five factors that he considered were relevant to sentencing for customs offences, as follows:

… the amount of duty evaded, the quantity of goods involved, repetition of the offence, whether the offender was acting on his own or was involved in a syndicated operation, and the role of the offender.

29    In my judgment, these can be distilled into four factors. The first is the quantity of tobacco product imported. Because this has a direct and virtually linear correlation to the amount of duty evaded the two should properly be regarded together.

30    The next factor is the repetition of the offence. While this will generally be relevant in all sentencing decisions, it should be noted that the Customs Act already provides for uplifts of sentences where the offender has committed previous customs offences: see ss 128L(3), 128L(5) and 128L(5A). The application of these sections would provide different starting points for the sentencing exercise. Hence, there might be no need to have separate regard to whether the accused has committed previous like offences if and to the extent that this has already been factored into the charges brought against the accused. However, where the prior offence is not a Customs Act offence or in any case is not an offence that has already been factored into the charge, the fact of the offender’s antecedents should rightly be considered within the rubric of general aggravating factors (see Sentencing Practice ([24] supra) at p 141).

31    The third factor is whether the offender was acting on his own or was involved in a syndicated operation. The fact that an offender commits the offence as part of a syndicate is an established aggravating factor that may justify an enhanced sentence in the interest of general deterrence: PP v Law Aik Meng [2007] 2 SLR(R) 814 at [25]. Having said that, in most cases where substantial weights are involved and require modifications of vehicles, this element will be found. The precedents will therefore often have incorporated some consideration of this factor.

32    As to the final factor, the role of the offender, this too might to some extent be encapsulated in the kind of charge that is preferred…..”

(c)     In applying the above High Court case, the following were relevant sentencing considerations as highlighted by the prosecution in the present case:

(i)       The quantity of duty unpaid cigarettes involved in the 1st charge was 262.313 kg and the excise duty evaded on these cigarettes was $153,036.80. This charge was for the delivery of uncustomed goods using a van and the AP had admitted in the SOF at the time of his arrest that he had knowledge and ownership of these goods.

(ii)       The quantity of duty unpaid cigarettes involved in the 3rd charge was 421.344 kg and the excise duty evaded on these cigarettes was $213,500. This charge was for the storage of uncustomed goods in a van and the AP had admitted in the SOF at the time of his arrest that he had knowledge and ownership of these goods.

(iii)       While the AP did not have any management control or profit share in the operations, he was a paid worker who performed the physical role of delivering and storing duty unpaid cigarettes on the syndicate’s instructions. He did so for monetary gains and was promised $1000 for each day of delivery.

(iv)       The prosecution had referred to the following sentencing frameworks as set out in Yap Ah Lai and Pang Shuo as follows:

(v)       For the quantity in the 1st charge, and considering the AP’s role, the benchmark sentence (as set out in Pang Shuo at [49]) is about 19 to 20 months’ imprisonment, while that (as set out in Yap Ah Lai at [46]) is between 18 to 24 months’ imprisonment.

(vi)       For the quantity in the 3rd charge, and considering the AP’s role, the benchmark sentence as set out in Pang Shuo at [49] is about 26 to 27 months’ imprisonment, while that as set out (as set out in Yap Ah Lai at [46]) is between 30 to 36 months’ imprisonment.

(vii)       The total quantity of duty unpaid cigarettes involved across both transactions was 683.657 kg (262.313 kg + 421.344 kg). If such a quantity was the subject matter of a single charge, the benchmark sentence from Pang Shuo would be more than 28 months’ imprisonment while that from Yap Ah Lai would be between 30 to 36 months’ imprisonment. I agreed with the prosecution that an uplift from the aforesaid proposed sentence is warranted as the offences in both proceeded charges arose from different occasions, which indicates a pattern of reoffending with 4 other similar GST or excise duty evasion charges to be taken into consideration. The 2nd charge in DAC-917089-2022 concerned the GST evaded on the 262.313 kg of duty unpaid cigarettes valued at $163.520.00 which amounted to $11,446.40. The 4th charge in DAC-917091-2022 concerned the GST evaded on the 421.344 kg of duty unpaid cigarettes valued at $242,750.00 which amounted to $16,992.50. The 5th and 6th charges in DAC-917092-2022 and DAC-917093-2022 pertained to the 5.682 kg of duty unpaid cigarettes which the AP stored in his residence at Block 364A Sembawang Crescent [redacted], Singapore and the excise duty evaded on these cigarettes was $2,732.80 while the GST evaded was $217.50.

(viii)       In my view, given the large quantities of duty unpaid cigarettes in both the 2 proceeded charges and the 4 TIC charges as well as the loss of government revenues from the large sums of unpaid excise duty and GST in all the aforesaid charges, there is a strong public policy interest to impose a specific and general deterrent sentence on the AP to deter him from future offending, and also to discourage other people from committing similar offences by setting an example of what the consequences of these offences can be.

(ix)       I found nothing mitigating in the AP’s oral mitigation plea (wherein he pleaded for leniency as he is the sole breadwinner and needs to support his family) as it is trite that hardship to an offender’s family is not normally a circumstance which will be taken into account for the purposes of sentencing, and the courts will not normally mitigate a sentence on the ground that the offender’s family will suffer because this is regarded as a normal concomitant of imprisonment[note: 4].

(x)       For all the above reasons, I accepted the prosecution submission for at least 11 months’ imprisonment to be imposed for the charge in DAC-917088-2022 and for at least 20 months’ imprisonment to be imposed for the charge in DAC-917090-2022.

(xi)       The general rule is that sentences for unrelated offences should be ordered to run consecutively as each offence should attract its own distinct consequence. Single offenders and multiple offenders should not generally be treated alike. Concurrent sentences for unrelated offences would not adequately serve, and in fact may undermine, the sentencing considerations that underlie the individual sentences comprising the aggregate term. Offenders who are sentenced at one sitting should not be treated materially more leniently or more seriously than offenders who are sentenced separately, and the Court needs to avoid any suggestion that a multiple offender may benefit from a bulk discount in sentencing.[note: 5]

(xii)       Accordingly, in terms of the global sentence, as the duty unpaid cigarettes in the 1st and 3rd charges in DAC-917088-2022 and DAC-917090-2022 respectively were unrelated and arose from separate and distinct transactions involving the delivery of uncustomed goods using a van and the storage of uncustomed goods using another van, I agreed with the prosecution that the sentences for both charges ought to run consecutively, resulting in a global sentence of 31 months’ imprisonment to be imposed on the AP.

(D)   CONCLUSION

6       In my view, the aforesaid global sentence of 31 months’ imprisonment which I have imposed on the AP was not manifestly excessive or inadequate, but entirely appropriate and consistent with the principles of proportionality and totality without the effect of a crushing sentence on the AP. The AP is presently serving his sentence and has filed an appeal against sentence in person on 3 April 2023.
