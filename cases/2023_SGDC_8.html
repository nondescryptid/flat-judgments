Introduction

1       The accused, a 60-year-old male Singapore citizen, pleaded guilty to two charges of incest under s 376G Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) (“Penal Code”) with his biological daughter (referred to as “D”). One of the charges (“enhanced 4th charge”) carried an enhanced punishment under s74A(2) Penal Code as the accused knew that D was a vulnerable person. The proceeded charges were as follows:

1st charge (DAC 915465 of 2021) (“1st charge”)

are charged that you, sometime in March or April 2019, at [address redacted], Singapore, did penetrate with your penis the mouth of your daughter [D] who was then 24 years old, knowing that she was your daughter, and you thereby committed an offence of incest under section 376G(1)(b) and punishable under section 376G(3) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

4th charge (DAC 915468 of 2021) (“enhanced 4th charge”)

are charged that you, sometime in early April 2020, at [address redacted], Singapore, did penetrate with your penis the mouth of your daughter [D] who was then 25 years old, knowing that she was your daughter and a vulnerable person, and you thereby committed an offence of incest under section 376G(1)(a) and punishable under section 376G(3) read with section 74A(2) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

2       Two charges of incest (one of which carried enhanced punishment under s74A(2) Penal Code) were taken into consideration for the purposes of sentencing (“TIC charges”). The TIC charges were as follows:

2nd charge (DAC 915466 of 2021) (“2nd charge”)

are charged that you, sometime between May 2019 and September 2019, at [address redacted], Singapore, did penetrate with your penis the mouth of your daughter [D] who was then 24 to 25 years old, knowing that she was your daughter, and you thereby committed an offence of incest under section 376G(1)(b) and punishable under section 376G(3) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

3rd charge (DAC 915467 of 2021) (“enhanced 3rd charge”)

3       All four charges involved D performing acts of fellatio on the accused.

Neurological and psychiatric assessments for D

Psychiatric assessment for the accused

Antecedents

Prosecution’s submission on sentence

28     The Prosecution submitted for the following sentences:

a)     For the 1st charge: 3 to 3½ years’ imprisonment; and

b)     For the enhanced 4th charge: 4 to 4½ years’ imprisonment.

29     The Prosecution submitted for both sentences to run consecutively, leading to a global sentence of 7 to 8 year’s imprisonment.

30     The Prosecution submitted that a sentencing framework for an offence of incest under s 376G Penal Code was unnecessary as there were few cases of incest prosecuted and hence any sentencing framework would be of limited utility.

31     Nonetheless, the Prosecution submitted that the present case fell at the higher end of the spectrum of gravity for offences of incest from the presence of the following four aggravating factors.

32     First, abuse of trust. The accused was D’s biological father and had a duty to protect and care for her. At the time of the offences, D’s mother was away from home for most of the afternoon and night due to her long working hours, thus giving the accused primary care for D (and unsupervised access to her) for lengthy periods of time. He abused this position of trust and parental responsibility by exploiting D for his sexual gratification. To make matters worse, D was abused in the privacy and sanctity of her own home, which ought to have been a safe haven sheltering her from the dangers of the outside world.

33     Second, D’s vulnerability. According to the NUS Clinical and Health Psychology Centre 2015 report, D was functioning in the extremely low range of intelligence. In 2020, IMH also assessed D to be intellectually disabled. D’s simple-mindedness was also illustrated by the fact that she once innocently brought home a used face mask that she had found outside, which was the catalyst for one of the offences. D’s mental capacity and lack of maturity would have made her susceptible to be sexually exploited by her father, who was aware that she was intellectually disabled.

34     Third, sexual grooming. The accused escalated his sexual acts with D over time and under the pretext of educating her in sexual matters. He started by getting her to shake and massage his penis around Chinese New Year in 2019. Emboldened by her compliance, he began getting her to not only masturbate but also fellate him. He showed her pornographic videos and self-servingly claimed to be protecting her from servicing other men outside who might harm her. The Prosecution submitted that it was especially perverse that the accused interwove the sexual acts into his parental role as a disciplinarian. By the time of the final incident, D had already internalised the fellatio to such an extent that she did not think twice about fellating her father when he was angry at her for misbehaving. The accused reinforced and normalised this state of affairs by praising D after she fellated him. He also took steps to prevent his offences from coming to light by coaching D not to disclose these acts to anyone else, including her mother.

35     Fourth, the multiplicity of offences. Besides the two proceeded charges, the accused also committed two other acts of incest (the subject of the 2nd and the enhanced 3rd charge), which are being taken into consideration for sentencing. The accused thus subjected D to not one but a series of abusive acts, over the course of about a year, and these additional offences warranted an increase in the sentence, see Ng Kean Meng Terence v PP [2017] 2 SLR 449 (“Ng Kean Meng Terence v PP”) at [64(a)].

36     By reason of these four aggravating factors, the Prosecution submitted that the present case ranked highly in the spectrum of severity for cases which may be prosecuted under s 376G Penal Code. The sentence imposed should thus fall at the higher end of the five-year sentencing range. The Prosecution accepted that the accused was genuinely remorseful and that some sentencing discount should be given for his decision to plead guilty, thus sparing D from the ordeal of testifying.

37     Further, in so far as the enhanced 4th charge was concerned, the enhanced maximum sentencing range afforded to this Court should result in an increase in the sentence meted out for that charge. Where Parliament has increased the maximum sentence, that typically means that sentences should increase too, as this signifies that “Parliament viewed the offence as more serious and therefore requiring stiffer punishments in line with current societal trends and circumstances”, see PP v Lam Leng Hung [2017] 4 SLR 474 at [387]; Mehra Radhika v PP [2015] 1 SLR 96 at [27].

38     Further, the enhanced 4th charge would represent multiple instances of reoffending by the accused as compared to the first charge, which was the first known instance he had committed the offence. This bore testament to the accused’s lack of remorse and repeated preying upon D, and should hence attract a more deterrent sentence.

39     The Prosecution submitted that a number of sentencing precedents which ranged from two to five years’ imprisonment for cases of incest. These were PP v Khamis bin Rahman (SC-903624 of 2021) (“Khamis bin Rahman”), PP v Sim Chin Beng (DAC 12714 of 2010) (“Sim Chin Beng”), PP v Khamis bin Ali (CC 9 of 1995) (“Khamis bin Ali”), PP v Ong Kan Chuan (CC 64 of 1994) (“Ong Kan Chuan’), PP v Hoe Chiew Kwee (CC 51 of 1999) (“Hoe Chiew Kwee”) and PP v Lias bin Sahmad (DAC 11069 of 1991 & ors) (“Lias bin Sahmad”). 40     The Prosecution submitted that the present case warranted a sentence lower than that the five years’ imprisonment imposed per charge in Hoe Chiew Kwee, but higher than that imposed in the cases of Khamis bin Rahman, Sim Chin Beng, Khamis bin Ali and Ong Kan Chuan. The Prosecution conceded that while the acts in the present case were fellatio rather than penile-vaginal intercourse as was the case in the sentencing precedents, this was counterbalanced by the aggravating factors it had highlighted which made the present case equally or more egregious to the sentencing precedents.

41     The Prosecution sought for both sentences to run consecutively as the individual sentences for unrelated offences should run consecutively as a general rule: PP v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 799 at [42] to [346]. In the present case, the two proceeded charges involve offences committed on different occasions. They were thus unrelated offences: PP v BND [2019] SGHC 49 at [94]. This was also consistent with principle, as the global sentence meted out must punish the accused for both offences in order to send an appropriately deterrent signal. Running the sentences concurrently would essentially grant the accused a “free pass” for one of his two offences, significantly undermine the principles of deterrence and retribution, and lead to a sentence that was grossly lenient when viewed against the backdrop of his offending.

Mitigation and Defence’s submission on sentence

42     The Defence submitted for the following sentences:

a)     For the 1st charge: 15 months’ imprisonment; and

b)     For the enhanced 4th charge: 21 months’ imprisonment.

43     The Defence submitted that both sentences of imprisonment could run consecutively leading to a global sentence of 36 months’ imprisonment.

44     Given the dearth of reported local authorities on this particular issue, the Defence submitted that the first port of call should be the United Kingdom (“UK”) given that section 376G Penal Code was adapted from section 64 of the UK Sexual Offences Act 2003.

45     To this end, the UK Sentencing Council published a Sexual Offences Definitive Guideline (the “UK Sentencing Guideline”) with applicable principles of sentencing for offences committed pursuant to the Sexual Offences Act 2003. The UK Sentencing Guideline relied on a sentencing bands approach.

46     In determining which category the offence falls at, the approach of the UK court was to determine culpability and harm caused or intended, by reference only to the following factors below, which comprised of the principal factual elements of the offence. The UK offence-specific factors were as follows:

47     Harm:

(a)     Victim is particularly vulnerable due to personal circumstances; and

(b)     Child conceived;

48     Culpability:

(a)     Grooming behaviour used against victim; and

(b)     Use of threats (including blackmail).

49     After determining the offence category, the Court should use the corresponding starting points to reach a sentence within the category range after taking into account any aggravating and mitigating factors.

50     The Defence referred to the UK case of R v Robert Lee Cole [2020] EWCA Crim 818 (“Robert Lee Cole”). In that case, the offender was reunited with his natural daughter in 2018, 15 years after she was taken from his care and placed first with her grandparents before staying with a series of foster carers. The offender’s daughter was diagnosed with a personality disorder and/or autism. The Court of Appeal affirmed the lower court’s decision to hold that the victim was particularly vulnerable, and considered the following:-

a)     She was a virgin,

b)     She had a personality disorder and/or autism, and

c)     She had a very difficult childhood and adolescence.

51     Bearing in mind that the maximum sentence under Section 64 of the UK Sexual Offence Act was an imprisonment term of two years, the High Court affirmed the lower court’s decision to impose a global 18-month custodial term (12 months for the 1st count and 6 months for the 2nd count).

52     Given the lack of reported decisions on section 376G Penal Code, the Defence had proposed a two-step sentencing bands framework for an offence under section 376G Penal Code noting that such an approach had been applied has been applied in a myriad of cases for other sexual offences including Kunasekaran s/o Kalimuthu Somasundara v PP [2018] 4 SLR 580, Pram Nair v PP [2017] 2 SLR 1015 (“Pram Nair”), and GBR v PP [2018] 3 SLR 1048 (“GBR”).

54     For offender-specific factors, the Defence submitted the proposed sentencing framework could consider aggravating factors included TIC charges, the presence of relevant antecedents and evident lack of remorse. Mitigating factors included display of evident remorse, youth, and advanced age.

55     Applying the Defence’s proposed sentencing framework to the present case, the Defence submitted that there was only one offence-specific aggravating factor in the present case, namely that D was vulnerable due to personal circumstances. The Defence disagreed with the Prosecution that abuse of trust was an aggravating factor aggravating factor was conceptually incongruous with an offence of incest. The Defence contended that while abuse of trust was a relevant factor for cases where sexual conduct was forced upon an individual against one’s will or cases which involved a vulnerable victim where consent is not relevant, this factor did not apply in an offence where two adults had given consent to perform sexual acts on each other. The Defence also noted that abuse of trust was not specified as an offence-specific factor in the UK.

56     As for grooming, the Defence submitted that the Prosecution’s reliance on sexual grooming had conceptual difficulties. The offence of sexual grooming could be found in section 376E and 376EA Penal Code and applied to individuals below the age of 18, specifically to any persons below the age of 16 and for individuals between the age of 16 to 18 if the accused and victim were in an exploitative relationship. Given that D was 25 years old at the material time, grooming was not relevant as an offence-specific factor the victim’s medical reports did not indicate that her mental age was that of a child below 18 years old.

57     The Defence submitted that the Prosecution’s statement that the accused had “sexually exploited her” was not entirely accurate as ultimately, consent was provided. More importantly, the clarification report provided by Dr Chandwani indicated that the victim was able to understand the nature and consequence of the sexual acts.

58     The Defence submitted that while it could be inferred that D may have felt that the act of fellatio was a means of apologising to the accused, there was no direct evidence to suggest that the accused had deliberately utilised scolding to manipulate D into performing such sexual acts on him and that it was equally reasonable to infer that such occurrences were happenstance and not deliberate. The Defence accordingly submitted that the court should place little or no weight on this statement in assessing the culpability of the accused.

59     With regard to the statement in the Statement of Facts that D “understood that her father’s action was not right, but she was unable to refuse his advances out of fear of incurring her parents’ anger and punishments”, the Defence submitted that this statement was ultimately the D’s subjective view of how she felt about the incident.

60     For the enhanced 4th charge, the Defence submitted that any uplift imposed should be carefully scrutinised and weighed against the countervailing difficulties when applying the uplift approach for the aggravated offence under section 74A Penal Code as well as the need to guard against double counting.

61     In so far as the Prosecution’s case precedents had no written grounds, the Defence submitted that they should not be given too much weight as “it will not be possible in such cases to discern what had weighed on the mind of the sentencing judge or why the sentencing judge had approached the matter in a particular way”, see Janardana Jayasankarr v PP [2016] 4 SLR 1288 (“Janardana Jayasankarr”) at [13(b)]. The Defence also highlighted that since 1 January 2020, section 376G Penal Code had been amended to state at section 376G(4) Penal Code that non-consensual acts of penetration which would constitute an offence under section 375(1)(a), 375(1A)(a), 376(1)(a) or (2) or 376AA(1) Penal Code would be excluded from acts of penetration as defined under section 376G(1) Penal Code. In so far as the Prosecution’s case precedents pre-dated the 1 January 2020 amendment, they may have included non-consensual acts prosecuted under the previous iteration of s 376G Penal Code which would then have attracted higher imprisonment terms.

62     The Defence also submitted that a back testing of the Prosecution’s sentencing precedents demonstrated that the Defence’s proposed sentencing framework was broadly in line with sentencing precedents. Specifically, the Defence highlighted the following:

(a)      Sim Chin Beng fell within Band 2 because of the following aggravating factors: (a) The victim was vulnerable as she was 20 years old, (b) There was the presence of grooming used against the victim as the offender had escalated his sexual acts with the victim over time since the victim was 18 years of age. The count of incest involved penile-vaginal penetration. As such, the indicative starting point was likely to fall in Band 2. The sentence of 3 years imprisonment was broadly consistent with the proposed sentencing framework although one may argue that the sentence should have been slightly lower than that in the case of Khamis bin Ali below.

(b)      Khamis bin Ali fell within Band 2 because of the following aggravating factors: (a) The victim was particularly vulnerable as she was 17 years old and (b) the victim became pregnant as a result. Further, the count of incest involved penile-vaginal penetration. The sentence of 3 years was consistent with the proposed sentencing framework.

(c)      Ong Kan Chuan fell within Band 2 because of the following aggravating factors: (a) The victim was vulnerable at 15 years of age and (b) There was predatory behaviour of using money to incentivise the victim. The sentence of 2 years’ imprisonment for each charge of incest was consistent with the proposed sentencing framework.

(d)      Lias bin Sahmad fell within Band 2 because of the following aggravating factors: (a) The victim was particularly vulnerable as she was 17 years old and (b) the victim had been having sexual intercourse with her father for the past 3 years. The count of incest involved penile-vaginal penetration. The sentence of 30 months (i.e. 2½ years) for the incest charge was broadly consistent with the proposed sentencing framework although one may argue that the sentence could have been higher given that the charges involved multiple types of sexual penetration and the presence of 4 TIC-charges.

(e)      Rahmat bin Abdul Rahim fell within Band 2 because of the following aggravating factors: (a) The victim was particularly vulnerable as she was 16 years old and (b) a child was conceived. The count of incest involved penile-vaginal penetration. The indicative starting point will likely fall around the middle to higher end of Band 2. The sentence of 30 months (i.e. 2½ years) was broadly consistent with the proposed sentencing framework.

63     An example of a case which might fall under the highest band 3 was Hoe Chiew Kwee. There, the offender was charged with 3 counts of incest. He not only engaged in sexual acts involving penile-vaginal penetration with his daughter, but there was also a commercial element in that he paid his daughter $100 on each occasion. The offender had further taken pornographic photographs of his daughter for his personal enjoyment and threatened to sell these photographs if she did not submit to his requests of subsequent sexual acts. The daughter was vulnerable in that she was 21 years old and was of borderline intelligence. The Defence submitted that Hoe Chiew Kwee clearly crossed the threshold to Band 3 because of the extent of degradation inflicted on his daughter and the intensity of the aggravating factors of (a) the victim was a vulnerable victim; (b) the use of blackmail on the victim and (c) the use of money to incentivise the victim to perform sexual intercourse, (d) the degradation inflicted on the victim such as the “shocking pornographic photographs taken of his daughter for his enjoyment”.

64     Applying the Defence’s proposed sentencing framework to the present case, the Defence submitted that an appropriate starting point for the 1st and enhanced 4th Charge would be 12 months and 18 months imprisonment respectively.

65     Taken into account offender specific factors including the TIC charges and the accused plea of guilt, the Defence submitted that a custodial sentence of 15 months should be imposed for the 1st Charge and 21 months’ imprisonment for the enhanced 4th Charge which would account for the uplift required under Section 74A (2) of the Penal Code with the sentences for both charges to be run consecutively, leading to a global sentence of 36 months’ imprisonment.

The court’s decision on sentence

66     Section 376G Penal Code has gone through various legislative iterations and the version of section 376G Penal Code which was repealed and re-enacted vide the Criminal Law Reform Act 2019 with effect from 1 January 2020 applied to the enhanced 3rd and 4th charges where the offences took place in February and April 2020 respectively.

67     That said, with regard to the punishment provided for the offence, the versions of section 376G Penal Code before and after 1 January 2020 which were applicable to all the charges in the present case carried a mandatory imprisonment term of up to five years. Additionally, the enhanced 3rd and 4th charges carried a mandatory imprisonment term of up to 10 years by virtue of section 74A(2) Penal Code which doubled the existing punishment as D was considered a vulnerable person and the accused knew this.

68     The Defence had proposed a sentencing framework based on a two-step sentencing bands approach for the court’s consideration. The Prosecution submitted that a sentencing framework was unnecessary as there were few cases of incest prosecuted and hence any sentencing framework would be of limited utility. As a first instance court, I did not think it was appropriate for me to adopt a sentencing framework for the offence of incest under section 376G Penal Code, as an appellate court would be better placed should it wish to do so, which would include deciding what type of sentencing framework was appropriate for an offence under section 376G Penal Code and the allocation of appropriate sentencing ranges to each band or box, as the case may be.

69     That said, in arriving at the appropriate sentence for the present case, I considered what, in my view, were the relevant offence and offender specific factors in this case, but I first considered the relevance of the UK Sentencing Guideline for an offence of incest.

The relevance of the UK Sentencing Guideline

70     The Defence had submitted that only the offence-specific factors cited by the UK Sentencing Guideline for an offence of incest under section 64 of the UK Sexual Offences Act 2003 should be relevant to an offence of incest under section 376G Penal Code. I disagreed with this limitation for the following reasons.

71     First, section 64 of the UK Sexual Offences Act 2003 was substantially different from section 376G Penal Code. Section 64(2) of the UK Sexual Offences Act 2003 stated that the ways in which the parties may be related for an offence of an incest are “parent, grandparent, child, grandchild, brother, sister, half-brother, half-sister, uncle, aunt, nephew or nephew”. This can be contrasted with section 376G Penal Code which defines a close family relative as “grandchild, child, sibling, half-sibling, parent or grandparent”. As can be seen, the UK definition of a “relative” UK was wider than Singapore and included an uncle, aunt, nephew and niece which was not included in section 376G Penal Code.

72     Second, the punishment provisions for an offence of incest in the UK and Singapore was significantly different in that in the UK, the sentencing options included a fine, community based sentences and a maximum of two years’ imprisonment whereas section 376G Penal Code carried with it only a mandatory imprisonment term of up to 5 years with no option for a fine or a community based sentence.

73     Third, our courts have, in other case involving sexual offences, set out offence-specific factors beyond those which was stated in the UK Sentencing Guideline (see, for example GBR which refers to an abuse of a position of trust at [29(c)]) and I saw no reason why those factors could not be appropriately applied to an offence of incest.

74     In my view, these material differences between the law of incest in the UK and Singapore underscore Menon CJ’s cautionary observation in Chan Chun Hong v PP [2016] 3 SLR 465 at [87]:

The policy considerations that are relevant to a Singapore court for a particular offence may well differ from those affecting a court in a foreign jurisdiction given the unique social mores that undergird each society.

75     Following from this, I did not find the UK case of R v Robert Lee Cole [2020] EWCA Crim 818 (“Robert Lee Cole”) and the sentence passed therein as helpful in considering the appropriate sentence in the present case.

Offence specific factors

76     In respect of offender specific factors, I found the following factors relevant in the present case.

Grooming

77     The Prosecution submitted that there was sexual grooming in that the accused had escalated his sexual acts with D over time under the pretext of educating her in sexual matters. The Defence submitted that this was not a relevant factor as grooming was a specific offence under s 376EA Penal Code and was only applicable to victims between the age of 16 to 18 and not to adults such as D who was 24 to 25 years old at the material time. On this point, I made the following observations.

78     First, there was no requirement that an offence specific factor needs to amount to an offence before it can be regarded as an offence-specific factor.

79     Second, one of the UK Sentencing Council’s Guidelines had a factor referred to as “grooming behaviour towards the victim”. As such, grooming in the UK was recognized as an offence specific factor for an offence of incest with an adult relative under section 64 of the UK Sexual Offences Act 2003 and there was no restriction to it being confined to cases involving minors. In Robert Lee Cole the UK Court of Appeal affirmed the lower court’s decision to find that there was grooming behaviour of the victim who was 19 years old at that time, noting at [13] as follows:

There were incidents of praise, of secret messaging between the two and there was talk of buying an engagement ring. She was clearly a young woman starved of any affection and family contact and the appellant, whatever his own difficulties, preyed upon her vulnerabilities in order to commence the relationship between them.

80     Third, a sentencing court must be able to consider all the circumstances of a case in order to assess the appropriate sentence realistically. In PP v Bong Sim Swan Suzanna [2020] 2 SLR 1001 (“Bong Sim Swan Suzanna”), the Court of Appeal held at [73] as follows:

In our opinion, the sentencing court must be able to consider all the circumstances of a case in order to assess it realistically. Where the Prosecution has proved relevant facts, we do not see why the court should pay no heed to them when considering the appropriate sentence on the sole ground that they might also amount to offences. We think it is important to consider the totality of the circumstances of a charged offence in order to have a true flavour of the offence as the overall perspective may have an impact on the level of the offender’s culpability and the extent of the victim’s suffering. Naturally, in applying this principle, the court must take a common-sense and contextual approach when considering the importance of the proved relevant facts.

[emphasis added]

81     While Bong Sim Swan Suzanna dealt with the specific question of whether a court should disregard facts which may amount to a separate offence which the offender was not charged with, in my respectful view, it also stood for the general proposition that a sentencing court must consider the totality of the circumstances of a charged offence in order to have a true flavour of the offence.

82     As such, it would be artificial to disregard the accused’s conduct in the lead up to the offences of incest in assessing the culpability of the accused.

83     In this regard, a summary of the grooming behaviour as recounted in the Statement of Facts (“SOF”) in the present case was as follows:

(a)     Around Chinese New Year in 2019, the accused showed his penis to D and told her to shake and massage it. He stated that he was doing this to teach her about sexual activities so that she would not get pregnant by other men. D complied and masturbated the accused’s penis until he ejaculated.

(b)     The accused showed pornographic videos to D on his tablet as an attempt to educate D about sexual activity. Thereafter, the accused asked D to remove his pants. As she was too shy, he removed his pants himself whereupon the accused her to masturbate his penis by massaging it, and D reluctantly complied.

84     Notably, in GBR, the High Court observed at [29(e)] that the showing of a pornographic video to a victim before the act of molest was an aggravating factor.

85     As such, I considered these preparatory acts of what purported to be sexual education of D by the accused which eventually led to the offences being committed as an offence-specific factor. I would add that these preparatory acts were also relevant given the nature of the relationship of between the accused and D as one of father and daughter which I next turn to.

Abuse of trust

86     The Prosecution submitted that as the accused and D were in a father-daughter relationship there was an abuse of trust. The Defence submitted that abuse of trust as an aggravating factor was conceptually incongruous with an offence of incest as incest involved two adults in consensual sexual acts.

87     With respect, I disagreed with the Defence’s submission. An offence of incest as defined by the section 376G Penal Code may be committed between different close family relatives but in my judgment, the nature of the relation was relevant as to whether this was an aggravating factor. In this regard, I agree with the Prosecution’s submission that as the accused was D’s biological father that imputed a duty to protect and care for her. He abused this relationship by purporting to educate her in sexual matters and then exploiting her for his own sexual gratification. I would add that it was Dr Chandwani’s view that given D’s intellectual disability, this rendered her dependent on others. In my view, D would a fortiori be dependent on the accused who was her primary care giver and who she resided with for shelter and money.

88     Turning to the Defence’s submission that this was not a relevant factor as incest was an act between two consenting adults, our courts have recognised that where the offender is in a position of responsibility towards the victim this would be an aggravating factor.

89     In Ng Kean Meng Terence the Court of Appeal observed at [44(b)]:

Abuse of position and breach of trust: This concerns cases where the offender is in a position of responsibility towards the victim (eg, parents and their children, medical practitioners and patients, teachers and their pupils), or where the offender is a person in whom the victim has placed her trust by virtue of his office of employment (eg, a policeman or social worker). When such an offender commits rape, there is a dual wrong: not only has he committed a serious crime, he has also violated the trust placed in him by society and by the victim.

[emphasis added]

90     While the facts of Ng Kean Meng Terence concerned a case of rape, the general point being made by the Court of Appeal was that where the offender was in a position of responsibility towards the victim, there was a dual wrong in that apart from the offence committed, there was also a violation of trust placed in him by society and by the victim.

91     In GBR, See J stated at [29(c)]:

The abuse of a position of trust: as noted in Terence Ng at [44(b)], this primarily concerns two types of cases. The first is where the accused is in a position of responsibility towards the victim, eg, parents and step-parents and their children (Azhar; GBJ v PP [2017] SGDC 6 (“GBJ”) and PP v ABO [2016] SGDC 80 (“ABO”)), medical practitioners and patients, and teachers and their pupils (PP v Lewis Ian District Arrest Case No 916213 of 2016). The second is where the accused is a person in whom the victim has placed her trust by virtue of his office of employment, eg, a bus driver and a school child (PP v Chan Boon Wee [2011] SGDC 199 (“Chan Boon Wee”)). Deterrence is a particular concern where there is an abuse of trust in an interfamilial context, given the difficulty in the detection of the offences and the considerable barriers faced by the victim in reporting them: see PP v NF [2006] 4 SLR(R) 849 at [40].

[emphasis added]

92     As noted by See J in GBR, deterrence was a particular concern where there was an abuse of trust in an interfamilial context, given the difficulty in the detection of the offences and the considerable barriers faced by the victim in reporting them. To further illustrate this point, abuse of trust may not feature as an offence-specific factor between two adult siblings committing incest whereas in the present case, the abuse of the accused’s trust as a father towards his daughter would be over and above the elements of the offence of incest.

Vulnerability of D

93     Both the Defence and the Prosecution agreed that this was an offence- specific factor.

94     The NUS Clinical and Health Psychology Centre psychological report suggested that D was functioning in the extremely low range of intelligence. Her verbal comprehension index, working memory index and processing speed index were all in the extremely low range, while her perceptual reasoning index was in the borderline range. On the Vineland Adaptive Behaviour Scales – Second Edition (Vineland-II), D’s overall level of adaptive functioning was found to be within the low range.

95     Dr Nisha Chandwani (“Dr Chandwani”) of the Institute of Mental Health (“IMH”) assessed D to be suffering from intellectual disability. Her current functioning was in keeping with that expected from her level of intellectual disability. In Dr Chandwani’s view, D’s intellectual disability did not impair her judgment at the time of the offences and while she understood that her father’s action was not right, she was unable to refuse his advances out of fear of incurring her parents’ anger and punishments.

96     Dr Chandwani’s further clarification report of 5 Sep 2022 stated the following:

a)     D’s intellectual disability rendered on others as she requires some form of assisted living and would struggle to cope with independent occupation. Her intellectual disability could also result in a concrete approach to problems and solutions, that was not in keeping with developmental normal peers of the same age.

b)     Since moving to Singapore, D had resided with and was dependent on her biological parents for shelter and money. The pattern of interaction between D and her biological parents whenever she has done wrong has reportedly been based on feat, scolding and punishment.

c)     This had resulted in a pattern of learned behaviour of submitting to her parents’ demands out of a fear of punishment, not in keeping with developmentally normal peers of the same age. D understood that her father’s actions were not right, and was able to state to him that she was not keen to participate in them. However, she struggled to protect herself from being subjected to these alleged acts at the time given her concrete problem-solving ability and dependency on her parents, which were a direct result of her intellectual disability and which in turn lead to the fear of incurring her parents’ anger and punishments if she did not do as she was told; and

d)     D was substantially unable to protect herself from her father by reason of the intellectual disability as compared to an ordinary person, even though she knew the acts were wrong.

97     To further illustrate this, the relevant parts of the SOF stated as follows:

[6]    Following that incident, the accused went on to receive blow jobs from [D] on at least four occasions. Most instances began with the accused scolding [D] for unrelated matters – for instance, because she accidentally broke the accused’s weight set while exercising, or because she brought home a used face mask that she had found outside. [D] then fellated the accused as a means of apologising to him and in the hopes of appeasing his anger.

[13]  The final incident of fellatio between the accused and D occurred in early April 2020. One morning, the accused and [D] were eating together in the Flat when [D] dipped her own spoon into a dish of food instead of using the serving spoon. The accused became furious and shouted at [D] about her lack of hygiene. Out of anger, he refused to talk to her. [D] felt very sad.

[14]  Later that day, [D] went up to the accused and said she was sorry. She sat down close to him on the sofa in their living room and looked at his groin. The accused inferred that she was offering to fellate him. The accused therefore pulled down his pants.

[16]  At the time of this offence, [D] was substantially unable to protect herself from abuse by reason of her intellectual disability (see [26] below). She was therefore a vulnerable person as defined in section 74A(5) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed). The accused knew that she was a vulnerable person.

[emphasis added]

98     The Defence submitted that while it could be inferred that D may have felt that the act of fellatio was a means of apologising to the accused, there was no direct evidence to suggest that the accused had deliberately utilised scolding to manipulate D into performing such sexual acts on him and that it was equally reasonable to infer that such occurrences were happenstance and not deliberate. The Defence accordingly submitted that the court should place little or no weight on this statement in assessing the culpability of the accused.

99     With regard to the statement in the SOF that D “understood that her father’s action was not right, but she was unable to refuse his advances out of fear of incurring her parents’ anger and punishments”, the Defence submitted that this statement was ultimately the D’s subjective view of how she felt about the incident.

100    With respect, I was unable to agree with the Defence’s submission. First, the accused had admitted without qualification the salient portions of the SOF which included the relevant assessment of Dr Chadwani which formed part of the SOF. In Woo Haw Meng v PP [2022] SGHC 204 Hoong J observed at [30] as follows:

In this regard, while “[t]he charges and the statement of facts constitute the four corners of the case” against a person who pleads guilty (Public Prosecutor v Development 26 Pte Ltd [2015] 1 SLR 309 at [16]), I accepted that a court may draw inferences from undisputed facts in determining the relevant factual matrix for sentencing purposes (Chang Kar Meng at [38]–[39]).

[emphasis added]

101    Simply put, these were undisputed facts which the accused had admitted to without qualification in the SOF.

102    Second, there was no opposing expert report to contradict Dr Chadwani’s expert findings in this regard and accordingly no basis for me to draw alternate inferences as suggested by the Defence. As such, I did not accept that there could be an alternative inference drawn that such occurrences were happenstance and not deliberate.

103    In the circumstances, notwithstanding that D was an adult, I found that D’s intellectual disability and vulnerability allowed the accused to exploit this for his sexual gratification. As noted by Dr Chandwani, while D’s intellectual disability did not impair her judgment at the time of the offences and while she understood that her father’s action was not right, she was unable to refuse his advances out of fear of incurring her parents’ anger and punishments.

Multiplicity of acts and duration of offending

104    The two proceed charges of incest took place over the course of a year in March/April 2019 and in early April 2020. The SOF stated that as D disliked fellating the accused, and sometime after the final act of incest in April 2020, D confided in her training officer at MINDS which lead to a police report being made on 6 April 2020.

Offender specific factors

105    There were two similar charges of incest which had been taken consideration for the purposes of sentencing which were committed between May and September 2019 and in February 2020. These two incidents took place between the two proceeded charges.

106    With regard to mitigating factors, I took into account the fact that the accused had cooperated with the authorities throughout the investigations, was remorseful and by pleading guilty had saved D the ordeal of having to testify against her own father at a trial.

107    In the IMH reports by Dr Tay on the accused, the accused was diagnosed with major depressive disorder with anxious distress, but Dr Tay opined that this was unlikely to be in a state of relapse at and around the time of the offences and that there was no contributory link between the accused’s mental health condition and the commission of the offences. Dr Tay assessed that the accused’s risk of reoffending was likely to be low.

Sentencing precedents

108    The Prosecution submitted a number of precedents on incest where the sentences ranged from two years’ imprisonment to five years’ imprisonment for each charge of incest, namely Khamis bin Rahman, Sim Chin Beng, Khamis bin Ali, Ong Kan Chuan, Hoe Chiew Kwee and Lias bin Sahmad. In so far as there were unreported precedents, it is well established that a sentencing court should approach such unreported precedents with caution as it may not be possible to discern what had weighed on the mind of the sentencing judge in those cases, see PP v Mahadi bin Muhamad Mukhtar [2022] SGHC 217 per Hoong J at [7] and Janardana Jayasankarr per Menon CJ at [13(b)].

109    Further, the facts and scenarios in these cases were quite diverse as the offenders in some of those cases had also been charged with other offences such as rape and outraging of modesty or non-consensual acts of incest which would lead to the individual and/or global sentences being significantly higher. That said, one data point that could be taken from the Prosecution’s precedents was that the sentences for incest ranged from 2 to 5 years’ imprisonment. A search of the Sentencing Information and Research Repository did not yield any further data.

110    Notably, one of the precedents, namely Hoe Chiew Kee had a reasoned Grounds of Judgment. This was a case of three counts of father-daughter incest where the victim was 21 years and was of borderline intelligence. The offender paid her $100 for sexual intercourse, as well as photographing her in the nude and removing her pubic hair with a shaver. In his Grounds of Judgment, Amarjeet JC observed at [11] that the offender had sexually exploited and morally corrupted his natural daughter whom he found psychological vulnerable to his advances by having intercourse with her over some six months.

111    Amarjeet JC imposed the maximum term of 5 years imprisonment per charge and ordered all three sentences to run consecutively, leading to a global term of 15 years’ imprisonment.

The appropriate sentence

112    In my view, the dominant sentencing principle for an offence of incest is deterrence. This is evidenced by the fact that Parliament has established a mandatory jail term for an offence of incest, with no option for a fine. This was further exemplified in Hoe Chiew Kee by Amarjeet JC who held at [11] that:

Society had a special interest in seeing that steps are taken to put a stop to incest which undermines the family unit which is a pillar of society.

113    In Angliss Singapore Pte Ltd v PP [2006] 4 SLR (R) 653 (“Angliss”) the High Court held at [84] that sentencing judges must take note of the maximum penalty and then apply their minds to determine precisely where the offender’s conduct fell within the spectrum of punishment devised by Parliament. For the 1st charge this would be a mandatory imprisonment term of up to five years and for the enhanced 4th charge, a mandatory imprisonment term of up to ten years.

114    Incest can be committed in a variety of defined sexual acts of penetration and between different types of close family relations. Both the Prosecution and the Defence were in agreement that penile-vaginal incest would be more serious than fellatio. I agreed that penile-vaginal incest was considered as more serious than fellatio as this was also in line with the pronouncements in Pram Nair v PP [2017] 2 SLR 1015 at [152] and Yap Lee Kok v Public Prosecutor [2021] SGHC 78 (“Yap Lee Kok”) at [24]. In Yap Lee Kok which was a case involving the offence of sexual penetration of a minor, Hoong J held at [24]:

The court in AQW (at [41]) held that the starting point for an offence under s 376A of the Penal Code involving penile-oral penetration of a minor who is 14 years old or above, and absent any pressure or abuse of trust, is ten to 12 months’ imprisonment. It bears noting that the charge in AQW was under s 376A(2) of the Penal Code. As penile-vaginal penetration is a graver violation than penile-oral penetration (Pram Nair v PP [2017] 2 SLR 1015 at [152]; BPH v PP and another appeal [2019] 2 SLR 764 at [62]), the starting position in this case should be in the region of 14–16 months’ imprisonment.

[emphasis added]

115    It was pertinent to note that the case precedents cited by the Prosecution involved penile-vaginal penetration as opposed to fellatio which was the case here.

116    The Defence’s submission for a sentence of 15 and 21 months’ imprisonment for the 1st and enhanced 4th charge respectively was fundamentally premised on the fact that there was only one offence-specific factor present in this case, namely the vulnerability of D in view of the limited number of offence-specific factors cited in the UK Sentencing Guideline for incest, which I had already rejected as non-binding for an offence under section 376G Penal Code.

117    I had highlighted a number of offence-specific factors namely grooming, abuse of trust, D’s vulnerability in view of her intellectual disability and multiplicity and duration of offending. Specifically, even though there may have been consent by D in the technical sense in that her judgment was not impaired at the time of the offences, she understood that the accused’s actions were not right, and was able to state to him that she was not keen to participate in them but struggled to protect herself from being subjected to these acts given her concrete problem-solving ability and dependency on her parents, which were a direct result of her intellectual disability.

118    In Hoe Chiew Kee the victim was of borderline intelligence but that was a case of penile-vaginal intercourse. Amarjeet JC held at [11] that “there as little to commend in the mitigation plea of the Accused” and cited “serious aggravating factors” such as the fact that the offender took pornographic photographs of his daughter for his enjoyment and blackmailing her into subsequent acts of sexual submission. Amarjeet JC held that “the Accused had sexually exploited and morally corrupted his own natural daughter, who he found psychologically vulnerable to his advances” and he had brought “much dishonour and emotional grief to his daughter, his wife and the other siblings in the family which dishonour and grief will stay with them.” He accordingly imposed the maximum sentence of 5 years’ imprisonment per charge,

119    In my view, given the serious aggravating facts of Hoe Chiew Kee, the individual sentences in the present case should be lower than the maximum term imposed in Hoe Chiew Kee. That said, there were four offence-specific factors present in the case in this case of grooming, abuse of trust, vulnerability of D and multiplicity of acts and duration of offending.

120    For offender-specific factors, I took into account the fact that there were two similar charges of incest (including one charge which carried enhanced punishment by virtue of s 74(A)(2) Penal Code) as an aggravating factor. As a mitigating factor, I note that the accused had pleaded guilty, was remorseful and had saved D the trauma of having to testifying at a trial and Dr Tay’s assessment that the accused’s risk of reoffending was likely to be low.

121    All things considered, for the 1st charge I was of the view that sentence should lie in the higher half of the sentencing range of 5 years imprisonment and I accordingly imposed a sentence of 3 years’ imprisonment for the 1st charge.

122    I turn to the sentence for the enhanced 4th charge. In the Penal Code Review Committee’s (“PCRC”) Report dated August 2018 which proposed lowering the threshold of vulnerability from “incapable of protecting themselves from abuse” to “substantially unable to protect themselves from abuse”, it was stated at [15]:

There is a need to increase the protection of vulnerable persons from exploitation and being targeted for crime through stronger deterrent measures. Where crimes are committed against these vulnerable persons, there is a concomitant need to set the prescribed punishment for offences committed against vulnerable persons at the level that reflects the greater culpability and society’s more severe disapprobation of such conduct. Therefore, the definition of “vulnerable person” in the Penal Code should be scoped more broadly to extend the protection of the law to persons who require it, and the sanction of the law to those who deserve it.

[emphasis added]

123    The PCRC’s recommendations were accepted and the legislative amendments came into force from 1 January 2020 which was after the incident in the 1st charge but captured the incident in the enhanced 4th charge which took place in early April 2020. As such, the enhanced 4th charge’s maximum imprisonment term was doubled from five to 10 years’ imprisonment in view of section 74A(2) Penal Code.

124    In my view, there should be a concomitant uplift from the sentence in the 1st charge. That said, the uplift should not be a linear and mathematical uplift as the fact that D’s vulnerability was factored in as an offence-specific factor for the 1st charge, but an uplift was nonetheless necessary in view of the higher maximum penalty and the need to determine precisely where the accused’s conduct fell within the spectrum of punishment devised by Parliament, see Angliss at [84]. In the circumstances, I imposed a sentence of 4 years’ imprisonment for the enhanced 4th charge.

125    As both offences were committed on separate occasions a year apart and were not part of a single continuous transaction. In PP v BND [2019] SGHC 49 Lee J held at [94] as follows:

In this case, the offences under both charges were unrelated in that they were carried out on separate occasions, more than one and a half months apart from each other, rather than as a single continuous transaction.

126    As such, the sentences should run consecutively leading to a total sentence of seven years’ imprisonment. The Defence did not submit that the imprisonment sentences should run concurrently but asked for a further adjustment on the basis of the totality principle.

127    Turning to the totality principle as a “last look”, I was of the view that a sentence of seven years’ imprisonment could not be said to be substantially above the normal level of sentences for the most serious offence committed (where the enhanced 4th charge carried a maximum term of 10 years’ imprisonment) nor could it be said to be crushing.

128    In my judgment, an aggregate sentence of seven years’ imprisonment had been arrived at on a principled and reasoned basis and commensurate with the accused’s overall criminal conduct for the multiplicity of offences visited upon D for over a year.
