Decision Date:03 May 2023

3 May 2023

District Judge Shawn Ho:

Introduction

1       Mr Tan Soon Hin (“the Accused”) claimed trial to two charges:

(a)     Theft of cup noodles valued at $4 under s 380 of the Penal Code 1871, and

(b)     Voluntarily causing hurt to a public servant (police officer), in the lawful discharge of her duty as such public servant, by hitting the said public servant on her left ear, an offence under s 332 of the Penal Code 1871.

2       All things considered, the Prosecution proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt. The Accused was convicted on both charges.

3       With at least 5 theft convictions within a 3-year period, his past assumed a certain salience. The Escalation principle, which incorporates the safeguard of proportionality, was engaged. I bore in mind that the cup noodles were valued at $4. Finally, I considered the totality principle.

4       The Accused was sentenced to 32 weeks’ imprisonment.

5       I set out my reasons.

Charges

6       The Accused claimed trial to two charges:

You, Tan Soon Hin, on 17 June 2022, at or about 8.17 p.m., at J-mart located at 112 East Coast Road, I12 Katong Shopping Mall, Singapore, a building used for the custody of property, did commit theft of one Toyo Menzukuri Toridashishio Instant Ramen: Chicken Salt cup noodles valued at SGD $4.00/-, in the possession of Chiah Pei San, the employee on J-mart, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 380 of the Penal Code 1871.[note: 1]

You, Tan Soon Hin, on 17 June 2022, at or about 8.30 p.m., outside I12 Katong Shopping Mall, located at 112 East Coast Road, Singapore, did voluntarily cause hurt to a public servant, Sergeant Michelle Ong Mei Xuan, a public officer of the Singapore Police Force, in the lawful discharge of her duty as such public servant, to wit, by hitting the said Sergeant Michelle Ong Mei Xuan on her left ear, intending to cause hurt to her, and thereby causing her to suffer pain, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 332 of the Penal Code 1871.[note: 2]

Prosecution’s case

7       The Accused stole the cup noodles. This was evidenced by the eye-witness account of Sergeant Michelle Ong Mei Xuan (“Sgt Ong”),[note: 3] the testimony of Ms Cheah Pei San[note: 4] (J-mart employee) and Sergeant Tan Jun Jie,[note: 5] and the CCTV footage.[note: 6]

8       The Accused also hit Sgt Ong on her left ear. This was shown by the testimony of Sgt Ong and Ms Jeslyn See Suay Kee.[note: 7] There was a photograph[note: 8] showing redness on Sgt Ong’s left ear.

Defence’s case

9       The Accused did not steal the cup noodles. He slapped Sgt Ong because his T-shirt was pulled. Sgt Ong’s injury to her left ear area was caused by her fall into the bushes.

Issues to be determined

10     The issues to be determined were:

a.     Did the Accused steal the cup noodles (“Issue 1”); and

b.     Did the Accused hit Sgt Ong’s left ear (“Issue 2”)?

11     Briefly, my answers were ‘Yes’ to both Issues 1 and 2.

Issue 1: Did the Accused steal the cup noodles?

12     Based on the evidence, I found that the Accused had stolen the cup noodles for four reasons.

13     First, J-mart’s CCTV footage[note: 9] clearly showed the Accused taking the cup noodles from the shelf. J-mart’s employee, Ms Cheah Pei San[note: 10] (“Ms Cheah”) confirmed that the cup noodles were the same as those in the photograph.[note: 11]

14     I accepted Ms Cheah’s evidence. Ms Cheah testified that from J-mart’s CCTV footage,[note: 12] the Accused had placed the cup noodles into his bag. The Accused had been holding onto the cup noodles, before making an action, which caused the cup noodles to no longer be in his hand. Essentially, he had opened his bag and put the cup noodles inside his bag.

15     Ms Cheah stated that one cup noodles were missing from J-mart. She confirmed that the cup noodles belonged to J-mart, which had its own label and bar code on its items.

16     Second, I accepted the eye-witness account of Sgt Ong.[note: 13] Sgt Ong was a police officer who was off-duty on 17 June 2022 at or about 8pm+. When Sgt Ong was inside J-mart, she saw the Accused place something white, which was the bottom part of the cup noodles, into his bag. He exited J-mart abruptly without paying for the cup noodles.

17     The Accused brought the cup noodles outside I12 Katong Shopping Mall. The cup noodles dropped onto the ground, which can also be seen in the video.[note: 14] When Sgt Ong pointed to the cup noodles and asked the Accused whether he had taken the cup noodles, the Accused nodded his head.

18     I accepted Sgt Ong’s evidence. She did not know the Accused prior to the incident and she had no reason to lie in her court testimony. Sgt Ong did not exaggerate or embellish her evidence. For instance, she repeatedly stated that she saw the Accused holding something “white”, which was then placed into his bag inside J-mart.

19     I had the opportunity to observe Sgt Ong’s demeanour in court. She testified in a matter-of-fact manner without any reservation and was forthright when giving evidence.  I found her testimony to be credible, cogent, and consistent in the material aspects.  Her testimony withstood the defence’s cross-examination. Her testimony had the ring of truth.

20     Third, I accepted Sergeant Tan Jun Jie’s[note: 15] testimony (“Sgt Tan”). The Accused admitted to Sgt Tan that he had taken the cup noodles and he had no receipt for them. Sgt Tan searched the Accused’s bag and retrieved the cup noodles from his bag and brought it to J-mart.

21     Finally, I accepted the evidence of another eye-witness, Mr Chng Cai Yuan Samuel.[note: 16] Mr Chng testified that the Accused was trying to escape — when Sgt Ong asked the Accused to stay put for further investigations, he continued to move away. This was consistent with the evidence of two other eye-witnesses:

(a)     Assistant Superintendent of Police Amanda Chong Kai Shi,[note: 17] who testified that the Accused was trying to run away from Sgt Ong, and

(b)     Deputy Assistant Commissioner Sng May Yen (“DAC Sng”),[note: 18] who testified that the Accused was trying to walk away from the scene.

Issue 2: Did the Accused hit Sergeant Ong’s left ear?

22     All told, I found that the Accused had hit Sgt Ong’s left ear. I accepted Sgt Ong’s testimony. Sgt Ong’s evidence was supported by a photograph[note: 19] showing redness on her left ear.

23     When Sgt Ong saw the Accused exiting J-mart with the cup noodles in his bag, the Accused noticed that Sgt Ong was looking at him. The Accused started climbing up an escalator. When he approached the top of the escalator, Sgt Ong and her friend, Ms Jeslyn See Suay Kee (“Ms See”),[note: 20] started to give chase.

24     As the Accused was walking relatively fast, Sgt Ong sprinted towards him. She asked him whether she could verify the contents in his bag. The Accused asked why he had to show her his bag and asked for her identity.

25     Sgt Ong told him that she was an off-duty police officer (undisputed by the Accused). When the Accused heard that, he repeated that she was an off-duty officer and swung his right hand and hit Sgt Ong on her left ear area and temple area. Sgt Ong described it as being like a slap from his palm, which was large enough to hit both her left ear area and temple area. Sgt Ong felt pain for a while. After that pain subsided, it felt like a bruise.

26     Sgt Ong’s testimony was corroborated by Ms See. Ms See testified that she saw the Accused swing his arm and she heard a loud smack when Sgt Ong was hit on the face. Due to this loud smack, Ms See held onto the Accused by pulling his T-shirt. I accepted Ms See’s evidence.

27     I did not accept that the Accused slapped Sgt Ong because his T-shirt was pulled. In so far as the Accused was raising the right of private defence vis-à-vis the pulling of his T-shirt, I agreed with the Prosecution that the right of private defence did not arise on the present facts.[note: 21] Amongst other things, s 106A(1) of the Penal Code states that:[note: 22]

There is no right of private defence against an act which does not cause the defender to reasonably believe that death or grievous hurt would result, if done, or attempted to be done, by a public servant acting in good faith under the actual or apparent authority of his office, though that act may not be strictly justifiable by law.

28     Sgt Ong testified that prior to the slap, while she had verbally identified herself as a police officer, she did not have the opportunity to reach for and bring out her police pass as it was inside her backpack. Subsequently, DAC Sng[note: 23] saw Sgt Ong flash her police pass and identify herself as a police officer to the Accused. This was consistent with the Accused’s own statement to the police, where he stated that “When I left the J-Mart, I was stopped by 2 women. One of them identified herself as a police officer by showing me her warrant card”.[note: 24]

29     I paused to consider whether Sgt Ong, being an off-duty police officer, was lawfully discharging her duty as such public servant. The answer was “Yes”. Sgt Ong had identified herself as a police officer to the Accused and she had asked him about the item he had stolen from J-mart. I agreed with the Prosecution’s closing submissions at [17(b)], where they stated:

While she was off-duty on that day, Sgt Ong gave evidence that she was vested with the power, even while off-duty, to detect crime and detain an offender. This is supported by s 23 of the Police Force Act 2004 which states: “Every police officer is, for the purposes of this Act, deemed to be always on duty when required to act as such and has to perform the duties and exercise the powers granted to him or her under this Act or any other written law at any time and every place where he or she may be doing duty.”

30     During the incident, there was a scuffle between the Accused, Sgt Ong and Ms See.[note: 25] Sgt Ong asked passers-by for help in calling the police as the Accused had stolen something. The Accused pushed Sgt Ong, who landed on her back in the bushes.[note: 26] Sgt Ong grabbed the Accused who also landed in the bushes.

31     On the day of the incident, Sgt Ong informed Sergeant Sharkhan bin Mohd Hanafi[note: 27] that her left ear was injured. Based on the totality of the evidence, I did not accept the Accused’s claim that Sgt Ong’s injury to her left ear area was caused by the bushes’ thorns. It was unlikely that thorns would cause redness on the left ear.

32     I accepted Sgt Ong’s evidence that she had sustained other injuries after being pushed into the bushes by the Accused. In this regard, the medical report[note: 28] listed the following injuries:

(a)     7cm vertical scratch of left forearm.

(b)     Abrasion over left hand.

(c)     Bruise over right upper arm with scratches.

(d)     Superficial abrasion at right forearm.

(e)     3cm superficial scratch at left ankle.

(f)     Abrasion over right ankle.

33     I accepted the evidence of the prosecution witnesses for the following reasons:

(a)     None of the prosecution witnesses, including the eye-witnesses and/ or police officers, knew the Accused prior to the incident. They had no reason to lie in their court testimony.

(b)     Sgt Ong did not exaggerate or embellish her evidence. For instance, she testified that:

(i)       The Accused swung his right hand and hit Sgt Ong on her left ear area and temple area. She described it as being like a slap from his palm. She only felt pain for a while. After that pain subsided, it felt like a bruise.

(ii)       Her injuries were neither serious nor had long-term consequences. Her scratches were as a result of falling in the bushes.[note: 29] She did not suffer any injuries to her head from falling onto the bushes.

(iii)       She did not require any medication, cream or lotion for her injuries.

(iv)       She did not require a medical certificate.

(v)       She did not require an ambulance.

(c)     Ms See did not exaggerate or embellish her evidence. For instance, she testified that:

(i)       She could not remember which side of Sgt Ong’s face was hit.

(ii)       As a result of the incident, she only suffered muscle aches and very minor scratches.

(iii)       She did not see the Accused stealing the cup noodles.

34     Finally, my view was fortified by the Accused’s own police statement where he admitted that he had “slapped the woman officer with my right hand on her left cheek but not with a lot of force” and he “slapped the officer”.[note: 30]

Verdict

35     Taken as a whole, the Prosecution proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt against the Accused. He was convicted on both charges.

Sentencing

Prescribed punishment

36     The prescribed punishment for s 380 of the Penal Code was imprisonment for a term which may extend to 7 years, and shall also be liable to a fine.

37     The prescribed punishment for s 332 of the Penal Code was imprisonment for a term which may extend to 7 years, and shall also be liable to fine or to caning.

38     An offence’s statutory maximum sentence signals the gravity in which Parliament views such offences. A sentencing judge ought to take this into account when determining precisely where the offender’s conduct falls within the entire range of punishment set by Parliament: Public Prosecutor v Kwong Kok Hing [2008] 2 SLR(R) 684 at [44] (Benny Tan, Assessing the Effectiveness of Sentencing Guideline Judgments in Singapore Issued Post-March 2013 and A Guide to Constructing Frameworks, (2018) 30 SAcLJ 1004 at [46]).

39     The court should ensure that the full spectrum of sentences enacted by Parliament is carefully explored in determining the appropriate sentence, viz. Completeness principle: Poh Boon Kiat v Public Prosecutor [2014] 4 SLR 892 at [60].

Prosecution’s submissions on sentence

40     The Prosecution sought a total sentence of 32 to 40 weeks’ imprisonment:

(a)     For s 380 Penal Code: 18 to 22 weeks’ imprisonment (consecutive) and

(b)     For s 332 Penal Code: 14 to 18 weeks’ imprisonment (consecutive).[note: 31]

41     For the s 332 offence, the proposed sentence was in line with precedent. This case was factually similar with Public Prosecutor v Yeo Ek Boon Jeffrey [2018] 3 SLR 1080, where the offender had pleaded guilty to one charge of s 332 of the Penal Code. The offender (untraced) had pointed at the victim, a police officer, and said “fuck” and slapped the victim’s left cheek once, causing tenderness. The court sentenced the offender to 10 weeks’ imprisonment.

42     In the present case, given the Accused’s egregious conduct of his defence and the fact that he had been convicted after trial, the Prosecution sought a sentence of 14 to 18 weeks’ imprisonment.[note: 32]

Mitigation plea

43     Apart from stating that the court could convict him, the Accused offered no mitigation.

Decision on sentence

44      Section 332 of the Penal Code. The relevant framework for s 332 offences was set out in Public Prosecutor v Yeo Ek Boon Jeffrey [2018] 3 SLR 1080 (“Jeffrey Yeo”):

Category

Circumstances

Sentencing Band

1

Lesser harm and lower culpability

Fine or up to 1 year’s imprisonment

2

Greater harm and lower culpability

Or Lesser harm and higher culpability

1 to 3 years’ imprisonment

3

Greater harm and higher culpability

3 to 7 years’ imprisonment



45     The High Court held that the sentencing framework applied to police officers and public servants who were performing duties akin to police duties at the material time: Jeffrey Yeo at [52(a)]. The High Court explained that “duties akin to police duties” would generally include “preventing and detecting crimes” and “apprehending offenders” (at [53(c)-(d)]), which Sgt Ong was endeavouring to do in the present case.[note: 33]

46     The present case involved lesser harm and lower culpability. I explain below.

47      Harm. Harm was a measure of the injury caused to society by the commission of the offence: Public Prosecutor v Koh Thiam Huat [2017] 4 SLR 1099 at [41].

48     For the s 332 offence, the harm caused was the injury suffered by Sgt Ong (redness on her left ear). I agreed that this was a mild injury.[note: 34] Sgt Ong described it as being like a slap from his palm. She only felt pain for a while. After that pain subsided, it felt like a bruise. Her injury was neither serious nor had long-term consequences. She did not require any medication, cream or lotion for her injury. She did not require a medical certificate.

49     I considered the Accused’s post-slap conduct of pushing Sgt Ong into the bushes after she had confronted him, resulting in abrasions on her forearms, ankle and hand.[note: 35]

50     For the s 380 offence, the harm caused to J-mart was the potential loss of the cup noodles.

51     I was mindful that in assessing the level of harm or potential harm, the sentencing court should be careful not to double-count any factors which may already have been taken into account in assessing the level of culpability: Ye Lin Myint v Public Prosecutor [2019] 5 SLR 1005 at [58] (see also Andrew Ashworth, Sentencing and Criminal Justice (6th Ed, 2015, Cambridge University Press) at [4.3] and [4.5]).

52      Culpability. Culpability was a measure of the degree of relative blameworthiness disclosed by an offender’s actions and was measured chiefly in relation to the extent and manner of the offender’s involvement in the criminal act: Public Prosecutor v Aw Tai Hock [2017] 5 SLR 1141 at [35] (see Andrew Ashworth, Sentencing and Criminal Justice (Cambridge University Press, 6th Ed, 2015) at [4.5]).

53     For the s 332 offence, the Accused had targeted a vulnerable part of Sgt Ong’s body by slapping her face.[note: 36] I considered the timing (dinner) and location of the offence (outside I12 Katong Shopping Mall).[note: 37] In particular, the offence was committed within the public’s view and hearing,[note: 38] as seen by diners such as Mr Chng[note: 39] and DAC Sng[note: 40] helping Sgt Ong.

54     For the s 380 offence, there was some planning on the Accused’s part. He brought a bag into J-mart, used his body to block the CCTV camera, before opening his bag and putting the cup noodles inside his bag. He had at least 5 theft convictions.

55      Claiming Trial. Although claiming trial was not an aggravating factor, no discount would be given to offenders who elect to claim trial: Kreetharan s/o Kathireson v Public Prosecutor and other matters [2020] 2 SLR 1175 at [37] (Kow Keng Siong, Sentencing Principles in Singapore (Academy Publishing, Second Ed, 2019) at [20.147]).

Conduct at trial

56     In Tuen Huan Rui Mary v Public Prosecutor [2003] 3 SLR(R) 707 at [46], the High Court held that an accused person’s bad behaviour in court can be considered an aggravating factor.[note: 41] While accused persons are free to prove their innocence, it must be made clear that this does not translate to a liberty to blatantly besmirch the repute of prosecution witnesses behind the shield of privilege in judicial proceedings: Lewis Christine v Public Prosecutor [2001] 2 SLR(R) 1318 at [38].[note: 42]

57     I agreed with the Prosecution that the Accused had conducted his defence in an egregious manner.[note: 43] Amongst other things, he would rant and call Sgt Ong and other people “idiots”.[note: 44] He described the Prosecutors as “Satan” and stated that they were part of a “bastard government”.[note: 45]

58     I took the view that the Accused’s behaviour in court was sufficiently serious to warrant it being taken into account as an aggravating factor.

Escalation principle

60     The Escalation principle is no more than a reformulation of the longstanding principle that specific deterrence may justify a longer term of imprisonment being imposed on a persistent offender in light of his antecedents, if these reflected a tendency for repeat offending or a marked proclivity toward criminal offending: Public Prosecutor v Low Ji Qing [2019] 5 SLR 769 at [56].

61     The appropriateness of escalation requires a scrupulous assessment of the particular factual matrix: Tan Kay Beng v Public Prosecutor [2006] 4 SLR(R) 10 at [34]. This is by no means a mechanical exercise. The court is required to assess whether the objective of preventing re-offending is in fact met by the use of escalation: Low Ji Qing at [66].

62     Applying the Escalation principle incorporates the safeguard of proportionality. Hence, before a court imposes an uplift on an accused person’s previous sentences, there must be a careful comparison with the accused person’s previous offending: Low Ji Qing at [74].

63     In order to have the correct factual basis for applying the Escalation principle, in addition to the Criminal Records Office record, it might sometimes be helpful or necessary to refer to, among other things, the charges, statement of facts, psychiatric reports, and grounds of decision (where available). It is not every case where this will be called for – but certainly where the Escalation principle is in play, a court should endeavour to have as complete a picture as practicable: Low Ji Qing at [76].

64     In this connection, I considered the charges and statement of facts pertaining to the Accused’s theft antecedents.[note: 46]

65     In the present case, while I was fully cognisant that the cup noodles cost $4,[note: 47] the Escalation principle was engaged for the theft offence. In my judgment, there needed to be an appropriate uplift to the Accused’s previous sentences. He had at least 5 previous theft convictions under s 380 of the Penal Code. All of these thefts were from supermarkets, reflecting a tendency for repeat offending.

66     Notably, several of these convictions were for theft of low value items:[note: 48]

(a)     In September 2017, he pleaded guilty to two s 380 PC offences (value: $9.45 + $4.20 from NTUC Fairprice) and was sentenced to 2 weeks’ imprisonment for both offences.[note: 49]

(b)     In November 2017, he claimed trial to a s 380 PC offence ($20.75 from NTUC Fairprice) and was convicted and sentenced to 4 weeks’ imprisonment.[note: 50]

(c)     In 2019, he claimed trial to s 380 PC offences, one of which ($39.20 from Paragon Market Place) he was convicted and sentenced to 6 weeks’ imprisonment.[note: 51]

(d)     In June 2020, he claimed trial to two s 380 PC offences ($2.80 from Cheers on 2 occasions) and was convicted and sentenced to 8 weeks’ imprisonment for both offences (sentences ran consecutively).[note: 52]

(e)     In December 2020, he pleaded guilty to one s 380 PC offence ($30.45 from NTUC Fairprice) and was sentenced to 10 weeks’ imprisonment.[note: 53]

67     The fact that the imprisonment terms imposed for his previous theft convictions have increased, had not deterred the Accused from reoffending.

68     On balance, the above points militated in favour of the Escalation principle applying. Ultimately, the assessment for the Escalation principle is necessarily a fact-specific and contextual one, requiring a judgment of experience and discernment: Low Ji Qing at [81].

69      Consecutive Sentences. As a general rule, sentences for unrelated offences should run consecutively, while sentences for offences that form part of a single transaction should run concurrently: Public Prosecutor v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 799 at [41], [54] and [98(b)].

70     In the present case, all things considered, I was satisfied that both of the Accused’s custodial sentences should run consecutively. Both charges related to different protected interests.

72      Proportionality Principle. I kept in mind the proportionality principle in sentencing: Public Prosecutor v Law Aik Meng [2007] 2 SLR(R) 814 at [30]. Under the proportionality principle, the sentence to be imposed must not only bear a reasonable proportion to the maximum prescribed penalty, but also to the gravity of the offence committed (Kow Keng Siong, Sentencing Principles in Singapore (Academy Publishing, Second Ed, 2019) at [06.091]-[06.093]).

73      Totality Principle. The sentences for the Accused were in line with both limbs of the totality principle: Mohamed Shouffee Bin Adam [2014] 2 SLR 998 (see also Public Prosecutor v ASR [2019] 1 SLR 941 at [150]).

74     I was aware that sentencing involves a holistic assessment. Sentencing is not a scientific exercise demanding mathematical precision – it is a matter of judicial discretion to be approached with a judicious measure of pragmatism and common sense, having regard to the offender-specific and offence-specific factors: S Elamparithi v Public Prosecutor (oral judgment, MA 9238/2019) at [20].

75     Ultimately, sentencing is an exercise in evaluative ethical judgment: Ng Kean Meng Terence v Public Prosecutor [2017] 2 SLR 449 at [48].

76     For completeness, the Institute of Mental Health report stated that the Accused had no mental illness, he was not of unsound mind at the material time and he was fit to plead in a court of law.[note: 54]

Conclusion

77     All told, the Prosecution proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt against the Accused. He was convicted on both charges. He was sentenced to 32 weeks’ imprisonment backdated to 6 February 2023.

78     It should be made clear to the Accused that if he refuses to cease his errant ways and persists in offending under similar circumstances, he should expect to receive longer imprisonment terms in future.

79     I am grateful for the Prosecution’s comprehensive and helpful submissions.
