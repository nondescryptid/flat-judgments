Judgment reserved.

District Judge Jonathan Ng Pang Ern:

1       This is an originating claim about an alleged watch swap. The Claimant sold two luxury watches to the Defendant. The Defendant made payment for one of the watches, and parties agreed that payment for the other would be deferred. However, the Defendant subsequently obtained reports from an expert stating that both watches were not authentic and, as a result, has withheld payment for the second watch.

2       The Claimant does not dispute that the watches examined by the expert were not authentic. Rather, his case theory is that the Defendant had swapped out the authentic watches sold to her with counterfeit watches purchased from other sources, and it was these counterfeit watches that were sent to the expert for examination.

3       Was there, on a balance of probabilities, a watch swap? And what are the legal consequences that flow from the answer to this factual question? This is my decision.

Background

4       The Claimant is a hairstylist by profession.[note: 1] The Defendant is in the business of beauty salons and spas.[note: 2] The Claimant got acquainted with the Defendant, who was his customer, in 2016 and the two eventually became friends.[note: 3] At one point, both the Claimant and the Defendant were operating their respective businesses from units in City Gate, a development located along Beach Road.[note: 4]

5       The Claimant’s pleaded case is that on or about 5 January 2022, he and the Defendant entered into an oral agreement (the “Agreement”) for him to sell, and for the Defendant to purchase, two of his pre-owned luxury watches (the “Watches”). The Watches were: (a) a Rolex Datejust Diamond (the “Rolex Watch”) and a Hublot Big Bang Unico Diamond (the “Hublot Watch”). The purchase price was $28,000 for the Rolex Watch, which would be paid first, and $16,000 for the Hublot Watch, which would be payable in three months or, at the Defendant’s option, by monthly instalments.[note: 5] The Defendant promptly made payment of $28,000 for the Rolex Watch on 5 January 2022,[note: 6] and the Watches were delivered to the Defendant on 6 January 2022.[note: 7] The $16,000 for the Hublot Watch was never paid,[note: 8] and it is this $16,000 that is the subject of the Claimant’s claim.

6       The Defendant does not dispute this version of events.[note: 9] Instead, her basis for withholding payment is that it was a “key term” of the Agreement that the Watches were authentic, but the Watches were examined by an expert and found to be counterfeit.[note: 10] The Agreement, says the Defendant, should therefore be rescinded as a result of the Claimant’s breach of contract and/or a failure of consideration.[note: 11] This is raised not just as a defence,[note: 12] but also as a counterclaim for a refund of the $28,000 the Defendant paid for the Rolex Watch.[note: 13] The Defendant claims that she did not doubt the authenticity of the Watches at the point they were delivered to her; she only became suspicious subsequently when she learnt that two Dior bags she had previously purchased from the Claimant (the “Dior Bags”) were counterfeit.[note: 14]

7       In response, the Claimant accepts that the sale of the Watches was on the basis, express and/or implied, that the Watches were authentic.[note: 15] However, he denies that the watches sent to the expert for examination were the Watches. According to him, counterfeit watches are readily available from other sources, including online sources, and the Defendant is raising the issue of authenticity as a pretext to obtain a refund given her current financial situation.[note: 16] Moreover, the Watches were inspected and accepted on delivery.[note: 17]

8       I heard the trial over the course of one day on 21 November 2022. Three factual witnesses gave evidence at the trial: the Claimant, Mr Teo Jun Hao (the Claimant’s friend and employee[note: 18]) and the Defendant. Expert evidence was given by Mr Eric Ong from Bonfield Private Limited (“Mr Ong”), who was the independent expert (or, as is commonly referred to in the State Courts, the single joint expert) appointed pursuant to O 12 r 3(5) of the Rules of Court 2021. After hearing the evidence, I gave directions for written closing submissions, which were eventually filed on 18 January 2023.

Issues

9       Against the above background, the issues that arise for my determination are as follows:

(a)     whether the Watches were authentic (“Issue 1”);

(b)     if the answer to (a) is “no”, whether it follows that the Claimant’s claim (for the balance sum of $16,000 for the Hublot Watch) and the Defendant’s counterclaim (for the refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch) should be dismissed and allowed respectively (“Issue 2A”); and

(c)     if the answer to (a) is “yes”, whether it follows that the Claimant’s claim (for the balance sum of $16,000 for the Hublot Watch) and the Defendant’s counterclaim (for the refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch) should be allowed and dismissed respectively (“Issue 2B”).

10     Issue 1 is a question of fact, while Issues 2A and 2B are primarily questions of law.

Issue 1: Whether the Watches were authentic

Mr Ong’s reports

11     The starting point of the analysis for Issue 1 is Mr Ong’s reports.

12     Mr Ong issued two reports: (a) watch authentication report number MB23SO1-4106, in respect of a Rolex watch[note: 19] (the “Rolex Report”); and (b) watch authentication report number MB23SO1-4112, in respect of a Hublot watch[note: 20] (the “Hublot Report”) (collectively, the “Reports”). The Reports are both dated 31 March 2021,[note: 21] but Mr Ong clarified at the trial that this is a typographical error and the dates should be 31 March 2022 instead.[note: 22] The Reports both indicate that they were requested by the Defendant.[note: 23]

13     The Rolex Report concludes that the Rolex watch examined by Mr Ong was “NOT a genuine Swiss-made Rolex Oyster Perpetual Datejust 41 Rolesor in Oystersteel and yellow gold”.[note: 24] The Rolex Report lists several reasons for this conclusion. Because of the Claimant’s position on the Reports (see [15] below), it is not necessary for me to set out these reasons.

14     In a similar fashion, the Hublot Report concludes that the Hublot watch examined by Mr Ong was “NOT a genuine Swiss-made Hublot – 411.OX.1180.RX.0904 Big Bang Unico King Gold Jewellery 45 mm in 18k/750 Pink gold”.[note: 25] Again, several reasons are given for this conclusion, but it is not necessary for me to set these out.

15     The Claimant does not dispute that the watches examined by Mr Ong were not authentic. Instead, and as mentioned earlier (see [7] above), the Claimant denies that the watches sent to Mr Ong for examination were even the Watches to begin with, and claims that counterfeit watches are readily available from other sources, including online sources. In this regard, the Claimant seeks to impugn the Reports by suggesting that they contain several discrepancies.[note: 26]

16     Thus, in respect of the Rolex Report, the Claimant pleads that:

(a)     First, the Rolex Report refers to a “yellow gold Rolex Perpetual Datejust 41 Rolesor, Model reference 126333”, whereas the Rolex Watch was rose gold.[note: 27]

(b)     Second, the Rolex Report contains photographs of the watch that was examined, but these photographs are different (in particular, in terms of the colour of the watch) from the photographs of the Rolex Watch taken by the Claimant before it was sold to the Defendant, and by the Defendant on the day the Rolex Watch was delivered.[note: 28] The relevant photographs (cropped where necessary) are as follows:

Photograph A: One of the photographs from the Rolex Report [note: 29]

Photograph B: Photograph of the Rolex Watch taken by the Claimant before it was sold to the Defendant [note: 30]

Photograph C: Photograph of the Rolex Watch taken by the Defendant on the day the Rolex Watch was delivered [note: 31]

17     As for the Hublot Report, the Claimant pleads that:

(a)     First, the Hublot Report refers to the watch that was examined using two different reference numbers, “Hublot 411.OX.1180.RX.1704” and “Hublot 411.OX.1180.RX.0904”.[note: 32]

(b)     Second, according to Hublot’s official website, the watches bearing these two reference numbers are black. On the other hand, the Hublot Watch was blue.[note: 33]

Was there, on a balance of probabilities, a watch swap?

18     Although not expressed in so many words, the Claimant’s case theory, as I understand it, is that the Defendant had swapped out the Watches (which were authentic) with counterfeit watches purchased from other sources, and it was these counterfeit watches that were sent to Mr Ong for examination.

19     Ultimately, Issue 1 (ie, whether the Watches were authentic) turns solely on the question of whether there was, on a balance of probabilities, a watch swap. If there was a watch swap, then the Reports become irrelevant since they are not reports on the Watches. In this eventuality, as the Defendant has not offered any other basis for alleging that the Watches were not authentic, the finding that must follow is that the Watches were authentic. If, on the other hand, there was no watch swap, then, as the Claimant does not dispute that the watches examined by Mr Ong were not authentic, the conclusion must be that the Watches were not authentic.

20     Pursuant to s 105 of the Evidence Act 1893, the burden of proving the “particular fact” that there was a watch swap lies on the Claimant:

Burden of proof as to particular fact

105.    The burden of proof as to any particular fact lies on that person who wishes the court to believe in its existence, unless it is provided by any law that the proof of that fact is to lie on any particular person.

21     Has this burden been discharged? In the following paragraphs, I will first examine the alleged discrepancies in the Reports. I will then look at the alleged motive for a watch swap. Finally, I will consider the inherent probabilities of a watch swap.

Alleged discrepancies in the Reports

22     I first examine the alleged discrepancies in the Reports.

23     With regard to the Rolex Report, the first alleged discrepancy is that the Rolex Report refers to a “yellow gold Rolex Perpetual Datejust 41 Rolesor, Model reference 126333”, whereas the Rolex Watch was rose gold (see [16(a)] above). The quoted words appear in the Rolex Report’s concluding paragraph as follows:[note: 34]

Upon examination, observations, and testing, I have determined that the article does NOT accurately identify the watch as an Oystersteel and yellow gold Rolex Perpetual Datejust 41 Rolesor, Model reference 126333. All white stones set on the watch case, dial, bezel and bracelet are synthetic.

24     Read it context, the reference to a “yellow gold Rolex Perpetual Datejust 41 Rolesor, Model reference 126333” is not a reference to the Rolex watch that Mr Ong examined. Instead, it is a reference to the original model in Rolex’s line-up that this watch was compared against. This much was confirmed by Mr Ong at the trial.[note: 35] Thus, all this reference conveys is Mr Ong’s opinion that the watch he examined was not an authentic “yellow gold Rolex Perpetual Datejust 41 Rolesor, Model reference 126333”. In relation to the issue of whether there was a watch swap, this alleged discrepancy is not really a discrepancy at all. Of course, one could go further and infer from this reference that Mr Ong took the view that the watch that he examined was yellow gold. But if this is the case, then this throws up a similar issue as the second alleged discrepancy, which I shall now turn to.

25     The second alleged discrepancy in the Rolex Report is that the Rolex Report contains photographs of the watch that was examined, but these photographs are different (in particular, in terms of the colour of the watch) from the photographs of the Rolex Watch taken by the Claimant before it was sold to the Defendant, and by the Defendant on the day the Rolex Watch was delivered (see [16(b)] above). I have reproduced the relevant photographs at [16(b)] above. At first glance, the colours of the watches in the three photographs look admittedly different. However, in my view, there is a plausible explanation for this alleged discrepancy.

26     This explanation was offered by Mr Ong at the trial, where he was referred to Photographs A and B. Although Mr Ong was not prepared to go so far as to say that the watches in these two photographs were in fact the same watch, he explained that the difference in colour could be attributed to factors such as the lighting in the room where the photographs were taken, as well as the white balance and colour temperature of the photographs.[note: 36] For completeness, I should add that I do not see any reason why this explanation cannot be extended to Photograph C as well.

27     I accept this explanation for two reasons. First, the Claimant’s case is that the watches in Photographs B and C are both the Rolex Watch. However, the colours of the watches in these two photographs also appear to be different. For example, the indices and the hour and minute hands of the watch in Photograph B have, as Mr Ong pointed out at the trial,[note: 37] a bluish cast over them. This bluish cast is not present in Photograph C. Furthermore, in terms of the colour temperature, Photograph B appears, on the whole, to be cooler than Photograph C, which is warmer. This shows that what is professedly the same watch can appear different (in terms of colour) in photographs.

28     Second, reliance need not be placed exclusively on colour in the present case. This is because the Watches were not standard production models but were instead modified from their original form. The Claimant’s evidence is that he had sent the Watches to third-party specialists to be encrusted with diamonds.[note: 38] I note, parenthetically, that the Reports do not refer to diamonds, but instead refer to synthetic white stones.[note: 39] In other words, the “diamonds” on the watches examined by Mr Ong were not even real diamonds. But the point here is that another yardstick by which Photographs A, B and C can be compared is the design of the watches in these photographs. On this yardstick, the watches in all three photographs appear to be indistinguishable. Significantly, I am unable to discern any difference in the arrangement of the diamonds/white stones on the watches in the three photographs. I accept that this is not conclusive: if there was in fact a watch swap, the encrusting of the counterfeit watch could just have been done immaculately. However, in my view, this nevertheless remains a factor that lends weight to Mr Ong’s explanation.

29     In the circumstances, I find that the watches in Photographs A, B and C could all very well have been the Rolex Watch. The second alleged discrepancy in the Rolex Report is therefore also not really a discrepancy at all.

30     Turning to the Hublot Report, the first alleged discrepancy is that the Hublot Report refers to the watch that was examined using two different reference numbers, “Hublot 411.OX.1180.RX.1704” and “Hublot 411.OX.1180.RX.0904” (see [17(a)] above). Both reference numbers appear in the following paragraph of the Hublot Report:[note: 40]

Under 10x microscopic inspection of the provided item, I have determined that it is NOT a genuine Swiss-made Hublot – 411.OX.1180.RX.0904 Big Bang Unico King Gold Jewellery 45 mm in 18k/750 Pink gold. The observed Model Ref: Hublot 411.OX.1180.RX.1704 Blue Strap; Case Nos.992212. …

31     At the trial, Mr Ong’s evidence was that the models for the two reference numbers look very similar on the outside. Accordingly, he had referred to both reference numbers because either of them could have been the true reference number of the watch that he examined.[note: 41] In my view, this is a satisfactory explanation, and this alleged discrepancy is thus not really a discrepancy at all.

32     The second alleged discrepancy in the Hublot Report is that, according to Hublot’s official website, these watches bearing these two reference numbers are black. On the other hand, the Hublot Watch was blue (see [17(b)] above). Mr Ong was asked to explain this discrepancy at the trial. However, his evidence on this point was rather unclear.[note: 42]

33     Be that as it may, the issue here is whether there was a watch swap. Once this is appreciated, it becomes evident that this second alleged discrepancy does not speak to this issue at all. This is because Mr Ong accepted at the trial that the watch he examined was blue.[note: 43] There is no issue whatsoever of Mr Ong having examined a black watch. This second alleged discrepancy in the Hublot Report is therefore also not really a discrepancy at all.

Alleged motive for a watch swap

34     I now turn to the Defendant’s alleged motive for a watch swap. The Claimant’s case is that the Defendant had changed her mind about the purchase because she was in a difficult financial situation and in desperate need of cash.[note: 44] In aid of this, the Claimant points to several Telegram text messages the Defendant had sent to him on 12 February 2022.[note: 45] It is not necessary for me to set these text messages out in full but, to the extent that the Defendant had mentioned in them that she was “short of cash” and “in real deep shit”, it does appear, from the face of these messages, that the Defendant was not doing well financially. The Claimant also relies on statements made by the Defendant at a meeting on 14 February 2022, including one to the effect that she just wanted the money back as she “presently need[ed] cash”.[note: 46] Finally, reliance is also placed on how the Defendant had closed her beauty salon at City Gate shortly afterwards in June 2022.[note: 47]

35     The Defendant’s version of events revolves around the Dior Bags. The context to this is that, in addition to the Watches, the Defendant had also purchased luxury bags, wallets, footwear and clothing from the Claimant.[note: 48] Among these were the Dior Bags, which the Defendant had purchased from the Claimant for her friend.[note: 49] However, when this friend tried to send the Dior Bags for maintenance and upkeep at two local Dior boutiques on or about 8 February 2022, she was told that the Dior Bags were not authentic.[note: 50] It was only then that the Defendant realised that some or all of the luxury goods that she had purchased from the Claimant might be counterfeit.[note: 51] However, the Defendant was concerned about ruining her relationship with the Claimant and did not want the Claimant to know that she had come to suspect that the goods were counterfeit. Hence, she came up with the story that she was in dire financial straits in order to gain sympathy from the Claimant.[note: 52]

36     I accept the Defendant’s version of events. At the trial, the Defendant came across as an honest and forthright witness. Her account is also largely coherent and not inconsistent with normal human behaviour. Perhaps more importantly, it is objectively corroborated to some extent. The Defendant gave evidence at the trial that, even after closing her beauty salon at City Gate (which she closed because she no longer wanted to have any contact with the Claimant), she still currently operates two beauty salons at Tanjong Pagar and Tampines.[note: 53] I accept that this does not necessarily mean that the Defendant was doing well financially (for example, both beauty salons could in fact have been loss-making). But in the absence of any other evidence, I do not think that the Defendant would be operating two beauty salons if she was in dire financial straits.

37     On this point, the Claimant further submits that there was no reason for the Defendant to come up with the story that she was in dire financial straits as she had already confronted the Claimant over the Dior Bags on 8 February 2022, and so he already knew about her suspicions by 12 February 2022.[note: 54] The Defendant’s response to this line of questioning at the trial was essentially that she was concerned over ruining her relationship with the Claimant.[note: 55] I accept that this does not quite answer the point about her not wanting the Claimant to know that she had come to suspect that the goods were counterfeit. However, I am of the view that this is not fatal. The core of the Defendant’s evidence is the same: she was simply trying to use her dire financial straits, as opposed to the authenticity of the goods, as a basis for returning the goods, because doing so would help preserve her relationship with the Claimant.

38     In the circumstances, the Defendant’s alleged motive for a watch swap seems to be more speculative than real.

Inherent probabilities of a watch swap

39     Finally, I consider the inherent probabilities of a watch swap. I do so from two perspectives: (a) time; and (b) logic.

40     First, from the perspective of time, it is not disputed that the delivery of the Watches took place on 6 January 2022 (see [5] above). According to the Defendant, she first approached Mr Ong on 22 March 2022.[note: 56] Mr Ong then furnished the Reports on 31 March 2022 (see [12] above). The span of time between 6 January 2022 and 31 March 2022 is slightly under three months. A watch swap would have involved the Defendant doing the following: (a) deciding, whether because she was in dire financial straits or otherwise, that she did not want to pay for the Watches; (b) formulating the watch swap plan; (c) procuring the counterfeit watches; (d) identifying Mr Ong to examine the counterfeit watches; and (e) allowing Mr Ong time to examine the counterfeit watches. In my assessment, it is unlikely that all this could have been done in less than three months.

41     Moreover, insofar as procuring the counterfeit watches is concerned, as mentioned earlier (see [28] above), the Watches were not standard production models but were instead modified from their original form. The significance of this is that, even if I accept the Claimant’s assertion that counterfeit watches are readily available from other sources (see [7] above), it is not as if the Defendant could have simply procured counterfeit watches from these sources and then send them off to Mr Ong for examination. She would first have had to encrust these counterfeit watches with synthetic white stones. The encrusting would also have had to be done in a manner similar, if not identical, to the encrusting done on the Watches. This would have taken a substantial amount of time. Indeed, the Claimant gave evidence that the process of encrusting took three to four months,[note: 57] while Mr Ong’s estimate was four to six weeks.[note: 58] From the perspective of time, a watch swap is inherently improbable.

42     Second, a watch swap is also inherently improbable from the perspective of logic. If indeed the Defendant had changed mind about the purchase for whatever reason, the logical thing to do would have been to simply request to return the Watches for a refund. This would have spared the Defendant: (a) the steps described at [40]-[41] above; and (b) the financial cost of procuring the counterfeit watches and encrusting them. In other words, there was a more straightforward and less costly means of achieving the same end. Yet, this means was never attempted. The Claimant’s evidence is that the Defendant first requested to return the Watches for a refund at a meeting on 14 February 2022 and that, on this occasion, there was already an allegation that the Watches were counterfeit.[note: 59]

43     When one considers the Claimant’s case (ie, that the Defendant had changed her mind about the purchase because she was in a difficult financial situation and in desperate need of cash (see [34] above)), a watch swap becomes even more improbable. If the Defendant was indeed in dire financial straits, there would, in addition to the points made at [42] above, be even less reason for her to expend the effort and money required for a watch swap. Her effort would have been better spent resolving her financial woes, and there would be absolutely no reason for her to throw in even more money on a ruse that had no guarantee of success.

Conclusion on Issue 1

44     Drawing the various threads together, I find that: (a) the alleged discrepancies in the Reports are not really discrepancies at all (see [22]-[33] above); (b) the Defendant’s alleged motive for a watch swap is more speculative than real (see [34]-[38] above); and (c) a watch swap is inherently improbable from the perspectives of time and logic (see [39]-[43] above). Accordingly, I conclude that there was, on a balance of probabilities, no watch swap. As mentioned earlier (see [19] above), as the Claimant does not dispute that the watches examined by Mr Ong were not authentic, the conclusion on Issue 1 is that the Watches were not authentic.

45     I make two further points at this juncture. First, it is not necessary for me to go further and decide whether the Claimant knew that the Watches were not authentic. This is not an issue that arises in this trial. I will, however, briefly observe that it is possible that the Claimant did not have such knowledge. The Claimant is a self-styled “personal shopper” and has apparently been featured in magazines and invited to speak at events.[note: 60] It therefore seems that he does have some reputation to protect. In addition, counterfeit watches are likely to be copyright and trademark-infringing, and selling them with knowledge of their counterfeit nature attracts criminal liability under s 444(1) of the Copyright Act 2021 and s 49 of the Trade Marks Act 1998. The Claimant is represented by solicitors and is also the party who commenced this originating claim. He would presumably have been advised by his solicitors that if the Watches were not authentic, and he knew about this, he would be exposing himself to potential criminal liability by commencing this originating claim.

46     Second, at the trial, the Claimant gave evidence that he had purchased the Watches from a platform called Chrono24, which apparently authenticates the watches sold on its platform.[note: 61] I have not given any weight to this in deciding Issue 1 for two reasons. First, the Claimant did not plead that the Watches were authentic on this basis. Second, no objective evidence (such as the receipts for the Watches, or printouts from Chrono24’s website explaining its authentication process) was furnished on either of these points.

Issue 2A: Whether it follows that the Claimant’s claim and the Defendant’s counterclaim should be dismissed and allowed respectively

47     As the answer to Issue 1 is “no”, Issue 2A (ie, whether it follows that the Claimant’s claim (for the balance sum of $16,000 for the Hublot Watch) and the Defendant’s counterclaim (for the refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch) should be dismissed and allowed respectively) falls to be determined (see [9] above).

48     I will start by considering whether the Defendant’s counterclaim should be allowed. As mentioned earlier, the Defendant’s basis for seeking a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch is that the Agreement should be rescinded as a result of the Claimant’s breach of contract and/or a failure of consideration (see [6] above). I will therefore examine these two bases in turn. Thereafter, I will consider whether the Claimant’s claim should be dismissed.

Defendant’s counterclaim based on breach of contract

49     I turn first to the Defendant’s counterclaim based on breach of contract. As: (a) the Claimant accepts that the sale of the Watches was on the basis, express and/or implied, that the Watches were authentic (see [7] above); and (b) the Watches were not authentic (see [44] above), there is no doubt that the Claimant breached the Agreement. But what is the consequence of such a breach? In the present case, the answer to this question turns on the interaction between the common law and the Sale of Goods Act 1979 (the “SOGA”). In the following paragraphs, I will first answer the question based on the common law. Thereafter, I will consider whether the statutory overlay of the SOGA affects this answer in any way.

Breach of contract under the common law

50     I start by considering the position under the common law.

51     The Defendant submits that the Claimant’s breach entitles her to rescind the Agreement. This point is made in the following paragraph in the Defendant’s Closing Submissions, which is short enough to reproduce in full:[note: 62]

We submit that it is a condition that the Rolex and Hublot must be authentic, and thus a key term of the Sale and Purchase Agreement would have been breached if the Honourable Court finds that the watches sold by the Claimant were not authentic. Hence, the Honourable Court should rescind the Sale and Purchase Agreement. [emphasis added]

52     The Defendant’s reference to the Agreement being rescinded is potentially confusing. This is because recission can be understood in at least two senses: (a) a contract being rescinded ab initio; and (b) a contract being discharged. This distinction was drawn in the decision of the House of Lords in Johnson and another v Agnew [1980] AC 367, where Lord Wilberforce observed (at 392-393) as follows:

At this point it is important to dissipate a fertile source of confusion and to make clear that although the vendor is sometimes referred to in the above situation as “rescinding” the contract, this so-called “rescission” is quite different from rescission ab initio, such as may arise for example in cases of mistake, fraud or lack of consent. In those cases, the contract is treated in law as never having come into existence. … In the case of an accepted repudiatory breach the contract has come into existence but has been put an end to or discharged. Whatever contrary indications may be disinterred from old authorities, it is now quite clear, under the general law of contract, that acceptance of a repudiatory breach does not bring about “rescission ab initio.” …

53     Thus, where a contract is rescinded ab initio, it is, as the House of Lords observed, “treated in law as never having come into existence”. In other words, the effect is retrospective. On the other hand, where a contract is discharged, “all prospective and unperformed contractual obligations of both the innocent party and the [defaulting party] are terminated” [emphasis in original] (The Law of Contract in Singapore (Andrew Phang Boon Leong gen ed) (Academy Publishing, 2nd Ed, 2022) (“The Law of Contract in Singapore”) at para 17.010).

54     Unfortunately, because the entirety of the Defendant’s submissions on rescission are set out in the single paragraph reproduced at [51] above, it is not clear in which sense the Defendant seeks to rescind the Agreement.

55     That said, rescission ab initio seems to be a legal impossibility in this case. The remedies available to an innocent party, vis-à-vis a breach of contract, comprise: (a) compensatory damages: (b) non-compensatory damages; and (c) specific remedies (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 20.002). Rescission ab initio does not fall into any of these categories of remedies. In other words, it does not form part of the suite of remedies available for breach of contract.

56     Thus, the Defendant’s reference to the Agreement being rescinded can only be a reference to the Agreement being discharged. But this raises an anterior question: was the Defendant even entitled to discharge the Agreement in the first place? To take a step back, an innocent party is entitled to discharge a contract in the four situations identified by the Court of Appeal in RDC Concrete Pte Ltd v Sato Kogyo (S) Pte Ltd and another appeal [2007] 4 SLR(R) 413 (“RDC Concrete”). For present purposes, it is necessary to only refer to two of these situations: (a) where the term breached is a condition (which the Court of Appeal termed “Situation 3(a)”) (RDC Concrete at [97]); and (b) where the breach will give rise to an event which will deprive the innocent party of substantially the whole benefit which it was intended that he should obtain from the contract (which the Court of Appeal termed “Situation 3(b)”) (RDC Concrete at [99]).

57     As mentioned earlier (see [51] above), the Defendant submits that it was a condition of the Agreement that the Watches were authentic. The Claimant disputes this.[note: 63] In my assessment, the Claimant’s breach would, at the very least, fall under Situation 3(b). Where there is a contract for the sale of luxury goods, and the authenticity of such goods is a term of the contract, authenticity is everything. Indeed, the Claimant himself accepted as much at the trial:[note: 64]

Q    … I will put you in the shoe of a purchaser, okay. Purchaser of items. Because you want me to rephrase it, I put you in the shoe of a purchaser---

A    Mm-hm.

Q    ---of luxury items. In the ordinary course of things, when you purchase luxury items from somebody else---

A    Yah.

Q    ---is it a condition to you that what you purchase must be authentic?

A     Yah, it must be authentic; that’s why you purchase it.

[emphasis added]

58     Thus, the fact that the Watches were not authentic (see [44] above) would, in my view, deprive the Defendant of substantially the whole benefit which it was intended that she should obtain from the Agreement. The Defendant was therefore entitled to discharge the Agreement.

59     As noted in The Law of Contract in Singapore (at para 17.305), what happens upon discharge is that the primary contractual obligation (which is “to perform consistently with the requirements stipulated by the contractual terms”) is replaced by a secondary obligation to pay compensatory damages. While the Defendant seeks a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch (see [6] above), I am of the view that this is can be characterised as a claim for damages based on the Defendant’s reliance loss.

60     To explain, compensatory damages are traditionally measured by reference to either the expectation loss or the reliance loss of the innocent party. The expectation loss is the “pecuniary or monetary gains that [the innocent party] expected as a result of full performance of the contract” [emphasis in original] (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 21.033). Damages measured by reference to the expectation loss are intended to put the innocent party in the position he would have been had the contract been performed (Turf Club Auto Emporium Pte Ltd and others v Yeo Boong Hua and others and another appeal [2018] 2 SLR 655 (“Turf Club”) at [125]). In contrast, the reliance loss is the monetary costs or expenses incurred by the innocent party in reliance on the defaulting party’s contracted-for performance that are wasted on account of the defaulting party’s breach (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 21.034). Damages measured by reference to the reliance loss are intended to put the innocent party in the position as if the contract had never been entered into in the first place (Turf Club at [126]). Save for cases involving a “bad bargain”, the innocent party can elect to claim for either his expectation loss or his reliance loss (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 21.039).

61     In my view, the $28,000 paid by the Defendant to the Claimant can be considered the monetary costs or expenses incurred by the Defendant in reliance on the Claimant’s contracted-for performance that are now wasted on account of the Claimant’s breach. But there is a caveat to this. Because the Defendant is now in possession of the Watches, regard must also be had to their value in assessing the Defendant’s reliance loss. Put another way, the Defendant’s reliance loss would be $28,000 less the value of the Watches. However, no evidence was led on the value of the Watches. Notwithstanding, in view of my earlier observations that counterfeit watches are likely to be copyright and trademark-infringing, and that selling them with knowledge of their counterfeit nature attracts criminal liability (see [45] above), I think it must follow that the value of the Watches is $0. Ascribing any other value to the Watches would amount to an implicit endorsement of what legislation explicitly prohibits. I will, however, observe that the Defendant has confirmed that she is prepared to return the Watches to the Claimant,[note: 65] and it would be to her credit if she did so even if no order to this effect is actually made.

62     Accordingly, the Defendant’s counterclaim based on breach of contract can be allowed under the common law, and the Defendant would be entitled to a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch.

Statutory overlay: the SOGA

63     However, as alluded to earlier (see [49] above), this is not the end of the matter because in the context of contracts of sale of goods, the SOGA operates as a statutory overlay to the common law (see ss 1(1) and 62(2) of the SOGA).

64     To this end, the Claimant submits that pursuant to s 35(1)(a) and (4) of the SOGA, the Defendant was deemed to have accepted the Watches when she intimated to the Claimant that she had accepted them and/or when a reasonable period of time had elapsed without the Defendant intimating to the Claimant that she had rejected them.[note: 66] These provisions provide as follows:

Acceptance

35.—(1) Subject to subsection (2), the buyer is deemed to have accepted the goods —

(a)    when he intimates to the seller that he has accepted them; or

…

(4)    The buyer is also deemed to have accepted the goods when after the lapse of a reasonable time he retains the goods without intimating to the seller that he has rejected them.

65     Consequently, says the Claimant, pursuant to s 11(3) of the SOGA, the Defendant is no longer permitted to reject the Watches or rescind the Agreement.[note: 67] This provision states that:

(3)    Subject to section 35A, where a contract of sale is not severable and the buyer has accepted the goods or part of them, the breach of a condition to be fulfilled by the seller can only be treated as a breach of warranty, and not as a ground for rejecting the goods and treating the contract as repudiated, unless there is an express or implied term of the contract to that effect.

66     Collectively, these provisions of the SOGA give rise to two questions: (a) whether the Defendant was deemed to have accepted the Watches pursuant to s 35(1) or (4) of the SOGA; and (b) if so, how such acceptance affects the common law analysis at [50]-[62] above.

67     On the first question (ie, whether the Defendant was deemed to have accepted the Watches pursuant to s 35(1) or (4) of the SOGA), the Claimant first points to the Defendant’s evidence at the trial that she was satisfied with the Watches and did not raise any complaint after taking delivery of them.[note: 68] However, I am of the view that this is insufficient to constitute the kind of intimation required for the purposes of s 35(1)(a) of the SOGA. To my mind, this provision seems to require something more positive and overt which was absent in this case.

68     As for s 35(4) of the SOGA, the Claimant relies on the decision of the Court of Appeal in Eastern Supply Co v Kerr [1971-1973] SLR(R) 834 (“Eastern Supply”) to submit that the proper place for examination and acceptance of the Watches was at the time of delivery.[note: 69] This decision concerned the provision equivalent to s 35(4) of the SOGA in the Sale of Goods Act 1893. However, while I accept that the Court of Appeal in Eastern Supply did appear to hold (at [21] and in obiter dicta) that on the facts of that case (which involved the sale of a car), the proper place for examination and acceptance was the place of delivery, I do not think that the Court of Appeal was laying this down as a general rule. First, it is clear from the wording of s 35(4) of the SOGA itself that rejection can take place at a time after delivery, so long as it is done within a reasonable time. Second, as the Court of Appeal itself recognised (at [17]), “[i]t is always a question of fact whether or not a reasonable time has elapsed so as to preclude a buyer from rejecting the goods”. Third, the Court of Appeal had (at [21]) plainly left open the possibility that rejection might take place after delivery:

Similarly, in the present case, there being no special circumstances present, the place of delivery, ie the appellants’ premises, was the proper place for examination and for acceptance. It was open to the respondent to have the car examined by an expert before having it driven away. He chose not to do so. Instead he used the car by the use made of it by his wife for about two weeks. When during the course of the first few days the various minor defects which we have earlier referred to became apparent to his wife, he again had the opportunity of having the car examined by an expert but chose not to do so. Had he done so and an expert had found the front offside wheel leaning inwards at the top by about 10° from the vertical, a three inch play on the wheel and a bright mark on the shock absorber and the expert could say that those defects were serious defects which in the expert’s opinion made the car unroadworthy and he had then rejected the car, the defence of a breach of the implied conditions under s 14 of the Sale of Goods Act in answer to the present claim by the appellants might well have found favour with the trial judge. [emphasis added in italics and bold italics]

69     In the present case, the Defendant’s position is that she had rejected the Watches by way of her solicitors’ letter dated 18 April 2022.[note: 70] Interestingly, the Claimant dates the Defendant’s rejection earlier on 9 March 2022, this being the date of another of the Defendant’s solicitors’ letters.[note: 71] A further possible date might be 14 February 2022, when the Defendant had apparently requested to return the Watches for a refund at a meeting (see [42] above).

70     In my view, whether one takes 14 February 2022, 9 March 2022 or 18 April 2022 as the date of rejection, a reasonable time had not elapsed between the date of delivery on 6 January 2022 and the date of rejection. The goods in question are luxury watches. They are not everyday consumer goods whose quality (including authenticity) can be readily assessed by a layperson at the point of delivery. Nor are they goods which, like the car in Eastern Supply, although not everyday consumer goods, give off tell-tale signs to a layperson if there are issues with their quality. The point here is that the Defendant would have required time to identify an expert to examine the Watches. This would have been no easy feat because it appears that Mr Ong is the only such expert in Singapore.[note: 72] Further, Mr Ong would himself also have required time to examine the Watches.

71     This conclusion is also fortified by s 35(2) and (5) of the SOGA, which essentially preclude a finding of deemed acceptance until the buyer has had a reasonable opportunity of examining the goods:

(2)    Where goods are delivered to the buyer and he has not previously examined them, he is not deemed to have accepted them under subsection (1) until he has had a reasonable opportunity of examining them for the purpose —

(a)    of ascertaining whether they are in conformity with the contract; and

(b)    in the case of a contract for sale by sample, of comparing the bulk with the sample.

…

(5)    The questions that are material in determining for the purposes of subsection (4) whether a reasonable time has elapsed include whether the buyer has had a reasonable opportunity of examining the goods for the purpose mentioned in subsection (2).

72     It is true that, as of the earlier dates of 14 February 2022 and 9 March 2022, the Defendant had not yet approached Mr Ong. But this misses the point. Reasonableness is an objective standard. Thus, the time taken for authentication should be taken into account in determining whether a reasonable time had elapsed, and this remains the case whether or not the Defendant had actually subjected the Watches to authentication. For the same reason, I also do not give any weight to the Defendant’s claim that she did not doubt the authenticity of the Watches at the point they were delivered to her, and that she only became suspicious subsequently when she learnt that the Dior Bags were counterfeit (see [6] above).[note: 73] These may all well be true. But ultimately, they are subjective (and, indeed, fortuitous) matters that are irrelevant to what is essentially an objective inquiry.

73     In these circumstances, I am of the view that a reasonable time had not elapsed between delivery and rejection. The Defendant was therefore not deemed to have accepted the Watches pursuant to s 35(4) of the SOGA.

74     As the Defendant was not deemed to have accepted the Watches pursuant to s 35(1) or (4) of the SOGA, the second question (ie, how the Defendant’s acceptance of the Watches affects the common law analysis at [50]-[62] above) does not arise. Put another way, s 11(3) of the SOGA is not engaged and the common law analysis is not affected by the SOGA. However, for completeness, I set out some brief observations on this second question. I start by reproducing s 11(3) of the SOGA again for ease of reference:

(3)    Subject to section 35A, where a contract of sale is not severable and the buyer has accepted the goods or part of them, the breach of a condition to be fulfilled by the seller can only be treated as a breach of warranty, and not as a ground for rejecting the goods and treating the contract as repudiated, unless there is an express or implied term of the contract to that effect.

75     As I understand it, the Claimant’s position is that the authenticity of the Watches was not a condition of the Agreement,[note: 74] but, even if it were, this provision would apply to preclude the Defendant’s counterclaim.[note: 75]

76     Neither party cited any authority on s 11(3) of the SOGA. However, on a plain reading, the phrase “rejecting the goods and treating the contract as repudiated” must, I think, refer collectively to a repudiation of the contract. In this regard, the term “repudiation” is commonly used to refer to circumstances entitling the innocent party to discharge the contract (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 17.009 read with para 17.010). As mentioned earlier (see [56] above), under the common law, an innocent party is entitled to discharge a contract in the four situations identified by the Court of Appeal in RDC Concrete, including: (a) where the term breached is a condition (ie¸ Situation 3(a)); and (b) where the breach will give rise to an event which will deprive the innocent party of substantially the whole benefit which it was intended that he should obtain from the contract (ie, Situation 3(b)).

77     As the provisions of the SOGA trump the common law where there is any inconsistency between the two (s 62(2) of the SOGA), what s 11(3) of the SOGA does is to effectively remove, in cases where the SOGA applies (and subject to the qualifications in the provision itself), the innocent party’s right to discharge a contract in Situation 3(a). However, without expressing a view on whether the Claimant’s breach would fall under Situation 3(a), I have earlier found that it would, at the very least, fall under Situation 3(b) (see [57] above). This then raises a rather interesting question as to whether s 11(3) of the SOGA, because it only refers to the breach of a condition, even applies to Situation 3(b). Nevertheless, as this question does not arise in the present case, and as this interpretation is not without its difficulties, I will say no more on this question.

Conclusion on the Defendant’s counterclaim based on breach of contract

78     In summary, I find that the Defendant’s counterclaim based on breach of contract can be allowed under the common law, and the Defendant would be entitled to a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch (see [50]-[62] above). I also find that the common law analysis is not affected by the SOGA (see [63]-[77] above). The Defendant’s counterclaim based on breach of contract should therefore be allowed.

Defendant’s counterclaim based on failure of consideration

79     Turning to the Defendant’s counterclaim based on failure of consideration, this is pleaded as an alternative basis for the refund of the $28,000 the Defendant paid for the Rolex Watch. Having found that the Defendant’s counterclaim based on breach of contract should be allowed, it is, strictly speaking, not necessary for me to consider this alternative basis. Moreover, failure of consideration is, for some reason, not mentioned at all in the Defendant’s Closing Submissions. Nevertheless, I will proceed to consider it for completeness.

80     A failure of consideration is not, in and of itself, a cause of action. Rather, it is one of the established “unjust factors” in a claim in unjust enrichment (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 24.016). As the Court of Appeal recently held in Benzline Auto Pte Ltd v Supercars Lorinser Pte Ltd and another [2018] 1 SLR 239 (“Benzline”) (at [45]), subject to any defences a defendant might raise, the three requirements of a claim in unjust enrichment are: (a) enrichment of the defendant; (b) at the expense of the claimant; and (c) circumstances which make the enrichment unjust (ie, the presence of an “unjust factor”). Being an “unjust factor”, a failure of consideration is one means by which the third requirement can be satisfied.

81     The first two requirements can be dealt with briefly in the present case. The Claimant, having been paid $28,000, was no doubt enriched. This is not affected by the fact that the Watches are now in the Defendant’s possession since I have earlier found that the value of the Watches is $0 (see [61] above). Such enrichment was also clearly at the expense of the Defendant, since the Claimant’s gain was attributable to the Defendant’s actions in making payment (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 24.056). Thus, as was the case in Benzline (at [45]), the crucial question is whether the third requirement is satisfied. Specifically, was there a failure of consideration?

82     As the Court of Appeal in Benzline observed (at [46], the inquiry as to a failure of consideration involves two stages: (a) what was the basis for the transfer in respect of which restitution is sought; and (b) did that basis fail? To explain the nomenclature, there is a reference to a “basis” here because a failure of consideration is also referred to as a failure of basis (Benzline at [46]).

83     At the first stage (ie, what was the basis for the transfer in respect of which restitution is sought?), the basis for the transfer must be objectively determined based on what is communicated between the parties, although a basis may be either express or implied (Benzline at [51]). In Benzline, the Court of Appeal referred to the decision of the English Court of Appeal in Rowland v Divall [1923] 2 KB 500 (“Rowland”). Because the facts of Rowland bear considerable similarity to the present case, I start by setting out the Court of Appeal’s summary of the facts in Rowland (at [51]):

… In that case, the English Court of Appeal found that the basis for a car buyer’s payment was that the seller had good title to the car to sell, even though nothing had been said in that regard. That the seller had good title to pass to the buyer was a fundamental feature of the sale and could be implied as part of the basis for the payment: see [Charles Mitchell, Paul Mitchell & Stephen Watterson, Goff & Jones: The Law of Unjust Enrichment (Sweet & Maxwell, 9th Ed, 2016)] at para 13-06. …

84     The Court of Appeal then relied on Rowland to make two points. First, “even where a basis is implied, that implication must be based on objective features of the transfer and its context, and not merely on a fortuitous overlap between the unexpressed expectation of the parties” (Benzline at [51]). Thus, the Court of Appeal observed (at [51]) that in Rowland, “the passage of good title was objectively fundamental to the sale of a car, and this would have been apparent to any observer”. Second, a transfer “may have more than one basis” (Benzline at [52]). The Court of Appeal cited (at [52]) Rowland an example of such a case: while the physical delivery of the car was no doubt a basis of the buyer’s payment, the transfer of good title was equally fundamentally so.

85     In the present case, I am of the view that the authenticity of the Watches can, at the very least, be implied as one basis for the transfer. As mentioned earlier (see [57] above), where there is a contract for the sale of luxury goods, and the authenticity of such goods is a term of the contract, authenticity is everything. To use the language of the Court of Appeal, the authenticity of the Watches was objectively fundamental to their sale, and this would have been apparent to any observer. Furthermore, while the physical delivery of the Watches was no doubt a basis of the Defendant’s payment, the authenticity of the Watches was equally fundamentally so.

86     As for the second stage (ie, did that basis fail?), given my finding that the Watches were not authentic (see [44] above), this basis for the transfer (ie, the authenticity of the Watches) has evidently failed. But does it follow from this that the basis for the transfer has failed? In Benzline, the Court of Appeal observed (at [52]) that in Rowland:

… The fact that the car was delivered without good title meant that a fundamental component of the basis had failed, such that it could … legitimately be said that the basis had wholly failed even though the buyer had physical possession of the car for several months before the defect in title was discovered. [emphasis added]

87     From this, it appears that where there is more than one basis for the transfer, the failure of any one basis for the transfer is sufficient to ground a finding that the basis for the transfer has failed. Thus, there was, in the present case, a failure of consideration.

88     In the circumstances, all three requirements of a claim in unjust enrichment are satisfied. The Defendant’s counterclaim based on failure of consideration should therefore be allowed.

89     In terms of the remedy, it appears that a refund is an appropriate remedy upon a claim in unjust enrichment succeeding. As noted in The Law of Contract in Singapore (at para 24.129):

… Where the contract between the contracting parties makes no express or implied provision for the making of refunds, the common law recognised that where the defendant promisor had entirely and completely failed to perform those of its obligations for which the advance payment had been made by the promisee, the promisor would be legally obliged to repay to the promisee a sum equivalent to that which had been received so as to effect a refund. [emphasis in original in italics, emphasis added in bold italics]

90     Thus, the appropriate remedy would for the Claimant to pay the Defendant $28,000 less the value of the Watches, this being the nett enrichment to the Claimant. As I have earlier found that the value of the Watches is $0 (see [61] above), the Defendant is accordingly entitled to the same remedy as she is under the breach of contract analysis: a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch.

91     Before concluding this section, I deal with the Claimant’s submissions on failure of consideration. These are that: (a) the Defendant had already received and enjoyed the benefit of the Watches; and (b) it is trite that consideration must be sufficient but need not be adequate, and the Court should therefore not investigate its adequacy or whether it is of equal value.[note: 76] The answer to the first objection has already been set out at [83]-[87] above. To reiterate, both the physical delivery of the Watches and the authenticity of the Watches were bases for the Defendant's payment. Where a transfer has more than one basis, the failure of any one basis for the transfer is sufficient to ground a finding that the basis for the transfer has failed. Thus, it is sufficient that, in the present case, one basis for the transfer (ie, the authenticity of the Watches) has failed. As regards the second objection, consideration in the law of unjust enrichment is not the same as consideration as understood in the contractual sense (Benzline at [48]). In the context of unjust enrichment, consideration “refers not to the promise of performance, but the actual completion of the contractual promises” [emphasis in original] (The Law of Contract in Singapore at para 24.115). I therefore do not think that it is relevant to refer to the principles of consideration as understood in the contractual sense.

Claimant’s claim

92     Finally, I consider the Claimant’s claim, which, in light of my findings on the Defendant’s counterclaim, can be dealt with briefly. The Claimant relies here on s 49(1) of the SOGA,[note: 77] which provides as follows:

Action for price

49.—(1)    Where, under a contract of sale, the property in the goods has passed to the buyer and he wrongfully neglects or refuses to pay for the goods according to the terms of the contract, the seller may maintain an action against him for the price of the goods.

93     The short answer to s 49(1) of the SOGA is that the Defendant cannot be said to have “wrongfully” neglected or refused to pay for the Hublot Watch. Under both the breach of contract analysis and the failure of consideration analysis, the Defendant is entitled to a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch (see [78] and [90] above). This necessarily implies that she is also not required to pay the Claimant the balance sum of $16,000 for the Hublot Watch. Otherwise, the refund will have to be for the larger sum of $44,000 (ie, $28,000 plus $16,000). Accordingly, there is nothing wrongful with the Defendant’s withholding of payment for the Hublot Watch. The Claimant’s claim should therefore be dismissed.

Conclusion on Issue 2A

94     For the above reasons, the conclusion on Issue 2A is that the Defendant’s counterclaim should be allowed on the basis on either breach of contract (see [49]-[78] above) or failure of consideration (see [79]-[91] above). Pursuant to this, the Defendant is entitled to a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch. The Claimant’s claim, on the other hand, should be dismissed (see [92]-[93] above).

Conclusion

95     I conclude by returning to the issues identified at [9] above. In answer to Issue 1, I find that the Watches were not authentic (see [44] above). Given this, it is Issue 2A that arises and, in answer to Issue 2A, it follows that: (a) the Defendant’s counterclaim should be allowed on the basis of either breach of contract (see [78] above) or failure of consideration (see [88] above); and (b) the Claimant’s claim should be dismissed (see [93] above). I also find that, pursuant to (a), the Defendant is entitled to a refund of the $28,000 she paid for the Rolex Watch (see [78] and [90] above).

96     Accordingly, I enter judgment for the Defendant against the Claimant for the sum of $28,000.
