Introduction

1       The accused person, Mohamed Rizhwan Bin Abdul Rahim, claimed trial to the following charge:

DAC-910983-2021 (“the Charge”)

You…are charged that you, on or before 29 April 2021, in Singapore, did consume a specified drug listed in the Fourth Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed), to wit, Methamphetamine, without authorization under the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed) or the regulations made thereunder, and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 8(b)(ii) of the said Act,

and further, that you, before the commission of the said offence, were on 17 January 2018 in District Court 5, vide DAC0938962/2016, convicted of an offence for consumption of a specified drug, to wit, Methamphetamine, under Section 8(b)(ii) of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed) and punished under Section 33A(1) of the said Act with 5 years imprisonment, and 3 strokes of the cane, which conviction and punishment have not been set aside to date, and you shall now be punished under Section 33A(2) of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed),

and further, that you, from 29 February 2020 to 28 October 2021, were subject to a remission order made by the Commissioner of Prisons under Division 2 or 3 of Part VB of the Prisons Act (Cap. 247, Rev. Ed. 2014) which remission order is subject to the basic condition under section 50S(1) of the Prisons Act and while the remission order is in effect you, on 29 April 2021, committed the aforesaid offence and upon conviction and the imposition of a sentence reflected under section 50S(1)(b) of the Prisons Act, shall be deemed to have breached the basic condition of your remission order, and you are thereby liable to be punished under section 50T(2)(b) of the Prisons Act with an enhanced sentence of imprisonment for a term not exceeding the remaining duration of the remission order of 183 Days from 29 April 2021 to 28 October 2021 for that offence.

2       I convicted the accused on the Charge at the trial’s conclusion.

3       The accused pleaded guilty to two other charges (DAC-918983-2021 and DAC-918984-2021). The accused admitted to and consented to another charge to be taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing (DAC-918985-2021). These charges pertained to offences committed under regulation 15(3)(f) read with regulation 15(6)(a) of the Misuse of Drugs (Approved Institutions, Medical Observation and Treatment and Rehabilitation) Regulations (Cap. 185, Rg 3, 1999 Revised Edition), and for which the accused was liable for enhanced sentences.

4       For the Charge, I sentenced the accused to an imprisonment term of seven years and six months with six strokes of the cane, and an enhanced sentence of 130 days’ imprisonment.

5       For DAC-918983-2021, I sentenced the accused to an imprisonment term of seven months and an enhanced sentence of 50 days’ imprisonment. For DAC-918984-2021, I sentenced the accused to an imprisonment term of seven months and an enhanced sentence of 50 days’ imprisonment.

6       I bore in mind the one-transaction rule and the totality principle as set out in Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998. I ordered the sentences for the Charge and DAC-918983-2021 to run consecutively. These charges were committed on separate occasions and involved offences that invaded distinct legally protected interests. I ordered the sentence for DAC-918984-2021 to run concurrently. The aggregate length of all the enhanced sentences must not exceed 297 days, as determined based on the date of the earliest offence committed (in this case, DAC-918983-2021).

7       The global sentence was an imprisonment term of seven years and 13 months with six strokes of the cane, and an enhanced sentence of 230 days’ imprisonment. The accused is presently serving his sentence.

8       The accused has appealed against the conviction and the sentence imposed for the Charge. He is not appealing against the sentences imposed for the charges to which he pleaded guilty. As such, I set out below the reasons for my decision in respect of the Charge.

The prosecution’s case

9       The prosecution’s position was as set out below:

(a)     The accused’s urine specimens were procured in accordance with the First Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations (Cap 185, Regulation 6, 1999 Revised Edition), as supported by the prosecution witnesses’ testimony in relation to the urine procurement process, instant urine test (“IUT”) and sealing of urine containers for analysis by the Health Sciences Authority (“HSA”);[note: 1]

(b)     Methamphetamine was found in the accused’s urine specimens as evidenced by the results of the urine tests conducted by HSA, which led to the conclusion that the accused had consumed methamphetamine prior to his arrest;[note: 2]

(c)     The accused admitted to consumption of methamphetamine without authorisation, as set out in the accused’s statement under section 22 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap. 68, 2012 Revised Edition) dated 30 April 2021 (“the 30 April 2021 Statement”) (Exhibit P9);[note: 3]

(d)     The accused was presumed to have consumed methamphetamine under section 16 of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Revised Edition) (“the section 16 presumption”), and he failed to rebut the presumption;[note: 4] and

(e)     The presumption of consumption under section 22 of the Misuse of Drugs Act (“the section 22 presumption”) applied as methamphetamine was found in the accused’s urine, and the Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations were complied with. The accused had not rebutted the section 22 presumption.[note: 5]

Presumptions under the Misuse of Drugs Act

16     Based on the HSA Certificate Lab No. 2107829-AT-001 and HSA Certificate Lab No. 2107829-AT-002, the accused’s urine from the two Blue Urine Containers were found to contain methamphetamine (Exhibit P5 and Exhibit P6). Pertinently, the accused admitted at the trial that he consumed methamphetamine:[note: 25]

“Q    I put it to you that your urine had tested positive for methamphetamine because you had consumed methamphetamine. Agree or disagree?

A    Agree.

…

Q    I put it to you that you had consumed methamphetamine without authorisation on or about 29th April 2021. Agree or disagree?

A    Agree.”

17     The section 16 presumption reads as follows:

“Certificate of analyst, etc.

16.A certificate purporting —

(a)to be signed by —

(i)an analyst employed by the Health Sciences Authority; or

(ii)such other person as the Minister may, by notification in the Gazette, appoint; and

(b)to relate to a controlled drug or controlled substance,

shall be admitted in evidence, in any proceedings for an offence under this Act, on its production by the prosecution without proof of signature and, until the contrary is proved, shall be proof of all matters contained therein.”

18     The HSA certificates were presumptive proof that the accused’s urine specimens contained methamphetamine, and the accused failed to rebut the section 16 presumption. Specifically:[note: 26]

(a)     The accused did not question Ng Chee Ann (PW9) and Maggie Tiong Su Su (PW10) (“the HSA analysts”);

(b)     When cross-examined, the accused did not dispute the findings of the HSA analysts; and

(c)     The accused admitted that his urine specimens contained methamphetamine because he had consumed methamphetamine.

19     The section 22 presumption reads as follows:

“Presumption relating to urine test

22.    If any controlled drug is found in the urine of a person as a result of both urine tests conducted under section 31(4)(b), he shall be presumed, until the contrary is proved, to have consumed that controlled drug in contravention of section 8(b).”

20     The section 22 presumption applied given that methamphetamine was found in the accused’s urine as evidenced by the HSA certificates. The Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations were complied with, and the accused failed to rebut the section 22 presumption. Notably, the accused’s allegations of non-compliance with the Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations were complete afterthoughts and without basis. In particular:[note: 27]

(a)     The accused confirmed that there were “multiple instances” in the past where his urine had been procured. The accused admitted that he “would have raised it” if there were “any deviation” from his previous experiences of urine procurement. However, the accused did not raise any of these “serious” urine procurement issues to PW2;[note: 28]

(b)     The accused did not raise these purported allegations when the Notice of Warning was administered, and an adverse inference should be drawn against the accused under section 261(1) of the Criminal Procedure Code;

(c)     The accused’s allegation that the urine tests only showed methamphetamine when he also consumed other drugs was a belated claim which did not rebut the section 22 presumption. The accused stated in the 30 April 2021 Statement that he did not abuse any other controlled drugs besides methamphetamine; and

(d)     The accused’s allegation of a break in the chain of custody was without merit. There was no requirement in the Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations for urine procurement to be done in the same location or for the IUT to be done in the accused’s presence.

The defence’s case

22     The defence’s case was summarised as follows:

(a)     PW1 lodged the arrest report, even though she was not the actual arresting officer. As PW1 arrived at the scene later without knowing what had actually transpired” there “might” be an inaccurate occurrence in the arrest report[note: 29]. In this regard, the arrest report, which only stated that the accused was arrested, reads as follows:

“On the above mentioned date, time and location, While performing L4R3C duties consisting of Sgt Elton, SC/SGT Iqbal and myself and L4R1C consisting of Sgt David and Sgt Adilah assisted L4P1C consisting of ASP Shen Xiangwei and Sgt Ren Hui, arrested the below mentioned subject for Section 8B Cap. 185 under the instructions of AIO Zhi Zhong”;

(b)     The accused “believed” that PW2, PW3 and PW5 were aware of the standard operating procedure (“SOP”) when handling a COVID-19 suspect. However, only PW3 abided by it, whereas PW2 and PW5 did not “don” “full PPE”. PW2 and PW5 “failed to uphold” the SOP when handling a COVID-19 suspect and lacked credibility. This meant they “might” not be able to recall “what actually happened” due to the case duration;[note: 30]

(c)     There were two versions of unsealing, testing, labelling and disposing, and the accused stated the following points:[note: 31]

(i)       The quantity of the polymer bags used for sealing and unsealing;

(ii)       There was no indication or identification on the polymer bag;

(iii)       The testing of the urine specimens was not done in the accused’s presence, so it could be anyone’s urine specimen;

(iv)       The labelling of the urine specimen bottles sent to HSA;

(v)       The disposal of the Red Master Bottle was not done by the accused, so it could be anyone’s master bottle;

(d)     From the start of the urine procurement until the testing of the urine specimens, there were breaches of the requirements set out in the First Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations;[note: 32]

(e)     There was no closed-circuit television (“CCTV”) footage available for this case. In this regard, the accused’s response was “I understand” when the prosecution made the necessary checks and confirmed that “no CCTV [was] available”;[note: 33]

(f)     PW11’s evidence about comparing hospitals with police stations was two different scenarios. Police stations have an IUT facility, but hospitals do not have an IUT facility;[note: 34] and

(g)     The accused cited the case of Mohamed Affandi bin Rosli v Public Prosecutor [2019] 1 SLR 440 (“Affandi”) to support the point that it was necessary that the chain of custody was an unbroken trail without gaps and discrepancies.[note: 35]

25     Notwithstanding these points raised by the accused, the accused testified that while he was escorted from Woodlands Police Division to Tanglin Police Division, he was “using both hands to grip” the anti-tamper polymer bag which was sealed.[note: 46] The accused added that he “held” the sealed anti-tamper polymer bag containing the two Blue Urine Containers at the Tanglin Police Division lock-up.[note: 47]

26     In response to a point during cross-examination that he “would have objected” to PW5 putting labels with his name on the two Blue Urine Containers if it were not his urine in those containers, the accused conceded that he “did not object”. The accused also agreed that he “put one blue urine container into hole number 1 of the metal security box” and “the other blue urine container into hole number 2 of the metal security box”. Further, the accused was “aware” that the urine in the two Blue Urine Containers would be sent to HSA for testing. However, in response to a point during cross-examination that he “would not agree” to send the urine specimens to HSA if they “had not been yours”, the accused stated that he “did not know” that the procedure was “as such”.[note: 48]

27     It is notable that the accused “did not think of contesting” the evidence of the HSA analysts, and the accused admitted that he “did not challenge it”. More significantly, the accused admitted that his “urine tested positive for methamphetamine as [he] consumed methamphetamine”.[note: 49] The accused acknowledged that he made no mention of consuming other drugs apart from “sejuk” (which is methamphetamine) in his statements, notwithstanding that he “consumed methamphetamine with ganja with other drugs”. The accused explained that “[t]here isn’t a need to mention it [in the Notice of Warning] because that result doesn’t appear in the HSA”.[note: 50] In this regard, the accused agreed that all he said in the Notice of Warning were the words “I wish for leniency”. When the accused was asked why he did not raise this point pertaining to “ganja with other drugs” during the prosecution’s case, the accused’s response was that he “cannot remember everything”.[note: 51]

The court’s decision

Finding

28     The integrity of the chain of custody of the urine specimens was critical. This turned on the facts of the case. Based on the evidence provided at the trial, I found that there was no break in the chain of custody. The accused held onto the urine specimens at all times, and when he was not doing so, the specimens were kept within his line of sight. This was the case while the accused was escorted from Woodlands Police Division to Tanglin Police Division, while he was changing his clothes, while he was at his cell at Tanglin Police Division, and while the IUT was taking place. It bore emphasis that the IUT result which was disputed by the accused was the same as the HSA result for the urine specimens which were in the accused’s line of sight throughout.

29     Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, PW3 and PW11 testified that all accused persons with COVID-19 symptoms had to be brought to Tanglin Police Division, which was the designated location for persons-in-custody with COVID-19 symptoms. Given the accused had COVID-19 symptoms, he was brought to Tanglin Police Division.

30     According to PW11, IUT could be carried out in the absence of an accused person. This was for cases where an accused person was warded in a hospital. It demonstrated that this practice was not unique or conducted for the first time. In addition, I noted the prosecution’s point that there was no requirement under the Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations for urine procurement, IUT and sealing to be carried out at the same location.

31     In my view, the prosecution witnesses provided detailed evidence and consistent testimony. I found them to be credible witnesses. Specifically, PW2 outlined the urine procurement process clearly, and PW5 was able to comprehensively depict the IUT and sealing process step-by-step.

32     On the accused’s allegation about the inaccurate IUT results, it was pertinent that paragraph 8 of the 30 April 2021 statement expressly stated that “Besides ‘sejuk’, I did not abuse any other controlled drugs.” The accused’s case theory shifted drastically to one where he consumed methamphetamine with ganja and other drugs, which should have been present in the IUT results. The accused had the opportunity to raise this in his statements, but he did not do so. Accordingly, I did not find this allegation from the accused to be convincing.

33     The accused raised four points at the trial for the first time, and I did not find them to be compelling or believable. These new points were questionable for these reasons:

(a)     The accused agreed that he had previous experience with the urine procurement process, and he would have raised concerns if there were any deviation from his previous experience. However, the accused did not raise concerns to PW2 during the urine procurement process. In addition, the accused’s statements did not contain any of these points which the accused identified as “serious issues with urine procurement”. If these were indeed serious issues in the accused’s opinion, the accused would have raised them during the urine procurement process or in his statements, especially since the accused had previous experience with the urine procurement process. On the contrary, the accused asked for leniency in the Notice of Warning.

(b)     I did not accept the accused’s explanation that he did not know the procedure when the prosecution posed the point to the accused that he would not have agreed to send the urine specimens to HSA if they had not been his. In fact, the accused admitted that he was aware his urine specimens would be sent to HSA for testing.

(c)     The accused did not challenge PW2’s and PW5’s testimony or cross-examine them about the alleged discrepancies which he raised. The accused also agreed that he did not object to PW5 labelling the urine specimens with his name and identification number.

34     In Affandi, the prosecution “failed to establish the chain of custody of the exhibits, given the inconsistent accounts given by different members of the arresting party”: at [57]. Essentially, the prosecution “must show an unbroken chain. There cannot be a single moment that is not accounted for if this might give rise to a reasonable doubt as to the identity of the exhibits: PP v Chen Mingjian [2009] 4 SLR(R) 946 (“Chen Mingjian”) at [4]”: Affandi at [39]. “That is not to say that speculative arguments about the possibility of contamination will be entertained: Chen Mingjian at [4]”: Affandi at [41].

35     Unlike the Affandi case, no inconsistent narratives emerged from the evidence at the trial here. In my view, the evidence of the prosecution witnesses was consistent and did not suggest that there was a break in the chain of custody. There was nothing out of the ordinary in this case that indicated otherwise. The movement of the urine specimens from the start to the end was accounted for.

36     Accordingly, I found that the prosecution had proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt. I convicted the accused of the Charge.

39     The accused also had many drug-related offences in 2012, 2013, 2015 and 2020, where he was placed under drug supervision.

The prosecution’s submission on sentence

40     On the sentencing objectives, the prosecution highlighted that general deterrence aimed to educate and deter other like-minded members of the general public by making an example of a particular offender and dissuading the public from committing drug offences. In the present case, specific deterrence also applied, as the accused was heavily traced for multiple drug, property and other offences involving weapons, gaming, affray and desertion from civil defence.[note: 52]

41     The prosecution sought an imprisonment term of seven years and six months to eight years, with six strokes of the cane. The prosecution submitted that a slight uplift from the mandatory minimum sentence of seven years’ imprisonment with six strokes of the cane was warranted, in view of the fact that the accused had been convicted after trial and the aggravating factors including the accused’s recalcitrance in committing multiple drug offences.[note: 53]

42     On the enhanced sentence, the prosecution sought an imprisonment term of 152 days. The prosecution submitted that the overall level of severity for the fresh offence was in the mid-point of Band 3 (high severity) of the framework in Abdul Mutalib Bin Aziman & ors v Public Prosecutor [2021] 4 SLR 1220 (“Mutalib”), which attracted a sentencing range of 2/3 to the full remaining duration of the remission order:[note: 54]

(a)      Gravity of the offence. The offence was a serious one of moderate to high gravity. This was a repeat offence warranting the imposition of at least the mandatory minimum sentence of seven years’ imprisonment with six strokes of the cane; and

(b)      Rehabilitative prospects. The accused’s consistent pattern of offending did not present an optimistic picture for his rehabilitative prospects. The accused’s long string of drug-related antecedents dating back to 2009 (including drug trafficking, drug consumption, drug possession, possession of drug utensils and failing to report for urine test offences) showed an overall lack of commitment to rehabilitation and reintegration. In addition, the offence was identical in nature to the offence for which the accused was placed on a remission order. Lastly, the offence was committed about mid-way through the remission order period.

Mitigation

43     The accused asked for sympathy and for the minimum sentence to be imposed. The accused promised that this would be his last offence, and that he would be useful to the community and do good for the society.[note: 55]

Decision on sentence

44     The accused has a litany of drug-related antecedents. There must be specific deterrence. The Charge was a Long Term 2 offence for which the accused claimed trial. The accused was a repeat offender who was convicted for drug consumption offences in 2009 and 2013 and for LT1 in 2018. I imposed an imprisonment term of seven years and six months with six strokes of the cane.

45     On the enhanced sentence for the Charge, the accused’s fresh offence was a serious one of moderate to high gravity. The fresh offence was the same as the offence for which the remission order was given. Further, the fresh offence was committed shortly over a year from the start date of the remission order. This demonstrated the accused’s inclination to reoffend. The accused’s rehabilitative prospects were low. I found that this fell within Band 3 (high degree of severity) of the Mutalib framework, which was 2/3 to the full remaining duration of the remission order at the time of the fresh offence. In my assessment, the appropriate enhanced sentence for this offence was 130 days. This did not exceed the main sentence for the Charge.

Conclusion

46     The accused was convicted on the Charge. Having considered the facts of the case, the prosecution’s submission on sentence and the accused’s mitigation, I imposed an imprisonment term of seven years and six months with six strokes of the cane, and an enhanced sentence of 130 days’ imprisonment for the Charge.

47     The accused is presently serving his sentence.
