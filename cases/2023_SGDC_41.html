District Judge Salina Bte Ishak:

Background

1       The accused, Mr Clement Tan Hao Feng, a male Singapore citizen presently 27 years old faced the following charges:

1stCharge

on 6 October 2021 at about 2.05 p.m., along Pan Island Expressway (PIE) towards the direction of Airport, Singapore, did drive a motor vehicle GBG6849T on a road without due care and attention, to wit, by failing to keep a proper control of the motor van thus resulting in a head to rear collision with FBP4225J, SMX8723P and SMT667S and hurt was caused to the rider of motorcycle FBP4225J, Irfan Audi Bin Idris, a Singaporean male Malay, age 27 years old by such driving and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 65(1)(a) punishable under Section 65(4)(a) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”).

2nd Charge

on 6 October 2021 at about 2.05 p.m, along Pan Island Expressway (PIE) towards the direction of Airport, Singapore, did drive a motor vehicle GBG6849T on a road without due care and attention, to wit, by failing to keep a proper control of the motor van thus resulting in a head to rear collision with FBP4225J, SMX8723P and SMT667S and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 65(1)(a) punishable under Section 65(5)(a) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”).

Procedural history

2       The accused first appeared in Court 7B on 6 June 2022 and had indicated his intention to plead guilty to both charges. As the accused claimed he had issues with his brakes and qualified his plea, his plea was rejected by the Court. His matter was subsequently adjourned several times for him to make representations as well as to engage Counsel.

3       On 15 February 2023, the accused finally pleaded guilty to both charges and admitted to the Statement of Facts without any qualification. After carefully considering the Prosecution’s submissions on sentence and the Defence’s plea in mitigation, I sentenced the accused as follows:

(a)     1st charge - fine of $2,500 in default ten days’ imprisonment and disqualified him from holding or obtaining all classes of driving licences for a period of 18 months with effect from 15 February 2023.

(b)     2nd charge - fine of $1,500 in default five days’ imprisonment and disqualified him from holding or obtaining all classes of driving licences for a period of 15 months with effect from 15 February 2023.

His global sentence was fine of $4,000 in default 15 days’ imprisonment and disqualification for a period of 18 months for all classes of driving licences with effect from 15 February 2022 until 14 August 2024.

4       As he had applied for his fines to be paid in instalments, a sum of $2,000 was paid on 15 February 2023 while the remaining $2,000 is to be paid by 15 March 2023.

5       The accused being dissatisfied with my decision has filed his Notice of Appeal against his order of disqualification on 15 February 2023. Pursuant to s 383(1) of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 68, 2012 Rev Ed)(“CPC”), an appeal does not operate as a stay of execution on any judgment, sentence or order pending the hearing of an appeal. As there is no order to suspend the operation of the disqualification pursuant to s 42(4) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”), the disqualification orders made for the present case are effective from 15 February 2023.

6       Having set out the background for the present case, as the appeal is only against the disqualification order, I now provide the reasons for my decision.

Salient Facts

7       The accused was the driver of motor van GBG6849T at the time of the accident. The first victim is Chen Jiaxiong, a Singaporean male Chinese, aged 33 years old and the driver of motor car SMT667S. The second victim is Ng Tech Wee, a Singaporean male Chinese, aged 46 years old and the driver of motor car SMX8723P. The third victim is Irfan Audi Bin Idris, a Singaporean male Malay, aged 27 years old and the rider of motorcycle FBP4225J.

8       On 6 October 2021 at about 2.05pm, the accused was driving on lane 1 of 3 lanes along Pan Island Expressway (PIE) towards the direction of Airport, Singapore. The accused had driven his motor van, GBG6849T, without due care and attention as the accused, while driving on lane 1 of 3 lanes, had failed to keep a proper control of the motor van thus resulting in a head to rear collision with FBP4225J, SMX8723P and SMT667S.

Injuries

9       The first and second victims were not injured while the third victim was conveyed by ambulance from the accident scene to Tan Tock Seng Hospital (TTSH). The third victim was diagnosed with left knee and ankle contusion. There were tenderness over his left lower limb, superficial abrasions over his knees and shins and some left ankle tenderness. There were no fractures or dislocation. The third victim was hospitalised from 6 October 2021 to 8 October 2021, ie for 3 days and was discharged with 17 days of hospitalisation leave from 6 October 2021 to 22 October 2021. He was reviewed by TTSH outpatient orthopaedic clinic on 21 October 2021 and his knee pain had significantly resolved. He was then given further hospitalisation leave from 21 October 2021 to 31 October 2021, ie for 9 days. In total, the third victim was given 26 days of hospitalisation leave. No surgery was performed.

Damages

10     The first victim’s motor car sustained scratches and cracks to the rear bumper. The second victim’s motor car sustained cracks, scratches, and dents to both the front portion and the rear bumper. The third victim’s motorcycle sustained cracks at the front portion, the rear left and rear top portion. The right side of the front fork was twisted. The accused’s motor van sustained cracks, scratches and dents at the front portion.

11     The inspection report from LKK Auto Consultants Pte Ltd dated 17 November 2021 of the accused’s motor van had stated that the engine system, steering system, braking system, and transmission system were all in serviceable condition. There was no evidence to suggest any possible mechanical failure to the motor van that may have caused and/or contributed to the accident.

Footages

12     The footage obtained from motor car, SMT667S shows motor car SMT667S travelling on lane 1 of 3 lanes and slowing down due to slow moving traffic on lane 1. Motor car SMX8723P, which was travelling behind SMT667S was also seen slowing down. Subsequently, it shows motor van GBG6849T, which was travelling on lane 1 of 3 lanes, colliding onto motorcycle FBP4225J, then motorcar SMX8723P, causing motorcar SMX8723P to collide into motorcar SMT667S.

13     The footage from an unknown passer-by shows motor van GBG6849T travelling on lane 1 of 3 lanes and overtaking two slow-moving vehicles which were on lane 2. There was ample space for motor van GBG6849T to change lane back to lane 2. Subsequently, vehicles on lane 1 were seen slowing down. Motor van, GBG6849T then rear-ended vehicles FBP4225J, SMX8723P, causing SMX8723P to rear-end SMT667S. The accused’s collision with the motorcycle also caused the third victim to be flung onto lane 2 of 3 lanes.

Cost of Repair

14     The cost of repairs was as follows:

(a)     Motor car SMT667S - $10,407.44

(b)     Motor car SMX8723P - $29,547.30.

(c)     Motorcycle FBP4225J sustained total loss of $13,800 from the accident.

Prevailing conditions

15     At the time of accident, weather was clear, road surface was dry, and the visibility was clear. The traffic flow was moderate.

16     By virtue of the foregoing, the accused has committed the following offences:

(a)     Driving without due care and attention to others causing hurt under s 65(1)(a) of the RTA punishable under s 65(4)(a) of the said Act.

(b)     Driving without due care and attention to others under s 65(1)(a) of the RTA punishable under s 65(5)(a) of the said Act.

Sentencing

Prescribed Penalties

17     The prescribed penalty for an offence of driving without due care and attention under s 65(1)(a) punishable under s 65(4)(a) of the RTA is fine not exceeding $2,500 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months or to both.

18     The prescribed penalty for an offence of driving without due care and attention under s 65(1)(a) punishable under s 65(5)(a) of the RTA is fine not exceeding $1,500 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months or to both.

19     In addition, s 42(1) of the RTA provides that a court before which a person convicted of an offence in connection with the driving of a motor vehicle may, where so required by this Act, order him to be disqualified from holding or obtaining a driving licence for life or for such period as the court may think fit.

Antecedents

20     On 1 April 2017, the accused paid composition of $150 for an offence of speeding under s 63(4) of the RTA. On 11 October 2019, the accused paid composition of $400 for an offence of failing to conform to a traffic red light signal under s 120(4) of the RTA.

Prosecution’s Submissions on Sentence

21     In respect of the first charge, the Prosecution sought a fine $2,500 and disqualification for a period of 15 to 18 months. For the second charge, the Prosecution sought a fine of $1500 and a similar disqualification period.

22     It was highlighted that the accused faced two charges and for both charges, it was the Prosecution’s position that his culpability was minimally at the higher end of low. He was driving at a fast speed and did not slow down when vehicles in front of him had slowed down. For the harm caused, it was submitted that there were three victims involved. The total cost of repairs to the vehicles was high and one of the victims was injured. It was further submitted that the harm in this case would be moderate after considering the costs of repairs and the injuries suffered.

Mitigation

23     The accused is self-employed and operates a logistics company that serves ‘Lazada’ customers. He is often personally involved in the collection and delivery of goods and greatly reliant on his driver’s licence. When his company is assigned to deliver high value items, the accused is expected to handle these deliveries personally and is unable to delegate such tasks to other drivers. It was submitted that a lengthy disqualification period would have a crushing effect on the accused’s livelihood.

24     It was further submitted that by pleading guilty, the accused had saved precious time and resources. He deeply regretted that his carelessness had caused both injury and financial loss to other road users. After the accident, he had remained at the scene to assist the victim and investigating authorities.

25     Accordingly, the Defence sought fines of $2,000 and 11 months’ disqualification for the first charge and $1,000 and 3 months’ disqualification for the second charge.

26     In support of the sentencing position for the first charge, the Defence had relied on five unreported cases in the table of sentencing precedents involving offences under s 65(4)(a) of the RTA. It was submitted that the present case is not more egregious as compared to the highlighted precedents.

27     In respect of the second charge, it was further submitted that a search on the Sentencing Information and Research Repository (“SIRS”) showed that the mean sentence among 58 cases prosecuted under s 65(5)(a) of the RTA is a fine of $1,000. It was highlighted in Public Prosecutor v Rizuwan Bin Rohmat [2021] SGDC 219 (“Rizuwan”), in respect of a charge punishable under s 65(5)(a) of the RTA, the offender was sentenced to a $1,000 fine and 3 months’ disqualification. The offender had failed to keep a proper lookout when forming up at a red traffic light junction and collided his motor van into a stationary vehicle. It was the Defence’s case that the second charge in the present case is not more egregious. Accordingly, a $1,000-fine and 3 months’ disqualification was appropriate.

Prosecution’s Reply

28     In response to the plea in mitigation, the Prosecution highlighted that the cases cited by the Defence were unreported. In addition, there was no mention of cost of damage to the vehicles. It was submitted that in the present case, one of the significant costs is the high cost of damage.

29     In addition, as the accused was driving a motor van, the culpability would be higher as compared to if he had driven a motor car. It is still a larger vehicle than a saloon car.

Decision on disqualification order

30     As the accused had been convicted of two charges in connection with the driving of a motor vehicle, pursuant to s 42(1) of the RTA, I had to consider whether an order disqualification should be imposed. S 42(1) provides that that a court before which a person convicted of an offence in connection with the driving of a motor vehicle may, where so required by this Act, order him to be disqualified from holding or obtaining a driving licence for life or for such period as the court may think fit. If the disqualification period imposed is one year or longer, an additional prescribed test of competence to drive needs to be taken by the offender under s 43(1)(b) of the RTA before the offender is allowed to drive again.

31     In the present case, the accused while driving his motor van, a vehicle larger than a normal motorcar, on an expressway with moderate traffic flow, was involved in a head to rear collision with three vehicles. Hurt was caused to one of the victims as he was hospitalised for three days and given 26 days of medical leave. In addition, substantial property damage was caused to the said three vehicles. From the footage from an unknown passer-by, the accused’s motor van was seen travelling on lane 1 of 3 lanes and overtaking two slow moving vehicles which were on lane 2. Subsequently, vehicles on lane 1 were seen slowing down. His motor van then rear-ended vehicles FBP4225J, SMX8723P, causing SMX8723P to rear-end SMT667S. The accused’s collision with the third victim’s motorcycle caused the third victim to be flung onto lane 2 of 3 lanes of the expressway. Hence, based on these circumstances, I agreed with the Prosecution that the harm caused should be pegged at moderate.

32     I found that it was clear from the footages that the accident was caused from the accused’s aggressive manner of driving and his failure to keep a safe distance and speed as well as to keep a proper lookout of the vehicles ahead of him in lane 1 as they slowed down. I took into account the fact that at the time of accident, the weather was clear, the road surface was dry and the visibility was clear. There was no evidence before me that his view of the vehicle ahead of him was obstructed as he drove along lane 1 of the expressway.

33     Although I had taken into account the accused’s plea of guilt, I was mindful that the accused did not have a clean driving record as he had compounded offences of speeding in April 2017 and failing to conform to a traffic red light signal in October 2019. The present offence was clearly not a one-off incident. Hence, I agreed with the Prosecution that his culpability was at the higher end of low.

34     Based on the circumstances of the commission of the offence including the accused’s manner of driving, the harm caused to the three victims as well as the potential risk and danger he posed to the other road users, I was of the view that a disqualification order should be imposed in respect of both charges. Although it was submitted by the Defence that a lengthy disqualification period would have a crushing effect on the accused’s livelihood, I placed little weight to this point as I did not find any exceptional circumstances in the present case not to impose a disqualification order.

35     In deciding on the appropriate period of disqualification, I was mindful that the disqualification order and the sentence imposed are not mutually compensatory; an increase in the quantum of the fine imposed should not be taken to mandate the imposition of a reduced period of disqualification than would otherwise has been ordered: Edwin s/o Suse Nathen v Public Prosecutor [2013] 4 SLR 1139 at [13].

36     In determining the appropriate length of the disqualification order, I was guided by High Court’s approach in Kwan Weiguang v Public Prosecutor [2022] SGHC 121 (“Kwan”). Although Aedit Abdullah J dealt with an appeal against a disqualification order of 15 months that was imposed on the appellant for an offence of dangerous driving under s 64(1) and punishable under s 64(2C)(a) of the RTA, the factors considered are relevant to the present case. He held at [59] and [61] that the objective of the disqualification order is as follow:

59    A driving disqualification order combines three sentencing objectives: punishment, protection of the public and deterrence (Edwin s/o Suse Nathen v Public Prosecutor [2013] 4 SLR 1139 (“Edwin Nathen”) at [13]). Often, due to the limited range of fines in the punishment provision, the impact of a disqualification order is likely to be felt much more acutely than any marginal increase in the quantum of the fine (see Edwin Nathen at [13]).

60    Ensuring the safety of others in public is an important consideration. As discussed in Public Prosecutor v Ong Heng Chua and another appeal (“Ong Heng Chua”)[2018] 5 SLR 388 at [61]:

61    the most important sentencing principles engaged in disqualification orders are the protection of society, because the objective of disqualification orders is to prevent future harm that the offender may cause to the public, and deterrence, because such orders deprive the offenders of the freedom to drive. In line with these principles, greater weight should be placed on the culpability of the offender in the commission of the offence as well as his driving record. These reflect how much of a danger he poses to society, and also indicative of the degree of specific deterrence necessary…

Protection is achieved by removing a dangerous driver from the roads to attenuate the risk of harm occasioned by bad or antisocial driving. Further, Parliament has made it clear that the ability to impose disqualification orders under s 42(1) of the RTA is meant to protect innocent road users from the potential danger posed by motorists who demonstrate violent or aggressive behaviour when reacting to situations connected with driving such as: flashing of head lamps, overtaking and disputes over parking spaces, etc (see Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (20 January 1999) vol 69 at col 1932 (Wong Kan Seng, Minister for Home Affairs).

61    A disqualification order also serves to remove the privilege to drive from those who have abused it, and this also reinforces the notion of shared usage of the roads. It must be remembered that driving is an inherently dangerous activity. The licence to drive is a privilege accorded to persons who, through a series of properly administered tests, have demonstrated that they are capable of meeting the standards expected of a reasonably competent driver (Jali bin Mohd Yunos v Public Prosecutor [2014] 4 SLR 1059 at [36]). The former Minister for Home Affairs, Prof S Jayakumar, had expressed the following view which reinforces this notion (see Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (28 March 1990) vol 55 at col 960): “… we must get every driver in Singapore to note that when he is issued a licence, it is in fact a very special privilege and it is granted on condition that he drives in a responsible manner bearing in mind the interest of others.”

37     In Kwan at [65] to [71], the relevant and material factors when determining the period of disqualification are:

(a)     Circumstances of the commission of the offence and

(b)     Continued ability to drive and attitude towards other road users.

38     In the present case, it was undisputed that a disqualification order was warranted in respect of both charges. What was in issue was the appropriate length. The Prosecution had sought a global disqualification period of between 15 to 18 months while the Defence had sought for a global period 11 months, a period which would not require the accused to take the additional prescribed test of competence for him to be able to drive again.

39     In respect of the first charge, after the accused first collided into his motorcycle, the third victim was flung onto lane 2 of 3 lanes of the expressway. He suffered left knee and ankle contusion and was hospitalised for three days with 26 days of medical leave. It was fortunate that he did not suffer more serious injuries or was run over by oncoming vehicles in lane 2 of the busy expressway. It was also fortuitous that the oncoming vehicle in lane 2 was able to stop the vehicle on time to avoid hitting him.

40     Having regard to the aggravating factors namely the accused’s manner of driving and the personal injury suffered by the third victim, I was of the view that an 18 months’ disqualification period was appropriate to achieve the three sentencing objectives of punishment, protection of the public and deterrence.

41     In my view, a period of 11 months’ disqualification, as submitted by the Defence, was inadequate in view of the aggravating factors present in this case. I disagreed with the Defence’s position that the present case is not more egregious as compared to the five unreported precedents. In my view, the unreported precedents were unhelpful as they lacked precedential value and could also be distinguished as they involved only one victim unlike the present case which involved three victims.

42     In respect of the second charge, the accused’s head to rear collision with FBP4225J, SMX8723P and SMT667S had resulted in substantial property damage as set out at [14] to the three vehicles. There was also a fair degree of potential harm to other road users considering the conditions at the material time, including the moderate flow of traffic on the expressway. Having regard to the accused’s manner of driving and the substantial property damage caused to the three vehicles, I was of the view that a period of 15 months’ disqualification period was appropriate to achieve the three sentencing objectives of punishment, protection of the public and deterrence.

43     I did not find the case of Rizuwan helpful as in that case, the offender had collided into the victim’s stationary motorcar which had come to a stop at a traffic red light signal on a normal road. Minor damage was caused to the victim’s motorcar. In the present case, the collision involving multiple vehicles had occurred along an expressway where vehicles travel at a higher speed with moderate flow of traffic. Hence, I disagreed that the second charge is not more egregious than the facts in Rizuwan.

44     All things considered, I was of the view that a period of disqualification of 18 months for all classes of driving licences for the first charge and a period of disqualification for 15 months for all classes of driving licences for the second charge with effect from 15 February 2023 was fair and appropriate based on the facts and circumstances of the case.
