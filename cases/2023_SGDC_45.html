Criminal Law – Possession of drugs – Possession of drug utensils

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in MA 9028/2023/01.]

9 March 2023

District Judge Tan Jen Tse:

Introduction

1       The accused, a 38-year old male Singapore citizen, was convicted after trial on one count of possessing methamphetamine and one count of possessing utensils intended for the consumption of a Class A Controlled Drug.

2       The charges were as follows:-

1st charge (DAC 916037-2021)

that you, on 21 July 2020, at or about 7.10 pm, at 25A Saint George’s Lane, Singapore 321025, did have in your possession a Class A Controlled Drug listed in the First Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed), to wit, one packet containing not less than 1.12 grams of crystalline substance which was analysed and found to contain not less than 0.75 gram of methamphetamine, without any authorisation under the said Act or the Regulations made thereunder and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 8(a) of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Chapter 185, 2008 Rev Ed) and punishable under Section 33(1) of the said Act.

2nd charge (DAC 916038-2021)

are charged that you, on 21 July 2020, at or about 7.10 pm, at 25A Saint George’s Lane, Singapore 321025, did have in your possession utensils intended for the consumption of a Class A Controlled Drug listed in the First Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed), to wit, one improvised bottle with glass bong and straw attached, without authorisation under the said Act or the Regulations made thereunder, and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 9 of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed) and punishable under Section 33(1) of the said Act.

3       The accused was sentenced to three months’ imprisonment for the first charge and to one month’s imprisonment for the second charge, with the sentences to run concurrently. He has filed an appeal against conviction. There has been no appeal against sentence by the accused or the prosecution.

Background facts

4       The essential facts were not in dispute.

5       The accused lived with his mother, Laiu Allen (PW6) (“Laiu”) at her apartment at 25A Saint George’s Lane (“the apartment”).

6       On 21 July 2020 at about 5 a.m., the accused prevented Laiu from going to work. He insisted that she rest at home instead. Laiu felt that the accused was behaving in a paranoid manner and called her ex-husband Steven Tan Chun Yang (“Tan”) for help. Tan told her that he was not able to come to the apartment as it was too early.

7       For unexplained reasons, Tan only called the police several hours later. At about 5.50 p.m., he called the police and reported, “My son is high on drugs now. He has not been eating and he is sleeping. Can you check on him?”[note: 1]

8       Four police officers were despatched to the apartment. They were Sgt Poon Hong Pin James (PW1), Sgt M Sathish (PW2), Sgt Muhammad Yazid (PW3) and Sgt Nur Shafiqah binte Roslan (PW4).

9       Upon their arrival, the police officers were directed to the accused by Laiu. He was sleeping on a sofa in an area of the hall which had been partitioned for his sole use. The police found some drug paraphernalia on the table next to him. This included Exhibit P6, which was an “improvised bottle with glass bong and straw attached”. Exhibit P6 was the subject matter of the second charge. It was not in dispute that it was an improvised utensil used for smoking methamphetamine.

10     The officers tried to wake the accused and lifted him to a standing position. They found a blue pouch on the sofa, which had been underneath him. In it, they found a sachet of white crystallised substance and a blue straw (Exhibit P8) together with other drug taking paraphernalia. The accused was then placed under arrest.

11     Exhibit P8 was sent to the Health Sciences Authority (“HSA”) for analysis. It was found to contain not less than 1.12 grams of crystalline substance which contained not less than 0.75 gram of methamphetamine.[note: 2] This was the subject matter of the first charge.

12     It was not in dispute that Exhibits P6 and P8 were in the accused’s possession. The accused claimed that an acquaintance had left these items at his residence many years ago after he co-hosted a barbeque.[note: 3] The prosecution also conceded that Exhibits P6 and P8 had been in the accused’s possession for years prior to 21 July 2020.[note: 4]

13     The accused had a history of mental illness. This was documented in two medical reports dated 20 October 2020 (Exhibit P9) and 22 March 2021 (Exhibit P10) which were put up by Dr Jonathan Lee Jie, Psychiatrist, Associate Consultant, Institute of Mental Health (“Dr Lee”) (PW7).

14     In Exhibit P10, Dr Lee opined that the accused was suffering from schizoaffective disorder and that he had a history of substance abuse. Dr Lee stated that there was no significant contributory link between his mental illness and the alleged offences. Dr Lee certified that the accused was not of unsound mind at the time of the alleged offences and that he was fit to plead in a Court of Law.

The issue

15     The main issue to be determined was whether the accused knew at the time of his arrest on 21 July 2020, that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine and that Exhibit P6 was a utensil intended for the consumption of drugs.

16     The prosecution submitted that the accused had actual knowledge of these matters. Alternatively, for the drug possession charge, it submitted that the presumption of knowledge under s 18(2) of the Misuse of Drugs Act (“MDA”) applied, but that the accused failed to rebut it.

17     On the other hand, the accused denied having any knowledge that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine or knowing the purpose of Exhibit P6.

Evaluation of evidence and findings

The accused knew that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine

18     To show that the accused knew that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine, and that Exhibit P6 was a drug utensil used to consume it, the prosecution relied on the medical reports from the Institute of Mental Health (“IMH”), Exhibits P9 and P10.

19     Exhibit P9 documented the accused’s admission to IMH from 19 August 2020 to 5 September 2020 for a mild relapse of his schizoaffective disorder. Dr Lee saw the accused once during this admission.

20     Exhibit P9 stated at [3], “He [the accused] informed us the police had found drugs at his house and that it was his old stock.” This indicated that the accused knew that what the police found (Exhibit P8) contained drugs. It should be noted that HSA’s drug certificate (Exhibit P7) (which found Exhibit P8 to contain methamphetamine) was dated 19 August 2020 i.e. the same date as the accused’s admission to IMH. It was most unlikely that the accused would have been informed or known of the contents of Exhibit P7 at the time of his admission. Notwithstanding this, he was able to definitively state that the police found drugs at his house. He even tried to minimize his culpability and claimed that the drugs were his “old stock”.

21     Dr Lee saw the accused on 7 January 2021 and 5 March 2021 for the purposes of preparing a forensic report. In Exhibit P10, Dr Lee stated at [11] that the accused’s account of the alleged offences was as follows:-

11    The subject stated that he was not aware of the charges he is facing. The charges were read to him. He was inconsistent in his accounts of the alleged offences. He stated that he came into possession of the drug and the “bong” through a friend who passed it to him during a barbeque “a long time ago”. In an earlier account, he stated that he was unaware of the content of the substance. In a later account, he said that he was aware it was “ICE” because the people who gave it to him told him so…

22     While the accused may have given Dr Lee inconsistent accounts, he eventually admitted that he was aware that the drugs were “ice” (the street name for methamphetamine). He thus voluntarily provided inculpatory information on matters which were adverse to him, and which were within his own knowledge. There was no reason to doubt the truth of his admission. He even provided a cogent reason for his belief that the substance was “ice” i.e. the people who gave it to him told him so. He would thus have known that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine. He also referred to Exhibit P6 as a “bong”, which is a utensil used to smoke drugs.

23     There was no reason to doubt the reliability of the IMH records. Dr Lee testified that he kept contemporaneous records of what the accused said. While speaking to the accused, he would type his discussion notes with the accused directly into his computer.[note: 5] The accused did not challenge the evidence of Dr Lee or the adverse parts of the IMH reports, even when these were pointed out to him.[note: 6] Instead, he stated:-[note: 7]

I would just like to inform Your Honour that the drugs were not my old stock, but rather it was from the previous batch of stock.

24     Exhibit P10 documented at [9], the accused’s eight admissions to IMH between 2014 and 2020. He refused to provide his urine for drug screening, except during an admission in 2017. On this occasion, his urine tested positive for methamphetamine. The accused did not challenge this evidence, which indicated that he had previously consumed methamphetamine. It was thus probable that he was familiar with methamphetamine and the utensils used for consuming it. This would go towards negativing his claim that he did not know that the crystalline substance (Exhibit P8) contained methamphetamine and that the “bong” (Exhibit P6) was used to consume drugs.

25     Further, the accused conceded in cross-examination that at the time of his arrest, he knew that Exhibit P8 contained “ice”:-[note: 8]

Q    So, at the time that P8 was seized from you on 21st July 2020, you knew that it contained Ice. Yes or no?

A    Yes.

Q    And Ice is another name for methamphetamine. Yes or no?

A    I believe so.

26     On the totality of the evidence, I found that the accused knew that Exhibit P8, which was in his possession, contained methamphetamine.

The accused failed to rebut the presumption under s 18(2) of the MDA

27     In the alternative, the prosecution relied on the presumption of knowledge under s 18(2) of the MDA, which provides:-

(2)    Any person who is proved or presumed to have had a controlled drug in his or her possession is presumed, until the contrary is proved, to have known the nature of that drug.

28     I was satisfied and found that based on the uncontroverted evidence of the police officers who went to the apartment and the accused’s own admission, that the accused was in possession of Exhibit P8, which contained a controlled drug. I agreed with the prosecution that the presumption under s 18(2) of the MDA was triggered, and that the accused would be presumed, until the contrary was proved, to have known the nature of that drug. The accused was thus presumed to know that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine.

29     To rebut the presumption, the accused had to prove on the balance of probabilities, that he did not have knowledge of the nature of the drug. In Obeng Comfort v Public Prosecutor [2017] 1 SLR 33 (“Obeng”), the Court of Appeal stated at [36]:-

… To rebut the presumption in s 18(2), the accused must prove, on a balance of probabilities, that he did not have knowledge of the nature of the controlled drug (in effect, that he did not have the mens rea of the offence). In Dinesh Pillai a/l K Raja Retnam v PP [2012] 2 SLR 903 (“Dinesh Pillai”), this court observed (at [18]) that the accused can do so by showing that “he did not know or could not reasonably be expected to have known the nature of the controlled drug”.

30     The court further stated at [39]:-

… The presumption under s 18(2) operates to vest the accused with knowledge of the nature of the drug which he is in possession of, and to rebut this, he must give an account of what he thought it was.

31     Further, in Gobi a/l Avedian v Public Prosecutor [2021] 1 SLR 180 (“Gobi”), the Court of Appeal stated at [58]:-

58    It is clear that the common thread underlying the past cases is that where the accused person seeks to prove that he lacked the actual knowledge presumed under s 18(2), it is incumbent on him to adduce sufficient evidence disclosing the basis upon which he claims to have arrived at that subjective state of mind.

32     The accused merely asserted that he did not know that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine. I agreed with the prosecution that this bare denial was insufficient to rebut the presumption under s 18(2) of the MDA. As the Court of Appeal stated in Gobi (at [57(a)]:-

(a)    As a matter of common sense and practical application, an accused person who seeks to rebut the s 18(2) presumption should be able to say what he thought or believed he was carrying, and a claim that he simply did not know what he was carrying would not usually suffice: see Zainal bin Hamad v Public Prosecutor and another appeal [2018] 2 SLR 1119 (“Zainal”) at [23(b)]; Obeng at [39].

33     The accused failed to give any account of what he thought or believed Exhibit P8 contained, or the reasons for his subjective belief that it did not contain methamphetamine. His denial of knowledge was also contradicted by the objective evidence contained in his medical reports from IMH (Exhibits P9 and P10) which indicated that he knew that Exhibit P8 contained methamphetamine. In particular, he admitted that those who gave him the substance told him that it was “ice”.

34     I therefore found that the accused had not rebutted the presumption under s 18(2) of the MDA.

The circumstances showed that the accused knew that Exhibit P6 was a utensil intended for the consumption of Exhibit P8

35     Station Inspector Muhammad Khair Bin Ali (PW9) (“SI Muhammad”), a senior investigation officer attached to the Central Narcotics Bureau (“CNB”), testified as to the use of Exhibit P6. He obtained his knowledge by conducting investigations and interviewing drug users over his 15 years’ service at CNB.

36     He stated that Exhibit P6 (also shown in photograph Exhibit P2.1) was an improvised smoking devise which had unique components. There were two attachments to the main body of the device. There was a glass bulb which is known as a “pop-eye”. The second attachment was a straw. There was a small opening on top of the glass bulb through which “ice” would be inserted. To consume “ice”, the user would use a small flame to heat the glass bulb from below. The user would then inhale the fumes which were released by using the straw. Water could also be poured into the bottle for filtration. From his experience, this type of devise was only used for “ice”. He had never come across cases where this kind of utensil was used for anything other than methamphetamine.[note: 9]

37     Exhibits P6 and P8 had been left together with the accused. It was clear that they complemented each other and were meant to be used together. Since the accused knew that Exhibit P8 was “ice”, he would also have known that Exhibit P6, which he referred to as a “bong”,[note: 10] would be used to consume “ice”. As SI Muhammad testified, Exhibit P6 could not be used for anything else. There was thus no other conceivable use of Exhibit P6, other than for the consumption of methamphetamine.

38     When asked about Exhibit P6 in cross-examination, the accused did not specifically deny knowing that it was to be used for the consumption of a controlled drug:-[note: 11]

Q    I put it to you that P6---now, exhibit P6, the bottle with a glass bong and straw attached to it, was in your possession on the 21st of July 2020. Agree or disagree?

A    Agree.

Q    And I put it to you that you knew that it was an improvised bottle with a glass bong and straw attached that was intended for the consumption of a controlled drug. Agree or disagree?

A    It doesn’t matter to me.

39     All considered, I found that the accused knew that Exhibit P6, which was in his possession, was a utensil intended for the consumption of methamphetamine, a Class A controlled drug under the MDA.

Conclusion

40     On the totality of the evidence, I was satisfied and found that the prosecution had discharged its burden of proving both charges against the accused beyond a reasonable doubt. He was therefore found guilty and was convicted as charged.
