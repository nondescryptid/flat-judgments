Gabriel Prieztiano Loke Jian Xun v Yong Kin Sen (administrator of the estate of Loke Siew Kee, deceased)
[2023] SGDC 80

Case Number:District Court Suit No 2990 of 2019, District Court Appeal No 9 of 2023
Decision Date:08 May 2023
Tribunal/Court:District Court
Coram: Lim Wee Ming
Counsel Name(s): Ms Foo Soon Yien, Ms Eileen Yeap (BR Law Corporation) for the plaintiff; Mr Amos Cai, Mr Yip Jian Yang (Yuen Law LLC) for the defendant
Parties: Gabriel Prieztiano Loke Jian Xun — Yong Kin Sen (administrator of the estate of Loke Siew Kee, deceased)

Contract – Illegality and public policy

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in HC/DCA 9/2023.]

8 May 2023

District Judge Lim Wee Ming:

Introduction

1       The plaintiff claims the sum of $209,357.61, being the value of part of the shares bequeathed to the plaintiff under the will of his father, Loke Soon Han (“the Deceased”).

2       The defendant disputes the claim on the basis that Loke Soon Seet (“LSS”), the brother of the Deceased, had transferred his shares to the Deceased, so that in the event of LSS’s bankruptcy, LSS would be able to retain his interest in the shares.

3       I allowed the plaintiff’s claim on the basis that the defendant cannot rely on a defence founded on a purported collateral agreement that would have allowed LSS to benefit from his fraud on his creditors. The defendant has appealed against my decision.

Facts

4       The Deceased, LSS and Loh Soon Yam (“LSY”) were brothers and held shares in Kian Hoe Loke Kee Pte Ltd (“the Company”). The other five shareholders in the Company were also brothers of the Deceased.[note: 1]

Share transfer agreements

5       The plaintiff pleaded that:

(a)     In or around 2002, LSS transferred his 59,523 shares in the Company to the Deceased.[note: 2]

(b)     Subsequently, LSY transferred 16,087 of his shares in the Company to the Deceased.[note: 3]

6       The defendant pleaded that:

(a)     There was a share transfer agreement (“the LSS Share Transfer Agreement”) for the transfer of 59,523 shares from LSS to the Deceased which was prepared and signed in or around 2002. However, the share transfer was not effected at the material time.[note: 4]

(b)     There was a share transfer agreement (“the LSY Share Transfer Agreement”) for the transfer of LSY’s shares to the other shareholders of the Company that was prepared and signed on or after 2002, but the share transfer was not effected at the material time.[note: 5]

7       In the defendant’s closing submissions, the defendant submitted that the letter of transfer for LSS’s shares was prepared “sometime in 1990-1995”,[note: 6] and the letter of transfer for LSY’s shares “was probably done in 1995”.[note: 7] However, the defendant did not plead this position. The defendant cannot change position and rely on a time frame that is contrary to the defendant’s pleading.

8       In the defendant’s closing submissions, the defendant questioned LSS’s signature on the letter of transfer.[note: 8] However, this is contrary to LSS’s evidence in his affidavit of evidence-in-chief that he signed the letter of transfer for his shares.[note: 9]

9       The notice of transfer of shares to the Deceased was dated 18 May 2011,[note: 10] well after the share transfer agreements were signed. The date of the notice was after the date the Deceased passed away.

Will of the Deceased

10     The Deceased passed away on 21 November 2010.[note: 11]

11     Under a will dated 16 November 2010 (“the Will”), the Deceased bequeathed all his shares in the Company to his son, the plaintiff.[note: 12]

12     Loke Siew Kee, the sister of the Deceased, was the sole executrix of the Estate of the Deceased.[note: 13] The shares in the Company were sold. As executrix of the Will, Loke Siew Kee received the proceeds of sale of the shares in the Company that were held by the Deceased, amounting to $398,715.22.

13     Loke Siew Kee paid the sum of $189,357.61 to the plaintiff, but retained the balance sum of $209,357.61.[note: 14] This balance sum of $209,357.61, are the proceeds of sale of the shares held by the Deceased, from the LSS and LSY Share Transfer Agreements.

14     The plaintiff claims the sum of $209,357.61, being the balance sum due to the plaintiff under the Will.[note: 15] The plaintiff’s claim was commenced against Loke Siew Kee.

15     Loke Siew Kee passed away after the commencement of the trial but before its completion. Yong Kin Sen, the administrator of the estate of Loke Siew Kee, was appointed to represent Loke Siew Kee.[note: 16] Yong Kin Sen was Loke Siew Kee’s husband.[note: 17]

Parties’ positions

16     The defendant disputes the plaintiff’s claim and has pleaded:

(a)     In relation to the LSS Share Transfer Agreement:

(i)       LSS signed the LSS Share Transfer Agreement as “the intention of the LSS Share Transfer Agreement was for the shares of the Company to be kept within the family. As LSS trusted the Deceased, and most of the brothers agreed that the Deceased would account properly to the family for LSS’s shares, the LSS Share Transfer Agreement was duly prepared in case LSS’s pending bankruptcy would cause LSS’s shares to be sold and the family deprived of the benefit of LSS’s shares.”[note: 18]

(ii)       In the defendant’s further and better particulars, the defendant pleaded that “The Deceased was to account properly by ensuring that the benefit for LSS’s shares would be given to LSS, if possible, or otherwise, to all brothers equally (including or not including LSS).”[note: 19]

(b)     LSY signed the LSY Share Transfer Agreement as “LSY intended for his shares to be given equally to his brothers. … because of LSS’s bankruptcy and for the sake of keeping the shares within the family, most of the brothers, including LSY and the Deceased, agreed that the Deceased would hold LSS’s shares on behalf of LSS. Hence, LSY transferred the 8,044 shares that was intended for LSS to the Deceased.”[note: 20]

17     LSS alleged in his affidavit of evidence-in-chief that:

(a)     “I thought that by showing the letter of transfer of my shares to my creditors, it would show that I was no longer a shareholder of the Company and my creditors would not look to the Company for payments.”[note: 21]

(b)     The board resolution that was prepared for the transfer, was for the same purpose.[note: 22]

18     The evidence from LSY[note: 23] and the other brothers were in line with LSS’s position.

19     The defendant alleges that the notice of transfer of the shares was lodged with the Company’s auditor in 2011, as Rina Loke (“Rina”), the family member who was helping to manage the Company at that time, wanted to extend its lease with the Jurong Town Corporation but was worried that LSS’s bankruptcy would hamper the transaction if he was a registered shareholder of the Company.[note: 24]

20     In essence, the defendant’s pleaded case is that notwithstanding the share transfer agreements, LSS retained the beneficial ownership of the shares. Effectively, the defendant’s position is that one should look beyond the face of the share transfer agreements, as there was a collateral oral agreement that LSS retained the beneficial ownership in the shares.

21     In the plaintiff’s reply, he has denied that there was such an agreement that LSS retained ownership in the shares transferred to the Deceased.[note: 25] The plaintiff further pleads that:

(a)     LSS was adjudged bankrupt by way of a bankruptcy order dated 21 March 2003 and was discharged on 20 March 2019.

(b)     The disposal of an asset to defraud creditors during the five-year period prior to the date of bankruptcy contravenes section 138(1) of the Bankruptcy Act (Cap 20, 2009 Revised Edition) (“the Bankruptcy Act”) and constitutes an offence under section 146 of the Bankruptcy Act.

(c)     If LSS remained the owner of the shares, LSS would further contravene sections 134, 135 and 137 of the Bankruptcy Act for non-disclosure, concealment and making false statements as to his assets and affairs.[note: 26]

Issue

22     The key issue is whether the defendant may rely on his pleaded defence that LSS retained a beneficial interest in the shares of the Company, that would allow him to benefit from his fraud on his creditors.

23     There were also arguments raised by the defendant in relation to the price of the shares, payment for the shares and the transfer of the shares, which will be addressed.

Defendant’s defence is founded on an illegality

24     I decided that the defendant’s pleaded defence cannot stand as it would allow a bankrupt to benefit from his fraud on his creditors.

25     In Ting Siew May v Boon Lay Choo (“Ting Siew May”) [2014] 3 SLR 609, the Court of Appeal held (at [46]):

On the general level of principle, the court will not permit the “guilty party” to benefit from his own … wrong as this would be an affront to public policy. As a matter of public interest, the court should not appear to reward or condone a breach of the law. …

26     The Court of Appeal further held (at [66]):

… where a contract is entered into with the object of committing an illegal act, the general approach that the courts should undertake is to examine the relevant policy considerations underlying the illegality principle so as to produce a proportionate response to the illegality in each case.

[emphasis in original]

27     The Court of Appeal summarised (at [70]), the factors to be considered by the courts in assessing proportionality, as including the following:

(a)     whether allowing the claim would undermine the purpose of the prohibiting rule,

(b)     the nature and gravity of the illegality,

(c)     the remoteness or centrality of the illegality to the contract,

(d)     the object, intent and conduct of the parties, and

(e)     the consequences of denying the claim.

28     A key consideration here is that LSS has been discharged from bankruptcy. This is not a case, where the claim on the shares is being pursued by the Official Assignee for the benefit of the creditors of LSS. LSS has been discharged from his bankruptcy and the defendant’s withholding of the proceeds of sale of the shares from the plaintiff is intended to benefit LSS, rather than the creditors of LSS.

Defendant’s pleaded defence is central to the illegality

29     What is central to the illegality in this case can be seen from the defendant’s pleading that:

(a)     “the LSS Share Transfer Agreement was duly prepared in case LSS’s pending bankruptcy would cause LSS’s shares to be sold”, [note: 27] and

(b)     “because of LSS’s bankruptcy … [it was] … agreed that the Deceased would hold LSS’s shares on behalf of LSS.” [note: 28]

30     The defendant’s effective position central to the illegality, is that beyond the face of the share transfer agreements, there was a collateral oral agreement that LSS retained the beneficial interest in the shares that would enable LSS to retain the beneficial ownership of the shares without declaring it to the Official Assignee.

31     In Ting Siew May, the Court of Appeal considered the centrality of the illegality to the contract, the most important factor to be considered in the court’s response to the illegality (at [85]). Although the weight of each of the five factors to be considered by the court, would depend on the facts of each case, I am of the view that the purported collateral oral agreement that LSS retained a beneficial interest in the shares being central to the illegality, is similarly the most important factor in this case, as the very purpose of this purported oral agreement was to defraud the creditors of LSS for the benefit of LSS.

32     The defendant has submitted that the burden is on the plaintiff to prove that the letter of transfer of LSS’s shares is legal.[note: 29] This argument is misconceived. There is nothing on the face of the share transfer agreements to suggest that the transfers are illegal. The share transfer agreements and LSS’s failure to disclose his alleged ownership of the shares to the Official Assignee, are prima facie evidence that the shares had been transferred.

33     It is for LSS to rely on the purported collateral oral agreement that LSS retained a beneficial interest in the shares, notwithstanding the transfer. As this purported collateral oral agreement is central to the illegality to defraud the creditors of LSS for his benefit, it cannot be relied upon by the defendant as a defence to the plaintiff’s claim.

LSS should not benefit through defrauding his creditors

34     As for the other factors to be considered in Ting Siew May, I am of the view that in the present case:

(a)     The purported collateral oral agreement that LSS retained a beneficial interest in the shares undermined the purpose of the prohibiting rule, as the very purpose of the purported collateral oral agreement was for LSS to defraud his creditors and benefit from the shares after he was discharged from bankruptcy.

(b)     The nature and gravity of the illegality went directly against the prohibiting rule. The gravity of the illegality was aggravated by the conduct of LSS in failing to disclose his purported beneficial interest in the shares to the Official Assignee.[note: 30]

(c)     Under the purported collateral oral agreement that LSS retained a beneficial interest in the shares, the object, intent and conduct of LSS would be to defraud the creditors of LSS and claim his interest in the shares after he was discharged from bankruptcy.

(d)     The consequences of denying the claim that there was a purported collateral oral agreement that LSS retained a beneficial interest in the shares, is that the plaintiff as the beneficiary under the Will of the Deceased, would benefit from the shares, rather than LSS. There is no justification for LSS to benefit from the purported collateral oral agreement that would allow LSS to benefit from hiding the shares from his creditors.

35     In the premises, I am of the view that upon considering the factors set out in Ting Siew May, a proportionate response in this case would be to disallow the defendant from relying on the purported collateral oral agreement that LSS retained a beneficial interest in the shares, as the very purpose of this purported agreement, was for LSS to defraud his creditors and benefit from the shares after he was discharged from bankruptcy.

36     The defendant sought to rely on interviews conducted by Yong Sio Geok, the daughter of Loke Siew Kee.[note: 31] These interviews were conducted with family members to support the defendant’s position that LSS was to have a beneficial interest in the shares, notwithstanding the share transfer agreements.[note: 32] Ultimately the interviews cannot be relied upon to advance the defendant’s case, as the defendant’s position seeks to benefit LSS through a fraud on the creditors of LSS.

Defendant’s arguments benefit LSS, not his creditors

37     The defendant relied on the following arguments:

(a)     The sale of the shares at 10 cents per share, was at an undervalue.

(b)     Payment was not made for the shares.

(c)     The shares were not intended to be transferred to the Deceased.

38     There are factual difficulties with each of these arguments that make them unsustainable. Furthermore, the defendant cannot rely on these arguments that are intended to benefit LSS, rather than his creditors.

Argument that shares sold at an undervalue

39     The defendant has argued that the shares were sold at an undervalue.[note: 33] I am unable to agree with this, in view of the Statement of Changes in Equity for the year ended 31 December 2002, supporting the plaintiff’s position that the average price of the shares was estimated to be $0.093064.[note: 34] There is no evidence from the defendant to show that the shares were worth more than that at the time of the sale. As the shares were sold at $0.10 per share, this would be higher than the aforesaid estimated price.

40     The defendant has raised the argument that the shares should be worth much more based on the Price-Earnings ratio of the Singapore Straits Times Index.[note: 35] This is unrealistic, given that the Straits Times Index relates to publicly listed companies which are traded on the stock exchange, whereas the company in this case is a private limited company. Moreover, there was no expert evidence adduced by the defendant to support this argument. The defendant did not adduce any evidence to show that the shares were sold at an undervalue at the time of the sale.

41     Furthermore, the defendant has not pleaded that the shares were sold at an undervalue.

42     In any event, under section 98 of the Bankruptcy Act, “where an individual is adjudged bankrupt and he has at the relevant time … entered into a transaction with any person at an undervalue, the Official Assignee may apply to the court for an order … restoring the position to what it would have been if that individual had not entered into that transaction.” It is clear from this provision that even if the transaction was at an undervalue, it is for the Official Assignee to make the application for the benefit of the creditors. In contrast, in the present case, the defendant is raising this allegation to benefit LSS, rather than the creditors of LSS.

43     Both parties relied on the case of Awyong Shi Peng v Lim Siu Lay [2008] SGDC 280 (“Awyong Shi Peng”) in their submissions. This was a case involving a gift made by a bankrupt prior to his bankruptcy, which I had decided in 2008. I had referred parties to that case at the start of the trial.

44     In Awyong Shi Peng, the plaintiffs’ father, Awyong Kah Lock (“AYKL”) was made a bankrupt. Before his bankruptcy, AYKL gave the sum of $200,000 to the defendant, Lim Siu Lay, with instructions that Lim Siu Lay pay $100,000 to each of his sons, Awyong Shi Peng and Awyong Shi Hong (“the Awyong brothers”), when they reached the age of 21 years. The Awyong brothers sued Lim Siu Lay, after he failed to pay them the aforesaid sum.

45     Lim Siu Lay denied receiving the aforesaid sum. He further raised the defence of illegality, that even if he did receive the sum of $200,000, the transaction was a scheme devised by AYKL to defeat his creditors and the Court should not assist AYKL to complete the transaction.

46     I decided the issue of illegality against the Awyong brothers, on the basis that “to allow the transaction to be completed by giving judgment in favour of [the Awyong brothers], would only encourage other potential bankrupts to devise such schemes that would wrongfully bestow a gift to the benefit of their relatives at the expense of their creditors. On policy grounds, there is no reason for the Courts to assist a bankrupt in completing a transaction designed to defraud his creditors” (at [27]).

47     The plaintiff submitted that this case supported their argument that the Court cannot allow the defendant’s defence to succeed as it would incentivise bankrupts to abuse the court processes in concealing their assets.[note: 36] The defendant relied on this case in support of their argument that the share transfer should not stand as it was at an undervalue.[note: 37]

48     I am unable to agree with the defendant’s submission, in view of my finding that there is no evidence that the sale of the shares was at an undervalue. In any event, as I have pointed out earlier, the argument that the transaction was at an undervalue may only be raised by the Official Assignee for the benefit of the creditors, rather than by the defendant for the benefit of LSS. I agree with the plaintiff that the defendant’s defence cannot stand as it would allow a bankrupt to benefit from his own fraudulent conduct.

Argument that payment not received from the Deceased

49     The defendant sought to rely on the following allegations from LSS and LSY that they did not receive any payment from the Deceased for the shares:

(a)     LSS alleged in his affidavit of evidence-in-chief that he did not receive payment for the shares, even though he signed the acknowledgment portion of the letter of transfer of his shares, that he had received payment. [note: 38]

(b)     LSY alleged in his affidavit of evidence-in-chief that the “Deceased passed me a cheque for the sum of $5,952.30 and told me to return the $5,952.30 to him in cash. I trusted the Deceased and therefore I did as he told me to do and did not question him on this.”[note: 39]

50     LSS’s allegation that he did not receive payment is contrary to the receipt that he signed, acknowledging that he received $5,952.30 from the Deceased in consideration for the transfer of his shares to the Deceased.[note: 40] LSS further signed a confirmation that he had received the aforesaid sum in cash from the Deceased.[note: 41]

51     LSY’s allegation that he did not receive payment for the shares is inconsistent with his acknowledgment that he had received payment.[note: 42] Furthermore, his allegation in his affidavit of evidence-in-chief that he followed the instructions of the Deceased to return in cash, the $5,952.30 which the Deceased had paid him by cheque,[note: 43] is inconsistent with his evidence in cross-examination that he did not take the cheque and that he “just returned [the Deceased] the cheque.”[note: 44]

52     LSY alleged in his affidavit of evidence-in-chief that he signed the letter of transfer of his shares on the understanding that “No money/consideration was to be given to me in relation to the transfer of my shares as it was a gift from me.”[note: 45] If this allegation were true, it begs the question why the Deceased would even take the trouble to give LSY a cheque, irrespective of whether the cheque or cash was returned.

53     Moreover, LSY’s allegations in relation to the payment make no sense in view of:

(a)     The aforesaid letter of transfer states that the sum of $5,952.30 was the consideration paid by all the transferees, not just the Deceased.[note: 46]

(b)     The sum payable by the Deceased would be less than $5,952.30, as only 16,087 out of 59,523 shares were transferred by LSY to the Deceased under the letter of transfer, with the balance transferred to the other five brothers.

54     Taking the aforesaid inconsistencies in the evidence of LSS and LSY into consideration, I am unable to accept their allegation that they did not receive payment for the shares.

55     Furthermore, as explained earlier, under section 98 of the Bankruptcy Act, an application to restore the position to that prior to the transaction that is purported to be at an undervalue, is to be made by the Official Assignee. As the defendant’s allegation that the payment for the shares was not made is intended to benefit LSS, rather than the creditors of LSS, this allegation cannot be relied upon by the defendant.

Argument that shares never intended to be transferred

56     The defendant further submitted that LSS never gifted or sold his shares and that his shares were never meant to be transferred to the Deceased.[note: 47] This submission by the defendant is inconsistent with:

(a)     The Schedule of Assets of the Deceased filed in Court when probate was granted to Loke Siew Kee,[note: 48] listing the shares of the Deceased in the Company, which included the shares which are the subject of the plaintiff’s claim. Loke Siew Kee had affirmed an affidavit in her application for probate, stating that “the contents of the Schedule of Assets … are true and accurate” and that she “does not know or have any reason to believe that any of the contents of the Schedule of Assets is false.”[note: 49]

(b)     The shares having been sold and the proceeds of sale having been received by Loke Siew Kee as the executrix of the Estate of the Deceased.[note: 50]

(c)     The cheque dated 15 November 2017 for the sum of $398,715.22, being the proceeds of sale of the shares in the name of the Deceased, was also to the Estate of the Deceased.[note: 51]

57     In the premises, this submission by the defendant cannot stand as it is inconsistent with the position taken by Loke Siew Kee who could not have sold the shares and received the proceeds of sale as the executrix of the Estate of the Deceased, if the shares were not owned by the Deceased. If LSS wished to make a claim for the shares, he should have done so before the shares were sold, but clearly he failed to do so because the Official Assignee would then have to be involved in the sale and LSS’s purported share in the proceeds of sale would have to be paid to the Official Assignee for the benefit of the creditors of LSS.

58     Furthermore, the aforesaid payment made on 15 November 2017 to the Estate of the Deceased, was prior to LSS being discharged from bankruptcy on 20 March 2019. If LSS had claimed the proceeds of sale at that time, the payment would have had to be made to the Official Assignee for the benefit of the creditors of LSS, instead of LSS.

59     LSS claimed that after the Deceased passed away in November 2010, he:

(a)     told Rina to “look for the Letter of Transfer of My Shares … and return to me” as “with the Deceased’s passing, there was no longer a need for it”,[note: 52]

(b)     instructed Rina to “transfer my shares equally to the living shareholders with the intention they safekeep my shares for me”,[note: 53] and

(c)     was shocked when Rina informed him that the shares were transferred to the Deceased instead and he asked for the shares to be transferred back to him.[note: 54]

60     As LSS was still a bankrupt at the time the Deceased passed away, his assertion that the letter of transfer was no longer needed when the Deceased passed away, is not logical. If the shares were still owned by LSS rather than the Deceased at that point in time, in view of LSS’s bankruptcy, LSS could not transfer the shares to the living shareholders.

61     Furthermore, I am of the view that what was crucial from LSS’s evidence of his “intention they safekeep my shares for me”, was the intention of LSS to defraud his creditors. The evidence of LSS in cross-examination was that throughout his bankruptcy, he never disclosed his purported ownership of the shares to the Official Assignee.[note: 55] LSS prevaricated over his failure to do so, claiming that he “got so many problem”[note: 56] and that he “can’t remember because at that time … headache”.[note: 57] LSS would have been aware that if the shares were in his name while he was still a bankrupt at that time, the Official Assignee would intervene in any sale of shares in his name, to be for the benefit of his creditors.

62     In summary, the defendant cannot rely on an argument that the shares were not intended to be transferred to the Deceased, in view of:

(a)     the conduct of LSS in relation to the Official Assignee was that the shares were no longer owned by LSS, and

(b)     the conduct of Loke Siew Kee in relation to the grant of probate and the sale of the shares was that the shares were owned by the Deceased.

Conclusion

63     In the premises, the plaintiff’s claim was allowed. Judgment was entered for the sum of $209,357.61, together with interest at 5.33% per annum from the date of writ to the date of judgment. Costs were awarded in favour of the plaintiff.
