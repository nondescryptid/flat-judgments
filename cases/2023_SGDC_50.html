District Judge Kow Keng Siong:

Introduction

1       This is a road rage case where a driver had used his vehicle as an instrument of intimidation and destruction.

2       The driver in question is Mr Agustinus Hadi (“the Accused”). He had pleaded guilty before me to the following dangerous driving charge under s 64 of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”):

You […] are charged that you, on 14 August 2020, from about 2.15pm to about 2.23pm, in Singapore, did drive a motor car bearing registration number SKC 994K (“your car”) in a manner which was dangerous to the public, to wit, by:

·     accelerating forward while the red ‘Kia Optima 2.0(A)’ car bearing registration number SKU 4399H (“the red car”) was filtering from the left lane to the right lane of Bartley Road East,

·    immediately cutting to the right lane from the left, stopping your car suddenly, reversing your car and ramming the rear of your car into the front left portion of the red car along Bartley Road East,

·     ramming the right side of your car into the left side of the red car along Bartley Road East,

·    accelerating towards the red car and hitting the rear of the car once along Tampines Avenue 1, and

·     ramming the front portion of your car into the rear of the red car thrice at a slip road along Tampines Avenue 1,

having regard to all circumstances of the case, including the nature, condition and use of the roads, and the amount of traffic which is actually at the time on the roads, and you have thereby committed an offence under s 64(1) and punishable under s 64(2C)(a) of the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed).

[emphasis added]

Facts

3       The circumstances of the Accused’s offence are as follows.

(a)     On 14 August 2020 sometime in the afternoon, Yap Soon Leong (“Yap”) and the Accused were driving along Bartley Road East at the right lane (“lane 1”). Yap’s car (a Kia Optima 2.0(A)) was travelling behind the Accused’s car (an Audi A7) at the material time.

(b)     At about 2.15pm, Yap filtered to the left lane (“lane 2”) and accelerated to overtake the Accused’s car. Yap then switched on his right signal, changed from lane 2 to lane 1 abruptly, and encroached into the path of the Accused’s car.

(c)     Instead of letting Yap’s car enter lane 1 safely, the Accused accelerated forward while Yap was changing lane. This resulted in a side swipe between the left portion of the Accused’s car and the right mirror on Yap’s car, thus causing the mirror to be folded inwards. Yap eventually filtered back to lane 1 and travelled behind the Accused’s car.

(d)     At about 2.16pm, Yap sounded his horn twice before filtering to lane 2. He then accelerated to about 75km/h, turned on his right signal, overtook the Accused’s car, and abruptly changed from lane 2 to lane 1. After overtaking the Accused’s car, Yap stepped on his brakes and reduced his speed to about 70km/h. The Accused swerved left to avoid a collision. Yap then immediately reduced his speed further to 56km/h. After this, Yap travelled in front of the Accused’s car on lane 1.

(e)     The Accused later filtered to lane 2, immediately cut to lane 1 without overtaking Yap’s car, and stopped his car suddenly, thereby encroaching into the path of Yap’s car. This caused the rear right side of his car to collide into the left front portion of Yap’s car.

(f)     After the collision, at about 2.17pm, the Accused drove forward for a short distance and stopped again. He then quickly reversed his car and rammed the rear of his car into the front left portion of Yap’s car before accelerating forward into lane 2. Yap waited for the Accused’s car to leave before driving forward.

(g)     While travelling on lane 1, Yap saw the Accused’s car on lane 3.

(h)     At about 2.18pm, the Accused (who was then travelling on lane 2) accelerated to Yap’s car (which was on lane 1) and rammed the right side of his car into the left side of Yap’s car. This caused Yap’s car to veer right and to hit the raised centre divider.

(i)     Thereafter, the Accused cut in front of Yap’s car on lane 1 before returning to lane 2, while Yap continued to drive on lane 1.

(j)     At about 2.19pm, the Accused slowed down along Tampines Avenue 10 and filtered from lane 2 to lane 1 to follow Yap’s car.

(k)     Yap eventually stopped at the T-junction of Tampines Avenue 10 and Tampines Avenue 1. At about 2.20pm, the Accused filtered from lane 2 to lane 1 and stopped next to (but slightly ahead of) Yap’s car. The Accused wound down his window and took a photograph of the front of Yap’s car. When the traffic light turned green, Yap turned right and drove along Tampines Avenue 1. The Accused followed suit.

(l)     At about 2.21pm, while Yap’s car was travelling on lane 2 along Tampines Avenue 1, the Accused filtered right to drive behind Yap’s car. He then accelerated towards Yap’s car and hit its rear once. Thereafter, the Accused continued driving behind Yap’s car.

(m)     At about 2.23pm, Yap filtered to the left lane, entered a slip road that led into Tampines Avenue 5, and stopped his car as there was another vehicle in front. At this juncture, the Accused rammed the front portion of his car into the rear of Yap’s car thrice.

(n)     Yap eventually entered Tampines Avenue 5 and turned into the car park of Blocks 902 to 946 Tampines Avenue 5, with the Accused’s car following closely behind. Yap stopped his car at a shelter, let his daughter alight, and then drove out of the carpark. Yap managed to escape the Accused as the latter’s car was stuck at the car park barrier.

(o)     Yap subsequently lodged a police report regarding the incident. Recordings from his in-car camera which showed the Accused’s dangerous driving were handed over to the Police.

(p)     At all material times, the traffic flow was moderate, the visibility was fair, and the road was wet as there was a heavy drizzle.

4       Miraculously, no one was injured during the incident.

5       The two cars were later found to have sustained the following damage:

(a)      Yap’s car. The front left and rear portions of the car were dented, and the right rear and front right tyre fender were scratched. The cost to repair the damage was S$13,296.88. Yap had recovered the cost from his car insurer.

(b)      Accused’s car. The front, sides and rear right portions of the car were damaged. The cost of repair is unknown. The car was scrapped.

The Prosecution is unable to produce any photo of the damage to the two cars.

6       To complete the narrative, Yap was convicted in 2021 of an offence dangerous driving under s 64(1) and punishable under s 64(2C)(a) of the RTA for his conduct described at [3(b)] and [3(d)] above. He was fined $4,000 and disqualified from holding all classes of driving licences for 20 months.

Sentencing submissions

7       The Prosecution submitted that the Accused ought to be sentenced to 3.5 months’ imprisonment and be disqualified from holding all classes of driving licences for four years.[note: 1] The bases for the Prosecution’s submission were as follows. First, the Accused had displayed a high level of culpability as he (a) had driven in an “aggressive manner” to retaliate against Yap’s irresponsible driving, and (b) had displayed a blatant disregard for the safety of other road users.[note: 2] Second, the Accused’s driving had posed “significant” harm given (a) the circumstances of the road condition at the material time, and (b) that extensive damage was caused to Yap’s and the Accused’s cars.[note: 3] Third, the mitigating effect of the Accused’s guilty plea ought to be balanced against the fact that (a) he had compounded offences for speeding in 2007, 2014 and 2019, and (b) the Prosecution would have no difficulty in proving the charge against him given that there was a recording of his dangerous driving.[note: 4] Finally, the proposed sentence of 3.5 months’ imprisonment was in line with Public Prosecutor v Aw Tai Hock [2017] 5 SLR 1141 (“Aw Tai Hock”), another road rage case, where five months’ imprisonment was imposed. The only material difference between the two cases was that the victim in Aw Tai Hock had suffered musculoskeletal injuries whereas Yap did not suffer any injury.[note: 5]

8       The Defence urged me to consider the following in sentencing. First, the Accused was under “extreme stress” at the time of the offence due to a confluence of financial and business issues brought on by the COVID pandemic.[note: 6] Second, Yap was the “aggressor” on the day in question and had triggered a chain of events which initially caused the Accused to be “shocked and stunned”, and eventually caused him to “[lose] control of his emotions” and to “[succumb] to road rage”. The Defence adduced a psychiatric report which stated that the Accused (a) had been labouring from adjustment disorder (with mixed anxiety and depressed mood) at the material time, (b) had acted out of character during the incident, and (c) had the general capacity for self-restraint. Finally, the Defence urged me to consider the fact that Yap had been sentenced to a fine when calibrating the Accused’s sentence.[note: 7]

My Decision

9       Having considered the matter, I imposed the following sentence:

(a)     Seven months’ imprisonment, and

(b)     Disqualification from holding all classes of driving license for three years.

10     In imposing the sentence, I have taken the following views:

(a)     The seriousness of an offence of dangerous or reckless driving is principally influenced by two factors. First, the level of the offender’s culpability for the offence. Second, the extent of harm caused by the driving.

(b)     With regards to harm, Parliament had provided that different punishment regimes are to apply based on whether the dangerous or reckless driving has caused death, grievous hurt, or hurt (“personal injury”).

(c)     Section 64(2C)(a) – the punishment provision in the present case – applies only where the dangerous or reckless driving does not cause personal injury (“non-personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving”). Parliament had provided that egregious cases of such driving can be punished up to a maximum of 12 months’ imprisonment.

(d)     The Accused had embarked on a prolonged course of dangerous driving to vent his road rage. In the process, he had caused significant property damage and endangered the lives and safety of road users. The Accused’s dangerous driving comes within the more serious range in the spectrum of dangerous driving cases that are punishable under s 64(2C)(a). Accordingly, a substantial term of imprisonment in the higher range of the sentencing spectrum of s 64(2C)(a) is warranted.

(e)     There is no reported decision under s 64(2C)(a) involving road rage driving as egregious as the Accused’s offence. As such, I will have to determine an appropriate sentence in this case without the benefit of similar precedents.

(f)     While there are offender-specific mitigating factors in the Accused’s case, these are counter-balanced by the offender-specific aggravating factors which are also present.

11     I will now explain why I have come to the above views.

Section 64

12     I begin with s 64 of the RTA. This provision stipulates the following:

Reckless or dangerous driving

64.—(1)    If any person drives a motor vehicle on a road recklessly, or at a speed or in a manner which is dangerous to the public, having regard to all the circumstances of the case, including the nature, condition and use of the road, and the amount of traffic which is actually at the time, or which might reasonably be expected to be, on the road, the person (called the offender) shall be guilty of an offence.

(2)     If death is caused to another person by the driving of a motor vehicle by the offender, the offender shall be punished with the following on conviction of an offence under subsection (1): …

(2A)   If grievous hurt is caused to another person by the driving of a motor vehicle by the offender, the offender shall be punished with the following on conviction of an offence under subsection (1): …

(2B)   If hurt is caused to another person by the driving of a motor vehicle by the offender, the offender shall on conviction of an offence under subsection (1) — …

(a)     be liable to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both;

(b)    where the person is a repeat offender, be liable to a fine not exceeding $20,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 4 years or to both;

(c)    where the offender is a serious offender in relation to such driving, be liable to a fine of not less than $2,000 and not more than $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months or to both, in addition to any punishment under paragraph (a) or (b); or

(d)    where the offender is a serious repeat offender in relation to such driving, be punished with a fine of not less than $5,000 and not more than $20,000 and with imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years, in addition to any punishment under paragraph (a) or (b).

(2C)   In any other case involving the driving of a motor vehicle by the offender, the offender shall on conviction of an offence under subsection (1) —

(a)     be liable to a fine not exceeding $5,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months or to both;

(b)    where the person is a repeat offender, be liable to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both;

(c)    where the offender is a serious offender in relation to such driving, be liable to a fine of not less than $2,000 and not more than $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months or to both, in addition to any punishment under paragraph (a) or (b); or

(d)    where the offender is a serious repeat offender in relation to such driving, be punished with a fine of not less than $5,000 and not more than $20,000 and with imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years, in addition to any punishment under paragraph (a) or (b).

…

[emphasis added]

13     Section 64(2C)(a) – the punishment provision applicable in the present case – was enacted in 2019. Despite having been in force for the past four years, there is a dearth of jurisprudence on how the provision ought to be applied in sentencing: Kwan Weiguang v Public Prosecutor [2022] SGHC 121 (“Kwan Weiguang (HC)”) at [32].

14     In the circumstances, I approached the sentencing of the Accused’s offence from first principles. I first considered what types of dangerous or reckless driving would be punishable under s 64(2C)(a). Thereafter, I determined where the Accused’s offence fell along the spectrum of such types of driving: Public Prosecutor v Tan Thian Earn [2016] 3 SLR 269 at [45]; Sentencing Principles in Singapore (Academy Publishing, 2019) (“Sentencing Principles”) at [03.018] – [03.020].

Section 64(2C)(a) applies only to non-personal injury cases

15     Regarding the first step of the sentencing analysis, I take the following views. First, only non-personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving are punishable under s 64(2C), Second, egregious cases of such driving can attract a substantial imprisonment term of up to 12 months’ imprisonment. I take these views for the following reasons.

Section 64(2C)(a) is a punishment provision

16     On a plain reading –

(a)      Section 64(1) makes it an offence to drive dangerously or recklessly. This is evident from the phrases “shall be guilty of an offence” in s 64(1) as well as “an offence under subsection (1)” in the chapeau of ss 64(2), 64(2A), 64(2B), and 64(2C). To make out an offence of dangerous or reckless driving, all that the Prosecution needs to prove are the elements in s 64(1) – namely, that (i) the offender has driven a motor vehicle, (ii) the vehicle has been driven on a road, and (iii) the vehicle has been driven recklessly or at a speed or in a manner which is dangerous to the public. The consequence of the dangerous or reckless driving (i.e., whether personal injury has been caused) is not an element of the offence.

(b)     Instead, the consequence of the dangerous or reckless driving goes towards the punishment. Specifically, s 64(2) to s 64(2C) set out the punishment for the offence of dangerous or reckless driving. This is clear from the phrases “shall … be liable to” and “shall … be punished with” in the provision.

17     In summary –

(a)     Parliament has “delinked” the offence-creating provision for dangerous or reckless driving from the punishment provisions. Once the elements of the offence in s 64(1) are proved, the offender will be punished based on the consequence of his driving.

(b)     If personal injury has been caused, then depending on the nature of the injury, the applicable punishment provision is s 64(2), s 64(2A), or s 64(2B).

(c)     Section 64(2C) applies only if no personal injury has been caused. This view is consistent with both (i) the express wording in s 64(2C) (which limits its application to “any other case” which does not fall within the other punishment provisions in s 64(2) to s 64(2B)), and also (ii) how the High Court has interpreted s 64(2C) in Kwan Weiguang at [32].

Parliament’s intent

18     Apart from statutory interpretation and dicta (Kwan Weiguang at [32]), my view that s 64(2C) covers only non-personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving is also consistent with the legislative history of s 64 and Parliament’s intent.

19     Prior to the 2019 amendments, s 64 (set out below) provided only a single range of punishment with no differentiation based on the consequence of the dangerous or reckless driving:

Reckless or dangerous driving

64.—(1)    If any person drives a motor vehicle on a road recklessly, or at a speed or in a manner which is dangerous to the public, having regard to all the circumstances of the case, including the nature, condition and use of the road, and the amount of traffic which is actually at the time, or which might reasonably be expected to be, on the road, he shall be guilty of an offence and shall be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $5,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months or to both and, in the case of a second or subsequent conviction, to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both.

…

[emphasis added]

20     In 2019, Parliament amended s 64 and enacted s 64(2) to s 64(2C). In making these amendments, Parliament had clearly intended that the punishment for dangerous or reckless driving must be pegged to the nature of personal injury caused (if any) by such driving. This intent is clearly reflected during the second reading for the Road Traffic (Amendment) Bill in Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (8 July 2019) vol 94. Among others, it was emphasised during the speech that –

(a)     There was a need “to raise sentencing norms” as well as “to avoid manifestly inadequate jail sentences” for egregious irresponsible driving offences;

(b)     The amendments were needed for a “stronger deterrence against irresponsible driving”; and

(c)     The approach under the amended s 64 would be that if a “motorist causes more harm, the level of punishment will be higher”.

21     If personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving are also punishable under s 64(2C), then this will frustrate Parliament’s intent of raising the “sentencing norms” for non-personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving. This is because significant custodial sentences under s 64(2C) will only be applicable for personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving: see [51(a)].

Summary

22     In summary – s 64(2C) is applicable only to non-personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving. These views are shared by the Prosecution[note: 8] and the Defence. Egregious cases of such driving can be punished up to 12 months’ imprisonment. A contrary view – that personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving can also be punished under s 64(2C) – is incompatible with the clear language in s 64 and Parliament’s intent.

Offence-specific sentencing factors

23     Having determined that s 64(2C)(a) covers only non-personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving, I next considered the factors which would have the greatest influence on the gravity of the offence. In this regard, the settled position prior to the 2019 RTA amendments is that the gravity of an offence of dangerous or reckless driving is principally influenced by two factors. First, the extent of harm caused by the driving. Second, the level of the offender’s culpability for the offence: Public Prosecutor v Koh Thiam Huat [2017] 4 SLR 1099 (“Koh Thiam Huat”) at [41].

24     In my judgment, this position applies equally to s 64(2C)(a) with one caveat – i.e., the extent of harm to be considered in sentencing under s 64(2C)(a) should not include personal injury that has been caused by the dangerous or reckless driving. This is because personal injury cases of dangerous or reckless driving are to be punished under s 64(2), s 64(2A) or s 64(2B), whichever is applicable.

25     Having reviewed the relevant sentencing precedents (both before and after the 2019 amendments), I have distilled the following offence-specific sentencing factors for dangerous or reckless driving.

Harm

26     The following are relevant when assessing the level of harm caused by dangerous or reckless driving:

(a)      Level of potential harm from the driving. There are two factors which affect the level of potential harm. First, the level of danger posed by the driving. Second, the number of persons exposed to the danger. See Sentencing Principles at [17.095] – [17.106]; Leong Sow Hon v Public Prosecutor [2020] SGHC 228 at [35]. Let me elaborate.

(i)       The first factor requires a consideration of the manner and nature of dangerous or reckless driving. Examples of driving that is highly dangerous include the following:

(1)       Excessive speeding/hell riding: Wu Zhi Yong at [36(e)]. In my view, the level of danger of such a conduct is heightened if it occurs in the context of competitive racing as other drivers will also be involved in similar acts of dangerous or reckless driving.

(2)       Executing a dangerous manoeuvre at high speed: Neo Chuan Sheng v Public Prosecutor [2020] 5 SLR 410 (“Neo Seng Chuan”) at [42].

(3)       Driving against the flow of traffic on a busy road: Aw Tai Hock at [38].

(4)       Dangerous or reckless driving involving vehicles which are more difficult to control (e.g., heavy vehicles and high-performance cars): Kwan Weiguang at [66].

(5)       Dangerous or reckless driving on a busy road or expressway when visibility is poor (e.g., due to bad weather).

(6)       Driving when the offender’s cognitive ability and judgment has been impaired by his failure to wear his visual aids, or by intoxication from alcohol and/or drugs.

(ii)       As for the second factor, more persons will be exposed to danger where, for instance, the dangerous or reckless driving takes place –

(1)       when the traffic volume is heavy,

(2)       within residential or school zones,

(3)       a traffic light or pedestrian crossings,

(4)       when the offender has many passengers.

See Neo Chuan Sheng at [22]; Wu Zhi Yong at [15] and [36(a)]; Public Prosecutor v Chow Chee Kin [2022] SGDC 45 at [40].

(b)      Extent of property loss or damage. The extent of any loss or damage caused to other vehicles, installations, and structures on the road is also relevant to sentencing. See Wu Zhi Yong at [36(b)].

(c)      Impact of driving to road users. It is aggravating if the dangerous or reckless driving causes significant inconvenience to other road users. An example is where the dangerous or reckless driving causes an accident which leads to a massive traffic jam on a busy road: Wu Zhi Yong at [15]; Sentencing Principles at [17.062] – [17.067].

27     For completeness, the fact that the dangerous or reckless driving has not caused any personal injury is an irrelevant sentencing consideration under s 64(2C). This is because if personal injury has in fact been caused, the offender will be punished under s 64(2), s 64(2A), or s 64(2B), whichever is applicable.

Culpability

28     I now come to the second key sentencing factor for dangerous or reckless driving – i.e., the level of an offender’s culpability. The relevant considerations in assessing this factor include the following:

(a)      Reason for dangerous or reckless driving. In general, an offender’s reason for committing an offence is a relevant sentencing consideration: Sentencing Principles at [11.037] – [11.043]. In the case of dangerous or reckless driving, the offence can be committed for various reasons. These include the following:

(i)       Dangerous driving due to a failure to appreciate the road condition and/or the risks posed by the offender’s driving: Jali bin Mohd Yunos v Public Prosecutor [2014] 4 SLR 1059 (“Jali”) at [32].

(1)       It can be mitigating if such a failure is contributed by, for instance, the driver’s inexperience (rather than his irresponsibility).

(2)       If the failure to appreciate the road conditions and/or risks is due to the driver’s own action which causes him to be distracted, such as using a handphone while driving, then this can be an aggravating factor.

(ii)       Dangerous driving due to an error of judgement.

(1)       It can be mitigating if such an error is contributed by, for instance, difficult weather conditions.

(2)       On the other hand, it can be aggravating if the erroneous judgment arises in the context of the offender taking calculated risks in his driving: Jali at [32]; Public Prosecutor v Teo Poh Leng [1991] 2 SLR(R) 541 at [4]; Abdul Ghani bin Tahir v Public Prosecutor [2017] 4 SLR 1153 at [120]; Mohamad Iskanda/r bin Basri v Public Prosecutor [2006] 4 SLR(R) 440 (“Mohamad Iskandar”) at [25].

(iii)       Dangerous or reckless driving due to extenuating circumstances.

The fact that that an offender had driven dangerously or recklessly in response to a genuine emergency can be a mitigating factor: Aw Tai Hock at [40]; Mohamad Iskandar at [25] and [28].

(iv)       Dangerous or reckless driving with disregard for the safety of others.

It is aggravating if an offender had driven dangerously or reckless without any concern for the well-being of other persons.

(1)       One example is where an offender drives dangerously or recklessly to intimidate another driver, e.g., in a display of road rage: Public Prosecutor v Lim Yee Hua [2018] 3 SLR 1106 (“Lim Yee Hua”) at [21].

(2)       Another example is where an offender drives dangerously or recklessly to evade apprehension: Neo Chuan Sheng at [42]. It is especially aggravating if such driving is directed at law enforcement officers, e.g., by intentionally driving into law enforcement officers at a roadblock, or by intentionally endangering their safety during a police chase: see, e.g., the facts in Public Prosecutor v Azman Bin Abdul Karim [2020] SGDC 276 at [20] – [22]; Sim Gek Yong v Public Prosecutor [1995] 1 SLR(R) 185 at [14]; Lee Koon Chye v Public Prosecutor [2008] SGDC 267; Public Prosecutor v Lee Cheng Yan [2020] SGDC 216. Law enforcement officers need to know that they will be protected by the very law that they seek to uphold. If dangerous or reckless driving directed at law enforcement officers are not met with stiff sentences, then this can affect their morale and their willingness to place themselves in harm’s way to preserve law and order and protect society: Public Prosecutor v Yeo Ek Boon Jeffrey [2018] 3 SLR 1080 at [48] and [49].

(v)       Dangerous or reckless driving with the intention of causing harm.

(1)       There have been cases where offenders chose to use their vehicles to cause harm to persons on the road: see e.g., Sentencing Practice in the Subordinate Courts (LexisNexis, 3rd ed, 2013) (“Sentencing Practice”) at p 305 (Ee Kuan Boon v Public Prosecutor [MA 327/92]), p 311 (Public Prosecutor v Yap Wei Wun Raymond [MA 207/09]), and p 1654 (Public Prosecutor v Sohanlal Giri s/o Piyarelal Giri [MA 262/99])).

(2)       There are also cases of offenders using their vehicles to cause property damage: see e.g., Sentencing Practice at p 1677 (Public Prosecutor v Loh Hong Sai [MA 18/97]).

(3)       Dangerous or reckless driving with the intention of causing harm constitutes probably the most culpable instance of the offence. Regardless of whether the intended harm materialises, such driving deserves strong condemnation and stiff sentences.

(b)     In general, an offender is more culpable if he has persisted in a course of offending conduct: Logachev Vladislav v Public Prosecutor [2018] 4 SLR 609 at [59]. In the context of dangerous or reckless driving, examples of such an aggravating factor being present include –

(i)       Dangerous or reckless driving which covers a long distance or duration: Aw Tai Hock at [64]; Neo Ah Luan v Public Prosecutor [2018] 5 SLR 1153 at [73].

(ii)       The offender repeatedly disregarding warnings to cease his dangerous or reckless driving.

(iii)       The offender driving despite knowing that he does not have a driving license or has been disqualified from driving: Tang Ling Lee v Public Prosecutor [2018] SGHC 18 at [29]; Public Prosecutor v Rizuwan bin Rohmat [MA No. 9176 of 2021] at [21(a)] (unreported); Public Prosecutor vs Periam Rij s/o Mathyalakan [2022] SGDC 245 at [38] – [41].

Summary

29     To summarise, the offence-specific sentencing factors for dangerous or reckless driving that is punishable under s 64(2C)(a) include following:

Harm

(a)

Level of potential harm from the driving.

(b)

Extent of property loss or damage.

(c)

Impact of driving to road users.

Culpability

(f)

Reason for dangerous or reckless driving.

(e)

Whether the offender has persisted in dangerous or reckless driving.



Utilising the full sentencing spectrum

30     Courts are obliged to consider the factual scenarios that can come within a prescribed punishment when calibrating an appropriate sentence. In the context of dangerous or reckless driving, I am of the view that examples of what can come within the two ends of the sentencing spectrum for s 64(2C)(a) are as follows:

Level of gravity

Example

(a)

Low level of offence gravity – Fine appropriate

 

(i) Low harm

Dangerous or reckless driving –

[1] carries a low level of potential harm (i.e., the driving is not highly dangerous, and the number of persons exposed to the danger is low),

[2] has not caused property damage, AND

[3] has little impact to other road users.

(ii) Low culpability

Dangerous or reckless driving –

[1] is due to an error of judgement or inexperience,

[2] occurs briefly, AND

[3] is not accompanied by any factor that shows the offender to be particularly blameworthy.

(b)

High level of offence gravity

 

(i) High harm

Dangerous or reckless driving –

[1] carries a high level of potential harm (i.e., the driving is particularly dangerous, and a number of persons have been exposed to the danger),

[2] has caused significant property damage, AND

[3] has adversely affected many road users.

(ii) High culpability

Dangerous or reckless driving –

[1] has been committed intentionally and with disregard for the safety of others, AND

[2] demonstrates a persistent course of offending.



31     I wish to state the following regarding [30(b)] above:

(a)     The example is not intended to represent the worst type of dangerous or reckless driving punishable under s 64(2C)(a). It is intended to simply show what type of dangerous or reckless driving can cross the threshold of high level of gravity for the offence.

(b)     An example of cases which can fall within [30(b)(ii)(1)] are road rage cases. When an offender maliciously uses his vehicle to cause harm and thus cause serious property damage, this clearly constitutes one of the more serious instances of dangerous or reckless driving under s 64(2C)(a). In such cases, the sentencing objectives of retribution and deterrence take centre stage.

Driving disqualification

32     Courts have the discretion to order an offender who has been convicted of dangerous or reckless driving to be disqualified from driving under s 42(1)(a) of the RTA. In Kwan Weiguang (HC) at [56], [57] and [68] – [71], the High Court provided the following guidance on how the period of driving disqualification may be calibrated for an offender sentenced under s 64(2C)(a):

 

Scenario

Driving disqualification

(a)

First-time traffic offenders with a clean driving record (especially those without any history of compoundable offences or speeding tickets).

AND

The degree of potential harm posed to other road users is relatively low.

AND

The offender did not display a contumelious or blatant disregard for the safety of other road users.

12 months or below

(b)

The offender demonstrated very dangerous behaviour or conduct showing a disregard for traffic rules, etiquette, and the interests of other road users.

12 months or more

(c)

The presence of any violence or threats of violence which are characteristic of road rage situations.

OR

Offender decided to pursue a personal vendetta or to settle a score from a driving-related dispute by seeking to force a confrontation with the other party by driving right in front of him and jamming the brakes multiple times.

OR

Past driving records reveal the offender to be an unsafe driver with a cavalier attitude towards road safety and demonstrating his unwillingness to comply with the law.

Longer disqualification period warranted



33     The High Court had also held that there is no necessary link between the period of the disqualification order and the fine and/or imprisonment sentence imposed on an offender. In some circumstances, it may be appropriate to impose a disqualification period even if the fine and/or imprisonment term imposed is relatively lenient. Conversely, an increase in the fine or imprisonment term imposed should not be taken to mandate the imposition of a reduced period of disqualification than would otherwise have been ordered: Kwan Weiguang (HC) at [72] – [74].

Offender-specific sentencing factors

34     Apart from offence-specific sentencing factors, offender-specific sentencing factors must also be considered when calibrating a sentence under s 64(2C)(a). This is because a sentence must not only fit the crime – it must also fit the offender.

35     In this regard, the offender-specific aggravating factors are well established. These include the following:

(a)     Offences taken into consideration for the purposes of sentencing.

(b)     The offender’s driving record.

(i)       If the offender is a “repeat offender”, then s 64(2C)(b) is the applicable punishment provision. The prescribed punishment under s 64(2C)(b) is twice that of s 64(2C)(a). A “repeat offender” is defined in s 64(3) to mean a person who –

… has been convicted (whether before, on or after 1 November 2019) on at least one other earlier occasion of any of the following offences:

(a)    an offence under subsection (1);

(b)    an offence under section 63, 65 or 116;

(c)    an offence under this section, or section 65 or 66, as in force immediately before 1 November 2019;

(d)    an offence under section 279, 304A, 336, 337 or 338 of the Penal Code 1871 where the act constituting the offence involves the use of a motor vehicle.

[emphasis added]

(ii)       Given the above, an offender who has antecedents involving any of the offences cited in s 64(3) will be punished as follows:

Disposition of antecedent

Punishment provision

Offence compounded

Section 64(2C)(a)

Conviction

Section 64(2C)(b)



(c)     Lack of remorse.

It is aggravating if an offender (i) is “belligerent or violent” upon arrest or (ii) fails to stop so as to avoid apprehension for his dangerous or reckless driving: Wu Zhi Yong at [36(f)]; Public Prosecutor v Lee Meng Soon [2007] 4 SLR(R) 240 at [33]; Haleem Bathusa bin Abdul Rahim v Public Prosecutor [2023] SGHC 41 (“Haleem Bathusa”) at [47] – [51].

36     Offender-specific mitigating factors include (a) evidence of genuine remorse and (b) an offender’s youth: Wu Zhi Yong at [48]; Koh Thiam Huat at [41].

Applying the principles

37     I now come to the second stage of the sentencing analysis – i.e., to determine where the Accused’s offence falls along the spectrum of possible offences under s 64(2C)(a): see [14] above.

High level of harm

38     In my view, there is a high level of harm in the Accused’s offence.

39     First, there is clearly a significant degree of potential harm in his dangerous driving.

(a)     The Accused’s acts of (i) ramming his car against Yap’s car multiple times and (ii) suddenly stopping his car in the middle of the road when there were other vehicles around are highly dangerous.

(b)     The danger posed by the Accused’s acts is further heightened by the fact that his vehicular assault occurred (i) on lane 1 of a major road where other vehicles were travelling at speed, (ii) when the road was wet, and (iii) at a time when there was a heavy drizzle. This potent combination of factors made it more difficult for Yap and the other drivers in the vicinity to control their vehicles.

(c)     The Accused’s dangerous driving had put several road users in harm’s way. From the video recordings, vehicles can be seen travelling close to Yap’s and the Accused’s cars at the material time. These recordings also show the following:

(i)       At about 2.16pm when the Accused suddenly stopped his car, reversed, and rammed his car into the front left portion of Yap’s car, a dark coloured car was travelling at a fast speed behind Yap’s car. When the Accused carried out his vehicular assault, the dark coloured car stopped suddenly, reversed slightly, and switched on its hazard lights to warn other drivers of the danger ahead.

(ii)       At about 2.18pm when the Accused’s car (then in lane 2) rammed into the left side of Yap’s car (then in lane 1), there were vehicles just in front of these two cars.

(iii)       At about 2.21pm when the Accused’s car hit into the rear of Yap’s car, there was a vehicle directly in front of Yap’s car.

(iv)       At about 2.23pm when the Accused’s car rammed into the rear of Yap’s car thrice on a filter road, a van was just in front of the latter’s car waiting at the stop line. At the material time, there were fast moving vehicles in front of the van on the main road.

(v)       Throughout the entire incident, Yap’s daughter was in his car.

40     Second, the Accused’s vehicular assault had caused significant damage to Yap’s car. The front left and rear portions of the car were dented. The right rear and front right tyre fender of Yap’s car were also scratched. The repair cost was S$13,296.88.

41     Finally, Yap must have been alarmed during the eight minutes when the Accused stalked and rammed into his car, all the way from Bartley Road East to a carpark at Tampines Avenue 5. This alarm must also have been felt by other drivers who had witnessed the Accused’s blatant and unrestrained display of aggression. Yap and these drivers would no doubt have been concerned for their own safety. As the Prosecution had rightly highlighted, the Accused’s sudden and dangerous manoeuvres on the roads made it difficult for other drivers to predict his behaviour.[note: 9]

High level of culpability

42     The Accused’s level of culpability is also high. He had deliberately driven in a dangerous manner with absolute disregard for the law, as well as the safety of Yap and other road users. The Accused’s vehicular assault was relentless and perpetrated over a long distance. The assault ended only because the Accused’s car was stuck at a carpark barrier.

Defence psychiatric report

43     To mitigate his dangerous driving behaviour, the Accused adduced a report from his psychiatrist dated 6 February 2023 (“Psychiatric Report”) to show that he was labouring from adjustment disorder (with mixed anxiety and depressed mood) at the time of the offence.

44     I did not find the Psychiatric Report to be helpful in sentencing.

(a)     First, I have doubts about the reliability of the diagnosis that the Accused was suffering from adjustment disorder at the material time. This diagnosis was based on, among others, (i) interviews with the Accused and his wife, (ii) psychological tests done on the Accused, and (iii) a report by Dr Ung Eng Khean dated 3 January 2022.[note: 10] It bears noting that the interviews and tests were not conducted soon after the incident – but more than two years later. Furthermore, information provided in the interviews were (i) essentially self-reported symptoms by the Accused and (ii) recollection of events by his wife who is in any event not an eyewitness to the incident. Additionally, the diagnosis is based on Dr Ung’s report – a piece of hearsay and opinion evidence prepared about 1½ years after the incident that was not produced for scrutiny before me.[note: 11]

(b)     Second, and in any event, the Psychiatric Report had stated unequivocally that there is no contributory link between the Accused’s alleged adjustment disorder and his dangerous driving. In other words, the Accused did not commit the offence due to a psychiatric condition that lessened his culpability.[note: 12]

Provocation from Yap

45     This brings me to the actual reason for the Accused’s offence. It is not disputed that the Accused had driven dangerously to settle a personal score with Yap. According to the Accused, he was provoked by Yap’s act of dangerous and aggressive driving towards him.

46     I did not find the Accused’s reason for his irresponsible and dangerous driving to be mitigating.

(a)     It is settled law that provocation by other road users does not entitle a driver to react maliciously: Teo Seng Tiong v Public Prosecutor [2021] 2 SLR 642 at [120]. Our roads are not playgrounds or battle arenas for the Accused and other angry drivers to taunt road users or to exact vengeance. It has been rightly held that “[t]he desire [of a driver] to avenge a wrong is a human failing that society must not encourage, as it would lead him to take the law into his own hands. It can lead to vigilante justice and is inimical to the maintenance of law and order, as such, it must be suppressed for the greater good”: Public Prosecutor v Tan Khoon Shan Terrance [2012] 4 SLR 1121 at [12] and [13].

(b)     In any event, I found the Accused’s actions to be unreasonable and grossly disproportionate to whatever provocation that Yap might have directed towards him. By deliberately executing highly dangerous manoeuvres on a major road to ram into Yap’s car, the Accused had displayed a blatant disregard for the safety of Yap and other road users. I agreed with the Prosecution that –

Even though the accused could have disengaged after reversing into the witness’s car along Bartley Road East … he chose to pursue and pester the witness over a long distance, thereby prolonging the incident. He had also shown himself to be a road bully who would not hesitate to use his car as a weapon against the witness. …[note: 13]

47     For completeness, I wish to comment on the opinion in the Psychiatric Report that the Accused has the general capacity to regulate his anger and to restrain himself in the face of provocation. The report stated that the Accused’s conduct during the incident was out of character, and that he has “a low risk of violent re-offending and a good chance of rehabilitation”.[note: 14] In my view, these views do not justify a reduction of an otherwise appropriate sentence. This is because the sentencing objectives for road rage cases are general deterrence and retribution. If an offender is shown to have a high risk of “violent re-offending”, then this would warrant an enhancement in the sentence to reflect the need for specific deterrence as well.

Yap’s sentence

48     The Defence had urged me to consider the fact that Yap was sentenced to a fine when calibrating the Accused’s sentence.

49     I am of the view that Yap’s sentence does not in any way fetter the exercise of my sentencing discretion in the Accused’s case.

(a)     Yap’s sentence would only be relevant in the sentencing of the Accused – if at all – under the parity principle. This principle applies where two or more offenders are sentenced for participating in the same criminal enterprise. Under the parity principle, the sentences passed on them should generally be the same, unless there is a relevant difference in their responsibility or their personal circumstances: Muhamad Azmi bin Kamil v Public Prosecutor [2022] SGCA 68 at [25].

(b)     In my view, the parity principle does not apply in the present case. It bears noting that the Accused and Yap had committed different acts of dangerous driving. Yap’s sentence is for the following charge of dangerous driving which took place before the Accused’s acts of dangerous driving:

You […] are charged that you, on the 14 August 2020, at about 2.00 pm, while driving from Bartley Road East to Tampines Avenue 5, Singapore, did drive motor car SKU4399H on a road in a manner which was dangerous to the public having regarding to all circumstances of the case, including the nature, condition and use of the road and the amount of traffic which was actually at the time or which might reasonably be expected to be on the road, to wit,

1)     by abruptly changing lane from the left lane to right lane, encroached into the path of motor car SKC 994K, resulting motor car SKC 994K left side to side swipe your right side mirror and

2)     you continued to again abruptly switched lane from the left into motor car SKC 994K path and jammed on your brakes, causing motor car SKC 994K to take evasive action by swerving left to avoid a collision

and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 64(1) of the Road Traffic Act, Chapter 276 punishable under Section 64(2C)(a) of the Road Traffic Act, Chapter 276 (2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”).

[emphasis added]

(c)     Yap’s act of dangerous driving is clearly less egregious than the Accused’s – both in terms of the level of harm and culpability. For instance, Yap did not ram his car into the Accused’s car.

(d)     Finally, I found Yap’s sentence to be of limited utility in sentencing the Accused. This is because no grounds of decision was issued for Yap’s sentence. In the absence of a reasoned decision, it is not possible to discern what had weighed on the mind of the judge when sentencing Yap: Janardana Jayasankarr v Public Prosecutor [2016] 4 SLR 1288 (“Janardana”) at [13(b)].

Sentencing precedents

50     At this stage, I wish to briefly comment on the sentencing precedents under s 64 submitted by the parties. I did not find these precedents to be helpful as they are not similar to the Accused’s offence. Let me elaborate.

Aw Tai Hock

51      Aw Tai Hock was decided before the amendment to s 64. In my view, sentences imposed in precedents such as Aw Tai Hock are of little precedential value in sentencing cases under s 64(2C)(a). My reasons are as follows.

(a)     As stated earlier, prior to 2019, s 64 prescribed only a single range of punishment (up to a maximum fine of $5,000 and a maximum term of imprisonment for 12 months) for all types of dangerous or reckless driving – including those which caused death and grievous hurt. Given that the sentencing for the entire range of dangerous or reckless driving cases was capped at 12 months’ imprisonment, significant terms of imprisonment under s 64 would logically be appropriate only where serious personal injury had been caused.

(b)     In other words, prior to the 2019 amendments, it would not be appropriate to impose significant imprisonment terms where no serious personal injury had been caused. This was so even where the dangerous or reckless driving was particularly egregious and serious property damage had been caused. That is why despite finding the case to be “clearly one of the worst cases of dangerous driving” (see judgement at [46]), the High Court in Aw Tai Hock imposed only five months’ imprisonment – i.e., less than half of the prescribed punishment – as only minor personal injury was caused in that case (the victim suffered only musculoskeletal injuries and was given four days’ medical leave).

(c)     This is no longer the case after the 2019 amendments. Courts can now sentence an offender under s 64(2C)(a) up to the maximum punishment of 12 months’ imprisonment even if his dangerous or reckless driving does not cause any personal injury. As stated earlier, the legislative intent behind the 2019 amendment is to provide stronger deterrence against irresponsible drivers and thus make our roads safer. It would thus be remiss of the courts for not applying the full sentencing spectrum in s 64(2C)(a) in egregious cases of dangerous or reckless driving simply because no personal injury has been caused.

52     Apart from the above, there are other reasons why the sentence in Aw Tai Hock should not set a glass ceiling for the Accused’s sentence.

(a)     First, if the same offence in Aw Tai Hock were to be committed today, it is possible that the offence will not be punished under s 64(2C)(a).

(i)       I say this because Andy (the subject of the offender’s road rage in that case) had suffered injuries after colliding into another car when attempting to evade the offender’s dangerous driving: Public Prosecutor v Aw Tai Hock [2017] SGDC 131 at [5]. If one takes the view that the offender’s dangerous driving had caused Andy to suffer injuries, then the applicable punishment provision will be s 64(2B)(a).

(ii)       Section 64(2B)(a) prescribes a higher punishment than s 64(2C)(a). Under s 64(2B)(a), the offender can be sentenced to a fine not exceeding $10,000, imprisonment not exceeding two years, or both.

(iii)       One way in which a court may sentence the offender in under s 64(2B)(a) is to adopt the sentencing uplift approach in Ng Soon Kim v Public Prosecutor [2020] 3 SLR 1097 at [12] – [17]. Under this approach, a court will first consider what would be an appropriate sentence under s 64(2C)(a) based on the gravity of the dangerous or reckless driving alone. Thereafter, the court will apply a suitable uplift to that sentence by having regard to the severity of the hurt caused. The maximum extent of this uplift will be $5,000/one year’s imprisonment – i.e., the difference between the maximum punishment under s 64(2B)(a) ($10,000/two years’ imprisonment) and the maximum punishment under s 64(2C)(a) ($5,000/one year’s imprisonment).

(b)     Second, if the offence in Aw Tai Hock is punishable under s 64(2C)(a), then it is likely that the offender will receive a sentence higher than the five months’ imprisonment originally imposed by the High Court. This is because –

(i)       Section 64(2C)(a) applies only to non-personal injury cases,

(ii)       Egregious cases of dangerous or reckless driving can be punished up to 12 months’ imprisonment under s 64(2C)(a), and

(iii)       The High Court had already described Aw Tai Hock as being “clearly one of the worst cases of dangerous driving”.

(c)     Third, and in any event, the Accused’s dangerous driving offence is more serious than that in Aw Tai Hock.

(i)       More road users were exposed to the danger in the present case than in Aw Tai Hock. This is because the Accused’s dangerous driving took place on a major road, whereas a significant part of the offender’s dangerous driving in Aw Tai Hock occurred in a carpark: see Aw Tai Hock (DC) at [5].

(ii)       The Accused’s dangerous driving lasted for a longer duration and covered a longer distance than that in Aw Tai Hock. See Annex A at [3(b)].

(iii)       Yap’s actions which triggered the Accused’s dangerous driving is less provocative than Andy’s actions in Aw Tai Hock. See Annex A at [3(a)].

(d)     Finally, the Accused had several driving related antecedents, including one for speeding just one year before the present offence. The offender in Aw Tai Hock did not have any antecedent. See Annex A at [4(a)].

Ryan Asyraf

53     I will now touch on Public Prosecutor v Ryan Asyraf bin Mohammad A’zman [2022] SGDC 15 (“Ryan Asyraf”). In this case, the offender had pleaded guilty to 13 charges and consented to 23 other charges being taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing.

54     One of the proceeded charges is an offence of reckless riding punishable under s 64(2C)(a). The offender had –

(a)     ridden his motorcycle at about 2.29 pm at speed,

(b)     made an illegal U-turn abruptly and thus caused an unknown car to apply its emergency brakes to avoid a collision, and

(c)     ridden his motorcycle in a manner that caused him to lose control of the motorcycle and to skid.

55     The offender had engaged in the above reckless riding to escape from a traffic police officer (on motorcycle patrol). The officer was trying to stop him for wearing a tinted visor, which was in contravention of the road traffic rules. After his motorcycle skidded, the offender ran away on foot. He was eventually apprehended by the traffic police officer. The offender was sentenced to one week’s imprisonment and disqualified from holding all classes of driving licence for two years.

56     The offender’s reckless riding offence is clearly less serious than the Accused’s offence. Ryan Asyraf involved a motorcycle – which is a less dangerous vehicle than a car (as in the Accused’s case). Apart from the unknown car which had to apply emergency brakes to avoid a collision, there is no evidence to show that the offender’s reckless riding (a) had carried a high level of potential harm, or (b) had caused property damage.

57     That said, the offender’s sentence of one week’s imprisonment appears to be lenient. I say this because the following aggravating factors were present in that case:

(a)     The offender had engaged in a persistent course of reckless riding to evade police apprehension.

(b)     The offender had ridden the motorcycle while under driving disqualification. In fact, he had multiple traffic related antecedents, including for driving without a licence.

(c)     The offender had committed the reckless riding offence while on bail for numerous other traffic related offences.

(d)     The offender had consented to another charge for an offence under s 64(1) and punishable under s 64(2C)(a) being taken into consideration for sentencing. According to this second charge, the offender had on another occasion driven recklessly by (i) failing to adhere to the instructions from a uniformed police officer to alight from his vehicle, (ii) reversing his vehicle such that it collided onto a stationary parked car, and (iii) driving away from the scene when the police officer was standing near to the driver’s side of the offender’s vehicle.

Kwan Weiguang

58     Finally, I turn to Kwan Weiguang (HC). In this case, the offender was convicted of an offence under s 64(1) and punishable under s 64(2C)(a) of the RTA. The offender had driven dangerously by (a) repeatedly changing lanes ahead of a car driven by a person that he was unhappy with (“the victim”) and (b) subsequently stopping his car in a position that was likely to cause danger to other road users on lane 2 of 3 lanes of Keppel Road at about 8.34 pm. The offender was fined $1,600 and disqualified from driving all classes of driving license for 15 months. The offender appealed against his driving disqualification. On appeal, the disqualification period was reduced to 12 months. The High Court did not comment on the adequacy of the fine imposed on the offender as there was no appeal against sentence.

59     On the facts, the offender’s dangerous driving is not particularly egregious – and certainly not as serious as the Accused’s offence. There are also mitigating factors in that case:

(a)     No damage was caused to the offender and the victim’s vehicles during the offender’s dangerous driving. There was a minor collision after the events stated in the offender’s charge. This collision – which occurred when the offender was moving off from a stationary position on lane 2 of Keppel Road to follow the victim’s car – resulted in some scratches to both vehicles: Public Prosecutor v Kwan Weiguang [2021] SGDC 204 (“Kwan Weiguang (DC)” at [9] and [11].

(b)     The dangerous driving lasted for only about 10 s: Kwan Weiguang (DC) at [8] and [9].

(c)     The offender was apparently trying to obstruct the passage of the victim’s car to confront the victim – rather than to cause him harm: Kwan Weiguang (DC) at [8] and [9].

(d)     The offender did not have any antecedent – not even compounded offences: Kwan Weiguang (DC) at [27].

(e)     The offender had demonstrated his remorse by pleading guilty at the first mention of the case and readily admitting to his fault in his mitigation: Kwan Weiguang (DC) at [27].

Starting point sentence

60     Having regard to all the circumstances of the case, including the nature, condition and use of the road, and the amount of traffic which was on the road at the material time, an appropriate starting point sentence for the Accused’s offence is as follows:

(a)     Seven months’ imprisonment.

This sentence is higher than that imposed in Aw Tai Hock. The reasons for this are set out in [51] and [52] above.

(b)     Disqualification from holding all classes of driving license for three years.

This disqualification period is in line with (i) the guidelines set in Kwan Weiguang (see [32] and [33] above) and (ii) the disqualification period imposed in Aw Tai Hock.

Sentencing factors relating to the Accused

61     The starting point sentence is based on the offence-specific sentencing factors present in the Accused’s case. I next considered to what extent this sentence ought to be adjusted based on the offender-specific sentencing factors in this case. In this regard, I have considered the following:

(a)     The Accused had pleaded guilty. This is mitigating because it shows that he is remorseful for his offence and has saved precious court’s time in dealing with his offence.

(b)     The mitigating effect of the Accused’s plea is however counter-balanced by two other considerations. First, is the existence of Yap’s in-car camera recording which objectively proved the Accused’s offence. Second, the Accused has antecedents for speeding, the last being in 2019, which is just one year prior to his present offence.

62     In the circumstances, I do not see the need to adjust the starting point sentence based on the offender-specific sentencing factors in the present case.

Conclusion

63     I am mindful that the sentence that I have imposed (seven months’ imprisonment) is twice the sentence sought by the Prosecution. It is well settled that a sentence proposed by the Prosecution should not be regarded as the upper limit of the sentence that may be meted out. This is because sentencing is ultimately a matter for the court: Janardana at [12]; Karthigeyan M Kailasam v Public Prosecutor [2016] SGHC 194 at [19]. In this decision, I have explained why a sentence higher than that proposed by the Prosecution is warranted, and is in line with the principle that the full sentencing spectrum in s 64(2C)(a) should be considered in sentencing.

64     Without a doubt, dangerous or reckless driving where vehicles have been used as instruments of intimidation and destruction constitute one of the most serious types of offences punishable under s 64(2C)(a). Right-minded members of the public will agree that drivers who callously disregard the safety and property of others by using their vehicles to vent their road rage, and thereby cause property damage, deserve strong condemnation.

65     Since the decisions in Ong Hwee Leong v Public Prosecutor [1992] 1 SLR(R) 458 at [7] and Public Prosecutor v Lee Seck Hing [1992] 2 SLR(R) 374 at [12] three decades ago, our courts have consistently imposed deterrent and punitive sentences in road rage cases prosecuted under s 323 and s 325 of the Penal Code. Custodial sentences have typically been imposed in such cases – unless the harm is insignificant and the offender’s culpability is low: Lim Yee Hua at [29], [30], [43], [45] and [48]; Haleem Bathusa at [43] – [45]; Public Prosecutor v Shi Ka Yee [2018] SGMC 21 at [90] – [97] and [121].

66     In my view, the intentional use of vehicles to harm road users in road rage is as serious – if not more serious – than the use of fists to voluntarily cause hurt or grievous hurt. This is so even if the road user does not suffer any personal injury as a result. No matter how serious the actions of a provocateur may have been, a vehicular assault on him or his vehicle can never be condoned. Driving is an inherently dangerous activity. Deliberate dangerous driving can kill.
