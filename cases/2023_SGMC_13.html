Damages – Assessment

Evidence – Admissibility of evidence

13 March 2023

Deputy Registrar Liu Zeming:

1       The Plaintiff’s motorcycle (the “Vehicle”) was damaged in a road traffic accident. He claimed $14,400 as “cost of repair”. A single joint expert (“SJE”) appointed by the court opined that the “Lump Sum Repair Cost” should be $5,600 instead. So far, nothing out of the ordinary.

2       As it turned out, the main (if not only) reason why the SJE recommended a significantly lower repair cost was because the SJE proceeded on the basis that he need not provide any estimate for numerous parts of the Vehicle which were in fact damaged, but, according to the SJE, not repaired or replaced by the repair workshop. These damaged parts were allegedly reused and fitted back to the Vehicle.

3       Against this background, parties proceeded to argue the case with intense focus on “whether or not the repairer repaired the damaged parts”.[note: 1] But was that the right question to ask?

Background

4       On 27 April 2018, a collision occurred between the Plaintiff’s motorcycle and the Defendant’s car.[note: 2] Repair on the Plaintiff’s motorcycle was done by Albert Motor Supply Pte Ltd (“Albert Motor”).[note: 3] Before the repairs were undertaken, the Plaintiff engaged L H Teo Appraisal Services (“LH Teo”) to conduct a pre-repair inspection of the motorcycle and to assess the cost for repairing the damage.[note: 4] The recommended repair cost from LH Teo was $14,400, and that was the amount eventually charged by Albert Motor.[note: 5] However, the Defendant was not agreeable to pay this amount. The Plaintiff therefore commenced the present action in October 2020, claiming compensation for damage to the Vehicle.

5       Since the dispute centred around the Plaintiff’s claim for “cost of repair” (and related claim for loss of use of the Vehicle during the period of repair), parties wished to appoint an expert witness to give an opinion on what was the reasonable repair cost for the Vehicle, as well as the reasonable duration for the repair. As the action was commenced in the Magistrate’s Court, a single joint expert had to be appointed pursuant to Order 108 r 5(3) of the Rules of Court (2014 Rev Ed). Mr Pang Kiah Kim of Form Team Consultancy (“Form Team”) was appointed as the SJE in this case.

6       The SJE issued his report on 9 November 2021 (the “SJE Report”).[note: 6] The SJE Report was addressed to “The State Courts of Singapore” care of the Plaintiff’s and the Defendant’s lawyers. It also referred to a set of “instructions on 27-Oct-2021” (the “Instructions”) and indicated that the SJE Report was prepared pursuant to those Instructions.[note: 7] I pause here to clarify three points.

(a)     First, despite having addressed the SJE Report to the State Courts, the SJE Report was not in fact delivered to the State Courts at the time of its issuance. The SJE Report was first filed in court on 29 May 2022, when the Plaintiff filed his bundle of documents.

(b)     Second, the court gave no “instructions” to the SJE prior to the preparation of his report. It would appear that the reference to “your instructions on 27-Oct-2021” in the SJE Report was a reference to joint instructions from the lawyers for the Plaintiff and the Defendant.

(c)     Third, the Instructions were not adduced in evidence in the AD Proceedings. Accordingly, I did not know the precise instructions given to the SJE.

7       After the SJE Report was issued, parties agreed to enter consent judgment on liability with the Defendant paying the Plaintiff 90% of the damages to be assessed. The assessment of damages proceedings (the “AD Proceedings”) eventually took place before me between June to November 2022.

The SJE Report and evidence

8       As mentioned, parties’ focus in the AD Proceedings was on the SJE Report and the SJE’s evidence. It is therefore appropriate to provide an outline of these first.

The SJE Report

9       The SJE stated in his report that a “single joint expert evaluation” to the Vehicle was conducted pursuant to the Instructions.[note: 8] He then listed his recommended repair or replacement costs for each part of the Vehicle that was damaged, as well as his recommended costs for labour, towing and other miscellaneous items. The SJE’s recommendations were presented alongside the repair costs submitted by (i) Albert Motor (i.e. the repairer); and (ii) LH Teo (i.e. the Plaintiff’s surveyor), as shown in the extract below by way of example:[note: 9]

10     The SJE’s total recommended repair cost was $6,980.07.[note: 10] The SJE then applied a 20% discount to this amount to arrive at what the SJE referred to as the “recommended Lump Sum Repair Cost” of $5,600 (rounded up to the nearest hundred).[note: 11] The SJE did not explain why a 20% discount was applied.

11     After setting out the “recommended Lump Sum Repair Cost”, the SJE listed the documents he was provided with in preparing the SJE Report.[note: 12] They were:

(a)     The Defendant’s accident report and police report with colour photographs.

(b)     The Plaintiff’s accident report and police report with colour photographs.

(c)     A survey report with 37 colour photographs from LH Teo.

(d)     The final repair bill from Albert Motor.

12     The SJE then stated as follows in his report:[note: 13]

Based on the survey report of [the Vehicle] from [LH Teo], no resurvey was conducted when/or during the repairs was carried [sic] and after the motorcycle has completed its repair. Therefore, what are the parts that is being repaired or replaced, the surveyor had no knowledge at all. [Form Team] conducted a post repair survey immediately after the bike completed its repair and the exhaust system was found to be reused. No photographs was [sic] substantiated by LH Teo to show the headlamp was cut.

13     The SJE Report also included five photographs of the Vehicle (“Photographs”), purportedly showing the Vehicle “[a]fter [r]epair”.[note: 14] Four of the Photographs were close-up shots on the exhaust system of the Vehicle. However, the SJE made no express reference to the Photographs in his report and it was not immediately apparent on the face of the SJE Report:

(a)     who took the Photographs;

(b)     when these Photographs were taken; and

(c)     the relevance of the Photographs to the SJE’s opinion.

14     None of the parties sought any clarification on the SJE Report and the SJE issued no supplementary / clarificatory report.

The SJE’s evidence

15     Three aspects of the SJE’s evidence at the AD Proceedings should be highlighted.

16     First, the SJE clarified the reason why the repair cost he recommended was significantly lower than that of LH Teo and the amount charged by Albert Motor – this was because he (i.e. the SJE) recommended zero repair costs for parts of the Vehicle which were, according to the SJE, “[d]amaged and not replaced”:[note: 15]

Q:    Pg 2 – 4 of your report – what LH Teo submitted and you dismissed. Are you saying these are not damaged, or damaged but no need to replace or not replaced?

A:     Damaged and not replaced. Has not been replaced and my surveyor found the old parts were fitted back.

Q:     For all these items submitted by LH Teo and dismissed by you?

A:     Yes.

[Emphasis added]

17     In other words, despite acknowledging that numerous parts of the Vehicle were in fact damaged and required repair (or replacement), the SJE did not provide for any repair cost for these parts because (according to the SJE) Albert Motor did not in fact repair or replace those parts.

18     A list of all the parts of the Vehicle which “LH Teo submitted” and “dismissed” by the SJE because they were “damaged and not replaced” is set out at Annex A.[note: 16] I shall refer to them collectively as the “Disputed Items”.

19     The second aspect of the SJE’s evidence worth noting was how he arrived at the conclusion that the Disputed Items were “not replaced”.

20     SJE did not personally inspect the Vehicle at any time.[note: 17] Instead, the SJE concluded that the Disputed Items were not replaced based on photographs of the Vehicle taken by one “Lim” (full name unknown) which were stored in certain filing system belonging to Form Team and to which the SJE had access. These photographs taken by “Lim” were not adduced in evidence by any of the parties. I set out the relevant parts of the SJE’s evidence on this below:[note: 18]

Q:    For [the Photographs], those were taken by Mr Lim?

A:    Yes. He is my surveyor who conducted a survey before repair and a re-survey immediately after repair on 31 August 2018.

…

Q:    So the sole dispute is that the items were said to be replaced but you found it was not. What’s your basis?

A:     We have the post-survey photographs in our system and we found that these parts were not replaced…

…

Q:    We don’t know who is Lim and we don’t have any report by him. Do you agree?

A:    Yes.

…

Q:    Main issue is you discounted multiple items on basis that they were not replaced. You say you have photographs to show they were not repaired or replaced – please explain to the court how you conclude they were not replaced?

A:     Photographs from Lim were not tendered in my report. I can submit if the court requires me to do so.

…

A:    Actually in my view counsel is trying to be difficult. I already explained that I referred to Lim’s photographs and I said that the photographs are not in the report. I can tender all the photographs. They are not in this SJE report.

…

Q:      Because we don’t have the photos, we cannot tell if these items were replaced or not.

A:      Yes.

[Emphasis added]

21     The final aspect of the SJE’s evidence worth highlighting is that in the course of being cross-examined by the Plaintiff’s lawyer, the SJE suggested, on his own initiative and for the first time in these proceedings, that the Vehicle was in fact a constructive total loss:[note: 19]

Q:    So the sole dispute is that the items were said to be replaced but you found it was not. What’s your basis?

A:    We have the post-survey photographs in our system and we found that these parts were not replaced. Before preparing this report, we found market value of this bike is $9,500 and the COE rebate value is $4500. So net loss is $5,000. So taking into consideration the Plaintiff’s surveyor’s recommended $14,400 is more than the market value. Our recommendation is more reasonable and already exceed net loss.

Q:    So according to you net loss was $5,000 and you used this figure to justify your amount?

A:    No. I went through all the parts…

[emphasised added]

22     The issue of the Vehicle being a constructive total loss was never raised by any of the parties, nor by the SJE in the SJE Report, prior to the commencement of the AD Proceedings. Despite that, it appeared that the SJE came fully prepared to elaborate on this issue because the SJE then proceeded to tender a two-page document (the “Market Value Documents”)[note: 20] which comprised:

(a)     (at page 1) copy of an undated screenshot from Sgbikemart.com.sg showing a listing price of $9,700 for a “Yamaha YZF-R15” registered in June 2015; and

(b)     (at page 2) copy of a document titled “Enquire PARF/COE Rebate for Registered Vehicle” showing that the “Total Rebate Amount” on the Vehicle was $4,578 if it was deregistered in May 2018.

23     The SJE neither composed nor made copies of the Market Value Documents.[note: 21] It was unclear on the evidence who did. The SJE’s evidence was that he printed the Market Value Documents from “Lim’s folder” (see above at [20] for context on “Lim’s folder”).[note: 22] Despite the lack of clarity as to their origins and the fact that the Market Value Documents were not previously disclosed by the parties, the Plaintiff’s lawyer informed me at the AD Proceedings that the Plaintiff accepted the authenticity of the Market Value Documents.[note: 23] Based on these documents, the SJE opined that it was “not economical to repair [the Vehicle] if more than $5,000”.[note: 24]

The parties’ positions

24     The Plaintiff sought the following damages in these AD Proceedings:

(a)     $14,400, being the costs of repairing the Vehicle;

(b)     $600, being damages for the Plaintiff’s loss of use of the Vehicle for a total period of 12 days, at the rate of $50 per day.

25     According to the Plaintiff:[note: 25]

The present dispute lies in whether or not the repairer repaired the damaged parts, since the SJE admitted that all the parts that were claimed to be damaged were actually damaged. Hence, the dispute technically is a factual one.

26     On that premise, the Plaintiff put forward various reasons why the SJE’s opinion “should be dismissed or given little weight”,[note: 26] including:

(a)     The SJE did not personally inspect the motorcycle.[note: 27]

(b)     The SJE’s position that the Disputed Items were not repaired was contradicted by factual evidence from the Plaintiff and the repairer, that the motorcycle was properly repaired.[note: 28]

(c)     The SJE “should have requested to physically inspect the motorcycle” and that if he had done so, “he would have been able to conclusively supply proof, for example updated photographs, on whether the disputed items were actually replaced.”[note: 29]

(d)     The SJE “made some unsubstantiated assertions that someone else…conducted a post repair survey” and there was “no concrete proof” that this post-repair survey was in fact conducted.[note: 30]

27     The Defendant, on the other hand, submitted that the Plaintiff “has not proven on a balance of probabilities” that the Disputed Items were “replaced and not reused”.[note: 31] The Defendant further submitted that the photographs referred to at [20] above must have been taken after the repairs were completed (and therefore the SJE was right to conclude that the Disputed Items were not replaced), because:

(a)     it “goes without saying that post-repair photographs are to be taken after the repairs to the Plaintiff’s motorcycle are completed”; [note: 32]

(b)     “[t]he SJE confirmed [Form Team] only attended at [Albert Motor] to take the post-repair photographs after they were informed that the motorcycle had been repaired”;[note: 33] and

(c)     “[t]here is no reason for the SJE or his co-workers to attend at [Albert Motor] before the repairs were completed and they would not know when the repairs were completed unless they were notified”.[note: 34]

28     In addition, the Defendant submitted that the Plaintiff’s claim of “$14,400 is unreasonable and does not properly represent the diminution in value of the chattel” and that “it was not economical to repair the Plaintiff’s motorcycle beyond $5,000” because “[t]he SJE has tendered evidence” to show the “market and rebate values” of the Vehicle, which supported this conclusion.[note: 35] Curiously, the Defendant then concluded that “a sum of $5,600 for costs of repairs is more reasonable”[note: 36] even though this sum also exceeded what the Defendant said was the economic repair value or market value of the Vehicle.

29     As to the Plaintiff’s claim for loss of use, the Defendant submitted that this should be assessed at $20 per day for a period of 6 days. The 6-day period comprised 2 days for “pre-repair loss of use”,[note: 37] and 4 days being the reasonable duration for repair as recommended by the SJE.[note: 38]

Issues

30     Given the way the evidence and arguments developed, the following issues arose for determination in these AD Proceedings:

(a)     First, whether the Defendant was entitled to argue that the Vehicle was a constructive total loss, such that the Plaintiff’s damages was limited to the market value (or cost of replacement) of the Vehicle at the time of the accident (“Issue 1”).

(b)     If Issue 1 was answered in the affirmative, then the court should go on to determine whether the Vehicle was in fact a constructive total loss and if so, what was the market value of the vehicle (“Issue 2”)? If Issue 1 was answered in the negative, then Issue 2 did not arise.

(c)     If Issue 1 was answered in the negative, then the Plaintiff’s damages should be assessed by reference to the diminution in value of the Vehicle, and the court therefore needed to assess what that diminution in value was (“Issue 3”)? If Issues 1 and 2 were answered in the affirmative, then Issue 3 did not arise.

(d)     Fourth, depending on the answers to Issues 1 – 3, the court needed to determine what was the Plaintiff’s damages for loss of use of the Vehicle (“Issue 4”).

31     I set out below a roadmap of the four issues for easy reference.

32     These issues were not structured in the same manner in which counsels organised their respective submissions, as I did not find the latter helpful. I would, however, in the course of addressing these issues, address specific arguments raised by parties.

Issue 1 – Whether the Defendant was entitled to argue that the Vehicle was a constructive total loss

33     The first issue which must be decided was whether the Defendant was even entitled to argue that the Vehicle was a constructive total loss. The was a procedural issue which arose because the Defendant only raised the argument belatedly in his closing submissions after all the evidence had been taken.

34     In my view, Issue 1 must be answered in the negative for the simple reason that it would be unfair to the Plaintiff to allow the Defendant to spring such a surprise at a very late stage of the proceedings. The issue or possibility of the Vehicle being a constructive total loss was raised for the first time by the SJE during the AD Proceedings (see [21] above), and then elaborated upon by the Defendant in his closing submissions. In making those belated submissions, the Defendant did not even proffer any explanation as to:

(a)     why the issue was not raised earlier;

(b)     what notice, if any, the Plaintiff had of the Defendant’s intention to advance such an argument;

(c)     what opportunities, if any, the Plaintiff had to address related factual and legal issues; and

(d)     ultimately, why the Defendant should be allowed to raise this argument at a late stage.

35     As far as the court records showed, the issue was not pleaded, not canvassed in evidence and not raised in the any legal submissions prior to the commencement of the AD Proceedings. The Plaintiff therefore had no notice of the position taken by the Defendant and did not have the opportunity to adduce evidence or address the issue in legal submissions. This was clearly not a case where it could be said that no prejudice would be caused if an unpleaded point was allowed to be taken: see V Nithia (co-administratrix of the estate of Ponnusamy Sivapakiam, deceased) v Buthmanaban s/o Vaithilingam and another [2015] 5 SLR 1422 at [40] and [41], because the argument that the Vehicle was a constructive total loss required evidence of the market value of the Vehicle at the time and place of damage. This was a factual issue which would not otherwise have been addressed by any of the parties. At the barest minimum, the Defendant must have first disclosed the material facts that would support his contention as to the market value of the Vehicle. But even that was not done.

36     As a matter of procedural fairness, therefore, the Defendant could not be allowed to spring a surprise on the Plaintiff at the tail-end of the proceedings.

Issue 2 – Was the Vehicle a constructive total loss?

37     Since Issue 1 was answered in the negative, Issue 2 did not strictly arise. However, even if I was wrong on Issue 1, I would have found that the evidence did not establish that the Vehicle was a constructive total loss.

38     I explained the concept of constructive total loss in Luxx Newhouse Pte Ltd v Big-Foot Logistic Pte Ltd and another [2021] SGMC 72 (“Luxx Newhouse”) at [21] and [22]. It refers to the situation where the cost of repairing a damaged article would greatly exceed the market value of the pre-damaged article, such that while the article is capable of being repaired, it would not be economical to do so, and the claimant’s damages is generally in those circumstances limited to the market value of the article plus consequential losses. This of course assumes that the article has not been destroyed beyond repair – if it had, then it would be an actual (and not constructive) total loss.

39     To determine whether a damaged article has been rendered a constructive total loss, one has to first determine its market value at the time and place of damage: see Luxx Newhouse at [29].

40     In this case, the only “evidence” of the market value of the Vehicle at the time of the accident came from the SJE. This “evidence” consisted of:

(a)     A statement made by the SJE in the course of the AD Proceedings that the “market value of this bike is $9,500 and the COE rebate value is $4500. So net loss is $5,000.” (the “SJE Statement”) (see above at [21]); and

(b)     The Market Value Documents tendered by the SJE (see above at [22]).

41     In my view, neither the SJE Statement nor the Market Value Documents constituted admissible evidence on this issue.

42     At the risk of stating the obvious, the SJE is not a witness of fact. He is an expert witness who gives evidence of his opinion upon a point of specialised knowledge, which, in this case, is an opinion on extent of damage occasioned to the Vehicle and how much it would cost to repair the damaged Vehicle to its pre-damaged state.

43     Because the SJE is not a witness of fact, he could not “prove” primary facts upon which his opinion is based by purporting to give evidence of such facts. That would be pulling oneself up by one’s own bootstraps. It is trite that if an expert relies on out of court assertions as evidence of the facts they refer to, such evidence must be admissible under the Evidence Act 1893 (the “EA”): see Jeffrey Pinsler, SC, Evidence and the Litigation Process (LexisNexis, 5th Ed, 2015) at [8.042]. In other words, the primary facts upon which the expert’s opinion is based must be proved by admissible evidence: see Gema Metal Ceilings (Far East) v Iwatani Techno Construction (M) Sdn Bhd [2000] SGHC 37 (“Gema”) at [74].

44     In this case, the SJE Statement was based on the Market Value Documents. However, the Market Value Documents themselves have not been adduced in evidence by any party and their contents were not proved by any witness. Neither did the Defendant seek to rely on any of the hearsay exceptions under any relevant provision of the EA. Although the Plaintiff’s lawyer took the somewhat surprising position in accepting the authenticity of the Market Value Documents, that was a separate issue as to their admissibility as evidence of the truth of the statements contained in these documents: see Super Group Ltd v Mysore Nagaraja Kartik [2018] SGHC 192 at [52]; Jet Holding Ltd & Ors v Cooper Cameron (Singapore) Pte Ltd & Anor [2005] 4 SLR(R) 417 at [146] and [147]. That (i.e. the contents of the documents) remained unproved.

45     Further, the SJE never claimed to have any expertise in the valuation of motor vehicles. According to the curriculum vitae submitted by the SJE in the course of the AD Proceedings (the “SJE CV”), in his “26 years as an Automobile Appraiser, [he] conduct survey to the damaged vehicles, site Surveys / Inspection, Third Party Re-inspections and paper Re-Surveys. In [his] course of work, [he] check and investigate damages sustained to accident vehicles.” Nowhere in the SJE CV did the SJE profess or claim to possess any expertise in valuation of motor vehicles. The SJE Statement was therefore not an opinion expressed by the expert falling within his field of expertise.

46     Accordingly, I found that the SJE Statement and contents of the Market Value Documents were inadmissible hearsay and/or opinion evidence which I ought to disregard.

47     In any event, even if I was wrong on the admissibility of the SJE Statement and the Market Value Documents, I would still have found that there was insufficient evidence to establish the market value of the Vehicle at the time of the accident. This was because both the SJE Statement and the Market Value Documents were unreliable and, therefore, should be given little weight.

48     As mentioned, the basis of the SJE Statement was the Market Value Documents. However, there was no evidence given (not even by the SJE) as to:

(a)     when the screenshot from Sgbikemart.com.sg referred to at [22(a)] above was taken; and

(b)     whether the model shown in the screenshot, being a “Yamaha YZF-R15”, was a comparable model to the Vehicle, which was a “Yamaha YZF-R1” (underlines added).

49     The date on which the screenshot from Sgbikemart.com.sg was taken was important because in order for the Defendant to succeed in showing that the Vehicle was a constructive total loss, the Defendant would need to show that the market value of the Vehicle at the time and place of the accident was lower than the cost of repair. It was therefore critical to ascertain whether the price shown on the screenshot was the listed price either on the date of the accident or, if not, at least of temporal proximity. Without this information, the screenshot was of little probative value (if any at all) as to the market value of the Vehicle at the time of the accident.

50     On the second issue, the SJE was in fact questioned in the course of the AD Proceedings on the difference between a “Yamaha YZF-R15” and a “Yamaha YZF-R1”. The SJE’s response was:

The number – R1 and R 15 – are different, that may be because some manufacturer just change [sic] the number. But the brand and model are the same.

[emphasis added]

51     It was entirely unclear what the SJE meant by “some manufacturer just change the number”. Did he mean that the brand and model were the same but certain specifications or fittings were different? If that was so, one would have expected the market value of vehicles with different specifications or fitting to be different, in which case the listed price for a “Yamaha YZF-R15” would not be a good proxy for the market value of the Vehicle. Alternatively, did the SJE mean that there was no difference between a “Yamaha YZF-R15” and a “Yamaha YZF-R1” and that “some manufacturer just change the number” fortuitously and for no particular reason? Evidently, the SJE was not sure. As far as the evidence in the AD Proceedings showed, no one could be.

52     In those circumstances, I was of the view that neither the SJE Statement nor the Market Value Documents could be safely relied upon by me to arrive at a finding of the market value of the Vehicle at the time of the accident. Accordingly, even if the Defendant was allowed to make the belated submission that the Vehicle was a construction total loss, I would have found that there was no admissible or reliable evidence on the market value of the Vehicle for me to arrive at such a finding.

Issue 3: What was the diminution in value of the Vehicle?

53     Since Issue 1 was answered in the negative and there was in any event insufficient evidence to support a finding that the Vehicle was in fact a constructive total loss, the Plaintiff’s loss was to be assessed by reference to the diminution in value of the Vehicle: see Lock Lay Chin v Kwan Kheng Ein Joseph [2022] SGMC 64 (“Lock Lay Chin”) at [21].

54     The amount by which the value of the chattel is diminished is usually evidenced by, and often equated with, the cost to repair the chattel to its pre-damaged state: see Luxx Newhouse at [22]. Such repair costs must however be reasonable, as otherwise it would not serve as useful evidence of the diminution in value: Lock Lay Chin at [21]. It is in this context that the opinion of an expert who has the experience, knowledge and proficiency in determining how to repair a damaged motor vehicle and how much such repair work would typically cost can be useful.

55     In this case, the Plaintiff’s evidence was that Albert Motor charged him $14,400 to repair the Vehicle.[note: 39] That constituted one form of evidence of the diminution in value of the Vehicle. As against this, the Defendant relied on the SJE’s opinion to support his counterargument that the repair costs of $14,400 was too high, and that the “appropriate award for costs of repairs [sic]” should be $5,600, as recommended by the SJE.[note: 40]

56     The problem, however, was that it became evident during the course of the AD Proceedings (see [16] and [17] above) that the SJE did not provide an opinion on how much it would cost to repair the Vehicle to its pre-damaged state. Rather, the SJE’s estimate of $5,600 was premised on how much it would reasonably cost to repair the Vehicle to the alleged post-repair state (i.e. without repairing or replacing the Disputed Items). As a result, parties’ attention was mostly diverted to addressing “whether or not the repairer repaired the [Disputed Items]”.[note: 41] This was problematic and ultimately unhelpful.

57     It was common ground that the Disputed Items were in fact damaged and there was no issue raised on causation of these damages. As such and in a case like this, it was simply immaterial “whether or not the repairer repaired the [Disputed Items]”.[note: 42] If a chattel was damaged, direct loss would have been suffered in the form of its diminution in value, and damages was recoverable regardless of whether the chattel was in fact repaired: see Waterdance Limited v Kingston Marine Services Limited [2014] EWHC 224 at [18]. There is no obligation on the claimant’s part to effect any repair before he can recover damages for diminution in value: see Coles v Hetherton [2015] 1 WLR 160 at [27]. Should the claimant be content to live with a damaged chattel and use the monies recovered for an overseas holiday instead, it would be entirely his/her prerogative to do so, and would not detract from the fact that the value of the chattel was reduced and the claimant suffered damage as a result.

58     For completeness, it may not always be the case that the claimant’s intention to effect repair is irrelevant. The existence and reasonableness of such intention may have to be investigated where the reasonable cost of repair would greatly exceed the diminution in value (i.e. where the chattel is a constructive total loss), and where the claimant is seeking to recover the higher cost of repair: see JSD Corp Pte Ltd v Tri-Line Express Pte Ltd [2022] SGHC 227 (“JSD Corp”) at [86]. In those circumstances, the claimant, in order to claim for the higher cost of repair, must first show that those costs have been incurred or that he intends to incur such costs: see JSD Corp at [87]. That is because in those situations, the reasonable cost of repair is no longer functioning as a proxy for diminution in value, but as a head of damage on its own. It could therefore no longer be assumed that the damage being claimed (i.e. the cost of repair) was suffered the moment physical damage to the chattel occurred.

59     However, for the reasons explained at [33] to [52] above, this was not a case where it could be said that the cost of repair greatly exceeded the diminution in value. Accordingly, parties were barking up the wrong tree in seeking to address whether the Disputed Items were in fact repaired or replaced. That was an irrelevant issue in this case.

60     What the SJE should have done was to provide an opinion on how much it would cost to repair the Vehicle assuming that the Disputed Items were or would be repaired or replaced. That would assist the court in determining what would be the reasonable cost to repair the Vehicle to its pre-damaged state. The SJE’s decision to make no provision for the cost for repairing the Disputed Items despite his acknowledgement that the Disputed Items were in fact damaged showed either that the SJE did not properly understand the issue on which his opinion was required, or that the parties did not frame the right issue for the SJE’s consideration. As the Instructions given to the SJE was not disclosed, I would not know which was the true state of affairs.

61     Further, even if the parties considered it important to investigate into “whether or not the repairer repaired the [Disputed Items]”,[note: 43] the parties should have adduced evidence from or cross-examined the relevant factual witness on this factual issue, instead of seeking to elicit such evidence from the SJE. The best person to be examined on this issue was the representative from Albert Motor (i.e. the repairer) (“PW2”) who gave evidence at the AD Proceedings that the “repairs were carried out according to [LH Teo]’s recommendations” at the cost of $14,400.[note: 44] However, PW2 was not cross-examined at all on whether the Disputed Items were in fact repaired or replaced. Instead, both parties submissions on this entirely factual issue were premised largely on the SJE’s evidence which were in turn based on alleged photographs of the Vehicle that no one in these AD proceedings sighted apart from the SJE (see [19] and [20] above). The evidential difficulties and the hearsay nature of the SJE’s evidence on this issue were patently obvious (see [42] and [43] above) yet not correctly addressed by any of the parties in their closing submissions.

62     For these reasons, I could not accept the SJE’s opinion that the reasonable repair costs for the Disputed Items was zero because such opinion was either illogical (if the SJE was seeking to provide an opinion on the reasonable cost to repair the Vehicle to its pre-damaged state) or irrelevant (if the SJE was seeking to provide an opinion on the reasonable cost of repair to the Vehicle to its alleged actual post-repair state). Insofar as the SJE was seeking to give evidence on whether the Disputed Items were in fact repaired or replaced, such evidence was inadmissible for the same reasons given at [42] and [43] above. Even though the SJE was the only expert in this case, I did not consider myself bound to accept his evidence. Just as a court is not obliged to accept uncontroverted expert evidence: see Saeng-Un Udom v Public Prosecutor [2001] 2 SLR(R) 1 at [26], a single joint expert’s evidence likewise should not be accepted if it was not logical or relevant: see Armstrong, Carol Ann (executrix of the estate of Peter Traynor, deceased, and on behalf of the dependents of Peter Traynor, deceased) v Quest Laboratories Pte Ltd and another and other appeals [2020] 1 SLR 133 at [90]; Gema at [74].

63     Without the SJE’s opinion, I was left with little assistance as to the reasonable cost of repair or replacement for the Disputed Items. The only other evidence before me was evidence from PW2 (corroborated by the Plaintiff’s own evidence) that the Plaintiff was in fact issued an invoice of $14,400 for the repair works based on the recommendations of LH Teo (which included costs for the repair and replacement of the Disputed Items). In this regard, I also noted that apart from the Disputed Items, the SJE’s recommended cost of repair did not differ in any material way from LH Teo’s recommendations, save for the following items:

(a)     Exhaust Pipe Complete (Front) ($100 difference)

(b)     Fork repair and alignment ($100 difference)

(c)     Workmanship ($250 difference)

64     Accordingly, I assessed the diminution in the value of the Plaintiff’s Vehicle at $14,200. I arrived at this sum by taking the Plaintiff’s actual cost of repair of $14,400, less the sum of $200 being the difference identified at [63(a)] and [63(b)] above. I did not take into account the $250 difference for costs of workmanship between the SJE’s estimate and the actual amount charged by Albert Motor because based on the SJE’s evidence given at the AD Proceedings, I was of the view that the reasonable inference was that the SJE had provided a lower estimate for the costs of workmanship because he was not accounting for the costs of workmanship to repair or replace the Disputed Items. Since I have, for the reasons explained, decided that it was wrong for the SJE to proceed on such premise, I declined to accept this aspect of the SJE’s evidence.

65     For completeness, I should also add that I was cognisant that the SJE had applied a 20% discount to his total recommended repair cost in order to arrive at what the SJE referred to as the “recommended Lump Sum Repair Cost” of $5,600 (see [10] above).[note: 45] However, since the SJE did not explain the reason for applying this 20% discount, I did not apply a similar discount to the $200 deductions referred to at [64] above.

Issue 4 – What was the Plaintiff’s damages for loss of use of the Vehicle

66     For the same reasons given above, I could not accept the SJE’s evidence on the reasonable duration of repair because the SJE’s opinion was predicated upon how long it should take to repair the Vehicle without repairing the Disputed Items. To be clear, the SJE was not cross-examined specifically on this by any of the parties, but looking at the SJE Report as a whole I found that the natural and reasonable inference was that that was the basis upon which the SJE arrived at an estimated 4-day duration for the repair works.

67     Again, as the SJE’s opinion was of limited assistance and the court had no expertise in determining how long it would reasonably take to repair the Vehicle to its pre-damaged state. The only evidence I was left with on this issue were:

(a)     from the Plaintiff, that the Vehicle was left with Albert Motor for repairs for a period of 10 days;[note: 46] and

(b)     from PW2, that Vehicle was brought to Albert Motor on 27 April 2018[note: 47] and that the motorcycle was left at the workshop for repair for 10 days.[note: 48]

68     These evidence showed that the Plaintiff was in fact deprived of the use of his motorcycle for a period of 10 days for the purposes of allowing repairs to be effected, and there was no other evidence which suggested that this period was unreasonable. Accordingly, I accepted the Plaintiff’s evidence that he had lost the use of the Vehicle for a period of 10 days as a result of the accident and should be compensated for such loss.

69     As for the applicable daily rate, the State Courts Benchmark Rates for Loss of Use of motorcycles is between $20 to $50 per day. The Plaintiff positioned his claim at the highest end at $50 per day, but provided no explanation as to why this was appropriate. The Plaintiff did not address this issue at all in his closing submissions. Accordingly, I took the lower end of the range and assessed the Plaintiff’s loss of use at $250 (being $25 x 10 days). I did not take the lowest end of $20 per day as there was sparing reference in the Plaintiff’s evidence that the motorcycle is a “high-end” “superbike”[note: 49], which the Defendant did not challenge or rebut.

Conclusion

70     In total, at 100%, the Plaintiff’s damages was assessed at $14,450 (i.e. $14,200 for diminution in value and $250 for loss of use). At 90% (i.e. the agreed apportionment), the amount payable by the Defendant to the Plaintiff was $13,005. I also ordered that interest should run on the sum $13,005 from the date of the writ to the date of judgment at the rate of 5.33% per annum. Further, having heard the parties on costs, I ordered that the costs of the proceedings be paid by the Defendant to the Plaintiff fixed at $6,000 plus GST, and that the Plaintiff’s reasonable disbursements be paid by the Defendant with the quantum to be taxed if not agreed.
