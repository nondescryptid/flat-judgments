(IV)   APPLICABLE LAW

5       There are three key elements to prove offences of cheating under section 420 of the Penal Code[note: 2]:

(a)     The victim must be deceived[note: 3]. In other words, deception must have been practised on the victim.

(b)     There must have been an inducement that caused the victim to deliver any property (e.g. money) to any person. There is no requirement that this inducement must necessarily be oral, although in most cases that would usually be the case. There is also no need for this inducement to be express, as compared to it being inferred. Furthermore, there is no need for the inducement to be the sole or even main reason for the delivery of property by the deceived party; so long as the deceiving party’s deception played some part in inducing the victim to deliver some property, this element of the offence would be satisfied.[note: 4]

(c)     There must be a dishonest or fraudulent intention on the part of the deceiving person to induce the victim to deliver the property (i.e. to cheat). Dishonesty is defined under section 24 of the Penal Code as an intention to cause wrongful gain to one person, or wrongful loss to another person[note: 5]. Wrongful gain or wrongful loss as defined under section 23 of the Penal Code is based on legal entitlement[note: 6]. This constitutes the mens rea for the offence.

(V)   SUMMARY OF THE PROSECUTION CASE

6       The prosecution case, as summarised in its submissions, is that the AP had deceived PW1 Tam, and PW13 Soh and her friends by making similar false representations, thereby dishonestly inducing delivery of various sums of money by way of cash and cheques for the alleged purpose of investment.

7       The AP deceived PW1 Tam[note: 7] and PW13 Soh[note: 8] by making similar representations about fictitious investment schemes of providing PRC visitors to Singapore with funds in Singapore Dollars (“SGD”) for their expenditure, particularly for gambling in the local casino(s)[note: 9]. She had enticed them to “invest” with her by purporting to offer attractive returns. For PW1 Tam, the AP primarily convinced him by returning him the first “investment” sum of SGD$100,000 with 10% interest in a very short period, and then using her personal details to encash the subsequent SGD$200,000 cheque. For PW13 Soh, the AP convinced her and her friends by offering specific percentage returns in a written agreement, and then offering higher percentage returns to entice them to “invest” more money.

8       The AP’s representations were false because the alleged investment schemes simply did not exist. The AP had no evidence of any business[note: 10] or employment[note: 11]. This was further corroborated by her bank documents[note: 12] and her ACRA screenings[note: 13]. At trial, the AP admitted that she had no evidence to prove the existence of the alleged investment schemes[note: 14]. She also had no explanation why she failed to provide any information about her alleged clients on whose behalf she claimed to have gambled for, both in her statements and at trial[note: 15]. In fact, the AP’s defences that she had allegedly taken loans from PW1 Tam and collected monies on behalf of PW13 Soh implicitly conceded that she had no investment schemes as she claimed[note: 16]. Consequently, the AP had knowingly made false representations about such investment schemes to PW1 Tam, PW13 Soh and their associates or friends.

9       The AP had thereby dishonestly induced PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s friends (through PW13 Soh) to deliver property in the form of money. PW1 Tam was dishonestly induced by the AP to deliver a total of SGD$1,426,100 to the AP by way of cash and cheques, from himself and his family members and friends. PW13 Soh’s friends were dishonestly induced by the AP (through PW13 Soh) to deliver a total of SGD$734,000 to the AP by way of cash[note: 17].

(VI)   CLOSE OF PROSECUTION CASE

10     At the close of the prosecution case, I was satisfied that there was some evidence not inherently incredible which satisfied each and every element of the 5 proceeded charges preferred against the AP. Accordingly, I administered the standard allocution to the AP who elected to give evidence in her own defence and to call one Tan Dai Keng (DW1) as her witness. However, the defence subsequently decided not to call DW1 as a witness.

(VII)   DISPUTED FACTS ARISING FROM THE TRIAL

11     Based on the submissions of the parties and taking into account the Statement of Agreed Facts and the evidence adduced at the trial in relation to the 5 charges preferred against the AP, the disputed facts were as follows:

(a)     Whether the AP had applied deception on the victims by making false representations about alleged investment schemes offering attractive returns to PW1 Tam, and PW13 Soh and her friends. The prosecution case is that the AP had made representations about an alleged currency exchange scheme involving PRC visitors/clients to induce them to hand over various sums of money to her for “investment”. The defence case is that the AP had never made such representations to PW1 Tam, PW13 Soh and her friends or otherwise asked them to invest with her, and disputed in relation to PW1 Tam that the document in P7 is conclusive evidence that monies were extended for the purpose of investment and that there was deception involved in the extension of the monies.

(b)     Whether the AP had dishonestly induced PW1 Tam (whether directly or indirectly through his associates) and PW13 Soh’s friends to deliver money totalling a sum of SGD$2,160,100 to her. The prosecution case is that the AP had used the false representations stated above to dishonestly induce PW1 Tam, and PW13 Soh and her friends (through PW13 Soh) to deliver various sums of money amounting to a total of SGD$2,160,100 to her by way of cash and cheques, which they would not have done if the alleged investment schemes did not exist. The defence case is that (i) the AP had obtained a sum of at least SGD$300,000 from PW1 Tam pursuant to a loan agreement with 10% interest payable, and (ii) PW13 Soh had dishonestly induced her friends to deliver the entire sum of SGD$734,000 to herself, not the AP.

(c)     Whether the AP possessed a dishonest or fraudulent intention and kept the sums of money that were handed over to her. The prosecution case is that the AP had intended to cause wrongful loss to PW1 Tam, and PW13 Soh and her friends and wrongful gain to herself by spending the sum of SGD$2,160,100 on her personal expenses despite knowing that she was not legally entitled to do so, with at least some of it used to fund her gambling in the casinos. The defence case is that (i) the AP had received at least SGD$300,000 from PW1 Tam on three or four occasions, and she had returned the entire sum of money that she took from PW1 Tam with 10% interest (i.e. PW1 Tam had recouped his capital sum with profit), and (ii) PW13 Soh had personally kept the entire sum of SGD$734,000 given by her friends and the AP was merely used as a cover story.

(d)     Whether the amount that the AP cheated PW1 Tam of was SGD $1,426,100 as charged, or SGD $300,000 (with SGD $330,000 returned) as the AP had claimed.

(e)     Whether the AP or PW13 Soh was the criminal mastermind, and consequently whether the undisputed amount of SGD $734,000 was delivered to the AP, or to PW13 Soh by PW13 Soh’s friends.

(VIII)   CREDIBILITY OF THE PROSECUTION WITNESSES

12     A judge can make a finding on the credibility of a witness based on some or all of the following: (i) His demeanour. (ii) The internal consistency (or lack thereof) in the content of his evidence. (iii) The external consistency (or lack thereof) between the content of his evidence and extrinsic evidence (for example, the evidence of other witnesses, documentary evidence or exhibits).[note: 18]

13     Having heard and observed the demeanour of all the prosecution witnesses and the AP, I found that in its entirety, the evidence of all the prosecution witnesses was inherently credible and they were all forthright and truthful witnesses when giving evidence in Court. Their demeanour displayed in Court clearly supports the conclusion that they were witnesses of impeccable veracity. Besides being internally and externally consistent with the extrinsic evidence in their testimonies, they had either not been rigorously cross-examined or had withstood cross examination by the defence which in any case did not mount any concrete challenge to the credibility of all the prosecution witnesses in its submissions. All the material evidence of the prosecution witnesses relevant to prove the present charges preferred against the AP remained unshakened during their cross-examination. Hence, I accepted the evidence of all the prosecution witnesses insofar as they were material to prove the present charges preferred against the AP. I deal with the evidence of the key prosecution witnesses below.

PW1 TAM’S EVIDENCE

14     I agreed with the prosecution that PW1 Tam was a credible and truthful witness for the following reasons as submitted by the prosecution:

(a)     PW1 Tam’s testimony was internally consistent as follows:

(i)       At trial, without reference to the exhibits, PW1 Tam estimated his total loss to be about SGD$1.4 million[note: 19]. He testified that he had been dishonestly induced by the AP into handing over money for two schemes: a Chinese scheme involving someone based in Beijing (“main China scheme”), and then subsequently a “short-term” scheme involving a Malaysian national/Singaporean PR[note: 20] (“short-term scheme”). The proposal of the short-term scheme was raised by the AP after she alleged that the money was frozen in the account in China[note: 21], claiming that it was a similar scheme that promised quicker returns[note: 22]. He also testified that most of the money was in the main China scheme, while he invested a smaller amount in the short-term scheme. He was also unequivocal that the purpose was for investment, not a loan[note: 23].

(ii)       PW1 Tam’s evidence was internally consistent under cross-examination. When it was put to PW1 Tam that he had continued to give to the AP funds despite not receiving repayment because he was her “partner” and not because of any deception, PW1 Tam unequivocally disagreed. Instead, he explained that he continued to extend funds to her because she claimed that the prior funds were frozen in the bank and she offered him the short term scheme. Even though it was suggested to PW1 Tam repeatedly that the money he extended was for a different purpose as a business partner, he was very clear that it was a result of the AP’s deception in alleging a scheme of providing funds to gamblers in the casino.

(iii)       PW1 Tam also consistently explained that he had persisted in giving money to the AP because he trusted her[note: 24]. In fact, this was why he failed to keep any formal record of his transactions with her[note: 25]. Even after he did not receive the returns as promised and felt things were suspicious, he still decided to trust that she would return the funds[note: 26], but chose to record the phone conversations as some form of evidence because the AP was evading him[note: 27]. Subsequently, he only decided to go the police almost a year later when he was convinced by reports of others being cheated by the AP.[note: 28]

(iv)       PW1 Tam’s evidence at trial was also internally consistent with his prior versions of events. First, contrary to the defence submission, PW1 Tam’s evidence at trial was internally consistent with his initial recount to the police in his First Information Report (Exhibit P1). There, he similarly recounted being deceived by two schemes: first, “an overseas investment in China” and then “another investment opportunity, whereby payouts and profits are distributed on a daily basis”, although the “type of investment was similar”. Additionally, he also recounted that the catalyst for the second scheme offering quicker returns was the AP’s claim that funds were “frozen in a bank in China”. Finally, the AP’s estimate of the total sum was approximately SGD$1.24 million, which is close enough to the estimate of SGD$1.4 million and eventual amount in the charge (SGD$1,426,100). The estimated sum of SGD$800,000 attributed to the main China scheme and the lesser estimated sum of SGD$400,000 attributed to the short-term scheme was also internally consistent with his memory of the proportions of money he “invested”. As PW1 Tam had testified in Court, he had not performed precise calculations of the total sum at the time of Exhibit P1, and hence his roughly consistent recount in fact goes to show his internal consistency even based on estimation.

(v)       PW1 Tam’s evidence at trial was also internally consistent with his own recount to the AP over the phone conversations, which were translated and transcribed in Exhibit P5. In those calls, he makes most references to the “China side”[note: 29], but also makes some references to the “Malaysia” side[note: 30]. This is internally consistent with his understanding that most of the money was in the main China scheme. His enquiry about “lift(ing) the freeze”[note: 31] in China is also internally consistent with his understanding that his money was being frozen in an account in China. Finally, his recount of the sum being “more than 1,200,000”[note: 32] and “SGD 1,230,000”[note: 33] is roughly consistent with his estimate at trial. Moreover, given the lack of precise calculation at the time, it would be more accurate to compare his estimate to that given in Exhibit P1, which is very similar (SGD 1,240,000). PW1 Tam was also very clear that the money was owed to him by making constant references to it and seeking explanations from the AP[note: 34], which is internally consistent with him being dishonestly induced to believe that he was investing with the AP.

(b)     PW1 Tam’s testimony was externally consistent with the exhibits and oral testimony of the other prosecution witnesses as follows:

(i)       PW1 Tam’s testimony was externally consistent with the prosecution exhibits. PW1 Tam’s testimony about the estimated SGD$1.4 million that he handed over to the AP was corroborated by the comprehensive documentary record (Exhibit P7[note: 35]) of the sums of money that he and his family members and friends had handed to the AP for the purpose of investment[note: 36]. He had prepared this document by cross-referencing his personal contemporaneous records with objective bank records32. In turn, Exhibit P7 was corroborated by PW1 Tam’s bank statement[note: 37].and reproductions of cheques issued by him and his family members[note: 38]. Not only were the sums correctly recorded, PW1 Tam’s records of particulars like the cheque numbers, the dates, and the origins of the respective sums were accurate when compared with the objective records. Notably, he had prepared Exhibit P7 without reference to most of the reproductions of the cheques that were subsequently obtained from the respective banks, suggesting that PW1 Tam had in fact kept an accurate personal record. Consequently, this lends great credibility to Exhibit P7, which in turn confers reliability on the total sum of SGD$1,426,100 from Exhibit P7 that was extended to the AP for investment.

(ii)       PW1 Tam’s evidence was also corroborated by the AP’s version in the phone conversations transcribed in Exhibit P5. The AP referred to both the “China side”[note: 39] and other money dealings in “Malaysia”[note: 40], which corresponded to PW1 Tam’s recounts of the two schemes. The AP also repeatedly raised and discussed the issue of chasing and receiving money or payment, including repayment by instalments[note: 41]. The AP’s continued engagement with the PW1 Tam over these monetary issues and the ostensible use of the collective pronoun (“we”[note: 42], “us”[note: 43], “our”[note: 44]) corroborated PW1 Tam’s evidence that he had handed the money to the AP for the purpose of investment. The AP also acknowledged and did not dispute the sums of money when highlighted by PW1 Tam (1,230,000, more than 1,200,000), which constituted implicit endorsement of the sum involved. This is further corroborated by the discussion of repayment in significant instalments of “tens of thousands of dollars, 100,000 or 80,000”[note: 45]. The AP’s clear understanding and engagement with PW1 Tam’s reference to the “seizure”[note: 46] and “(judicial) freeze”[note: 47] in China corroborates his evidence that she had claimed that his money had been frozen in an account in China, which she used as an excuse to dishonestly induce him to hand over more money. In this regard, I rejected the defence submission on the need for the prosecution to produce evidence of any bank account belonging to the AP in China since this was merely the AP’s claim which she was unable to prove.

(iii)       PW1 Tam’s evidence was also corroborated by the oral testimonies of no less than eight family members and friends[note: 48]. All eight witnesses had no motive to lie. None of PW1 Tam’s family members and friends contradicted his evidence. While some of them knew slightly more about the AP’s representations and intended purpose of the monies, most of them simply extended money to PW1 Tam as a personal loan requested by him out of goodwill and trust. This was consistent with PW1 Tam’s evidence that he did not necessarily share about the scheme when asking for money, but instead sought personal loans for which he bore personal responsibility. For most of them, they displayed independent personal recollection (e.g. in relation to the sums of money given and/or mode of transfer to PW1 Tam) without the aid of the documentary exhibits. Additionally, for those who knew more about the situation like PW3 Tam Fei Fong and PW4 Tam Fei Wen, their recounts of meeting the AP to chase for repayment was externally corroborated by Exhibit P45 (note).

(c)     PW1 Tam was an honest and forthright witness in his demeanour. He readily answered questions to the best of his knowledge in a direct manner. Under cross-examination, the defence counsel appeared to sense that PW1 Tam was being defensive or guarded, or even evasive, and requested him to be direct in his answers[note: 49]. However, PW1 Tam explained that his cautious demeanour arose from his appreciation of the seriousness of the occasion in Court and he felt “responsible” for “every sentence”[note: 50].

(d)     PW1 Tam also answered questions with clarity and candour under cross-examination, He responded clearly to the defence counsel’s frequent and tricky compound questions[note: 51]. Where he did not understand frankly confusing or unfair questions by defence counsel, he candidly sought clarification without hesitation or speculation[note: 52]. He was unfazed and steady in correcting the record despite defence counsel’s misquotation of the evidence or attempts to insinuate inconsistency[note: 53]. Where he could not remember specific details, he candidly admitted to the limits of his recollection without embellishment or speculation[note: 54]. Nonetheless, he was not evasive and tried his best to assist in offering possible helpful answers[note: 55].

(e)     PW1 Tam also provided a consistent and credible explanation for his lack of any official record for his transactions with the AP. It was put to him in cross-examination on multiple occasions that if he were telling the truth, he should have kept records, especially since he was a businessman[note: 56]. Fundamentally, PW1 Tam consistently explained that he did not see a need to have “black and white” records because he “trusted” the AP[note: 57]. In response to the defence counsel’s suggestion that his version was illogical, he explained the basis for his trust. First, his trust was cultivated after he received 10% returns from the AP as she had promised on the first loan of SGD$100,000[note: 58]. Second, his trust was further cultivated when the AP dared to use her identity to encash his cheque of SGD$200,000[note: 59]. Third, he was aware that she had some familial roots in Singapore[note: 60], which suggested she would not run away. Fourth, he never thought that the AP would harm or cheat him given their good personal relationship[note: 61]. His trust was not only genuine, but it was also justified and rational. Ultimately, his trust was also not completely blind; like any rational human being, he still kept some informal personal records[note: 62] (that later was relied upon to create Exhibit P7).

(f)     The defence submissions do not explicitly address PW1 Tam’s credibility, perhaps due to its misguided focus on Exhibit P7. On the balance of arguments, PW1 Tam’s credibility remains largely intact. Nonetheless, as submitted by the prosecution, there were several isolated claims in the defence submissions that appear to cast doubt on PW1 Tam’s credibility that need to be addressed for completeness as follows:

(i)       The defence claim that PW1 Tam’s evidence that he had obtained the three police report numbers from WeChat was an “untrue statement” is completely speculative[note: 63]. PW1 Tam had clearly testified that he had obtained the report numbers from unknown individuals who had uploaded their reports into Wechat[note: 64]. The assertion that it was untrue because he was “unlikely to have access to all three persons who made the report” is irrelevant because PW1 Tam testified that he did not know these individuals, and it was perfectly plausible that he had gotten them anonymously on a chat group.

(ii)       Doubt appeared to be cast on the reliability of Exhibit P7 because of PW1 Tam’s failure to provide the informal personal records that he relied upon to prepare Exhibit P7[note: 65], and a claim that the reliance on Exhibit P7 as a “truthful document” was somehow “one sided”[note: 66] However, the fact that PW1 Tam had kept contemporaneous personal records made Exhibit P7 more reliable, and he could explain that he reproduced and tidied the records into Exhibit P7[note: 67]. There was no identifiable inconsistency between Exhibit P7 and the objective bank records[note: 68], and no specific allegation of inaccuracy was made by the defence beyond speculative scepticism. Hence, Exhibit P7 should be taken as reliable and further evidence supporting PW1 Tam’s credibility.

(iii)       The defence appeared to suggest that PW1 Tam was untruthful in failing to disclose that “some of the monies had been refunded” as it was a “fact” that some of the monies were paid direct to the so-called contributors”[note: 69] This point was never put to PW1 Tam in cross-examination, so it is unclear what lack of admission the defence was referring to. In any case, the fact that some of the witnesses (e.g. PW7 Tang Qing Feng and PW9 Matthew Chan) had received some or all of their monies back is not inconsistent with PW1 Tam’s evidence that he did not receive any refunds[note: 70]. Instead, they were clear in their evidence that the repayment was from PW1 Tam’s sister[note: 71], and not him. Hence, it is perfectly consistent that PW1 Tam did not receive any repayments or refunds from the AP, while his sisters used their personal funds to help him.

(g)     For all the above reasons, I found PW1 Tam to be a credible and truthful witness.

PW13 SOH’S EVIDENCE

15     I also agreed with the prosecution that PW13 Soh was a credible and truthful witness for the following reasons as submitted by the prosecution:

(a)     PW13 Soh’s testimony was internally consistent as follows:

(i)       At trial, without reference to the exhibits, PW13 Soh estimated the total amount that her friends had believed to have invested with the AP was SGD$800,000, although the actual amount handed over was lesser (“700 plus thousand”[note: 72]) because there was deduction of some initial returns[note: 73]. PW13 Soh was very clear that these sums of money were handed over as investments, and not loans[note: 74]. She testified that the alleged investment scheme offered by the AP was about providing money for Chinese nationals to spend in Singapore[note: 75]. In return, the AP would offer attractive returns in the range of 5 to 9%[note: 76]. The AP had also offered more attractive returns to encourage her friends to invest more money[note: 77]. Her friends’ transactions with the AP in her presence were all done in cash at the AP’s request[note: 78]. She also testified that these transactions were sometime in November and December 2019[note: 79].

(ii)       Contrary to the defence submissions, PW13 Soh’s testimony at trial was internally consistent with her previous recount offered in her police report (Exhibit P2). She had reported that she was introduced to the AP to “invest in businesses that she (knew)”, and she gave her SGD$900,000 for this purpose. The purpose of investment into businesses or schemes that were offered by the AP is internally consistent with her evidence at trial. The stated sum of SGD$900,000 is also consistent, because as PW13 Soh clarified at trial, she had mentally included an initial SGD$100,000 (to allegedly help her to deal with her husband and his mistress) that she had given the AP to the stated total of SGD$800,000[note: 80]. Additionally, PW13 Soh’s recount that the AP had paid back “a total of $71,000 in investment returns” was generally consistent with the fact that there were initial returns that the AP offered, which brought the actual sum handed over to “700 plus thousand” dollars[note: 81]. Furthermore, the stated returns of “around 10% per month” was consistent with the range of 5 to 9% cited at trial, and also consistent with the cited range of 8-10% in Exhibit P6. The reported time frame of November till December 2019 was also consistent with her evidence at trial, as well as her claim in Exhibit P2 that the returns (deductions) were paid “over a period of 1 month”.

(iii)       PW13 Soh’s testimony was internally consistent on the point that she had “trusted” the AP and referred her friends out of goodwill[note: 82]. In fact, she only referred her long time friends because she trusted the AP, and in turn she knew that they would trust her[note: 83]. In her mind, the referral was a win-win opportunity[note: 84].

(iv)       PW13 Soh could also explain the rational basis for her trust. First, she associated her trust for one ‘Esther’ with the AP[note: 85]. It was the AP who had cited Esther’s participation in the alleged investment scheme to cultivate her trust[note: 86]. Nonetheless, she was also clear that it was eventually the AP who made the representations to her about the alleged investment scheme[note: 87], not Esther. Second, she was also convinced to trust the AP because she met ‘Big Brother Yi’ (‘Yi Da Ge’), whom the AP had introduced as her friend[note: 88]. She had witnessed them transacting[note: 89] and the AP represented that the transactions were part of her business that she asked PW13 Soh to join[note: 90]. Third, she also placed her trust in their personal relationship and belief in the AP’s good intentions[note: 91]. The AP had cultivated PW13 Soh’s trust by purporting to be her friend and helping her with her personal matters, which made the AP feel trustworthy[note: 92]. In fact, PW13 Soh reposed trust in her friendship with the AP thinking that she would not cause her harm, especially as the AP became aware of her personal difficulties after undergoing the divorce[note: 93]. Finally, she also trusted that the AP would not abscond as her daughter was schooling in Singapore[note: 94].

(v)       PW13 Soh had also explained that it was her trust that led her to act her in the way that she did. First, the AP convinced her to transact in cash because she told PW13 Soh that they did not want anything to be seen by the bank[note: 95]. Despite PW13 Soh’s preference to issue cheques, she trusted the AP and transacted in cash instead[note: 96]. Second, it was this trust in the AP that led PW13 Soh to agree to interpose herself between the AP and PW13’s friends to be “responsible” for the debt. She only agreed to take responsibility for sums of money despite her lack of personal benefit because she trusted the AP[note: 97], and her subsequent assumption of this responsibility when things went wrong reflected her retrospective recognition of her trust as the basis[note: 98].

(b)     PW13 Soh’s testimony was externally consistent with the exhibits and the oral testimonies of her three friends as follows:

(i)       PW13 Soh testified that her friends had handed over sums of money in cash to the AP during physical meetups at her house for the purpose of an alleged investment scheme proposed by the AP that offered attractive returns. This was corroborated by several exhibits and the independent oral testimonies of three of her friends, PW17 Marilyn, PW19 Lim and PW20 Tan.

(ii)       PW13 Soh’s testimony that the AP was the one who proposed the alleged investment scheme was corroborated by Exhibit P6. It was undisputed that Exhibit P6 showed a screenshot from PW13 Soh’s phone showing some Whatsapp messages and some missed calls from the AP[note: 99]. The messages proposed a “project” offering “8 to 10 growth percentage points”, and the AP was asking PW13 Soh if she was interested to join her because she was “short of two partners for collaboration”[note: 100]. PW13 Soh testified that this was the original unsolicited offer from the AP[note: 101]. Even after PW13 Soh ignored the initial messages, the AP was persistent. She “kept contacting” her and “kept mentioning” this investment scheme to her, and cited Esther’s involvement[note: 102]. When PW13 Soh said she had no money to invest, the AP asked her to look for others[note: 103], eventually prompting her to refer her long time friends who were looking for avenues for investment[note: 104]. The date of the message (7 October 2019) also predates all the transactions in November to December 2019, which further corroborated PW13 Soh’s version.

(iii)       PW13 Soh’s testimony that the AP was the one perpetrating the deception in relation to the alleged investment schemes is also corroborated by the agreement documents (Exhibits P34 to P38). In every one of the agreement documents, the AP’s name and signature corresponded to ‘Party A’, who was the party “in charge of the project operation and sourcing of customers”. By contrast, PW13 Soh or her friends would sign as ‘Party B’, who was the party contributing the capital. This corroborated PW13 Soh’s description of the arrangement, and she verified this understanding in the evidence at trial.

(iv)       As PW13 Soh had testified, the AP was the one who prepared these agreement documents[note: 105], read and explained the terms to her friends[note: 106] and subsequently received the cash from her friends[note: 107]. PW13 Soh was merely a witness and did not participate in the transactions[note: 108], except to sign on behalf of two of her friends[note: 109] (Lydia and PW20 Tan). In fact, PW13 Soh recalled that the AP had explained that, since she was the one doing the investment, she and her friends were not to interfere or question her about its operation or returns[note: 110]. Exhibits P37 and P38 further corroborate PW13 Soh’s claim that the AP had offered more attractive returns in exchange for more investment, as PW17 Marilyn’s rate of return increased from 6% to 7% after she invested an additional SGD$100,000 on top of her original SGD$100,000[note: 111]. According to PW13 Soh, this was the similar situation with how PW20 Tan received the eventual returns rate of 9% for SGD$300,000, with a lower rate of 7% offered for her initial investment of SGD$200,000[note: 112].

(v)       PW13 Soh’s testimony was corroborated by the oral testimonies of her three friends. They could physically identify the AP in Court[note: 113], corroborating their claims that they had met her physically to hand over cash as investment. All three friends repeatedly testified that they had trusted her[note: 114], and that she had assumed responsibility for the transactions as the referrer as she claimed due to her trust in the AP[note: 115]. Nonetheless, they all confirmed that they were dishonestly induced by the AP to hand over the various of sums of money for the purpose of alleged investment, and not for any other purpose[note: 116]. They all had no doubt that the AP was the one with whom they were investing with[note: 117], and consequently to whom they handed over the respective sums of cash witnessed by PW13 Soh[note: 118]. In fact, they offered rather specific recollections of the AP handling the money. PW19 Lim recounted the AP taking out the cash from the envelope and counting it[note: 119] - she had a distinct memory because the AP counted “very fast” that made PW19 Lim worried that she might have counted wrongly[note: 120]. Similarly, PW20 Tan testified that the AP had counted the money, and then placed it in her red bag – she had provided a picture (Exhibit P53) that she took as corroborative evidence[note: 121]. In fact, her memory was distinct enough that she could correct the defence counsel’s incorrect identification of an ‘envelope’ in the picture, which was in fact a piece of paper used for calculations[note: 122].

(vi)       The oral testimonies of PW13 Soh’s three friends are also highly reliable as they were honest and forthcoming witnesses. They had no reason to lie in Court. In fact, PW19 Lim and PW20 Tan were prominent media personalities who could have done without the risk of media exposure. Nonetheless, they came forward to testify to tell the truth of what happened to the best of their knowledge. It was evident from their testimonies that they did not know the full extent of the alleged investment schemes but reposed their trust in PW13 Soh. They displayed their credibility in not embellishing details and simply providing factual recounts of their understandings and observations in their respective dealings with PW13 Soh and the AP.

(vii)       The oral testimonies of PW13 Soh’s three friends were also corroborated by the exhibits. PW17 Sim’s and PW19 Lim’s testimonies that they had signed agreement documents were corroborated by Exhibits P37[note: 123] and P38[note: 124], and P36[note: 125], respectively. PW20 Tan’s testimony that PW13 Soh had signed the agreement document on her behalf was also corroborated by Exhibit P35[note: 126]. The templated consistency of Exhibits P34 to P38 and the linguistic similarity of the agreement documents with Exhibit P6 further corroborated their claim that the AP was the one who prepared and produced these agreement documents.

(XI)   AP HAS NO VALID DEFENCE

18     I found that the AP has no valid defence to the 5 charges preferred against her. I agreed with the prosecution that the AP’s different defences respectively to PW1 Tam’s charge and PW13 Soh’s friends’ charges are inherently incredible, and the only logical conclusion to be drawn is that these defences were so inconsistent and unbelievable as they were fabricated and were afterthoughts to explain the incriminating evidence against the AP for the reasons as advocated by the prosecution as follows:

(A) In respect of PW1 Tam’s charge:

(a)       The AP claimed that she had borrowed at least SGD$300,000 from PW1 Tam on a maximum of four occasions and that she had repaid with 10% interest. At trial, the AP conceded that PW1 Tam had personally “handed over…money” to her[note: 174]. However, instead of the sum of SGD$1,426,100 on at least 24 occasions that she had been charged with, the AP claimed that she received a total of at least SGD$300,000[note: 175] on three or four occasions[note: 176]. While the AP claimed that there may have been more loans, the sum can only reliably be taken as SGD$300,000 as she could only identify three cheques (Exhibits P10, P18 and P22). Furthermore, instead of dishonestly inducing PW1 Tam to hand over the sum of SGD$300,000 by deception, the AP claimed that she had taken the various sums of money from PW1 Tam as loans[note: 177]. In fact, the AP claimed to have returned the entire sum of alleged loans from PW1 Tam with 10% interest.[note: 178] In other words, the AP alleged that PW1 Tam earned a profit of SGD$30,000 from the loan arrangements[note: 179].

(b)       The AP went further to claim that PW1 Tam also took loans from her. There was one other cheque (Exhibit P11) for SGD$18,600 issued by PW1 Tam that was encashed by the AP’s husband, Mr Tan Dai Keng. When confronted with this objective evidence, the AP claimed that this was repayment for a loan that PW1 Tam had taken from her due to cash flow issues[note: 180]. According to her version, PW1 Tam had asked for this loan “2 to 3 days”[note: 181] after she met him to return the loan of SGD$200,000 with 10% interest (i.e. SGD$220,000)[note: 182], which was 2 weeks after the loan was extended[note: 183]. She also claimed that PW1 Tam had taken further loans from her of at least SGD$2,300 to pay fines that he did not repay[note: 184].

(c)       I agreed with the prosecution that the AP’s claim that PW1 Tam was an unreasonable moneylender who fabricated an outrageous cheating allegation against the AP over a dispute of SGD$5,000 is simply unbelievable. In essence, the AP’s version is that PW1 Tam was an unreasonable moneylender[note: 185] who made a false police report against her. According to the AP, PW1 Tam had made a profit of at least SGD$30,000 in interest payments from extending loans to her and benefited from goodwill loans of SGD$2,300 that he did not repay. Yet, the AP alleged that PW1 Tam fell out with her over a dispute over her failure to pay him SGD$5,000[note: 186] and suggested that this had motivated him to make an alleged false police report[note: 187]. Quite apart from the relatively small sum involved, the AP claimed that this SGD$5,000 was a result of a sudden and unexplained demand of 20% interest for a loan of SGD$50,000[note: 188], in breach of their existing agreement of 10% interest that the AP had been faithfully abiding by[note: 189].

(d)       The AP had admitted that the “only dispute”[note: 190] that she could think of to justify why PW1 Tam might make a false police report was her refusal to pay his unreasonable demand of SGD$5,000. Regardless of any other possible reasons, she pointed to this as the “eruption point”[note: 191]. The only logical conclusion that can be drawn, as submitted by the prosecution, is that this was a fabricated afterthought that would have been entirely rejected by PW1 Tam if it had been put to him.

(g)       I agreed with the prosecution that the AP’s claim that she had received a lesser sum of monies as loans from PW1 Tam was an unsubstantiated and self-serving fabrication. (XII)   NO MERIT IN THE DEFENCE SUBMISSIONS

19     After considering the submissions of both parties, I found no merit in and rejected the defence submissions in their entirety for the reasons as submitted by the prosecution as follows:

ISSUE OF DECEPTION

(a)     On the issue of the factual element of deception, taking the AP’s case at its highest, it not only concedes but relies on the fact that the alleged representations were made. The AP’s own version was that she had “told”[note: 327] PW1 Tam about and “introduce(d)” him to her “business”[note: 328], which involved the “service”[note: 329] of providing capital to “China friends or customers”[note: 330] (which she herself claimed was interchangeable[note: 331]) which she “state(d)”[note: 332] was for the purpose of their “gambling” at the “casino”[note: 333] and/or to “buy” things[note: 334], and she would receive alleged “service fees”[note: 335] or “profit”[note: 336] in exchange. The quoted terms in bold were the AP’s own words.

(b)     Similarly, the AP claimed that she had shared with PW13 Soh about the fact that she was a “businessperson”[note: 337], who allegedly “serve(d) (her) China friends to come to Singapore for a holiday”[note: 338] that included to “gamble in the casino, or…spend on expenses”[note: 339], which was the very basis that she claimed PW13 Soh used her as a cover story to allegedly deceive her friends[note: 340]. In fact, even the defence submissions also conceded that the AP did make representations about her alleged business project involving PRC nationals alleged by PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh[note: 341].

(c)     Under cross-examination, the AP’s defence was thus exposed as an attempt to fabricate an account incorporating the substance of false representations that she had in fact made to PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh, but asserting technical distinction to dispute the charge. When confronted, she could not meaningfully distinguish her alleged representations from the representations recounted by PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh[note: 342], and then resorted to blatant evasiveness in refusing to answer [note: 343].

(d)     The AP’s lack of credibility was so apparent that learned defence counsel felt a need to interrupt the prosecution’s cross-examination to intervene on her behalf[note: 344]. Ironically, this only served to clarify on the record the artificial and illusionary line that the AP was futilely trying to draw (e.g. willingness to admit she had made representations about “business”, “service”, and even “profit”, but selectively rejecting the language of “investment”in the charges[note: 345]). The AP’s attempt at non-existent technical distinction in the face of overwhelming substantive similarity of the representations must be rejected as self-serving. To further illustrate this point, the near-identical substance of the AP’s evidence to PW1 Tam’s testimony by disregarding the self-serving terminology of “loans” and “interest” is exemplified in the AP’s cross-examination in the following excerpts from the NE:

Q: …you said these loans were for your China friends, those who come to spend or gamble in the casino, and then you earn service fees, right?

A:    Yes, correct.

Q:    And it is because of these service fees that you pay 10% interest to Tam Kwong Yoeng?

A:     Correct [note: 346] . (emphasis added)

(e)     Consequently, the evidence at trial (and submissions arising from that evidence by both parties) discloses no apparent dispute over the reality that the substantive representations constituting the subject of the deception in the charges were made. Hence, the only dispute remaining is whether the representations were true. On this point, the defence effectively conceded that the representations were untrue, perhaps in a misguided focus on trying to draw illusory distinctions. The prosecution had adduced positive evidence to show that the AP’s representations were false given that the evidence showed her lack of any business, employment or even source of funds[note: 347]. The prosecution clearly put the lack of evidence to the AP in cross-examination[note: 348] and explicitly sought any reasonable explanation[note: 349] and/or positive evidence[note: 350] In response, the AP herself conceded that she came to Singapore “not…to do a business”[note: 351] and “not to work”[note: 352]. She also conceded that she lacked any “evidence” or “proof”[note: 353] It must be emphasized that any hint of an excuse that she gave for not producing evidence was unsubstantiated and plain illogical[note: 354]. For completeness, she even clarified that it was “not that (she) lost (the evidence)” – she just had “nothing”[note: 355].

(f)     Taking the prosecution unrefuted positive evidence and the AP’s complete concessions when confronted by the prosecution case in cross-examination together, the defence had clear notice that they needed to provide evidence to substantiate the existence of any “business” or “investment” that the AP had claimed. At the very least, an explanation would be expected. On the contrary, the defence was silent. Not a single piece of rebuttal evidence was provided. The issue was completely omitted in re-examination. When this issue was finally confronted in the defence submissions, the resort to utter speculation revealed their concession[note: 356]. Ironically, the defence submissions even inadvertently helped to prove the prosecution case by arguing in favour of the falsehood of the AP’s representations[note: 357].

(g)     Accordingly, the evidence adduced at the trial proves beyond a reasonable doubt that there was deception applied by the AP on PW1 Tam, and PW13 Soh and her friends in the form of false representations.

(h)     Given that there is no dispute on the factual element of deception, the AP must have been dishonest. Dishonesty is defined under section 24 of the Penal Code as an intention to cause wrongful gain to one person (i.e. the Accused) or wrongful loss to another person[note: 358]. It is indisputable that the truth of the AP’s representations (i.e. whether such a business or investment in fact existed) would be entirely within her personal knowledge. As shown above, the AP had effectively admitted to victims’ recount of her representations and subsequently failed to provide any evidence or explanation to substantiate the alleged truth of these representations despite being made painfully aware of the need to do so. Hence, the only logical conclusion, as submitted by the prosecution, is that the AP had knowingly and intentionally made false representations to the victims. Consequently, assuming that the said false representations had induced the victims to hand over monies to the AP (directly or indirectly), the AP’s intention to cause wrongful gain to herself and/or wrongful loss to them could be inferred, as there was in fact no business as she had claimed.

PW1 TAM’S CASE AGAINST THE AP

(a)     The evidence clearly shows that there was inducement applied by the AP on PW1 Tam to deliver monies as a result of her deception (i.e. false representations). In fact, the AP effectively admitted to the inducement in her own evidence. PW1 Tam consistently and clearly testified that he was induced by the AP’s representations about alleged investment schemes involving PRC customers to hand over monies[note: 359]. He repeated that the AP had placed a clear emphasis that it was primarily for gambling funds, which convinced him because of the intuitively reasonable prospect of profit derived from winnings that she played on[note: 360]. Critically, PW1 Tam’s recount of the representations that induced him to hand over monies was undisputed by the defence. He displayed credibility and believability by being able to explain the basis for his inducement. He detailed a chronological recount of the representations relating to the two schemes to illustrate his state of mind[note: 361], particularly the AP’s claim of “frozen” accounts to propose the “short term” scheme that achieved the dual purpose of stalling the outstanding sum while extracting further sum of money. To the extent that the defence makes suggestions about certain gaps or inability to recall in his recount, particularly on non-material points, I have rejected them as “adequate allowance must be accorded to the human fallibility in retention and recollection”[note: 362], especially when a significant period of time has lapsed (at least 5 years in the present case.

(b)     PW1 Tam’s evidence of inducement is undisputed for three reasons as follows:

(i)       At trial, PW1 Tam’s evidence of inducement was unchallenged under cross-examination. It was never put to PW1 Tam that his recount of the representations was false or incorrect, or that he was not induced by them. On the contrary, the case put to PW1 Tam by the defence appeared to implicitly concede the fact of inducement by false representations[note: 363], and only disputed the amount of money[note: 364].

(ii)       PW1 Tam’s evidence of inducement was similarly uncontroverted by the AP’s own evidence at trial. The AP’s own recount of the representations was at best a more vague and general version of PW1 Tam’s recount. Notably, the AP admitted to the causal link between her representations about her alleged ‘business’ and returns in relation to PW1 Tam’s delivery of money[note: 365], which corroborates PW1 Tam’s evidence of inducement. Furthermore, the AP’s own claim that PW1 Tam wanted to join or participate in her alleged ‘business’[note: 366] further corroborated PW1 Tam’s extent of inducement that he was not satisfied with only handing over monies, but also wanting to be directly involved.

(iii)       PW1 Tam’s evidence of inducement was not directly confronted in the defence submissions. Prima facie, the defence summary of PW1 Tam’s evidence[note: 367] is erroneous. Given the lack of citations from the Notes of Evidence, the basis of the submissions are unclear. In any case, PW1 Tam did not testify that there was “a loan which carried an interest or commission” – he had clearly testified that the monies were all handed over for the purpose of the business or investment.

(c)     The only tangential allusion to this issue of inducement in the defence argument was that PW1 Tam’s evidence allegedly “defies logic”[note: 368]. The suggestion appears to be that PW1 Tam could not logically have been induced by the AP’s representations to hand over such a large sum of monies consisting of at least 24 transfers over the course of about 6 months, especially since he claimed that the AP had not given any returns as promised. As submitted by the prosecution, PW1 Tam’s evidence was, on the contrary, entirely logical and internally consistent for the following reasons:

(i)       The defence has effectively made an argument against its own case and did nothing to knock the internal consistency or logic of PW1 Tam’s evidence. The defence submissions clearly premised its analysis on the assumption that the monies extended by PW1 Tam to the AP were for the purpose of “loans” (see also “lending money” and “owing money”)[note: 369]. The claim that the monies extended by PW1 Tam were “loans” was made by the AP, not PW1 Tam. In fact, he adamantly resisted even a hint of such a characterization[note: 370]. Consequently, following the defence argument, PW1 Tam’s behaviour “defies logic” according to the AP’s version of events. PW1 Tam’s evidence makes no sense in the context of extending loans to the AP, but it makes perfect sense in the context of being deceived into extending monies for the purpose of investment. Not only did the defence argument fail to attack the prosecution’s case, but it also effectively scored an own goal.

(ii)       To dispense with this issue of internal consistency conclusively, the evidence unequivocally rebuts the strongest version of what the defence argument could have been. The defence argument would only have any force in challenging the “logic” of PW1 Tam’s behaviour if they had provided analysis of the lack of internal consistency based on PW1 Tam’s own account (i.e. investment). Accordingly, what the defence argument arguably should have been was that it did not make sense that PW1 Tam would hand over such a large sum of money over a course of several months if his evidence of inducement were true (i.e. he was induced to hand over monies for investment, and not loans). However, the evidence shows that PW1 Tam’s claim of his inducement by investment is logical as it is both internally and externally consistent.

(iii)       The AP’s cultivation of PW1 Tam’s trust and subsequent manipulation explained his behaviour. First, PW1 Tam’s trust was cultivated by the AP[note: 371]. He was internally consistent across his EIC and cross-examination in explaining and defending his basis for trust in the AP based on the initial transactions[note: 372]. PW1 Tam’s behaviour could be explained by the AP’s manipulation. She had employed manipulation strategies of creating artificial urgency (“urgently”[note: 373], “hurriedly”[note: 374], “quickly”, “chase”[note: 375]) and scarcity (competition with others)[note: 376] in demanding further sums of money from PW1 Tam. This explained the catalyst for the PW1 Tam’s frequent and regular delivery of monies despite lack of returns. This also explained the undocumented cash transactions[note: 377] as she often claimed she needed the funds in cash urgently and had no time to encash cheques[note: 378]. This also explained why PW1 Tam was so pressed for funds that he took loans from his friends and family members to give to the AP for the purpose of investment that he believed he would eventually recoup.

(d)     PW1 Tam’s evidence on inducement is also logical from the perspective of external consistency. First, the testimonies of his eight family members and friends corroborated the fact that PW1 Tam was dishonestly induced to hand over monies for the purpose of investment, and not loans. In fact, this can be seen from the defence respective summaries of their evidence in the defence submissions. Fundamentally, all of them testified that they handed over monies to PW1 Tam as personal loans. This corroborated PW1 Tam’s testimony about taking personal loans for the purpose of investment. There was no mention of them investing in any moneylending business which the AP alleged PW1 Tam was running; in fact, when asked about it, they denied any knowledge of such moneylending business[note: 379]. For some who knew slightly more like his sisters or closer friends, they clearly recounted their understanding that PW1 Tam introduced the AP as his “business partner”[note: 380] with whom he was doing “investment”[note: 381], which corroborated PW1 Tam’s evidence that he was induced to handed monies over for investment, not as loans. In fact, five of the eight witnesses testified that they had personally met the AP[note: 382], with their accounts being mutually[note: 383] and externally corroborated[note: 384]. CASINO RECORDS USED TO SHOW TRACING

(a)     The AP’s casino records were used by the prosecution to show tracing (i.e. Exhibits P39 to P42, P50 to P51). These exhibits showed that about 63% of the total amount in PW1 Tam’s charge (i.e. SGD $897,820 out of SGD $1,426,100) and about 75% of the total amount in PW13 Soh’s charges (SGD $548,000 out of SGD $734,000) were traceable to casino deposits[note: 456]. The tracing exercise is reliable not just because of the proximity of time and amounts, but also because of the lack of any verifiable source of income or funds to explain the deposits[note: 457] (i.e. lack of alternative sources of inflow), and the undisputed evidence that significant funds had been squandered in gameplay[note: 458] (i.e. onward tracing to account for funds). The defence baseless and speculative assertions to try to discredit the incriminating evidence were pointless and rejected by this court for the following reasons, as submitted by the prosecution:

(i)       The defence claims that the fact that the sums of money obtained by the AP from PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s friends could be traced into her casino accounts was “merely coincidence” and “the money could have been derived from other sources”[note: 459]. This is wholly unsatisfactory. If it were indeed a coincidence, there must be other sources of funds to account for these deposits. Given the AP’s own admission of her lack of such funds, and the objective lack of any evidence to show otherwise, the allegation of a “coincidence” based solely on the speculative assertion of the mere possibility of other funds is clearly self-serving and unreliable.

(ii)       The defence seems to claim that the casino records are “not conclusive proof” given that the similarity in dates (and amounts) is “merely circumstantial”. The defence submission that the records are not conclusive proof because they constitute circumstantial evidence is legally incorrect. A trial judge can convict an accused person wholly on circumstantial evidence if the cumulative effect of all the evidence leads to the irresistible conclusion that an accused had committed the crime, subject to any reasonably possible explanation.[note: 460] The casino records are corroborative evidence to substantiate the direct evidence of PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh which were corroborated by 53 exhibits and 18 other witnesses. However, the strength of the casino records as circumstantial evidence is undeniable given the significant traceable sums and the complete lack of any reasonably possible explanation proffered by the AP, much less any evidence offered in support. Consequently, the irresistible conclusion is that the AP had committed the cheating offences and provides compelling evidence that the AP had taken SGD $1,426,100 from PW1 Tam (as opposed to SGD $300,000 as she claimed), and had kept the sum of SGD $734,000 from PW13 Soh’s friends (as opposed to PW13 Soh keeping it as she claimed).

(iii)       The defence suggested some prejudice to the AP by virtue of the casino records allegedly on being shown to her at trial, suggesting that there was “no way one can verify the contents”. It was also suggested that the originality of the records was not verified. This argument should be summarily rejected since it is untrue that the AP was not shown the records previously, as PW18 IO Sky had clearly testified that he had confronted her with the gameplay losses in Exhibit P49, which she had signed to acknowledge. Even if the AP did not have a chance to verify every single entry in the records, her gambling activity would be something clearly in her personal knowledge. Even more so, any alternative source of funds to account for the transactions would also be a matter of personal knowledge. Presumably, that was how the AP managed to offer a response that she was helping others to gamble, in addition to her own funds, in Exhibit P49. However, the fact that the AP provided absolutely no evidence or even the slightest bit of verifiable information shows that this was simply another of her fabrications. Ultimately, regardless of what happened before, the records were placed completely before the AP at trial and she was directly confronted with the tracing of the cheating proceeds into her casino accounts. If she had any evidence or witness to prove otherwise, she would have provided it at trial. Yet, despite being unequivocally asked for an explanation, she simply attributed it to a “coincidence”.

(b)     Accordingly, I agreed with the prosecution submission that the evidence provided by the casino records, while circumstantial in nature, is highly probative in further corroborating the victims’ credible testimonies on the disputed issues, and conclusively rebutting the AP’s versions as well as showing her to be a thoroughly disingenuous and unreliable witness who should not be believed at all.

(XIII)   AMENDMENT OF THE CHARGE IN DAC 905203/2021

20     Section 128 of the CPC empowers the court to alter a charge in substitution for the existing charge at any time before judgment is given, and the amended charge must be read to the AP to enter her plea and to state whether she is ready to be tried on this amended charge under section 129 of the CPC. In the exercise of my power under section 128 of the CPC, the prosecution agreed with this Court’s proposal to amend the charge in DAC 905203/2021[note: 461] in order to state the delivery of property from PW13 Soh’s friend, Lydia instead of from PW13 Soh. For consistency with the rest of PW13 Soh’s friends’ charges, the amended charge (with the Court’s amendments indicated in bold below) stated as follows:

“”…you, sometime between November 2019 and December 2019, in Singapore, did cheat one Lydia Gloriana Seow Li Chen (“Lydia”), by making representations through one Soh Choon Heong (“Soh”), to wit, by deceiving Lydia into believing that you had a fictitious investment scheme offering projected monthly returns of 5% to 9% on the capital sum, a fact that you knew to be false, and by such manner of deception, you dishonestly induced Lydia to deliver property, to wit, a total of S$95,000/- in cash, to you, which Lydia would not have done had she not been so deceived, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 420 of the Penal Code (Cap. 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

21     Pursuant to section 129 of the CPC, the above amended charge in DAC 905203/2021 was read to AP on 4 January 2023 before the delivery of my verdict and she claimed trial to it. I accepted the prosecution submission that based on the evidence adduced at the trial, the prosecution has proved the above amended charge beyond a reasonable doubt for the following reasons as submitted by the prosecution [note: 462]:

(a)     Even though Lydia did not testify, PW13 Soh had clearly testified that the money originated from Lydia, explaining the calculation of SGD $95,000 (first month deduction of 5% from SGD $100,000), her signing of Exhibit P34 on Lydia’s behalf and the receipt of the sum of SGD $95,000 in cash by the AP. This evidence of the origin and sum of the money was undisputed by the defence in PW13 Soh’s cross-examination and consequently, the aforesaid evidence should be accepted by this court.

(b)     In the AP’s own evidence, the AP never disputed (i) the total sum of SGD $734,000[note: 463], and (ii) the fact that monies originated from PW13’s four friends (whom she could name, identify and distinguish Lydia[note: 464]). Hence, the evidence showed the source and sum of money from Lydia who was involved by virtue of PW13 Soh’s unrebutted testimonial evidence and corroborated by the AP’s own admission, both of whom were present (i.e. mutually corroborative testimony of two eye-witnesses).

(c)     In fact, the AP never once disputed the origin or sum of money from Lydia specifically, but simply adopted a holistic defence that the entire sum of monies was taken by PW13 Soh as part of her deception on her friends by using the AP’s name. Hence, the only doubt raised by the AP in relation to the sum of SGD $95,000 from Lydia (which is the same as all the other sums of monies from PW13 Soh’s friends) is that it was PW13 Soh who sought and received these monies. The AP has raised no other doubt, reasonable or otherwise, in relation to the sum of SGD $95,000.

(d)     By virtue of adopting a generic defence, and the AP’s unequivocal admission to the underlying facts (i.e. the total sum and the number and identity of friends, including Lydia), the charge is proven beyond a reasonable doubt as I have found earlier that the AP’s defence is wholly unbelievable based on (i) the inherent incredibleness, (ii) internal inconsistency, and (iii) contradiction by extrinsic evidence. Moreover, the credibility of PW13 Soh’s evidence is starkly contrasted to the completely unreliability of the AP’s evidence, and hence PW13 Soh’s evidence should be preferred. The defence had made no attempt to save, or made arguments in support of, the AP’s credibility despite it being thoroughly damaged in cross-examination, which can only be taken as an implicit concession of her unbelievability. Naturally, this means that PW13 Soh’s evidence is to be preferred over the AP’s evidence.

(XIV)   CONCLUSION FOR THE TRIAL

22     Having applied my mind to all the evidence presented by both parties, carefully sifted and reasoned through the evidence and in the light of my above determinations on the issues arising from this trial, I found the AP has no defence with no merit in the defence submissions which failed to throw any reasonable doubt in the prosecution case, and I was satisfied that a finding of the AP’s guilt was grounded entirely in logic and fact. After careful consideration and weighing of the merits of each party’s arguments made in their respective submissions, my findings of facts in relation to the 5 charges (including the above amended charge in DAC 905203/2021) preferred against the AP are as follows:

(a)     The AP had clearly applied deception on the victims by making false representations about alleged investment schemes offering attractive returns to PW1 Tam, and PW13 Soh and her friends. The AP had made representations about an alleged currency exchange scheme involving PRC visitors/clients to induce them to hand over various sums of money to her for “investment”. In this regard, I rejected the defence case that the AP had never made such representations to PW1 Tam, PW13 Soh and her friends or otherwise asked them to invest with her.

(b)     I rejected the defence submission that the monies were extended for the purpose of investment with no deception involved as I found that PW1 Tam’s testimony about the estimated SGD$1.4 million that he handed over to the AP was corroborated by the comprehensive documentary record in Exhibit P7 of the sums of money that he and his family members and friends had handed to the AP for the purpose of investment. PW1 Tam had prepared this document by cross-referencing his personal contemporaneous records with objective bank records. In turn, Exhibit P7 was corroborated by PW1 Tam’s bank statement[note: 465].and reproductions of cheques issued by him and his family members[note: 466]. Not only were the sums correctly recorded, PW1 Tam’s records of particulars like the cheque numbers, the dates, and the origins of the respective sums were accurate when compared with the objective records. Notably, he had prepared Exhibit P7 without reference to most of the reproductions of the cheques that were subsequently obtained from the respective banks, suggesting that PW1 Tam had in fact kept an accurate personal record. Consequently, this lends great credibility to Exhibit P7, which in turn confers reliability on the total sum of SGD$1,426,100 from Exhibit P7 that was extended to the AP for investment.

(c)     The AP had dishonestly induced PW1 Tam, PW13 Soh and her friends to deliver money totalling a sum of SGD$2,160,100 to her. The AP had used false representations to dishonestly induce PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s friends (through PW13 Soh) to deliver various sums of money amounting to a total of SGD$2,160,100 to her by way of cash and cheques, which they would not have done if the alleged investment schemes did not exist. I rejected the defence case that the AP had obtained a sum of at least SGD$300,000 from PW1 Tam pursuant to a loan agreement with 10% interest payable, and that PW13 Soh had dishonestly induced her friends to deliver the entire sum of SGD$734,000 to herself and not to the AP.

(d)     The AP possessed a dishonest or fraudulent intention and kept the sums of money that were handed over to her in that the AP had intended to cause wrongful loss to PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s friends and wrongful gain to herself by spending the sum of SGD$2,160,100 on her personal expenses despite knowing that she was not legally entitled to do so, with at least some of it used to fund her gambling in the casinos. I rejected the defence case that the AP had received at least SGD$300,000 from PW1 Tam on three or four occasions, and she had returned the entire sum of money that she took from PW1 Tam with 10% interest (i.e. PW1 Tam had recouped his capital sum with profit), and that PW13 Soh had personally kept the entire sum of SGD$734,000 given by her friends and the AP was merely used as a cover story.

(e)     The amount that the AP cheated PW1 Tam of was SGD $1,426,100 as charged and not SGD $300,000 (with SGD $330,000 returned) as the AP had claimed. PW1 Tam’s testimony was internally consistent and provided corroboration by the weight of extrinsic evidence in the form of eight (8) witnesses’ testimonies[note: 467] and thirty-nine (39) exhibits[note: 468]. The defence submissions left PW1 Tam’s credibility and consistency effectively intact.

(f)     The AP and not PW13 Soh was the criminal mastermind and the undisputed amount of SGD $734,000 was delivered to the AP and not to PW13 Soh by PW13 Soh’s friends. PW13 Soh’s testimony was internally consistent and provided corroboration by the weight of extrinsic evidence in the form of three (3) prosecution witnesses’ testimonies[note: 469] and twenty-seven (27) exhibits[note: 470]. The defence submissions left PW13 Soh’s credibility and consistency effectively intact.

23     By contrast, the defence submissions were founded on the AP’s internally inconsistent and completely unbelievable testimony, which is contradicted by the weight of extrinsic evidence. The defence submissions effectively gave up on the AP’s credibility and consistency. On this point, the evidence of the casino records proves critical for both set of charges. For PW1 Tam’s charge, there was irrefutable evidence of deposits of SGD $897,820 into the AP’s casino accounts corresponding to the period of the charge (i.e. August 2016 to January 2017). For PW13 Soh’s friends’ charges, there was irrefutable evidence of deposits of SGD $548,000 into the AP’s casino accounts corresponding to the period of the charge (i.e. November 2019 to December 2019). The AP’s failure to provide any verifiable explanation or information for the source of funds, or in the case of PW1 Tam’s charge, any evidence in support of the sum of SGD $300,000 (and the alleged returns with interest) is the final nail in the coffin. Taken together, the evidence adduced at the trial proved beyond a reasonable doubt that the AP had cheated the victims collectively of SGD $2,160,100 in respect of the 5 charges preferred against the AP, including the above amended charge in DAC 905203/2021.

24     I agreed with the prosecution that the AP is a con artist who has caused significant harm to the victims in the present case. She deceived them with false representations and thereby dishonestly induced them to hand over to her the total sum of SGD$2,160,100, which she enjoyed for her personal use and appeared to have squandered away in casinos. Not only was she unremorseful, but she also then wasted 12 days of all the parties’ time to uncover her blatant lies. It was clear to me that the AP has no defence and that the prosecution has proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt in respect of the 5 charges (including the above amended charge in DAC 905203/2021) which have been preferred against the AP.

25     Accordingly, I found the AP guilty and convicted her of the aforesaid charges.

SENTENCE FOR DAC-928505-2020 INVOLVING PW1 TAM

26     I accepted the prosecution submission for a sentence of 7 years’ imprisonment to be imposed on the AP for DAC-928505-2020 involving PW1 Tam based on the egregious circumstances of the offence and grounds as submitted by the prosecution as follows:

(a)     The harm caused by the AP based on the sheer quantum of loss is severe. About S$1,426,100 was cheated of the victim on at least 24 occasions over a period of about 6 months. No restitution was made.

(b)     The AP’s culpability is high given the following aggravating factors:

(i)       The AP was the sole perpetrator and beneficiary of her deception. She conceived of the entire plan, executed it on her own, and entirely enjoyed the benefits that arose from her criminality.

(ii)       The AP’s deception was planned and premeditated. The fact that offences are committed with a high degree of planning and premeditation as opposed to spontaneity constitutes an aggravating factor.[note: 471] The AP had re-initiated contact with PW1 Tam for the purpose of perpetrating the deception, capitalizing on his vulnerable situation given the decline of his businesses (oil and gas industry). The AP had also planned an initial façade of trustworthiness to cultivate PW1 Tam’s trust – she returned his first “investment” of S$100,000 within two weeks with 10% interest, and encashed the first cheque of S$200,000 with her own identity documents. These initial acts were designed precisely to induce PW1 Tam into believing in the alleged legitimacy of her false representations. She had also invited PW1 Tam and his family members to MBS to further reinforce his belief that she had dealings at the casinos related to the alleged business.

(iii)       The AP’s deception exploited and abused PW1 Tam’s trust. The AP had manipulated PW1 Tam’s friendship and goodwill to let his guard down, thinking that the AP would not harm him. The AP also further manipulated PW1 Tam by making him believe that they were business partners, while gaslighting him into accepting a minor role without questioning to avoid him uncovering her deception (i.e. by refusing to let him be “more involved” in the business, meeting “clients”). The AP sought to assure PW1 Tam’s concerns about her trustworthiness by citing her roots in Singapore (i.e. her daughter studying in Singapore, ownership of property in Singapore). She had even introduced her family from China to PW1 Tam to let him believe that she was a trustworthy person.

(iv)       The AP’s deception was sophisticated. She had manipulated real-life justifications to bolster the perceived reliability of her false representations about providing funds to PRC tourists (i.e. PRC’s capital controls) and reasons for delay of repayment (i.e. PRC’s judicial freezes). She exploited psychological tricks of scarcity and urgency (i.e. citing the presence of competitors and need to act quickly) to request frequent and immediate fund transfers from PW1 Tam in the form of untraceable cash transactions (i.e. by claiming that she needed to hand over SGD in cash and had no time to encash cheques or convert transfers into cash). In particular, the phone conversations presented clear evidence of the AP’s web of lies that were designed to exploit psychological biases (e.g. sunk cost fallacy, gaslighting) to manipulate PW1 Tam and maintain her deception.

(v)       The AP also sustained and enhanced her deception for a significant period of about six months. Beyond the mere duration of the deception, the evidence showed that she enhanced her deception by making further false representations to induce delivery of more funds from a desperate PW1 Tam. First, she claimed that PW1 Tam’s funds were frozen in China, which allowed her to cite her PRC nationality in claiming that she had connections to delay PW1 Tam’s requests for repayment. Second, at the same time, she invented further false representations of a shorter turnover period involving another unknown Malaysian individual to capitalize on PW1 Tam’s desperation to hand over more monies to try to make up for the outstanding balance. She even offered a false address to send PW1 Tam and his friends on a wild goose chase.

(c)     There are no offender-specific mitigating factors in the present case. The AP made no restitution, was uncooperative with authorities throughout investigations in fabricating false accounts and showed no remorse all the way through the trial.

(d)     There are numerous offender-specific aggravating factors in the present case as follows:

(i)       The AP’s personal enrichment from the proceeds of her cheating offences (i.e. S$1,426,100) enhances her culpability[note: 472]. The evidence showed that the AP had significant expenses with no source of funds or income to sustain them. No restitution was made. The defence had offered no explanation or evidence of the AP’s use of cheated funds. Hence, the only logical conclusion is that the AP had personally benefitted from the entire sum of S$1,426,100 that were the proceeds from her cheating offences.

(ii)       The AP’s attempt to evade detection constitute an aggravating factor.[note: 473] As noted in Law Aik Meng, the difficulty of detection and/or apprehension of offences (particularly commercial ones) is a powerful factor precipitating a heavy sentence if and when such crimes are eventually unrecovered through painstaking investigation. First, the AP tried to ensure that she obtained funds from PW1 Tam in cash as much as possible to ensure that it could not be directly traced to her. The deliberate nature of this act was revealed by the AP’s convenient but rigid defence at trial that the amounts were untraceable. Second, the AP tried to give numerous excuses and invented falsehoods, especially during the phone conversations, to try to placate PW1 Tam to prevent him from discovering the deception. This perpetuated the deception and delayed PW1 Tam’s reporting for almost a year. Third, when the AP was investigated, she invented false accounts of loans and repayments that obstructed investigations and incurred unnecessary difficulties and costs.

(iii)       The AP’s lack of remorse by her conduct of her trial is an aggravating factor[note: 474]. The AP had made vexatious allegations against PW1 Tam for being an unreasonable moneylender who had allegedly threatened her with violence and reprisal and cast spurious aspersions about PW1 Tam allegedly abusing his connections to the Singapore government and police force, and relations with triads in Geylang. Specific deterrence dictates that unremorseful offenders are to be punished more severely.[note: 475]

(e)     A sentence of 7 years’ imprisonment for a s 420 PC charge involving S$1,426,100 is appropriate based on the precedents. 29     The above corporate fraud precedents typically involve companies obtaining loans from banks through invoice financing or trade receipt financing. In most cases, the offenders falsified invoices to overstate business activity and portray a false image of the company’s health. In gist, the custodial sentences imposed on those cases are far lower than the sentence sought in the present case, even for cases that involve significant higher quantum. The higher sentence imposed in the present case is entirely appropriate and justifiable given that the category of precedents that relate to corporate fraud should be distinguished for several reasons as follows:

(i)     The victims in the corporate fraud cases were financial institutions. This is clearly distinguishable from the private individuals who suffered losses in the present case. While the loss suffered in the corporate fraud cases may have been higher in quantum than the present case, the significance and impact of loss to the private individuals in the present case is much more severe and crushing. In fact, as the evidence showed, the victims are still struggling to recover from the losses caused by the AP’s offending.

(ii)     The total net loss caused in the category of precedents that relate to corporate fraud do not even come close to cheated amounts of the charges in the present case[note: 482]. The highest amount of net loss (S$932,282) does not even come close to the net loss caused in the present case (S$2,160,100). In some cases, there was no net loss caused as the fraud had been used to obtain financing that was eventually repaid.

(iii)     The intention of the offenders in the corporate fraud cases is to obtain financing to keep their companies afloat which are distinguishable from the purely selfish desire for direct financial gain in the present case. The High Court had noted[note: 483] that an offender’s intention to save his company’s fortunes by using financing to tide over a tight cash flow situation was a “striking feature”, and while this does not relieve the offender of his culpability, this situation was held to be “vastly different from one where the perpetrator commits an offence for direct financial gain or to repay gambling debts.” (as is the present case ).

(iv)     In summary, the category of precedents that relate to corporate fraud are clearly of a different nature and the Courts have imposed sentences that reflect a different treatment of these cases. Consequently, any comparison to these cases in an attempt to calibrate sentences based on the quantum alone would be misplaced and inappropriate.

CONSECUTIVE AND GLOBAL SENTENCE

31     I accepted the prosecution submission for the three sentences I have imposed for DAC-928505-2020, DAC-905204-2021 and DAC-905205-2021 to run consecutively for the reasons as submitted by the prosecution as follows:

(a)     The general rule is that sentences for unrelated offences should be ordered to run consecutively as each offence should attract its own distinct consequence. Single offenders and multiple offenders should not generally be treated alike. Concurrent sentences for unrelated offences would not adequately serve, and in fact may undermine, the sentencing considerations that underlie the individual sentences comprising the aggregate term. Offenders who are sentenced at one sitting should not be treated materially more leniently or more seriously than offenders who are sentenced separately, and the Court needs to avoid any suggestion that a multiple offender may benefit from a bulk discount in sentencing.[note: 486]

(b)     Running three sentences for DAC-928505-2020, DAC-905204-2021 and DAC-905205-2021 consecutively accords with the principle of running sentences for unrelated offences consecutively. For DAC-905204-2021 and DAC-905205-2021, while the deception was applied primarily on PW13 Soh in both cases, the funds originated from two separate individuals, PW13 Soh’s friends, who sustained the separate and distinct losses. Furthermore, the funds were handed over on separate and distinct occasions. Accordingly, they should be considered as unrelated offences.

(c)     Running three sentences consecutively also gives effect to the AP’s overall criminality and in in keeping with her record and prospects. It is necessary to achieve a sentence that is commensurate to the devastating amount of loss caused to the victims and gross amount of illegally obtained personal benefit enjoyed by the AP. To do otherwise would be to result in the AP benefitting from the court’s failure to have regard to the enhanced culpability in the multiplicity of offences that have been committed by the AP[note: 487].

(d)     Furthermore, running three sentences consecutively also produces a sentence that is firmly in line with the totality principle. The sentence is not crushing on the AP because:

(i)       The aggregate sentence of at least 12 years’ imprisonment is not substantially above the normal level of sentences for the most serious of the individual offences involved[note: 488]. The maximum punishment for cheating offences under s 420 of the Penal Code is 10 years’ imprisonment, and the use of the maximum punishment as a yardstick in this case is appropriate given the severity of the AP’s offending[note: 489].

(ii)       The effect of the aggregate sentence does not impose on the AP a crushing sentence not in keeping with her past record and future prospects[note: 490]. The fact that the AP is untraced is a lack of an aggravating factor, and not a mitigating one, particularly given her multiplicity of offences. Given the AP’s worrying propensity for criminality and persistent desire to use illegal means for personal gain, a harsh and deterrent sentence is precisely necessary and beneficial in the interest of specific deterrence against further offending.

(e)     As submitted by the prosecution, the AP is a brazen and shameless criminal who displayed a blatant disregard for the law. She had perpetrated a sophisticated and carefully premeditated scheme of deception to fleece her victims of S$2,160,100 out of pure greed and self-interest. She had no qualms about exploiting and abusing their trust to extract monies from them and their family and friends for her personal benefit, the majority of which she squandered at the casinos. Even after her deception was discovered, she displayed an utter lack of remorse in refusing to admit to her transgressions and instead re-victimized PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh with various outrageous allegations. Her baseless and ridiculous contestation of the charges wasted time and resources over 12 days of trial. The AP’s predisposition for criminality and clear disregard for the law is evidenced by her commission of the series of cheating offences over a course of four years. After deceiving PW1Tam over a six-month period in 2016-2017, the AP targeted PW13 Soh and her friends at the end of 2019, despite being investigated for her deception of Tam at the time. Furthermore, her adoption of similar sophisticated schemes against both victims exhibit her high degree of planning and premeditation in committing offences involving financial dishonesty out of greed. The AP’s undeniably cunning and devious personality, coupled with a reprehensible lack of remorse, necessitates a harsh sentence to deter her from perpetrating such deception on more victims.

(f)     Accordingly, I ordered the sentences of 7 years, 3 years and 2 years which I have imposed respectively for the charges in DAC-928505-2020, DAC-905204-2021 and DAC-905205-2021 to run consecutively giving a global sentence of 12 years’ imprisonment to be imposed on the AP and backdated to date of remand on 21 Feb 2020.

(XVI)   NO MERIT IN THE DEFENCE MITIGATION AND SENTENCING SUBMISSIONS

32     I see no merit in and rejected the defence mitigation and sentencing submissions for reasons as submitted by the prosecution as follows:

(a)     The defence mitigation offers no independent mitigating factors that warrant a downward adjustment of the individual and global sentences and did not propose any alternative and specific individual or global sentencing position.

(b)     The defence has provided no reasoning for its submissions for two sentences to run consecutively. It has simply made the bare assertion that this “would suffice to meet the needs”. The defence notion of what the “needs” of this case (e.g. the circumstances of the case, the AP’s overall culpability) itself is incorrectly founded on its self-serving mischaracterization of some of the findings of fact made by this Court which had been read to both parties at the time of verdict delivery on 4 January 2023.[note: 491]

(c)     The mere citation in the defence submissions of three cases, Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v PP [2014] 2 SLR 998 (“Shouffee”), PP v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 796 (“Raveen Balakrishnan”) and ADF v PP [2010] 1 SLR 874 (“ADF”), achieves nothing without any application or reasoning by the defence. The defence has offered no arguments to explain why based on Shouffee, the aggregate sentence as proposed by the prosecution would fall foul of the first limb of the totality principle and why the sentence would be crushing on the AP, considering the brazen and repeated nature of her criminality and the attendant need for specific deterrence.

(d)     In my view, while the prosecution had explained why based on Raveen Balakrishnan, its proposed aggregate sentence is sufficient and proportionate to the AP’s overall criminal liability, including being in accordance with the general rule that sentences for unrelated offences should be ordered to run consecutively (which is notably omitted from the citation in the defence mitigation), the defence submission for two sentences to run consecutively would yield an aggregate sentence that is insufficient and disproportionate to the AP’s overall criminal liability, considering the egregious circumstances in the present case.

(e)     The defence offered no reasoning why based on ADF, the present case should not fall within the category of cases where more than two consecutive sentences should be imposed as the AP has been found by this Court to be a persistent offender given the multiplicity of offending over several years and also a pressing public interest in deterring the AP’s criminality (i.e. perpetrating dishonest schemes for personal gain).[note: 492]

(f)     The defence submission to run two sentences consecutively consists of unsubstantiated assertions and unreasoned citation of case law as it has conspicuously failed to address the glaring issues of unrelated offences and proportionality in relation to the AP’s overall criminality.

(g)     The defence claim that the prosecution was listing general aggravating factors that do not apply to the AP’s offending is simply incorrect as I have already found[note: 493] that the prosecution had provided comprehensive and cogent factual basis to substantiate the identification of aggravating factors, precisely reflecting the need to provide distinct and specific factors because “every case is different”. The defence submission in this regard is simply an opportunistic attempt to divert attention from its failure to sufficiently controvert the aggravating factors.

(h)     The defence mitigation has failed to rebut the aggravating factors as identified by the prosecution and its challenges to the aggravating factors are generally based on erroneous findings of fact inconsistent with this Court’s findings of fact which had been read to both parties at the time of verdict delivery on 4 January 2023. The defence mitigation and responses to the individual aggravating factors as identified by the prosecution were based on blatantly inaccurate facts and at some points, appears to rehash some of its arguments made in its closing submissions for trial. This is entirely misguided. As this Court has found the AP guilty and delivered its findings of fact which have been read to both parties and recorded in writing in the Claim Trial Minute Sheet[note: 494] (“Court’s findings”), both parties’ submissions on sentence must be bound by this Court’s findings. Where the defence relies on or suggests facts that are inconsistent with this Court’s findings, these submissions must be summarily rejected. I note that the prosecution has highlighted four different offender-specific aggravating factors for both PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s cases which are consistent with this Court’s findings and are effectively unchallenged by the defence. Any claims raised by the defence in their mitigation in addressing these factors are irrelevant and/or pointless – in fact, they often serve to further accentuate the very aggravating factors that the defence tries to dispute. The aforesaid four aggravating factors are as follows:

(i)       The AP was personally enriched from her offences as she had benefitted fully to the tune of SGD$2,160,100. This is consistent with this Court’s findings and the defence provided no evidence at trial or in mitigation to suggest anything to the contrary. Accordingly, the defence has conceded this point. The attempt to rehash the AP’s false claim of a loan of SGD$300,000 and to cast doubt on PW1 Tam’s evidence of the sum of SGD$1,426,100 is pointless given this Court’s acceptance of the sum as reliable based on the credibility of Exhibit P7 and utter rejection of the AP’s evidence as “internally inconsistent and completely unbelievable” .

(ii)       The AP acted out of pure self-interest and greed, being motivated to commit the offences for her personal expenses, including to fund her profligate gambling . This is consistent with this Court’s findings and the defence provided no evidence at trial or in mitigation to suggest anything to the contrary. Accordingly, the defence has conceded this point. The defence only express response to this point on self-interest in its mitigation makes a completely bizarre claim that PW1 Tam was acting in self-interest instead, and not the AP. Not only is the fact that PW1 Tam was acting in self-interest does not absolve the AP in any way, it also merely reinforces the sheer extent of the AP’s deception in manipulating PW1 Tam to believe her enough to even take monies from his family and friends and pass the monies to the AP.

(iii)       The AP had attempted to evade detection by obtaining the funds in cash, giving numerous excuses and invented falsehoods even during investigations . This is consistent with this Court’s findings and the defence provided no evidence at trial or in mitigation to suggest anything to the contrary. Accordingly, the defence has conceded this point. The defence suggestion that the AP did not evade detection simply because she stayed physically close to PW1 Tam or had opportunities to confront her is wrong. As the recorded phone conversations (Exhibit P5) showed, PW1 Tam did chase the AP for answers, but it was her excuses and falsehoods that allowed her to evade detection. In fact, it is likely because she did not run away or completely cut off contact that contributed to PW1 Tam’s mistaken belief that there was still hope.

(iv)       The AP was unremorseful in her conduct of trial in making vexatious allegations against the witnesses, including the victims themselves. This is squarely in line with this Court’s findings. The defence has rightly not suggested anything to the contrary in their mitigation. Given that the AP’s defence essentially rests on re-victimizing PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh with her vexatious allegations, this Court’s rejection of the defence in its entirety alone is sufficient to establish this aggravating factor. To drive the final nail in the coffin, the defence reiteration of similar vexatious insinuations against PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh somehow being responsible for the AP’s offending in the defence mitigation despite this Court’s findings only serves to accentuate the AP’s lack of remorse and shamelessness.

(i)     The defence attempted challenges to the prosecution’s offence-specific aggravating factors were misguided. The AP was the sole perpetrator and beneficiary. The AP had conceived of the entire plan, executed it on her own and entirely enjoyed the benefits from her criminality. This is consistent with this Court’s findings and the defence has left this point undisputed at trial and in mitigation. Accordingly, this point remains unchallenged.

(j)     The AP’s deception was planned and premeditated. She had planned and premeditated her deception of PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh by cultivating their trust as friends and expanded her reach by convincing them to obtain funds from friends and family members when they personally lacked funds. This is consistent with this Court’s findings in accepting PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s testimonies as credible and consistent, while rejecting the AP’s evidence in its entirety and rightly branding her a blatant liar. The defence has left this point undisputed in mitigation. Accordingly, this point remains unchallenged.

(k)     This Court had found that the AP had exploited and abused the trust of PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh and manipulated them using their friendship and goodwill. In PW13 Soh’s case, the AP had exploited PW13 Soh by leaving her to deal with the fallout with her friends as the proxy. The defence responses in PW1 Tam’s case on this point are clearly misguided. The defence assertion that there was “interest” offered as a saving grace is bizarre, because it was simply part of the AP’s false representations used to deceive PW1 Tam. Furthermore, any actual interest paid out was merely part of the deception and further adds to the exploitation and abuse of the trust. The defence reliance on the evidence of repayment by some of the witnesses in PW1 Tam’s case is misguided – the repayment was not made by PW1 Tam, but by his sisters on his behalf after the deception was discovered. Moreover, even if some of the victims were repaid in full, this does not in any way detract from the AP’s exploitation and abuse of PW1 Tam’s trust.

(l)     The defence claim that there was no exploitation and abuse of PW1 Tam’s trust because he was “fully aware (of) the stakes involved in the investment (and) the AP had not lied to him (about) the purposes of the money and they were for PRC nationals to use at the casinos” . This submission is utterly bizarre. By asserting PW1 Tam’s full awareness of the stakes involved in the fictitious investment, the defence is simply reinforcing the depth of the AP’s deception. Furthermore, this Court has unequivocally found that the representations about investment involving PRC nationals were false and the AP had in fact personally gambled a significant portion of PW1 Tam’s monies away in the casino. Hence, the defence mitigation is accurate that the AP did not lie to PW1 Tam to the shameless extent that her reference to the PRC national(s) using PW1 Tam’s monies in the casino was in fact herself.

(m)     The defence has simply made a baseless suggestion of doubt on the presence of exploitation and abuse of trust which has been rejected by this Court in its entirety in PW13’s case. Furthermore, the defence repetition of PW13 Soh’s importance in the whole scheme in getting her friends on board simply reinforced the fact that the AP had exploited and abused PW13 Soh’s trust, that led her rope in her friends who in turn “trusted” her.

(n)     The AP had employed a sophisticated criminal scheme. As found by this Court, the prosecution had provided extensive reasoning from the trial evidence to show that the AP’s offending was sophisticated both against PW1 Tam (DAC-925802-2020) and PW13 Soh and her friends (DAC-905203-2021 to DAC-905206-2021). The sophistication in the AP’s deception was clear from PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s testimonies and the documentary exhibits admitted at trial. Crucially, PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s testimonies are reliable because this Court had found their “credibility and consistency to be effectively intact”, and that their testimonies “provided corroboration” to the prosecution exhibits. In both cases, as found by this Court, the evidence at trial showed that the AP had manipulated real-life justifications to create a veneer of reliability for her false representations. In PW1 Tam’s case, she had further utilized psychological tricks and exploited psychological biases for the purpose of manipulation. In PW13 Soh’s case, the AP had used documentation to create a façade of legitimacy, incorporated incentives as baits and crafted a situation that prevented discovery of her deception.

(o)     The defence sweeping conclusion that the AP’s deception was “not a sophisticated criminal scheme” because she did not cheat an institutional entity or use complex technological means is erroneous. Sophistication simply refers to criminal schemes with a degree of complexity or intricacy[note: 495]. It does not only take the form of institutional cheating or technological complexity, and this was certainly not the sophistication of the AP’s criminal scheme in the present case. The Skillsfuture fraud case[note: 496] cited by the defence does not define sophistication in cheating offences; it is merely one example. Specific to the present case, the defence only substantively responded to the sophistication in PW1 Tam’s case and failed to engage with the sophistication in PW13 Soh’s case[note: 497]. Consequently, the prosecution submissions on sophistication in PW13 Soh’s case are effectively intact.

(p)     For PW1 Tam’s case, the defence claim of the lack of sophistication is logically fallacious. First, the defence summary that “the sophistication claimed by the prosecution is based on the loan being extended to China Nationals (for) purposes of gambling” completely ignores this Court’s findings that the AP’s deception was sophisticated[note: 498]. Second, the fact that the AP had deceived a “friend” (PW1 Tam) is not mutually exclusive from the AP’s application of a sophisticated criminal scheme. In fact, as the prosecution had submitted, this in fact allowed the AP to exploit the friendship. Third, the defence mention of the absence of computers is bizarre. The sophistication in the present case has nothing to do with technology, online application for monies, a bank or financial institution or a government agency. As clearly submitted by the prosecution and found by this Court, it involved the manipulation of reality, creation of false legitimacy and exploitation of psychological tricks and biases to create a complex web of lies.

(q)     The AP had sustained and enhanced her deception for about six months in PW1 Tam’s case and at least two months in PW13 Soh’s case. Not only was the duration considerable, she had enhanced her deception by making further false representations to induce the delivery of more funds. This is consistent with this Court’s findings in accepting PW1 Tam and PW13 Soh’s evidence. To this extent, the defence suggestions in its mitigation to the contrary must be rejected[note: 499].

(r)     The defence main response to the deception point in PW1 Tam’s case is bizarre. Besides the AP rehashing her rejected version of repaid loans of SGD$300,000 with interest, the defence harped on the evidence by PW1 Tam’s family members that they understood PW1 Tam to be business partners with the AP. The answer is simple – while PW1 Tam was never in fact business partners with the AP, she had deceived him into believing this, which was the understanding that he conveyed to his family members and/or friends[note: 500]. This was merely one of the tactics that the AP used to sustain and perpetuate the deception, including extracting further monies by offering him the subsequent “short-term” returns, as found by this Court.

(s)     This Court has accepted the prosecution submission that there are no offender-specific mitigating factors in the present case[note: 501]. The AP made no restitution, was uncooperative with authorities throughout investigations and showed no remorse. This was unchallenged by the defence in their mitigation. The defence has at best alluded to two potential offender-specific mitigating factors in highlighting the AP’s circumstances in the defence introduction to the mitigation, but both do not carry any mitigatory weight. First, the suggestion of her unexceptional personal circumstances, particularly the daughter who is still studying, does not warrant any mitigatory weight[note: 502]. Second, the suggestion of her untraced record merely highlights the lack of an aggravating factor, which does not count as a mitigating factor.[note: 503]

(t)     While I had accepted the prosecution’s reasoned comparison with an extensive set of reported precedents to justify the calibration of both the proposed individual and global sentences, I found that the defence has not provided any reasoning from the precedents cited to justify a different calibration. Notably, the defence has even cited two of the cases relied upon by the prosecution (namely, Kea Meng Cheng and Stefanovic Nenad) without any dispute with the prosecution’s calibration of sentences from these cases, or offering any alternative reasoning. The only logical inference to be drawn is the defence’s acceptance that the prosecution’s calibration of sentences from these two precedents is justified and reasonable.

(u)     The only precedent that the defence cited repeatedly and appeared to rely on most heavily are the unreported cases of PP v Ng Cheng Kwee (SC-911771-2017) and PP v Lee Lai Leng (SC-910681-2017). The High Court has expressly cautioned on the limited precedent value of unreported cases given the absence of detailed facts and circumstances that precludes any intelligent comparison being made[note: 504]. In the present case, no Grounds of Decision were provided to explain how the courts in the aforesaid unreported cases arrived at its sentencing outcome. Accordingly, this Court had accepted the prosecution submission to calibrate the sentences in the present case from the extensive reported precedents cited by the prosecution, instead of the unreported precedents cited by the defence for which any comparison is likely to be misleading due to lack of information. In any case, the aforesaid unreported cases are likely to be inappropriate comparators to the present case for the purpose of calibrating the appropriate sentence. As the defence rightly pointed out in its mitigation, despite both cases involving cheating offences, there are significant differences in the circumstances of those unreported cases with the present case, like the use of technology and the different nature of the victim (institutional entity vs. individual), just to name two.

(XVII)   CONCLUSION ON SENTENCE

33     For all the above reasons, I sentenced the AP to 7 years, 3 years and 2 years for the charges in DAC-928505-2020, DAC-905204-2021 and DAC-905205-2021 respectively and ordered them to run consecutively with a global sentence of 12 years’ imprisonment which, in my view, was not manifestly excessive or inadequate, but wholly appropriate and commensurate with the nature and extent of the AP’s culpability, and was a sufficient deterrent sentence tempered with proportionality without the effect of a crushing sentence.
