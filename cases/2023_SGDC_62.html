District Judge Kenneth Chin:

Introduction

1       The accused pleaded guilty to a single proceeded charge punishable under s.6(a) read with s.29(a) of the Prevention of Corruption Act (Cap 241, 1993 Rev Ed) (“PCA”) with one other charge taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing (“TIC”). After considering parties’ submissions, I sentenced the accused to a fine of $8,500 (in default 40 days imprisonment), with an additional penalty of $2,300 (in default 15 days imprisonment) pursuant to s.13 of the PCA. Being dissatisfied with the sentence, the Prosecution has appealed against the sentence imposed. I now set out the reasons for my decision.

The charges

2       The accused pleaded guilty to the following charge.

DAC-901273-2022 (“2nd Charge”)

You… are charged that you, sometime in August and September 2020, in Singapore, being an agent, to wit, a Dormitory Isolation Assistant in the employ of S11 Granuity Management Pte Ltd (“S11”), did abet by engaging in a conspiracy with Duraisamy Jaganathan (“Jagan”) and Navinder Prashad (“Navin”) to corruptly accept gratification from one Chandramohan S/O Bala Krishnan (“Mohan”), a Director of Shikaa’s Kitchen Pte Ltd (“Shikaa’s”), as an inducement to do acts in relation to your principal’s affairs, to wit, to further the business interests of Shikaa’s with S11, and in pursuance of that conspiracy and in order to the doing of that thing, a series of acts took place, to wit, Jagan did accept gratification from Mohan on at least two occasions between 19 August 2020 and 25 September 2020 of a total sum of $4,000, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under s 6(a) read with s 29(a) of the Prevention of Corruption Act (Cap 241, 1993 Rev Ed)

3       The TIC charge was as follows:

DAC-901273-2022 (“1st Charge”)

You… are charged that you, on 18 August 2020, in Singapore, being an agent, to wit, a Dormitory Isolation Assistant in the employ of S11 Granuity Management Pte Ltd (“S11”), did corruptly accept gratification of a sum of S$600/- from one Chandramohan S/O Balakrishnan (“Mohan”), a Director of Shikaa’s Kitchen Pte Ltd (“Shikaa’s”), as an inducement to do an act in relation to your principal’s affairs, to wit, to further the business interests of Shikaa’s with S11, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under s 6(a) of the Prevention of Corruption Act (Cap 241, 1993 Rev Ed)

Facts

4       The relevant facts, as presented in the Statement of Facts (“SOF”), are reproduced below. The accused admitted to these facts without qualification.

The accused is Piradeep s/o Sathish, a 31 year old male Singaporean bearing NRIC No. XXXXXXXXX. At the material time, he was a dormitory isolation assistant employed by S11 Granuity Management Pte Ltd (“S11”).

2.    The co-accused persons are:

a.    Navinder Prashad (“Navin”), male/ 40 years old. At the material time, he was the Head of the Crisis Management Team employed by S11.

b.    Duraisamy Jaganathan (“Jagan”), male/ 29 years old. At the material time, he was a dormitory manager employed by S11.

c.    Chandramohan s/o Bala Krishnan (“Mohan”), male/ 44 years old. At the material time, he was a director and shareholder of Shikaa’s Kitchen Pte Ltd (“Shikaa’s”).

3.    The involved party is Lawrence Lee Ter Chian (“Lawrence”). At the material time, Lawrence was the Business Development Director of S11. S11 is an accommodation provider for foreign workers in Singapore and runs various dormitories in Singapore, including PPT Lodge 1B of Punggol Dormitory (“the Dormitory”).

4.    As a result of the Covid-19 pandemic, there was a lockdown of the Dormitory from April 2020. S11 then set up a Crisis Management Team (“CMT”) to ensure the smooth running of the Dormitory during the Covid-19 pandemic. The CMT members comprised of the accused, Navinder, Jagan and one Rishibaraj Mohan (“Rishi”). Lawrence was in charge of the CMT and its members.

5.    From April to July 2020, the food for the workers living in the Dormitory was provided by caterers engaged by the Ministry of Manpower (“MOM”). Sometime in July 2020, as the Covid-19 situation in the foreign worker dormitories had improved, MOM informed S11 that it would cease its provision of catering services for the dormitory residents. S11 thus had to take over the responsibility of providing food to the Dormitory’s residents, and accordingly, source and engage their own catering vendors. S11 initially appointed a managing agent, M/s Unity Gourmand (“Unity Gourmand”), which utilised their catering vendors to provide food for the Dormitory. However, there were issues with the food provided by Unity Gourmand’s existing catering vendors as the vendors could not cope with the food order volume. Following a discussion headed by Lawrence, S11 decided to source for and engage other external catering vendors. As the employers of the foreign workers staying at the Dormitory were already paying Unity Gourmand for the workers’ food, it was further decided that the payments to the external catering vendors would be made by Unity Gourmand.

6.    Lawrence thus tasked Navin to source for prospective catering vendors and left it to him to sort out the administrative matters of engaging the vendors with Unity Gourmand. Navin in turn delegated the task of source for prospective catering vendors to Jagan. The accused and Rishi were also roped in to help with the sourcing of caterers.

7.    The accused knew of a number of catering companies as he previously worked as a wedding planner. Accordingly, the accused approached Mohan, who indicated interest in the catering opportunity. In the course of the discussion, Mohan offered the accused gratification in the form of a “referral fee” to introduce him to someone who could recommend Shikaa’s to cater for S11, so as to further Shikaa’s dealings with S11. The accused accepted Mohan’s offer and gave him Navin and Jagan’s contact details so that Mohan could liaise with them directly. Shortly after liaising with Jagan, Shikaa’s was awarded a catering contract for S11. Pursuant to Mohan’s earlier corrupt agreement with the accused, Mohan transferred $600 to the accused on 18 August 2020.

8.    After Shikaa’s was awarded the S11 catering contract, Mohan entered into another corrupt agreement with Navin and Jagan for Mohan to provide $4,000 as gratification to further Shikaa’s business interests with S11 by ensuring smooth business dealings between Shikaa’s and S11 and for Shikaa’s to continue being a caterer for S11. Mohan arrived at the sum of $4,000 after calculating the profits Shikaa’s had made from the S11 catering job. The accused thereafter informed Navin and Jagan that he should also have a share of the money from Mohan as he was the one who had brought Shikaa’s in. After internal discussion, Navin, Jagan and the accused decided to split the $4,000 as follows: $1,700 to the accused, $1,000 to Navin and Jagan each, and the remaining $300 to the dormitory residents who assist in the food distribution.

9.    Accordingly, from August to September 2020, Mohan gave Jagan a total of $4,000 in cash and via bank transfer. The money was then distributed to Navin, the accused and the dormitory residents pursuant to the agreement set out in paragraph 8 above. Navin transferred the accused his share (i.e. $1,700) via PayNow. The accused has since spent his share.

10.    S11 later terminated its catering contract with Shikaa’s sometime in September 2020 after several instances of late delivery of food and spoilt food.

11.    By virtue of the foregoing, the accused, pursuant to a conspiracy with Jagan, Navin and Mohan, did, together with Jagan and Navin, corruptly obtain gratification amounting to a total of $4,000 from Mohan as an inducement to do acts in relation to the accused’s principal, S11’s affairs to further the business interests of Shikaa’s with S11. The accused has therefore committed an offence under s 6(a) read with s 29(a) of the Prevention of Corruption Act (Cap 241, 1993 Rev Ed).

Prescribed penalties

5       The prescribed punishment for an offence punishable under s.6(a) read with s.29(a) of the PCA is a fine not exceeding $100,000 or imprisonment for a term not exceeding 5 years, or both.

Antecedents

6       The accused had no prior antecedents.

Parties’ submissions

Prosecution’s submission on sentence

7       The Prosecution submitted for a short custodial sentence with the duration of the custodial term left to the Court’s discretion. Additionally, the Prosecution sought a penalty order under s.13 PCA for $2,300 to disgorge the gratification received by the accused. In support of this position, the Prosecution made the following arguments:

(a)     First, the main sentencing considerations in corruption cases are deterrence and punishment (PP v Ang Seng Thor [2011] 4 SLR 217 at [33]. The present case is a private sector corruption case which falls within category one of the three broad and non-exhaustive categories set out by Sundaresh Menon CJ in PP v Syed Mostofa Romel [2015] SGHC 117 (“Romel”). The applicable sentencing framework in the present case is set out in Goh Ngak Eng v PP [2022] SGHC 254 (“Goh Ngak Eng”). which applies to private sector corruption cases under s.6(a) and s.6(b) of the PCA. The Prosecution assessed the present case to fall under slight harm and low culpability within the Goh Ngak Eng framework, and identified the following factors:

(i)        Actual loss to principal: There was no actual quantifiable loss. However, S11 suffered loss of ensuring that the best caterers were engaged as the award was not based on competitive quotes or quality of food submitted, but on account of the bribes that were given to the accused and other members of the CMT.

(ii)        Benefit to the giver of gratification: Mohan was able to secure the catering contract from S11.

(iii)        Type and extent of loss to third parties: Third party vendors were deprived of an opportunity to be considered for the catering contract.

(iv)        Presence of public health risk: The present facts involve the provision of food to a large group of dormitory residents and it was of utmost importance for S11 to ensure that the caterer(s) engaged were able to provide food fit for consumption in a timely manner. Otherwise, the health of the dormitory residents consuming the food would be put at risk. The public health risk can be inferred from the fact that the engaged caterer was terminated after only two months due to several instances of late delivery and spoilt food.

(v)        Amount of gratification received: $1,700 out of $4,000 for the proceeded charge.

(vi)        Offender’s motive in committing the offence: The accused was motivated by greed.

(vii)        Role played by the accused in the corrupt transaction: After receiving gratification from Mohan on a previous occasion, the accused actively requested a portion of further gratification Mohan offered to the S11 staff.

(b)     Second, the presence of public health risk taken together with the other offence-specific factors in the present case warranted a custodial sentence.

(c)     Third, the Prosecution relied on two unreported cases involving S11 where the offenders had received custodial sentences. The Prosecution argued that the sentencing principle of parity should apply.

Defence’s submissions on sentence

8       The Defence submitted for a fine of $3,000 to be imposed together with an appropriate penalty which was not disputed. In support of its position, the Defence made the following arguments:

(a)     First, the Defence accepted that the Goh Ngak Eng framework applies in the present case and agreed that the present case falls under slight harm and low culpability within the framework. Nevertheless, the applicable framework does not preclude the imposition of a fine in appropriate cases.

(b)     Second, the defence disagrees that there is the presence of public health risk as an offence-specific factor in the present case.

(c)     Third, a fine is warranted in the present case as the accused’s culpability is the lowest amongst all concerned in the commission of the offence, and his participation in the scheme, minimal.

(d)     Fourth, the approach taken by the Prosecution in its submissions on sentence was wrong, and unfair.

(e)     Fifth, imposing a fine in the present case would not be inconsistent with precedents as there have been cases post-Goh Ngak Eng where fines have been imposed.

Issues

9       Arising from parties’ submissions, these were the following issues before me:

(a)     Whether there was public health risk involved in the present case.

(b)     Whether the custodial threshold has been crossed for the appropriate sentence in this case.

The law on private sector corruption

10     In Romel at [26], Menon CJ sets out three broad and non-exhaustive categories of cases in which private sector corruption can occur as follows:

(a)     Category one – where the receiving party is paid to confer on the paying party a benefit that is within the receiving party’s power to confer, without regard to whether the paying party ought properly to have received that benefit. This is typically done at the payer’s behest. In this category, whether the custodial threshold is crossed depends on the facts.

(b)     Category two - where the receiving party is paid to forbear from performing what he is duty bound to do, thereby conferring a benefit on the paying party. This also is typically done at the payer’s behest. In this category, cases frequently attract custodial sentences.

(c)     Category three - where a receiving party is paid so that he will forbear from inflicting harm on the paying party, even though there may be no lawful basis for the infliction of such harm. This is typically done at the receiving party’s behest. In this category, the receiving party can generally expect a custodial sentence.

11     The applicable sentencing framework for private sector corruption was set out in Goh Ngak Eng where the Court of three judges laid down the following 5-step sentencing framework for accused persons convicted after trial for offences under s.6(a) and s.6(b) of the PCA.

Decision on sentence

17     In my decision on the appropriate sentence, I applied the sentencing framework in Goh Ngak Eng.

Whether there is public health risk

18     As highlighted above, the Prosecution argued that the present facts involve the provision of food to a large group of dormitory residents. The public health risk can be inferred from the fact that the engaged caterer was terminated after only about two months due to several instances of late delivery and spoilt food. The Prosecution also contended that the accused did not perform his duty to mitigate or manage the risks to the dormitory residents’ health, and they stood to be impacted by such risks that they otherwise would not have if the offending conduct had not taken place.

19     The Defence argued that the Prosecution’s extrapolation of public health risk in the present case was unsound, in fact and in law, for the following reasons:

(a)     There was no evidence that Shikaa’s was an unlicensed caterer or in breach of food safety standards, such that their engagement would pose a public health risk.

(b)     The accused, as a dormitory isolation assistant, was not entrusted to ensure that the food delivered by caterers were fit for consumption. Hence, it would be unfair to blame the accused for any performance issues by Shikaa’s.

(c)     There was no causal link between the accused’s corrupt conduct and any injury or risk suffered by the dormitory residents, whether public health related or otherwise. The accused merely made introductions of the relevant people.

20     Having considered parties’ submissions, I was unable to agree that there was a presence of public health risk in the present case for the following reasons:

(a)     First, I cannot accept the Prosecution’s starting premise that there is public health risk in any case where food is provided to a large group of people. If so, every corruption case involving a food catering contract would invoke public health risk as an offence-specific factor, regardless of the nature of the corruption concerned and whether there was any causal connection. This cannot be correct. As pointed out by the Defence, this is too simplistic an argument and is unduly wide. The Prosecution did not proffer any authorities to support this general contention. Therefore, I am unable, without more, to accept the Prosecution’s contention in this regard.

(b)     Second, the SOF was deficient in supporting the submissions the Prosecution was seeking to make. In the present context, I would agree that there would be public health risk if the facts revealed that Shikaa’s was an unqualified caterer, and that they were appointed without regard to their suitability as a caterer because of the accused’s corrupt conduct, resulting in potential health risk to the dormitory residents. However, this was not the case here based on the facts admitted by the accused in the SOF.

(i)       Firstly, it was undisputed that Shikaa’s was a qualified and licensed food caterer.

(ii)       Secondly, the SOF did not disclose that Shikaa’s was appointed regardless of their suitability as a caterer as there was nothing in the SOF to suggest this. Based on the sentence in [7] of the SOF which states:

“Shortly after liaising with Jagan, Shikaa’s was awarded a catering contract for S11”,

the Prosecution argued that it is “plainly reasonable” to argue from this fact that (i) Shikaa’s was favoured for the catering contract without an objective assessment of their services, which harmed S11 as it was against its interest; and (ii) Shikaa’s was engaged without much or any consideration of other caterers, who were, as a result harmed by not being on a level playing field.

I did not agree. Whilst I accept that it would be entirely reasonable for the Prosecution to put forward appropriate submissions to invite the court to draw suitable inferences from the facts which have been set out in the SOF (Lee Chee Keet v PP [2016] 4 SLR 1316 at [20]), there must be a limit to this as the Prosecution cannot be given carte blanche liberty to make submissions which are not grounded by underlying facts set out in the SOF. As a general rule, parties should not stray too far away from the SOF in making their submissions on sentence (Biplob Hussain Younus Akan v PP [2011] 3 SLR 217 at [9]). Notably, Lee Chee Keet at [20] states:

Sentencing submissions by the Prosecution following a plea of guilty should properly be circumscribed by the facts contained within the SOF and not attempt to introduce new facts or details which may potentially be contested and have not been admitted to by the accused.

In my view, it would certainly take more than a one-liner in the SOF to substantiate the kind of broad submissions that the Prosecution was seeking to make above. In particular, I agree with the Defence that the one-liner did not support the following submissions of the Prosecution:

(A)       That S11 did not carry out any “objective assessment” of Shikaa’s before they were engaged by S11;

(B)       That this “harmed S11 as it was against its interest”;

(C)       That there were “other caterers” involved;

(D)       That Shikaa’s was engaged “without much or any consideration of other caterers”;

(E)       That such other caterers were “as a result, harmed by not being on a level playing field”

If it was the Prosecution’s intention to make such submissions, then the underlying facts supporting these submissions should have been properly fleshed out in the SOF such that the accused can be given the choice of admission without qualification or otherwise.

(iii)       Thirdly, the SOF contained scarce details on the responsibilities held by the accused as a dormitory isolation assistant and a member of the CMT. I can accept from [6] of the SOF that the accused was tasked to source for suitable caterers. However, it was a stretch for the Prosecution to say that the accused was entrusted to do more than recommending caterers such that he should be held accountable for subsequent performance issues beyond his control. It would take even more to say that the dormitory residents “were reliant on him, as they stood to be impacted by such risks that they otherwise would not have if the offending conduct had not taken place” (Goh Ngak Eng at [72]). As explained above, the SOF was inadequate in that it did not set out the facts of how S11 appointed Shikaa’s as the caterer following the accused’s introduction of Mohan to Navin and Jagan for them to liaise directly. From the facts presented, the accused was not the decision maker in appointing Shikaa’s and his role regarding the appointment of Shikaa’s did not go beyond making this introduction. Therefore, the crucial link between the accused’s offending conduct in making the introduction for gratification and any potential health risk was lacking.

(iv)       Fourthly, there was also no causal connection between the accused’s subsequent receipt of gratification pursuant to the conspiracy and the provision of spoilt food. If the Prosecution’s intention was to submit that the accused’s corrupt conduct resulted in the continued provision of spoilt food to the dormitory residents, this was unsupported by the SOF. From the SOF, this was not a situation where the catering contract was maintained due to the accused’s corrupt conduct notwithstanding the performance issues. The SOF did not state whether the accused had any involvement or authority to maintain the catering contract as the communication was between Navin, Jagan and Mohan. In fact, the catering contract was terminated shortly after.

Whether the custodial threshold has been crossed

21     It was undisputed, and I agree, that this was a Romel category one case. In this category, whether the custodial threshold is crossed depends on the facts. As a starting point, I understand there is no presumption in favour of a non-custodial sentence where private sector corruption is concerned (Ang Seng Thor at [10]). Whether a custodial sentence is warranted in a particular case is determined upon a careful consideration of sentencing principles such as the public interest and other policy considerations, as well as the gravity of the offence including the particular facts and circumstances thereof: PP v Tan Fook Sum [1999] 1 SLR 523 as cited in Lim Teck Chye v PP [2004] 2 SLR(R) 525 at [65].

22     It was also uncontroversial between parties, which I agree, that the present case falls under slight harm and low culpability within the Goh Ngak Eng sentencing framework, where a fine or imprisonment up to 6 months is appropriate as a starting range. Where the Prosecution and the Defence disagrees is whether the custodial threshold has been crossed within this starting range (i.e., Step three of the Goh Ngak Eng sentencing framework). I therefore elaborate on my reasons in applying Step three of the sentencing framework, which requires a more granular assessment of the offence-specific factors in Step one to identify the indicative starting point within the identified sentencing range.

23     I assessed the following offence-specific factors going towards harm to be relevant:

(a)      Actual loss to principal: I accept the Prosecution’s concession that the accused’s principal suffered no actual quantifiable loss but nevertheless had been deprived of the opportunity to obtain best value for its needs, given that the procurement process had been compromised by the accused’s corrupt conduct. However, based on the facts in the SOF, the extent to which the procurement process had been compromised by the accused’s involvement (apart from making the introduction) was neither presented by the prosecution nor admitted by the accused.

(b)      Benefit to the giver of gratification: I accept that Mohan may have been enabled to secure the catering contract for Shikaa’s from S11 which he may not have been able but for the introduction made by the accused. However, the SOF did not reveal that the accused had exercised any control or influence over the eventual appointment of Shikaa’s, or that he had divulged confidential or material information to Mohan to give him an unfair advantage over other prospective caterers.

(c)      Type and extent of loss to third parties: The Prosecution submitted that third party vendors were deprived of an opportunity to be considered for the catering contract although this was unsupported by the SOF. I therefore did not accord much weight to this contention.

(d)      Presence of public health risk: For the reasons elaborated above at [20], I did not find the presence of public health risk in the present case.

(e)      Offences committed as part of a group or syndicate: Whilst the Prosecution did not submit on this offence-specific factor to be relevant, I was of the view this was relevant in the present case. The proceeded charge was part of a conspiracy between the accused, Navin and Jagan. Group offences have been observed to generally result in greater harm (Goh Ngak Eng at [106(d)]).

(f)      Public disquiet, involvement of a transnational element, engagement of public service rationale, involvement of a strategic industry: The Prosecution rightly did not submit for these offence-specific factors to be relevant. I pause only to comment that whilst the Ministry of Manpower had previously provided the caterers, the subsequent delegation of this responsibility for S11 to source for their own caterers was a commercial one which did not extend the public service rationale.

24     I assessed the following offence-specific factors going towards harm to be relevant:

(a)      Amount of gratification received: All other things being equal, an agent who received or demanded a bigger gratification must be more blameworthy than one who has received a smaller amount (Goh Ngak Eng at [88(b)]). The accused received $1,700 out of $4,000 for the proceed charge.

(b)      Offender’s motive in committing the offence: I accept the Prosecution’s submission that the accused was motivated by greed.

(c)      The role played by the offender in the corrupt transaction: The facts did not reveal that the accused played a more prominent role in compromising the procurement process beyond his introduction of Mohan to Navin and Jagan for them to liaise directly.

(d)      Other factors: The corrupt conduct was not premeditated, have a low level of sophistication, and was not sustained over a long period. There was also absence of any threat, pressure, or coercion.

25     At the outset, I understand that offending conduct coming within the slight harm and low culpability category can nevertheless attract a custodial sentence if the facts warrant it. It is also abundantly clear that Singapore takes private sector corruption very seriously and that deterrence is an important consideration in sentencing corruption cases. However, the sentencing principle of deterrence is not served exclusively by the imposition of a custodial sentence as the objective can be similarly achieved with an appropriate high fine. Having considered all the offence-specific factors above and based on the facts presented before me in the SOF together with the appropriate inferences to be drawn, I am of the view that the custodial threshold has not been crossed in the present case and that a suitably high fine would be sufficient to meet the justice of the case. This is in line with Menon CJ’s observation in Romel at [20] that “where private sector agents are concerned, offences which register a lower level of culpability can be dealt with by the imposition of fines”.

26     For completeness, I address the cases which parties raised in their arguments, although I did not find them to be very helpful as they were all unreported decisions.

27     At the plead guilty mention on 16 January 2023, the Prosecution highlighted orally the cases of PP v Rishibaraj Mohan and PP v Saifulislam bin Abdul Ghani. Both cases involved S11 and both accused persons had received custodial sentences for offences under s.6(a) or s.6(b) of the PCA. As the relevant papers for the two cases were not placed before the court and the Defence did not have the opportunity to go through the two cited cases beforehand, the mention was adjourned, and the Prosecution undertook to upload the necessary papers by 23 January 2023 with leave granted to the Defence to file submissions in response. The Prosecution failed to upload the necessary papers by 23 January 2023 and only did so on 30 January 2023.

28     Having gone through the papers uploaded by the Prosecution, which consisted of the proceeded charges and SOF, I am of the view that the cases were of limited use as they can be distinguished on account of the difference in culpability between the accused persons in those two cases and the accused in the present case. Each case should be decided on its own facts. Furthermore, although both cases similarly involve S11, the corrupt transaction involved a different caterer, and the arrangement was of a different nature. The two offenders were also not co-accused persons to the present accused. In any case, I am mindful of the caution against relying on unreported decisions. Menon CJ in Yap Ah Lai v PP [2014] 3 SLR 180 at [11(d)] reiterated the observation of Chao Hick Tin JA in Ong Chee Eng v PP [2012] 3 SLR 776 that sentencing precedents without grounds or explanation should bear little, if any, weight because they are unreasoned. As a result, it will not be possible in such cases to discern what had weighed on the mind of the sentencing judge or why the sentencing judge had approached the matter in a particular way. Hence such precedents are not likely to be persuasive. For the same reasons, I also did not find the unreported decisions cited by the Defence to be helpful.

29     In determining the appropriate sentence, I primarily applied the Goh Ngak Eng sentencing framework which is binding on me.

30     Applying Step four of the sentencing framework, the accused’s plea of guilt was balanced against his other TIC charge. Step five was not necessary as the accused faced a single proceeded charge. Therefore, there was little, if any, adjustments to be made in Stage 2 of the sentencing framework.

Conclusion

31     I accordingly sentenced the accused to a fine of $8,500 (in default 40 days’ imprisonment), being five times the gratification he received from the proceeded charge. Additionally, I ordered the accused to pay a penalty under s.13 of the PCA for a sum of $2,300 to disgorge the full sum of gratification he received for all the charges against him.

32     The accused has paid both the fine and the penalty.
