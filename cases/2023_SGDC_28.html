2       At the conclusion of the trial, I was satisfied that the Prosecution had proven its case against the Accused on the four charges beyond a reasonable doubt. Accordingly, I found the Accused guilty of all four charges and convicted him.

3       I imposed a sentence of eight years and four strokes of the cane for the charge under s 376(1)(b) of the Penal Code, and a sentence of 10 months’ imprisonment for each of the charges under s 354(1) of the Penal Code. The global sentence was a term of eight years and 10 month’ imprisonment, and four strokes of the cane.

4       The Accused has appealed against both the conviction and sentence, and is currently out on bail.

Introduction

5       The Accused and victim were close friends at the material time, and the victim was staying over at the Pasir Ris flat. The offences were alleged to have taken place in the bedroom of the Pasir Ris flat, when only the Accused and the victim were present. The victim maintained that he was sleeping when the incidents happened and he did not consent to these acts. The Accused denied two of the incidents, and claimed that for the other two incidents, the victim had consented to the acts, as they were in a close and intimate relationship at the material time.

The court’s decision on conviction

The law/legal position

87     The incidents in April, May and June 2018 were framed as outrage of modesty offences, under s 354(1) of the Penal Code. For these charges, two elements must be proven[note: 56]:

(a)     criminal force was used on the victim; and

(b)     the Accused knew it to be likely that this would thereby outrage the modesty of the victim.

88     The incident in July 2018 was framed as a charge of sexual penetration under s 376(1)(b) of the Penal Code. For this charge, the following elements must be proven:

(a)     Accused had caused the victim to penetrate his mouth with the victim’s penis; and

(b)     The victim did not consent to the penetration.

An assessment of the victim’s evidence

89     The Prosecution’s case relied heavily, although not solely, on the victim’s evidence. Due to various case management reasons unrelated to the victim, he ended up being on the stand for close to two years, from July 2020 to March 2022. Throughout this time, and despite having undergone intense, repetitive and sometimes aggressive cross examination, I found his evidence to be largely consistent and credible. He came across as a frank witness who was not afraid to admit to various things he did, such as changing the Accused’s contact on his phone to the Malay equivalent of “Sick Bastard” after the incidents, punching the Accused on at least two occasions (after the second incident and on 29 July 2018), and disposing of the Accused’s mobile phone on 29 July 2018. He was also candid in admitting that his relationship with the Accused was a close one prior to the fall out on 29 July 2018, and they had shared many experiences together. Although there were many specific events the victim could not remember the details of, he was firm that he never engaged in any intimate acts with the Accused and his relationship with the Accused was that of close friends and nothing more.

90     That said, there were some inconsistencies in the victim’s evidence which required a closer examination, and I list three of them below.

Inconsistency in the number of skin-to-skin incidents

91     During his examination in chief in July 2020, the victim confirmed that the incidents in April and May involved skin-to-skin contact directly on his penis, while the June incident involved a touch over his pants. However, one year later in July 2021 and while under cross examination, the victim stated that there was no skin-to-skin contact during the April and May incidents. When confronted over this inconsistency, the victim conceded that he had actually forgotten and that the version he had first told the court was probably the correct one. However, he went on to say that there was only one incident where there was skin-to-skin contact.[note: 57]

92     In my view, this inconsistency in the victim’s evidence was not fatal to the credibility of his evidence in its entirety, and certainly not directly relevant at the conviction stage. I noted that the charges for the April and May incidents did not state explicitly whether the groping or rubbing action was over or under his clothes. All the Prosecution had to prove to make out their case for conviction was that the Accused had groped the victim’s penis in April 2018, and rubbed the victim’s penis in May 2018. This was something the victim consistently maintained throughout his evidence in court.

93     It is also apposite at this stage to refer to the reminder by the High Court in PP v Singh Kalpanath [1995] 3 SLR(R) 158 at [60] to give adequate allowance to the “human fallibility in retention and recollection”, and to appreciate that no one can describe the same thing exactly in the same way over and over again. I should add that the only person who can do that is perhaps someone who has memorised a script.

94     Having assessed the entirety of the victim’s evidence, I accepted that this inconsistency did not detract from his credibility, and that it was not unreasonable for him not to be able to remember every detail of each incident which happened more than three years ago. In so far as conviction was concerned, I was therefore satisfied that the victim’s evidence was still sufficiently clear and consistent to make out the actus reus for each charge against the Accused. However, the fact remained that there was a little uncertainty as to which incidents were skin-to-skin, and this was something which would have an impact on sentence. I deal with this later.

17 May incident could not have taken place in the afternoon

95     This was not so much an inconsistency as an inaccuracy. According to the victim, he recalled that the second incident took place on 17 May 2018 sometime after 12 p.m. when he woke up.[note: 58] However, this was shown to be inaccurate because the Accused would have been working at the relevant time, according to his attendance sheet tendered as exhibit D9. Based on this evidence, I accepted that the incident could not have taken place in the afternoon as stated by the victim.

96     Nevertheless, I viewed this as a minor inaccuracy which could be attributed to a reasonable lapse in memory on the part of the victim. It is not uncommon for dates and times to get hazy when one is trying to recall an incident that occurred a long time ago. In the present case, there was some support for the fact that the incident had indeed occurred on 17 May 2018, which the victim remembered as the second day of the fasting month. The Accused confirmed in his investigative statement at exhibit P8 that he had indeed touched the victim’s penis on the second day of the fasting month.[note: 59] The victim recalled he had punched the Accused following the second incident, and the Accused also confirmed the same in exhibit P8. Given this, I was satisfied that the victim’s evidence remained largely consistent and accurate, and the second incident had indeed occurred on 17 May 2018, with the only uncertainty being the time at which it occurred. This was clearly not fatal to the charge, which as drafted only refers to the second incident occurring sometime in May 2018. This was something the victim never wavered from.

The date of the July incident

97     There was some inconsistency when the victim referred to the July incident. While he initially confirmed it had happened on 28 July 2018, he later stated that he thought it occurred on 29 July 2018.[note: 60] However, he eventually confirmed again that it occurred on 28 July 2018.[note: 61] To my mind, this was not significant, given that his evidence with regard to what actually happened during the incident was consistent.

98     Apart from the three inconsistencies listed above, the victim’s evidence was largely coherent and consistent. Importantly it was also externally consistent with other evidence, which I will deal with later.

99     There were other aspects of the victim’s evidence which had been highlighted by the Defence in the course of cross examination and submissions, and I will now deal with them.

Why did the victim continue staying on in the Pasir Ris flat?

100    According to the victim, he chose to continue staying at the Pasir Ris flat with the Accused, even after the first, second, and third incidents. This might seem unusual, given that the victim was free to move, and might have been expected to do so if his modesty had indeed been outraged by the Accused. In fact, not only did the victim continue staying in the Pasir Ris flat, but he appeared to have continued his usual interactions with the Accused – hanging out together and even buying matching Hari Raya clothes. As the Defence pointed out, all the way until the evening of 28 July 2018, the victim appeared to have maintained a cordial relationship with the Accused.[note: 62] On the evidence, I accepted that this was the case.

101    The first principle to be observed is that victims of sexual crimes cannot be straightjacketed in the expectation that they must act or react in a certain manner. (GBR v PP & Anor Appeal [2018] 3 SLR 1048 (“GBR”) at [20].) Not every victim will be able to confront their attacker directly and immediately after a sexual assault.

102    When asked why he did not leave the Pasir Ris flat after the incidents, the victim explained that his cousin was still living in the Tampines flat, and he had forgiven the Accused, as the Accused had helped him when he was in need. He also did not want to trouble anyone else when he was already staying with the Accused. [note: 63]

103    In my view, the victim’s explanation was well within the realm of reasonable possibilities, given how close the victim and Accused were. It was not disputed that they spent a lot of time together in the period 2017 – 2018, and that the Accused had paid for much of the victim’s expenses, including the overseas trips to Bali and Bangkok. In fact, it was put to the victim during cross examination that he was financially dependent on the Accused. The victim also agreed that he depended on the Accused for financial support during the period April to July 2018 when he was staying at the Pasir Ris flat, although he also pointed out that he had returned some of the money to the Accused.[note: 64] The evidence therefore pointed to a close-knit relationship between the two, one in which the victim was in some respects dependent on the Accused, and which I accepted might not have been so easy and quick for the victim to walk away from.

Victim’s work at the dental clinic

104    One of the reasons the victim gave for staying at the Pasir Ris flat from April 2018 onwards was his work at a dental clinic, Luminous, nearby. However, according to the victim’s CPF statements during the relevant period[note: 65], the last contribution from Luminous was in March 2018, thus indicating that the victim was no longer working there from April 2018. In fact, the victim did confirm that he had resigned from the job in the first or second week of April 2018.[note: 66]

105    This was a point raised by the Defence several times in the course of cross examination, and it was put to the victim that he was lying about his reason for wanting to stay at the Pasir Ris flat, and that he had in fact lied to the Accused as well. However, I did not find that the victim had lied about this, or that this significantly impacted on the victim’s evidence. It was never the victim’s position that his sole or main reason for staying at the Pasir Ris flat was because of his job at Luminous. [note: 67]

106    It was also clear from the evidence that the victim had not lied to the Accused about his job at Luminous. According to the Accused’s own evidence in court, the victim told him sometime in the middle of April that he had been fired from his job at Luminous.[note: 68] There was therefore no basis to find that the victim had either lied to the Accused in order to get the Accused to agree to let him stay at the Pasir Ris flat, or that the victim had lied to the court about the length of his employment at Luminous.

107    In any case, I failed to see how this point fitted in with the Defence’s case that the Accused and victim were already in a relationship at that point. If that were true, then presumably the victim would not have had to lie to the Accused in order to stay at the Pasir Ris flat anyway.

The victim’s post incident behaviour after 28 July 2018

108    Even after the fourth incident which involved an act of fellatio, the victim did not immediately leave the Pasir Ris flat. It was only after the Accused got angry at him for sending the message on the first page of exhibit D6 to delete the video on his mobile phone and chased the victim out of the flat, did the victim eventually leave.

109    When confronted with this behaviour, the victim explained he did not know where to go since his cousin’s family was still at the Tampines flat. There were too many things on his mind and he was still shocked over the incident. He was not able to think straight and decide what was the best course of action for himself. Although he intended to leave the Pasir Ris flat, he had not decided when he would do so.[note: 69] The victim also said he was surprised when the Accused chased him out of the house and he kept asking the Accused for an explanation because he felt he was the one who was supposed to be angry, but instead the Accused got angry. This made the victim mad, which resulted in the quarrel and short burst of violence between the Accused and victim before the victim left the Pasir Ris flat.

110    While it may seem odd at first glance that it was the Accused who eventually chased the victim out of the flat, rather than the victim taking the initiative to leave, I was mindful of the contextual background against which this took place, and in particular the close and dependent relationship the victim shared with the Accused. The victim was staying at the Accused’s flat and had no ready place to move to. His evidence was that he had been scared during the fellatio incident, and had not reacted so that the Accused would not know he knew the Accused had fellated him. In these circumstances, it was not unreasonable for the victim to be still trying to process what happened and decide on a reaction.

111    I noted in particular that on the day following the incident, although the victim could not recall with clarity what happened, he mentioned that he had probably met his friends that night, and he just wanted to get out of the house and not spend time with the Accused.[note: 70] This was in fact borne out to a certain extent by the evidence that the victim had declined to go rock climbing with the Accused’s family that evening, and although he engaged in seemingly normal banter with the Accused on WhatsApp, he had also stayed out late[note: 71] and at 1.55 a.m. on 29 July 2022, checked with the Accused if he was still awake.[note: 72] This would be consistent with the victim trying to avoid the Accused as much as he could, such that the victim only returned after the Accused had fallen asleep. The victim also explained that he was trying to give the impression to the Accused that he did not know anything as he was still scared and not sure how the Accused would react if he knew that the victim was aware of what had happened.[note: 73]

112    Against this background, I was satisfied that the victim’s behaviour after the fellatio incident did not contradict his evidence and was still within the realm of reasonable possibilities of how a person would react after having been molested.

113    I also noted that there was no significant gap of time between the fourth incident and police report, which was lodged just a few days later, on 1 August 2018. I accepted the victim’s explanation that he had to think before lodging a police report as the Accused was like a brother who had helped him in the last few months.

114    In summary, I found the victim’s evidence as a whole to be credible, coherent and consistent, bearing in mind that the incidents occurred at least two years ago from the start of the trial, and the fact that the victim himself was on the stand for a period of close to two years. More importantly, the victim’s evidence was also externally consistent and corroborated by other evidence.

The victim’s evidence was corroborated

115    I found that the victim’s evidence was corroborated by the post-incident WhatsApp messages exchanged between the victim and the Accused, the Accused’s investigative statements, the Accused’s IMH report and evidence from PW6.

The WhatsApp messages

116    The WhatsApp messages exchanged between the victim and Accused on 30 July 2018 (as reflected in exhibit P4) painted a very clear picture of the Accused apologising for the wrongs he committed against the victim, wrongs which warranted a report to the “authorities” and for which the Accused could end up in prison. In the conversation, the victim also told the Accused he had given the Accused many chances. The messages did not explicitly refer to the acts of molest, but there were references made to sexually transmitted diseases, the Accused having “many penises … to suck” in prison, and the Accused admitting that he was not strong enough to control his “lust”. On a reading of the messages as a whole, I was satisfied that the conversation was wholly in line with the victim’s account of events, and that the Accused was trying to placate the victim and apologising for the acts of molest which he had committed against the victim. No other credible interpretation of the messages was proffered by the Accused in evidence.

The Accused’s investigative statements

117    The victim’s evidence was also corroborated by the Accused’s investigative statements, where he admitted to having fondled the victim’s penis on one occasion, and fellating the victim on another. According to the Accused, he thought that the victim was sleeping on both occasions, and he had done the acts out of curiosity. In these statements, the Accused also admitted that the victim had confronted the Accused over his sexuality, and even punched him after the incident on the second day of the fasting month. The Accused admitted he could not control himself and he did not obtain any consent from the victim before touching the victim’s penis or fellating him. This was all consistent with the victim’s account.

118    I noted that the Accused did not challenge the admissibility of his statements, although he tried to explain that he had omitted to say certain things because of his state of mind at the relevant time, and his failure to appreciate the legal niceties around consent. I did not accept this explanation, simply because it did not sit well at all with the contents of the statements, which were straightforward. At one point in the course of recording exhibit P8, the Accused was asked point blank if he had obtained any consent from the victim before touching him or fellating him, and the simple answer he gave was “No”.[note: 74] If indeed, the sexual acts between the victim and the Accused had been consensual, this would surely have featured in the Accused’s response to the police at some point. Tellingly, it did not. I found the Accused’s explanation in court for his statements to amount to no more than an attempt to re-interpret the contents of those statements, with no credible reason put forward as to why the court should have to go through leaps of logic to put a spin on what was otherwise a very straightforward statement. Accordingly, I placed weight on the contents of the Accused’s investigative statements as they were.

The Accused’s IMH report

119    The contents of the IMH report prepared by PW8 were strikingly similar to the contents of the Accused’s investigative statements. This had the effect of reinforcing the evidential strength of each, since the contents had been given by the Accused on two separate occasions to two separate persons, and with very different purposes. PW8 was not there to investigate the truth of the matter, but simply to assess the Accused’s mental state. Yet, PW8’s understanding of what the Accused was trying to say, was also consistent with the contents of the Accused’s investigative statements.

120    According to the IMH report, the Accused had touched the victim a few times “inappropriately”, the victim had eventually “confronted” him about it and asked about his sexuality, and the Accused had apologised to the victim. PW8 also recalled that he had asked the Accused if the victim had been asleep at the relevant time and the Accused did not know. This was consistent with the Accused’s investigative statements as well as the victim’s own account of events, and had the effect of corroborating the victim’s evidence.

Evidence from PW6

121    According to PW6, the victim had called her on 28 July 2018 at about 9 p.m. to complain to her about the Accused fellating him. This was consistent with the victim’s account in two aspects. The first was that the act of fellatio had occurred before this time i.e. on 28 July 2018 at about 4 a.m., instead of the night of 28 July 2018 as claimed by the Accused. The second and more important aspect is that the act was non-consensual. If the Accused and the victim had been in an intimate relationship and engaged in mutually consensual sexual acts on a regular basis, there was no reason for the victim to suddenly call PW6 on 28 July 2018 to complain.

122    In addition, PW6 did not report having known of anything beyond friendship going on between the victim and the Accused, despite having gone on an overseas trip to Bali with the two of them.

123    Therefore the victim’s evidence was shown to be both internally and externally consistent – a coherent set of facts which fell in line with the other pieces of evidence tendered during the trial.

Accused’s account lacked credibility and corroboration

124    In contrast, the Accused’s account lacked coherence and consistency.

125    The relationship he described with the victim was one where the victim constantly made advances towards him. For example, the victim was the one who wanted to be massaged; the victim took the initiative to kiss him; the victim used all sorts of excuses to try and touch his penis (e.g. while wrestling, while body painting, while in Bali); the victim became “touchy” and laid down on the Accused’s groin area while in Bali; and the victim asked to stay with the Accused at the Pasir Ris flat. Throughout these interactions, the Accused’s evidence appeared to be that he was constantly shocked and surprised by the victim’s actions, even for those actions which took place when they were already well into their “intimate” relationship. According to the Accused, their relationship already became “a lot more romantic” and “intimate” when the Accused stayed over at the Tampines flat from 2016 to 2017 and he described them as being happy staying with each other and spending time together. Yet according to the Accused, he was constantly surprised whenever the victim showed him affection and always “wondering” if they were in a genuine relationship. From his evidence, I could not see much reciprocity in terms of affection on his part, apart from the fact that he was always the one who massaged and touched the victim’s penis, and never the other way round. In my view, the Accused’s evidence of an intimate relationship between himself and the victim lacked substance and mutuality, and was not a realistic account. It appeared more likely to be a false narrative the Accused was painting in order to support his defence of consent by the victim.

126    This lack of substance to the relationship narrative was also amply demonstrated in the inexplicable way the relationship broke down on 29 July 2018, with a simple message from the victim asking the Accused to delete a video from his mobile phone. Rather than seeking to clarify further with the victim, the Accused’s response was to throw his mobile phone at the victim and ask him to leave the house. I did not think this was how a person in a loving and happy relationship would have reacted. According to the Accused, the relationship was always a harmonious one, and they hardly quarrelled with each other. This made it even more difficult to understand the Accused’s reaction and their mutual interactions on 28 July 2018. His explanation that he had to kick the victim out because otherwise the victim would not leave by himself [note: 75] was equally perplexing. I was unable to understand this explanation against the background of a rosy relationship which the Accused had described. On the other hand, how the events unfolded on 28 July 2018 made sense in the context of the victim’s account that he had already confronted the Accused a few times over his actions of molesting the victim, and it finally culminated in a frustrated outburst by both of them on 28 July 2018.

127    Importantly, the Accused’s account was also inconsistent with the Whatsapp conversation he shared with the victim on 30 July 2018, as well as his own investigative statements.

128    As I have referred to above, the WhatsApp messages pointed very clearly to a conversation where the Accused apologised unreservedly for transgressions he had committed against the victim. There was no reference at all in the messages to any past relationship (beyond that of friendship) which they might have shared or why they had “broken up”, as it were. Critically there was no query at all on the part of the Accused as to why the victim should be accusing him of something which they had mutually consented to doing. The Accused’s attempt to explain away the WhatsApp messages was not convincing. He claimed that some of the messages were referring to his actions of kicking the victim out of the house, while conceding that some of the messages were about the intimate acts they did. This made no sense, and there would be no logical flow to the messages if the Accused’s explanation was accepted.

129    The Accused also claimed that whenever he admitted his wrong, he was merely admitting that he was wrong from a moral and religious standpoint, not a legal one. However, I would point out that according to the Accused’s own evidence, he would have been sent a screenshot of the Penal Code offence provision before this conversation took place, and would therefore have known that the victim was referring to him doing something not only morally or religiously wrong, but legally wrong as well. Yet there were no references at all in the messages to the Accused trying to clarify this with the victim. Ultimately the Accused never gave an adequate explanation for the messages which would explain why it should be interpreted in a different way. In fact, up to the time he filed his closing submissions, he was still not able to give a good explanation for his messages, as evidenced by constant references in the Defence Closing Submissions to the fact that a message (sent by the Accused) “may refer to”, “could refer to” and even at times simply conceding that it was unclear what the Accused was referring to![note: 76]

130    In his statements to the police, the Accused had admitted to touching the victim’s penis on at least one occasion and fellating the victim, and he also confirmed he did not obtain the victim’s consent before performing these acts. He explained that he could not control himself and he also described how the victim had confronted him and even punched him for his actions. He denied harbouring any romantic feelings towards the victim, or that he was in an intimate relationship. His account to the forensic psychiatrist PW8 was similar.

131    This was starkly different from the evidence the Accused gave in court of a loving relationship with the victim where sexual acts had taken place by consent. Ultimately the Accused failed to properly account for the statements he gave to the police and PW8, and I was not satisfied that there was any other reasonable interpretation to these statements other than what the contents were on their face. In particular, I noted that even when the Accused was served the fellatio charge for the purpose of recording a cautioned statement under s 23 of the Criminal Procedure Code 2020 (“CPC”), and would presumably have known by then that he was being accused of a serious offence, he still made no mention of any intimate relationship he had with the victim or that the victim had consented to these acts. It did not require someone to be familiar with the legal niceties of consent and the elements of the offence in order to do a simple clarification with the police, or to at least mention that it was a mutual act. However, the Accused did not do so. I was therefore left to the conclusion that the defence of consent must clearly have been an afterthought.

132    There were other aspects of the Accused’s evidence which did not hold up as well:

(a)     According to the Accused, after he performed the act of fellatio on the victim on the night of 28 July 2018, the victim went out of the room and returned shortly, presumably to go to the toilet. The two of them then went to sleep. In this context, the message from the victim sent to the Accused’s phone on 29 July 2018 at 1.55 a.m.[note: 77] asking if the Accused had slept yet, made no sense. On the other hand, the message was consistent with the victim’s account of having left the house on the night of 28 July 2018 and would also have been consistent with the victim trying to avoid the Accused and therefore checking if he was asleep before he returned.

(b)     The Defence accused the victim of trying to frame the Accused because he was angry at the Accused for kicking him out of the house, and he was also afraid that the Accused would lodge a police report against him for disposing of the Accused’s mobile phone. In my view, any such retaliation would have been grossly disproportionate, having taken into account their previous happy relationship and the trouble which the victim would have to go through in order to see through his false allegation against the Accused. I also noted that in evidence, the victim never shied away from admitting that he had taken away the Accused’s phone and was clearly unapologetic for his actions. [note: 78] He certainly did not strike me as someone who would be afraid of the Accused lodging a police report against him.

(c)     While cross examining the victim, it was put to him that the act of fellatio never took place. This was quite different from Accused’s own evidence when he took the stand. The case of consensual fellatio was never put to the victim, and he was never given a chance to respond to this. This furthered my view that the defence of consent was merely an afterthought.

133    I therefore found that the Accused’s account was not credible or coherent and not supported at all by the other evidence in this case.

DW2’s evidence did not assist the Accused

134    DW2 never saw any sexual acts between the Accused and the victim, and although his evidence was that he thought there may have been something romantic going on between the Accused and the victim, he did not know for sure.

135    DW2 is the Accused’s brother and would understandably be eager to help the Accused. Even so, there were some aspects of his evidence which appeared to be inconsistent with the Accused’s account. According to DW2, he could not recall anyone being home on the night of 28 July 2018 when he returned home from rock climbing with the Accused and their father. This was inconsistent with the Accused’s account that the victim was already home by the time they returned from rock climbing. In addition, DW2’s evidence was that the Accused and the victim often slept late and could be seen together in the kitchen at about 2 a.m. to 4.30 a.m. on weekends. This was again inconsistent with the Accused’s evidence that he would sleep early almost everyday, and before the victim.

136    Therefore, I found that the evidence from DW2 did not assist the Accused at all.

Conclusion on conviction

137    I agreed with the Prosecution that the “unusually convincing” standard need not apply to the victim’s evidence in the present case, because the victim’s evidence was not the only piece of evidence relied on by the Prosecution. In particular, there was also evidence from the Accused’s own investigative statements and IMH report. Nevertheless, it remained the case that the burden was on the Prosecution to prove all four charges against the Accused beyond a reasonable doubt.

138    As I had set out above, I found that the victim’s evidence was credible and coherent, and also consistent with other evidence such as the WhatsApp messages, the Accused’s investigative statements, the contents of the IMH report and other evidence from PW6 and DW2. On the other hand, the Accused’s account was incoherent, sometimes incomprehensible and starkly inconsistent with the other pieces of evidence. Weighing the evidence in its totality, I was satisfied that the four incidents of sexual assault in April, May, June and July 2018 had taken place as described by the victim, and that the victim had not consented to any of the acts. There was no intimate relationship between the Accused and the victim, and in touching the victim’s penis and fellating him, the Accused must have known that this was likely to outrage the victim’s modesty. In these circumstances, I was of the view that the Prosecution had proven the four charges against the Accused beyond a reasonable doubt, and convicted him accordingly.

145    The Prosecution accepted that the benefit of the doubt should be given to the Accused with regard to whether the actions had involved skin-on-skin contact, since the victim had been inconsistent in his evidence on this point. The offender and victim had been acquainted for a few days and the offender hosted the victim in his room when the victim was in Singapore. The incident occurred when the victim was asleep. The offender was sentenced to a term of seven years’ imprisonment with three strokes of the cane.

(b)      GCO v PP [2019] 3 SLR 1402 (“GCO”), where the offender pleaded guilty to two charges, one of which was for outraging the modesty of a friend by touching her vagina area over her underwear when she was asleep. This was a charge under s 354(1) of the Penal Code, and for which the offender was sentenced to a term of eight months’ imprisonment.

147    Given that two of the sentences had to run consecutively under s 307 of the CPC, the global sentence sought for by the Prosecution amounted to between nine years’ and 10 months imprisonment, and 11 years’ imprisonment, and between six and eight strokes of the cane.

Mitigation

148    In mitigation, the Defence highlighted that there was no premeditation, deception or the use of force in the present case. The Defence also stressed that this was not a case involving an abuse of trust since the Accused was not in a position of authority or responsibility towards the victim, and that in fact the victim was slightly older than the Accused. The victim was not a particularly vulnerable person.

149    With regard to the charge under s 376(1)(b) of the Penal Code, and referring to the sentencing bands set out in Pram Nair, the Defence submitted that the present case fell within Band 1. Defence also relied on the following precedents:

(a)      ABC v Public Prosecutor [2022] SGHC 244 (“ABC”) involving offences against a 14 year old victim, including sexual assault under s 376(2)(a) of the Penal Code. While initially sentenced to six years and three strokes of the cane, this was later reduced on appeal to a term of three years and six months, with no caning.

(b)      Public Prosecutor v CJH [2022] SGHC 303 (“CJH”) involving an offender who had raped and sexually assaulted his younger sister. The charges were framed under s 376A(1)(a) punishable under s 376A(3) of the Penal Code for the sexual penetration of a minor below the age of 16 years. He pleaded guilty and was sentenced to a term of 10 years’ and eight strokes for one of the charges, and eight years and four strokes of the cane for the other two charges. The global sentence was a term of 18 years’ imprisonment and 16 strokes of the cane. At the time of the offences, the offender was between 15 and 18 years old.

150    With regard to the charges under s 354(1) of the Penal Code, the Defence argued that the facts of this case were less aggravated than the facts in Kunasekaran, as that involved an offence committed on a bus and against a 14 year old victim.

151    In summary, the Defence submitted for a term of not more than seven years’ imprisonment for the charge under s 376(1)(b) of the Penal Code and a term of less than five months’ imprisonment for the charge under s 354(1) of the Penal Code.

152    The Defence also highlighted that the Accused had been remanded from 1 August to 3 September 2018.

Court’s decision on sentence

153    There was no dispute between parties that the applicable sentencing frameworks were set out in Pram Nair and Kunasekaran.

154    With regard to the charge under s 376(1)(b) of the Penal Code, I agreed with the Prosecution that the following offence-specific aggravating factors could be taken into account:

(a)     The Accused had taken advantage of the victim while he was asleep and therefore vulnerable.

(b)     The Accused and the victim were close friends at the material time, and the Accused had abused the trust reposed in him as a friend when he committed the offence. This offence also took place against the background of the victim having come to seek shelter with the Accused. The Accused’s own evidence was that during the period in question, he had financially supported the victim. The Accused would therefore have known that the victim relied on him to a certain extent. In addition, prior to the fellatio incident, the victim had already indicated to the Accused on more than one occasion that he did not welcome these sexual advances from the Accused. While the Accused was not strictly speaking in a position of responsibility towards the victim, he had abused the trust which the victim had in him as a close friend, and exploited his position as a close friend, and someone whom the victim relied on and trusted.

155    That said, I also noted that the victim did not belong to a particularly vulnerable class of victims, and the abuse of trust was not as egregious as in instances where the offender stood in a position of responsibility towards the victim. I was therefore of the view that the facts of the case put it in Band 1, with a starting point of about eight years’ imprisonment and four strokes of the cane.

156    In relation to offender-specific aggravating factors, I found it troubling that the Accused had already been confronted by the victim on previous occasions, and yet he still went on to escalate his offending actions. This was not a one-off incident which was out of character, but appeared to demonstrate a pattern of behaviour which the Accused was not planning to get out of on his own.

157    I did not find any significant mitigating factors, save for the fact that the Accused was only about 22 years old when he committed the offences, and prior to this had a clean record. I also noted that he had spent about one month in remand in Aug 2018.

158    All in, I was of the view that a term of eight years’ imprisonment and four strokes of the cane for the fellatio charge would be appropriate. As against the case in Perez Ryan, an uplift in the present case was warranted on the basis of the greater abuse of trust and the pattern of escalating offending behaviour.

159    I did not place much weight on the cases of ABC or CJH. ABC was a case which was explicitly decided on the basis of case law prior to Pram Nair, and involved a different offence provision in the context of a minor who had consented to the acts and an offender who pleaded guilty. It was difficult to draw comparisons with the present case. As for CJH, it could arguably be said to be more aggravating than the facts of the present case, but the offender was very young at time of the offence, and had pleaded guilty. Again, I found it difficult to draw comparisons and on the whole, did not find these two cases helpful.

160    With regard to the three charges for outrage of modesty, I agreed that the benefit of the doubt had to be given to the Accused and that it was only safe to sentence the Accused on the basis of him touching the victim over his clothes, and not skin-on-skin.

161    In trying to place the case within the correct sentencing band as set out in Kunasekaran, I found that the following offence-specific aggravating factors were present:

(a)     The degree of sexual exploitation, while not the most severe, was nevertheless significant. The Accused had groped and rubbed the victim’s penis. The touch was not fleeting, as it woke the victim from his sleep and the victim had to move away before it stopped.

(b)     There was some degree of persistence on the part of the Accused, given that he had molested the victim on more than one occasion, and even after the victim had confronted him about it. This was not a one-off act of impulse by the Accused.

(c)     The Accused had exploited his friendship with the victim, taking advantage of the victim’s reliance on him for shelter to commit the offences. The Accused had also molested the victim when he was asleep and vulnerable.

(d)     The victim suffered some harm and had indicated that he was now fearful of “gay people”. He also shared that he tried his best not to recall the incidents, but that it was hard to cope. When his friends sometimes make jokes about homosexuals, it would also hurt his feelings.[note: 79]

162    Bearing the above in mind, I agreed with the Prosecution that this case fell somewhere in the middle of Band 2, and that a starting point of 10 months’ imprisonment would be appropriate. While there was no skin-on-skin contact with the victim’s penis, there was groping and rubbing, and a number of aggravating factors as listed above.

163    As with the case of the fellatio charge, I did not find any significant mitigating factors.

164    Comparing the facts of this case with GCO, I was of the view that the present case was more aggravated. In GCO, the molest had been opportunistic and fleeting, and appeared to be one-off. In contrast, the Accused’s actions in the present case were more predatory, taking place on three separate occasions over the course of three months, and against the background of an abuse of friendship trust.

165    In addition, I did not agree with counsel’s characterisation of the present case as being significantly less aggravated than the facts in Kunasekaran, Kunasakaran involved a very different factual context between strangers on a public bus, and a 14 year old victim. While there were certainly some aggravating aspects in Kunasekaran which were not present here, there were similarly aggravating features in the present case which were not present in Kunasekaran, such as the pattern of escalating conduct, the type of touch (groping and rubbing), a sleeping victim, the background of relationship between the Accused and the victim etc. I therefore found it more helpful to focus on the factors in the present case, and to see how it fared against a case like GCO, which was more similar on its facts.

166    Having examined the aggravating and mitigating factors, as well as the relevant precedents, I saw no reason to depart from the starting point of 10 months’ imprisonment, and imposed the same for the three charges under s 354(1) of the Penal Code.

Conclusion

168    The fact that the incidents had taken place behind closed doors and in the Accused’s bedroom when only the victim and the Accused were present, necessarily meant that much of this case had to turn on an assessment of the credibility of the victim and Accused. At the end of the day, I found that the victim’s evidence held up against close scrutiny, and presented an account which cohered with the other supporting evidence to form a compelling narrative of events. The Accused’s account, riddled with inconsistencies and incoherence, failed to raise a reasonable doubt in the Prosecution’s case and he was convicted accordingly.

169    The total sentence was a term of eight years and 10 months’ imprisonment, and four strokes of the cane. I was of the view this sentence was in accordance with the sentencing precedents, and was justified and appropriate in the circumstances.
