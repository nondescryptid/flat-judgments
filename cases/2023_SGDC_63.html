Pre-sentence report

8       Section 304(2) CPC provides as follows:

(2)    Where a person 30 years of age or above —

(a)    is convicted before the General Division of the High Court or a District Court of an offence punishable with imprisonment for 2 years or more, and has been convicted in Singapore or elsewhere at least 3 times since he or she reached 16 years of age for offences punishable with such a sentence, and was on at least 2 of those occasions sentenced to imprisonment or corrective training; or

(b)    …

then, if the court is satisfied that it is expedient for the protection of the public that the person should be detained in custody for a substantial period of time, followed by a period of supervision if released before the expiry of his or her sentence, the court, unless it has special reasons for not doing so, must sentence him or her to preventive detention for a period of 7 to 20 years in lieu of any sentence of imprisonment, or any sentence of imprisonment and fine.

9       As the accused satisfied the technical requirements for PD under s.304(2)(a) of the CPC, I called for a pre-sentencing report (“PSR”) on 27 February 2023 to assess the accused’s suitability for PD under s.304(3) of the CPC.

10     On 16 March 2023, Principal Correctional Rehabilitation Specialists Lau Kuan Mei and Troy Jack Thevathasan submitted their PSR on the accused. The PSR was accompanied by a medical memorandum that certified the accused physically and mentally suitable for the PD regime.

Presentation and behaviour during interview

11     The accused was willing to undergo the interview conducted in English and appeared to be in a positive mood. He was attentive throughout the session and was able to respond to questions coherently. Whilst he reported a past diagnosis of depression and schizophrenia, he appeared to be of stable mental condition as he did not present with any symptoms of the said mental illnesses.

Report findings

12     The PSR highlighted the general risk factors as follows:

(a)      Family/marital circumstances. The accused reported a cordial relationship with his mother although she could not be reached to corroborate the information during the preparation of the PSR. His father has passed away and his sister is no longer in contact with the family. Despite the reported cordial relationship, familial support appeared to be limited. The accused neither shared his problems with his mother nor considered seeking her help.

(b)      Employment history. The accused reported infrequent participation in the labour market and that he did not work longer than six months due to frequent incarcerations. During the recent period in the community, the accused was not able to work due to mental health issues and reported that the side effects from his medication made it difficult for him to hold a job. He had enrolled in two courses to enhance his chances of employment but failed to complete them. The accused received financial help from the Social Service Office but reported that the financial aid was insufficient.

(c)      Companions. The accused admitted socialising mainly with peers who either had a criminal record or were currently involved in antisocial activities. He said some of his friends who were drug abusers had offered him drugs and that they were open to illegal means of financial gain.

(d)      Leisure/recreation activities. The accused’s account indicated an absence of organised prosocial activities in his routine although his choice of recreational activities like watching movies at home and taking walks alone was not an area of concern.

(e)      Alcohol/drug issues. The accused reported regular alcohol consumption with his friends and indicated it had little interference with his life. He also has a history of drug use but informed that his last consumption was prior to his last sentence. The accused recognised the need to stop taking drugs and cited his mother as his source of motivation.

(f)      Anti-social patterns. The accused presented with early and diverse antisocial behaviour and a pattern of generalised trouble. For the current offences, the accused reported facing financial difficulty which resulted in him spending a large portion of his time on illegal activities to supplement the financial aid he was receiving.

(g)      Attitudes/orientation. The accused reported that he committed the current offences as a quick solution for a lot of money. His financial difficulty was a significant problem as he informed that he continued to offend during the period he was released on court bail and when a warrant of arrest was issued against him. He was cited as saying “what to do, no money?” to express a lack of options to explain his continued offending. The accused reported a motivation to change but had re-offended because of dire circumstances in the community with no employment and limited support.

(h)      General risk of criminal recidivism. The accused was categorised into the “Moderate-high risk/need” level of criminal re-offending. He belonged to a group of prisoners with a 43.8% probability of recidivism within two years of release.

(i)      Protective factors. The PSR indicated that the accused reported being able to continue ties with his family and generally presented with a support for convention.

Prosecution’s Submissions on Sentence

13     The Prosecution submitted for the accused to be sentenced to PD for a term of 7 years for the protection of the public given the accused’s long history of offending, the risk factors identified in the PD report, the absence of significant protective factors, and the high likelihood of recidivism.

Mitigation & Defence’s Submissions on Sentence

14     The Defence submitted for the accused to be sentenced to regular imprisonment as the accused was not a recalcitrant offender – he had committed the current offences not because of his criminal tendencies but out of financial desperation and for a material need.

Decision on Sentence

The law on preventive detention

15     In Re Salwant Singh s/o Amer Singh [2019] 5 SLR 1037 at [52], Menon CJ held:

It is well established that the foundation of the sentence of preventive detention is the need to protect the public. This is clear from the wording of s 304(2) of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 68, 2012 Rev Ed) itself … which states that the court shall sentence the accused to preventive detention if the court is satisfied that “it is expedient for the protection of the public”.

16     The Court of Appeal in PP v Rosli Bin Yassin [2013] 2 SLR 831 (“Rosli”) at [11] observed that:

The overarching principle is the need to protect the public … Put simply, if the individual offender is such a habitual offender whose situation does not admit of the possibility of his or her reform, thus constituting a menace to the public (and this would include, but is not limited to, offences involving violence), a sentence of preventive detention would be imposed on him or her for a substantial period of time in order to protect the public. As Yong Pung How CJ put it in the Singapore High Court decision of PP v Wong Wing Hung [1999] 3 SLR(R) 304 (“Wong Wing Hung”) at [10], the “sentence [of preventive detention] is meant essentially for habitual offenders, who must be over the age of 30 years, whom the court considers to be beyond redemption and too recalcitrant for reformation”. The court will look at the totality of the offender’s previous convictions. (see the Singapore High Court decision of Tan Ngin Hai v PP [2001] 2 SLR(R) 152 at [7]). [emphasis in original]

17     In Ravindran s/o Kumarasamy v PP [2022] SGHC 197 (“Ravindran”), Hoong J held at [47]-[48] that:

To summarise, if an individual offender is such a habitual offender whose situation does not admit of the possibility of his reform, thus constituting a menace to the public, a sentence of PD would appropriately be imposed on him for a substantial period of time in order to protect the public. In its assessment, the court will have regard to the totality of the offender’s previous convictions viewed together with the circumstances of the offender’s present offending.

Importantly, since a sentence of PD is underpinned by the need to protect the public, it differs from a sentence of imprisonment and different considerations may apply in determining the appropriate duration and implementation of the sentence. As Yong CJ explained in Public Prosecutor v Perumal s/o Suppiah [2000] 2 SLR(R) 145 at [38]:

In this regard, I must reiterate my earlier exhortation in PP v Wong Wing Hung … at [10] not to confuse the concept of preventive detention and imprisonment, which are distinct sentences and are underpinned by different objectives and rationales. The former is essentially aimed at the protection of the public while the latter reflects the traditional policies of prevention, deterrence, rehabilitation and retribution. They are different in duration, character and implementation. As such, it would be a mistake to view them as fungible sentences.

The need to protect the public

18     It was not disputed that the technical requirements under s.304(2)(a) CPC for the imposition of PD were satisfied in the present case. Both parties also accepted the findings of the PSR without challenge. Therefore, the key issue was whether it was expedient for the protection of the public for a sentence of PD to be imposed on the accused, as opposed to a regular term of imprisonment: Sim Yeow Kee v Public Prosecutor and another appeal [2016] SGHC 209 (at [107])

19     In my view, there is a real need to protect the public against future re-offending by the accused such that a sentence of preventive detention should be imposed. In arriving at this conclusion, I had regard to the findings of the PSR and considered the accused’s history of offending, the circumstances of the present offences, and the risk of future re-offending.

History of offending

20     The accused’s criminal history spanning over 30 years spoke for itself. Three conclusions can be drawn.

(a)     First, from the accused’s long list of antecedents starting from 1988, there was no doubt that the accused was a habitual offender. The PSR noted that the accused committed his first offence at the age of 11 years old and at age 13, he was sentenced to two-year probation for the offence of Robbery with hurt under s.394 PC. Since then, the accused has been charged with new offences and was ordered to stay at the Singapore Boy’s home and had gone through multiple incarcerations including Reformative Training and two stints of Corrective Training of 8 and 10 years respectively. The history of offending behaviours included drug consumption, violence, theft, cheating, forgery, driving a motor vehicle without license, house-breaking and fraudulent possession of property. The latest set of offences related to committing harassment on behalf of unlicenced moneylenders, repeat offending for driving a motor vehicle without license, and cheating.

(b)     Second, the accused demonstrated a propensity to resort to criminality when released into society. The accused has been unable to refrain from crime for any significant period whenever he was released from incarceration, with a maximum period of about two years in between incarcerations. The accused was last released in January 2021 and barely three months after being released, committed another offence in April 2021.

(c)     Third, all the earlier punishments appeared to have limited rehabilitative or deterrent effect on the accused and were insufficient to deter the accused from turning to a life of crime and to commit further offences. Significantly, the accused had previously served two terms of Corrective Training of 8 and 10 years respectively. Barely three months after being released, the accused committed fresh offences and continued to offend even whilst on court bail and after a warrant of arrest was issued against him.

Circumstances of present offences

21     The current offences for which the accused has been convicted were serious ones and the circumstances of the accused’s present offending were consistent with the conclusions drawn above. Undeterred by his previous Corrective Training stint of 10 years, the accused shortly after release committed crimes of a similar nature including cheating and driving a motor vehicle without a license. As highlighted by the Prosecution, the accused’s present offences has also escalated to serious offences of UML harassment through the use of fire, racially sensitive gestures (placing pork meat at a unit occupied by a Malay family) and direct engagement with the occupants of various residential units, including both debtors and innocent occupants. The accused had even roped in and instructed other accomplices to commit the crimes in question (1st and 3rd to 6th charges). The accused’s criminal conduct directly impacted members of the public.

22     Whilst the Defence has argued that the accused had committed the present offences out of financial desperation, the facts also reveal the accused’s resort to the utility of crime to resolve immediate problems and the tendency to minimise the severity of the present offences. Notably, the accused’s comment of “what to do, no money?” as recorded in the PSR, speaks volumes.

23     As for the amalgamated charges of cheating by personation, driving a motor vehicle without license and driving a motor vehicle without insurance, the sustained period, and numerous occasions on which the offences were committed revealed the accused’s blatant disregard for the law.

Risk of future re-offending

24     The general risk factors highlighted in the PSR were concerning in three material aspects.

(a)     First, the accused has no significant protective factors in his favour. The accused’s social circle continued to be made up of peers who were negative influences. In particular, he continued to associate with peers who offered him drugs and have pro-criminal tendencies. The reported familial support appeared to be limited and there is an absence of organised prosocial activities in the accused’s routine. Furthermore, the accused’s employment history revealed infrequent participation in the labour market, and he faced difficulty staying employed. While the accused’s attempt to improve himself by enrolling in courses was commendable, he failed to complete the two courses which were meant to enhance his chances of employment.

(b)     Second, the accused’s behaviour and attitude suggested that he viewed crime as the quick solution to immediate problems. As the accused committed the HUML offences to gain quick monies, there is a real risk that he will continue to re-offend in the same manner, especially if he remains unemployed. While the accused expressed hope and motivation to change, he did not provide any concrete plans or proposals.

(c)     Third, I was of the view that the accused’s risk factors significantly outweighed any protective factors in this case. The accused is assessed in the PSR to have a moderate to high risk of criminal re-offending and belongs to a group of prisoners with a 43.8% probability of recidivism within two years of release. This probability was derived through a qualitative assessment of the various risk factors which were highlighted in the PSR and was not merely a matter of statistics. In PP v Iskandar bin Tahar [2017] SGDC 266, the offender had a 30% probability of recidivism within 2 years of release and PD was nonetheless imposed. In Rosli, the offender was sentenced to 20 years’ preventive detention in circumstances where his probability of recidivism was assessed to be 49% to 60%. The accused’s assessed probability of 43.8% in the present case, which falls between the cases of Iskandar and Rosli, does not preclude the possibility of a PD sentence.

25     Considering all the above in its totality, there was in my judgment a clear need to protect the public from the real risk of the accused’s future re-offending and to prevent him from being a menace to the public. He has further shown that he has a very low possibility of reform as the two previous lengthy stints of Corrective Training has failed to steer him away from a life of crime. I therefore did not think that a third stint of Corrective Training was appropriate as I had little confidence in its utility in reforming the recalcitrant accused. Section 304(2) CPC provides that once the court is satisfied that it is expedient for the protection of the public to impose a term of PD, it should do so unless it has special reasons for not doing so. Special reasons would generally refer to an accused’s physical and mental suitability for PD, and in any event, must be exceptional: PP v Perumal s/o Suppiah [2000] 2 SLR(R) 145 at [41]. As the accused has been assessed to be physically and mentally suitable to undergo PD, there were no special reasons in the present case not to impose PD.

28     The appropriate length of incarceration should not be less than 4-5 years in any event and a PD sentence for a period longer than the likely regular imprisonment term that would have been imposed was justified because the sentencing considerations behind PD sentences are inherently different. In Ravindran, the High Court held at [92] that the duration of the term of PD was guided by the extent to which the public requires protection from the offender. The imposition of a term of imprisonment would generally incorporate the consideration of proportionality. However, such a principle would not apply rigorously where PD was concerned as the overriding consideration must be the need to protect the public: Kuah Teck Hin (at [21])

29     In the circumstances, the Prosecution’s submission for the minimum of seven years PD is therefore appropriate and not wholly disproportionate given the accused’s history of offending and prior stints of lengthy Corrective Training terms.

30     I accordingly sentenced the accused to seven years of PD.

Backdating of the PD term

31     There is no prohibition against the backdating of PD sentences. Section 318(3) CPC provides as follows:

(3)    To avoid doubt, a court may under subsection (1) direct that a sentence of imprisonment, corrective training or preventive detention is to take effect on a date earlier than the date the sentence is passed.

32     Under s.318(4) and s.318(5) of the CPC, the court must consider any period that the offender had spent in custody and any period that the accused was released on bail when imposing PD to take effect on a date earlier than the date the sentence is passed.

33     In this case, the Prosecution did not object to the PD sentence being backdated to the date of arrest. I was also guided by the High Court in the case of Ravindran, where the accused’s PD sentence in that case was similarly backdated to the date of the offender’s arrest.

Conclusion

34     I accordingly sentenced the accused to 7 years of PD backdated to the date of his first arrest on 7 October 2021 to account for the period of remand but excluding the period the accused was released on bail. The accused was released on bail from 15 October 2021 to 22 May 2022.

35     The accused is currently serving sentence.
