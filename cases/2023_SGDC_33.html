Pre-sentence report

Calling of Pre-Sentence Report

21     On 3 November 2022, I was satisfied that the technical requirements under section 304(2)(b) Criminal Procedure Code (“CPC”) had been met and called for a pre-sentencing report to assess the accused’s suitability for PD.

22     On 21 November 2022, Ms Valerie Goh, Principal Correctional Rehabilitation Specialist, and Troy Thevathasan, Principal Correctional Rehabilitation Specialist, submitted their pre-sentence report (“PSR”) on the accused.

23     The PSR enclosed a Medical Memorandum which stated the accused was suitable for the regime of PD.

Past Criminal History

24     The PSR noted that the accused presented with an offending history of 42 years. His first brush with the law started at age 14 for common intention to commit robbery, which resulted in him being sentenced to probation. Theft and robbery-related offences continued to feature in accused’s offending history between 1980 to 1993.

25     From then to date, he was charged with offences such as voluntarily causing hurt (VCH) and drug-related offences. In 2000 and 2009, the accused was sentenced to his first PD term of 7 years with caning for a variety of offences including his first drug possession charge, and his second PD term of 8 years for a drug consumption charge, respectively.

26     During his first PD sentence in 2000, he committed the offence of voluntarily causing hurt while in prison, resulting in him being charged in court after his release and sentenced to another 6 months’ imprisonment.

27     After the accused’s second PD release in March 2017, he reported to have successfully completed his drug supervision in the community and was discharged after 11 months.

28     With the exception of an offence of wilful trespass on property to which he was given an $800 fine, the longest period he spent in the community was from March 2017 to Oct 2019, which was 2 years 7 months.

Presentation and behaviour during interview

29     Several face-to-face interview sessions were conducted with the accused.

30     At the first interview on 9 November 2022, while the accused sat opposite the interviewer, he did not provide any response when engaged with questions as to his name, whether he could understand English, or the nature of the interview. He would look at the interviewer for a couple of seconds before closing his eyes and seemed to fall asleep. He would open his eyes and make eye contact when his name was called by the interviewer, then close his eyes again. When the prison officer was notified and returned to ask the accused whether he preferred for the interview session to be another time, he nodded and was escorted away.

31     On 14 November 2022, the accused appeared for the interview and politely greeted the interviewer. When asked, he said that on 9 November 2022 he was unresponsive as he had consumed medication just before. While the accused initially verbalised his thoughts that the interview would not help him in any way given his past experiences, the accused grew in his responsiveness to questions, made good eye contact, and required little prompting for elaboration. He was similarly cooperative during the second interview on 16 November 2022 and appeared wearing a white cloth over his head, reflective of his adoption of Sikhism.

32     The accused’s account was generally consistent with other sources of information. Based on the observations in the PSR, it was assessed that the findings in the PSR were valid.

General Risk Factors

33     The accused’s account indicated that he associated with friends, both prosocial and antisocial.

34     The accused informed the interviewer that he disassociated with friends who asked him to join them drinking in pubs where they were also involved in fights, as he did not want to get into trouble. While he continued to associate with friends whom he knew during his incarceration as a youth, he reported that they have desisted from offending, were married, and advised him to do the same. The accused also had a group of childhood friends who were prosocial, supported him when he needed assistance such as raising bail, and who advised him to change, to which he would acknowledge. According to his wife, he socialised mainly within the home context, where she would be present.

35     Within the antisocial peer group, the accused appeared to have had friends who introduced him to methamphetamine during his most recent time in the community and to contacts who sold the drug, leading to his current trafficking and consumption charges. His association with these drug-taking friends, including his elder brother who took drugs, resulted in him continuing his drug consumption and incurring more drug-related charges.

36     The accused and his wife’s account suggested that his free time after work was spent mostly within the home context, where they socialised with neighbours over meals and music, or with his wife chatting and watching television programmes or movies. He would also engage in spending time with his wife’s family regularly during gatherings or visiting his mother.

37     The accused’s use of drugs and alcohol appeared to be linked to his current charges and offending history.

38     The accused reported being introduced to drugs such as marijuana and heroin during his youth but stayed away from them as he did not enjoy their effects. While he was convicted of a drug consumption charge resulting in his second PD sentence, he said that it was from a spiked drink laced with methamphetamine. The accused reported intentionally consuming methamphetamine in 2018 after being discharged from urine supervision upon introduction by a friend. He enjoyed its effects as it made him feel “energetic”.

39     He would consume the drug daily at a friend’s place (they lived in the same block) before going to work. His consumption reportedly increased over time, up until about 1-2g per day. When he could not obtain the drug, at times on weekends, he would experience being “sian” (without energy) and would replace it with alcohol instead. The accused and his wife’s account indicated that his wife was unaware of his drug taking habits until he was arrested in 2019, as it did not appear to interfere with their couple life and daily activities.

40     The accused reported a history of alcohol use, with him socialising with friends over meals and drinks at his place and at home after his release in the community in 2017. The accused said that after being dependent on prescribed drugs during his second PD sentence, he turned to alcohol as a substitute upon release. This enabled him to complete his urine supervision and be successfully discharged after 11 months. According to the accused’s wife, she was fully aware of his alcohol intake as part of his daily routine where she was often present, and she observed no noticeable changes in behaviour. His drinking, appeared to be linked to his trespass offence in April 2019, where he visited his mother and upon discovering she was not at home, attempted to enter the flat through climbing in from the outside. The accused said that as he had consumed alcohol prior, he mistakenly entered a neighbour’s flat instead of his own and was fined.

41     The accused presented with problems of adjustment in childhood, given his reported childhood trauma due to witnessing repeated physical abuse towards his mother and brothers, his running away from home in Primary Six, being placed in a Boy’s Hostel and subsequently a Boy’s Home after he ran away from the former. His criminal record reflected this last incident as a probation sentence at the age of 14 years old.

42     The accused also presented with charges of voluntarily causing hurt and institutional offences of self-inflicted harm. By his account, it appeared that he frequently reacted with violent behaviours when experiencing strong emotions of, for instance, frustration. He said that the physical pain of self-inflicted injuries helped him cope with his emotional pain, for example, during times where he was kept alone in a cell.

43     During the last two years in remand, the accused was observed to not display any of these behaviours or be charged for such institutional offences. He reported that apart from taking prescribed medications, he engaged in daily meditation and prayer, which he found helpful in managing his emotions.

44     The accused presented with some minimization of his offences, in terms of him not being the one to have printed out the wedding cards with the word “Sarajumbo”, and him claiming not to know that trafficking of methamphetamine could potentially lead to a capital charge. The accused alluded to “not thinking” when he pursued his drug habit and said that did not think he would be apprehended one day. Pertaining to the secret society “Sarajumbo” which he had founded, while others had started to follow him during the time where gangs were commonplace in 1996, he said that it naturally become inactive during his second PD sentence as prisons had improved over the years and there were systems to keep inmates safe. He appeared to have thought the word was inconsequential on the printed wedding card, given this context, and did not pay much heed given that the card had many other words on it.

45     The accused appeared to have adopted a positive shift in many pro-criminal attitudes; he reported that he used to think he was a “hero” but reflecting on the years past, he felt that he had wasted his life away. While he had his mother visit him over the many incarcerations, he said that this time around with his marriage, he had impacted another - his wife and her life. In relation to substances, he hoped to reduce reliance on current prescribed medications and be weaned off them eventually.

46     The accused displayed conventional attitudes as he regularly engaged in work, fulfilled his role as a husband by supporting his wife and taking care of the household, planned to study if given the opportunity, and was open to any intervention programmes, which he reported to have not received during his earlier sentences.

Risk Findings

47     Based on Singapore’s norms, the accused’s LS/CMI score placed him in the Moderate Risk/Need level of criminal re-offending. He belonged to a group of prisoners with a 30% probability of recidivism within 2 years of release.

48     The PSR stated that based on available sources of information, the accused presented with strengths in the areas of access to prosocial resources in the form of his wife and friends and ability to engage in regular employment. These protective factors appeared to facilitate him being in the community for the longest period of 2 years 7 months and also adopt the prosocial identity and role of a partner to his wife.

Conclusion

49     The PSR concluded that the accused presented with a moderate risk/need level of criminal re-offending. This appeared to be contributed primarily by his long history of offending, and drug and alcohol use contributing to law violations.

Additionally, his history of using various substances (medication, alcohol and most recently methamphetamine) to either cope with emotions or to feel better presents another pathway back to crime.

Prosecution’s submission on sentence

53     The Prosecution submitted for the accused to be sentenced to PD for a term of at least 16 to 17 years.

54     As the technical requirements for PD were met, and the PSR had certified that the accused was fit to undergo PD, the Prosecution submitted that it was expedient to sentence the accused to PD, having regard to his lengthy history of offending, the seriousness of the present offences, and the substantial risk of future offending.

55     Specifically, the Prosecution submitted that PD should be imposed for the following reasons:

56     First, the accused’s antecedents showed that he was a habitual and recalcitrant offender, who has continued offending undeterred by lengthy sentences. As observed in the PSR, the accused has a history of offending dating back 42 years. His offences included serious property offences (vandalism, robbery with hurt) and violence offences (rioting, voluntarily causing hurt to deter a public servant from his duty), as well as drug offences (possession and consumption of a controlled/specified drug).

57     The accused was previously sentenced to 7 years’ PD with caning in 2000 and 8 years’ PD in 2009. It was particularly concerning that these lengthy periods in custody had not deterred the accused from further offending. Indeed, by engaging in drug trafficking where he had previously been convicted of drug possession and consumption, the accused has demonstrated an escalation in offending behaviour in spite of the previous sentences of PD.

58     The accused had tended to turn back to crime after release from prison. For example, having been sentenced to 7 years’ PD in March 2000, he was sentenced again in 2007 for committing a hurt offence while in prison, before receiving a further sentence of PD in 2009. The PSR discloses that the longest period he spent in the community was from March 2017 to October 2019, being 2 years’ and 7 months. In the context of his antecedents, the Prosecution submitted that this was not to be regarded as a substantial period of time.

59     Second, the present offences were serious and supported the assessment that the accused was a hardened offender and a menace to the public.

a)     The trafficking charge. The supplying of controlled drugs in Singapore was clearly a serious harm to the public, which warrants a tough deterrent stance: Vasentha s/o Joseph v PP [2015] 5 SLR 122 at [7]-[13] (“Vasentha”). In the present case, the accused possessed a substantial quantity of methamphetamine (not less than 166.99g) for the purposes of trafficking. It was further aggravating that he had taken to trafficking to sustain his drug consumption and had attempted to escape from officers in the course of his arrest.

b)     The consumption and possession charges. The accused consumed methamphetamine on or before 3 October 2019 and 18 November 2020. The second occasion of consumption was committed while he was on court bail which also included the possession charge, further demonstrating the accused’s inability to stay away from drugs.

c)     The secret society charges. The accused has also admitted to membership of a secret society (of which he was the Senior Member and Founder) and affirmed his association with the secret society by printing its name on his wedding invitations.

60     Third, the accused had a substantial risk of future offending. The PSR discloses an assessment of the accused to have a Moderate Risk/Need level of criminal reoffending, with a 30% probability of recidivism within 2 years of release. His drug use was identified as a contributor to this risk. The Prosecution submitted that the 30% probability remained significant and did not preclude a finding that PD was appropriate: see PP v Iskandar bin Tahar [2017] SGDC 266, where the offender similarly had 30% probability of recidivism within 2 years of release and PD was nonetheless imposed.

61     It was doubtful whether the accused was truly remorseful for his actions. Despite being caught red-handed in possession of the drugs, the accused did not confirm an intention to plead guilty until after the Case for Prosecution had been served. Consistent with this, the PSR disclosed that the accused presented with some minimisation of his offences, noting his allusion to “not thinking” when he pursued his drug habit and claiming that he did not think he would be apprehended.

62     While the PSR disclosed some protective factors, such as his relationship with his wife and regular employment, these appeared to be of limited efficacy in preventing the accused from reoffending. Indeed, it was noted in the PSR that the accused’s wife was unaware of the accused’s drug taking habits, suggesting that the accused had taken steps to conceal such habits from her.

63     Having regard to all the circumstances, the Prosecution submitted that it was expedient for the protection of the public that the accused is kept in custody for a substantial period of time.

64     Given the escalation in offending, and the ineffectiveness of the previous periods of PD in deterring the accused from offending, the Prosecution submitted that a substantial uplift was warranted for the protection of the public.

65     To this end, the Prosecution submitted that the length of PD should not be less than the imprisonment term which the accused would receive had he been sentenced to regular imprisonment (for which the consideration of protecting the public was tempered by other considerations): see PP v Kamis bin Basir [2022] SGDC 297 at [39].

66     In this regard, the Prosecution submitted that the following sentences would have been imposed on the accused:

(a)     For the trafficking charge, a sentence of at least 16 years’ imprisonment would have been appropriate. Applying the conversation rate in Loo Pei Xiang Alan v PP [2015] 5 SLR 500 (“Loo Pei Xiang Alan”) at [17] to the sentencing ranges in Vasentha at [47], the indicative starting sentence for trafficking in 166.99g of methamphetamine would be 15 years’ imprisonment and 11 strokes of the cane. Having regard to the aggravating factors, and given his antecedents, an uplift of at least 1 year would be appropriate.

(b)     For the enhanced consumption charge, the minimum sentence of at least 3 years’ imprisonment would have been sufficient.

(c)     For the enhanced possession charge, the minimum sentence of at least 2 years’ imprisonment would have been sufficient, given the small amount (0.67g) of methamphetamine involved.

(d)     For the Societies Act charge, a sentence of at least 3 months’ imprisonment would have been appropriate. In PP v Muhamad Naqiuddin Khan Bin Jhangir Khan and ors [2021] SGDC 269 at [22] and [67], the court observed that the precedents indicated a sentencing tariff of about 3 months’ imprisonment for offences under section 14(3) Societies Act, where there had been long years of membership of higher participation in the society’s activity. In the present case, the accused held the position of Senior Member and Founder and had printed his Society’s name on his wedding invitations.

67     The Prosecution accordingly submitted for term of at least 16 to 17 years’ PD.

68     As for the backdating of the term of PD, the Prosecution acknowledged that section 318 CPC was amended (with effect from 31 October 2018) to specifically allow for the backdating of sentences of corrective training or preventive detention. Nonetheless, the Prosecution submitted the observations on backdating by the Court of Appel in PP v Rosli bin Yassin [2013] 2 SLR 831 (“Rosli”) at [201] were still relevant:

Given the overarching principle to protect the public, if, in fact, the offender’s situation is an extremely serious one, then we would think that the court would not consider taking into account the time the offender has spent in remand. We think that this is likely to be the norm rather than the exception simply because, in principle, situations warranting a sentence of preventive detention are likely to be very serious to begin with. Indeed, in the most extreme situations, the court might not only disregard the time the offender has spent in remand but also sentence him or her to the maximum period of 20 years of preventive detention. However, as alluded to above, we would not rule out the exceptional situation where, whilst a sentence of preventive detention is warranted, there is nevertheless some justification for sentencing that offender to less time in preventive detention, which would, inter alia (and in substance at least), take into account the time the offender has already spent in remand.

69     That said, the Prosecution submitted that it would not press the point in the present case that there should be no backdating of the PD term.

The court’s decision on sentence

Whether PD should be imposed

91     Section 304(2) CPC provides as follows:

Where a person of the age of 30 years or above —

(a)    is convicted before the General Division of the High Court or a District Court of an offence punishable with imprisonment for 2 years or more, and has been convicted in Singapore or elsewhere at least 3 times since he reached the age of 16 years of offences punishable with such a sentence, and was on at least 2 of those occasions sentenced to imprisonment or corrective training; or

(b)    is convicted at one trial before the General Division of the High Court or a District Court of 3 or more distinct offences punishable with imprisonment for 2 years or more, and has been convicted and sentenced in Singapore or elsewhere to imprisonment for at least one month since he reached the age of 16 years for an offence punishable with imprisonment for 2 years or more,

then, if the court is satisfied that it is expedient for the protection of the public that he should be detained in custody for a substantial period of time, followed by a period of supervision if released before the expiration of his sentence, the court, unless it has special reasons for not doing so, shall sentence him to preventive detention for a period of 7 to 20 years in lieu of any sentence of imprisonment, or any sentence of imprisonment and fine.

92     It was not in dispute that the technical requirements under s 304(2)(b) CPC for the imposition of a sentence of PD were satisfied in the present case.

93     As such, the key issue for the court’s determination was whether it was expedient for a sentence of PD to be imposed on the accused as opposed to a term of imprisonment.

94     In Re Salwant Singh s/o Amer Singh [2019] 5 SLR 1037 Menon CJ held at [52]:

It is well established that the foundation of the sentence of preventive detention is the need to protect the public. This is clear from the wording of s 304(2) of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 68, 2012 Rev Ed) itself … which states that the court shall sentence the accused to preventive detention if the court is satisfied that “it is expedient for the protection of the public”.

95     In Rosli the Court of Appeal observed at [20] that:

The overarching principle is the need to protect the public … Put simply, if the individual offender is such a habitual offender whose situation does not admit of the possibility of his or her reform, thus constituting a menace to the public (and this would include, but is not limited to, offences involving violence), a sentence of preventive detention would be imposed on him or her for a substantial period of time in order to protect the public. As Yong Pung How CJ put it in the Singapore High Court decision of PP v Wong Wing Hung [1999] 3 SLR(R) 304 (“Wong Wing Hung”) at [10], the “sentence [of preventive detention] is meant essentially for habitual offenders, who must be over the age of 30 years, whom the court considers to be beyond redemption and too recalcitrant for reformation”. The court will look at the totality of the offender’s previous convictions. (see the Singapore High Court decision of Tan Ngin Hai v PP [2001] 2 SLR(R) 152 at [7]). [emphasis in original]

96     In Ravindran s/o Kumarasamy v PP [2022] SGHC 197 (“Ravindran”) . Hoong J held at [47]-[48] as follows:

To summarise, if an individual offender is such a habitual offender whose situation does not admit of the possibility of his reform, thus constituting a menace to the public, a sentence of PD would appropriately be imposed on him for a substantial period of time in order to protect the public. In its assessment, the court will have regard to the totality of the offender’s previous convictions viewed together with the circumstances of the offender’s present offending.

Importantly, since a sentence of PD is underpinned by the need to protect the public, it differs from a sentence of imprisonment and different considerations may apply in determining the appropriate duration and implementation of the sentence. As Yong CJ explained in Public Prosecutor v Perumal s/o Suppiah [2000] 2 SLR(R) 145 at [38]:

In this regard, I must reiterate my earlier exhortation in PP v Wong Wing Hung … at [10] not to confuse the concept of preventive detention and imprisonment, which are distinct sentences and are underpinned by different objectives and rationales. The former is essentially aimed at the protection of the public while the latter reflects the traditional policies of prevention, deterrence, rehabilitation and retribution. They are different in duration, character and implementation. As such, it would be a mistake to view them as fungible sentences.

97     From the accused’s long list of antecedents starting from 1980 since he was 14 years of age, there was no doubt that the accused was a habitual offender. In this regard, the PSR noted that the accused presented with an offending history of 42 years. His conviction history included serious property offences (vandalism, robbery with hurt), violence offences (rioting, voluntarily causing hurt to deter public servant from his duty, voluntarily causing grievous hurt, wrongful confinement and offences related to scheduled weapons) and drug offences (possession and consumption of controlled/specified drug) for which substantial terms of imprisonment and caning had been meted out.

98     What was especially significant was that the accused has previously served two terms of PD, namely 7 years PD in 2000 with caning for drug related and violence related offences; and 8 years PD in 2009 for a drug related offence. When imposing the second PD term in 2009 in PP v Karnan s/o Arummugam & Anor [2009] SGDC 136, the District Judge observed at [127]:

There was in this case no doubt, from the antecedent record of the accused, that he is an incorrigible and recalcitrant offender.

99     The present offences were committed in 2019 and 2020, some 2 years 7 months after he was released from his 2nd PD term. Furthermore, the 5th, 6th, 7th, 10th and 11th charges were committed whilst the accused was on court bail.

100    The PSR noted both risk factors and protective factors. The PSR stated that from the accused’s antisocial peer group, the accused had friends who introduced him to methamphetamine during his most recent time in the community and to contacts who sold the drug, leading to his current trafficking and consumption charges. His association with these drug-taking friends, including his elder brother who took drugs, resulted in him continuing his drug consumption and incurring more drug-related charges.

101    The PSR clarification report stated that the accused’s long history of offending continued to pose a risk to him because of criminal and drug-using acquaintances he has made over time and behaviours and attitudes that have become a part of his repertoire. Additionally, his history of using various substances (medication, alcohol and most recently methamphetamine) to either cope with emotions or to feel better presented another pathway back to crime.

102    The accused had continued his association with his antisocial peer group even after he was charged in court on 4 October 2019. To this end, the Societies Act offence (10th charge) for which he was convicted showed that he was a member of the Sarajumbo Secret Society until 21 August 2020, for which the accused held the position of “Founder” and “Senior Member”, having founded the Society in 1996. Further, the Statement of Facts stated that he had printed the Society’s name under his name on 20 invitation cards to his wedding in his possession on 21 August 2020 (11th TIC charge), to show the pride in the gang he had founded.

103    I noted from the PSR that the accused presented with some minimization of his offences, in terms of him claiming not to know that trafficking of methamphetamine could potentially lead to a capital charge. The accused alluded to “not thinking” when he pursued his drug habit and said that he did not think he would be apprehended one day. Further, while he was convicted of a drug consumption charge after trial which resulted in his second PD sentence in 2009, he said that it was from a spiked drink laced with methamphetamine. However, his appeal against conviction and sentence was dismissed by the High Court. Pertaining to the secret society “Sarajumbo” which he had founded, while others had started to follow him during the time where gangs were commonplace in 1996, he said that it naturally become inactive during his second PD sentence. He claimed to not being the one to have printed out the wedding cards with the word “Sarajumbo”, and appeared to have thought the word was inconsequential on the printed wedding card and did not pay much heed given that the card had many other words on it.

104    For protective factors, the PSR stated that the accused presented with strengths in the areas of access to prosocial resources in the form of his wife and friends and the ability to engage in regular employment. These protective factors appeared to facilitate him being in the community for the longest period of 2 years 7 months and also adopt the prosocial identity and role of a partner to his wife. That said, in the context of his 42 years of criminal offending, I agreed with the Prosecution that a period of 2 years 7 months could not be regarded as a substantial period of time.

105    Significantly, I noted that the PSR stated that the accused and his wife’s account indicated that his wife was unaware of his drug taking habits until he was arrested in 2019 as it did not appear to interfere with their couple life and daily activities. This suggested that the accused had taken steps to conceal his drug-taking habits from her as she was quite oblivious of his drug activities despite being the person closest to him. This was even though he was consuming drugs on a daily basis at a friend’s place in the same block before going to work. In my view, his wife, prosocial friends or the fact of regular employment had little influence on preventing the influence of the accused’s antisocial peer group and his elder brother which led to his drug related offences, including some which would committed while on court bail. Simply put, these protective factors were of limited effectiveness in preventing the commission of current offences.

106    The current offences for which the accused has been convicted were serious ones which included trafficking of a significant amount (166.99g) of methamphetamine together (maximum of 20 years’ imprisonment and 15 strokes of the cane) with the enhanced consumption and enhanced possession charges (maximum of 10 years’ imprisonment and/or fine of $20,000) and Societies Act charges (maximum of 3 years’ imprisonment and/or fine of $5,000). While the total amount of methamphetamine in A1A, A1B and A1C1 was found to be more than 166.99g of methamphetamine, the Prosecution had proceeded in the 1st charge of trafficking of not less than 166.99g of methamphetamine.

107    The Defence had highlighted the accused’s conversion to Sikhism as an example for the accused’s possible reform. In my view, the fact remains that the conversion by the accused to Sikhism in prison took place in a closed environment and had yet to be tested against external influences such as the accused’s anti-social peer group. In any event, while his conversion to Sikhism had been documented in the PSR, it was not highlighted as a significant protective factor in his favour.

108    I disagreed with the Defence’s submission that the accused’s conduct was self-destructive and did not pose a threat to others or a harm to society. It should be noted that his second PD term was imposed for an offence of consumption, and this was upheld by the High Court on appeal. As noted by Yong CJ in Tan Ngin Hai v PP [2001] 3 SLR 161 at [8], offences of drug possession and consumption can attract a PD term:

In my view there is no rule of law which states that protection of the public necessarily refers to protecting them from physical bodily harm. Offences against property such as theft, and that against the peace such as affray, or that against society in general, such as the possession and consumption of drugs, are all equally offensive to the community who deserve to be well protected from the repeat perpetrators of such crimes. As such the imposition of preventive detention ought not to be restricted only to persons of violent behaviour as exhibited through the commission of violent crimes. Instead the real test is whether or not the degree of propensity towards any type of criminal activity at all is such that the offender ought to be taken out of circulation altogether in order that he be not afforded even the slightest opportunity to give way to his criminal tendencies again.

[emphasis added]

109    I agreed with the Prosecution that there has been an escalation in the accused’s behaviour from his previous drug related offences which were for possession and consumption given his current conviction for trafficking in not less than 166.99g of methamphetamine. The accused had also exhibited a blatant disregard for the law as the enhanced possession, enhanced consumption and Societies Act charges were committed while he was on court bail.

110    The great harm occasioned by drug abuse and drug trafficking and the need to deal sternly with drug traffickers has been comprehensively set out by Menon CJ in Vasentha [7] to [11] as follows:

The drug problem is a scourge. The binds of addiction make it difficult for those ensnared to break free. Singapore has recognised the evils of drug abuse and enacted legislation to address the problem since the early part of the last century. For instance, the Straits Settlements Deleterious Drugs Ordinance (No 27 of 1910) made it an offence to import, administer or possess any deleterious drugs such as diamorphine. In more recent times, legislation such as the Dangerous Drugs Ordinance (No 7 of 1951) and the Drugs (Prevention of Misuse) Act (Cap 154, 1970 Rev Ed) were passed to address the drug problem. These two pieces of legislation were the predecessors of the MDA which was enacted in 1973.

At the second reading of the Misuse of Drugs Bill in 1973, Mr Chua Sian Chin, then Minister for Home Affairs, explained that the tough penalties for traffickers were there by design in order to suppress the drug trade.

….

In 1975, the MDA was amended to provide even harsher penalties for drug traffickers. The prescribed minimum and maximum sentences for trafficking in controlled drugs were adjusted upwards; and the death penalty was introduced for trafficking in more than 30g of morphine or 15g of diamorphine (commonly known as “heroin”).

Later, in 1977, Mr Chua Sian Chin clarified that the harsh penalties for drug traffickers were underpinned by considerations of both general and specific deterrence: Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (27 May 1977) vol 37 at cols 34–35.

The MDA was subsequently amended several times to provide stiffer penalties for trafficking in controlled drugs such as cannabis mixture, methamphetamine (commonly known as “ice”) and ketamine (see Misuse of Drugs (Amendment) Act (Act 40 of 1993); Misuse of Drugs (Amendment) Act (Act 20 of 1998) and Misuse of Drugs (Amendment) Act (Act 2 of 2006)). These amendments were necessitated by changes in the local and global drug situation (including the emergence of new synthetic drugs) and they seek to ensure that we can continue to effectively curb drug abuse and drug trafficking in Singapore.

[emphasis added]

111    The PSR’s assessment of 30% probability of recidivism on the part of the accused remained significant and did not preclude a finding that PD was appropriate. In PP v Iskandar bin Tahar [2017] SGDC 266, the offender had a 30% probability of recidivism within 2 years of release and PD was nonetheless imposed. To this end, the PSR had identified the accused’s drug use as a contributor to this risk of recidivism which was in fact how the current set of offences (the majority of which were drug offences) came to be committed.

112    In the circumstances, I was of the view that risk factors significantly outweighed the protective factors in this case. As I had earlier noted, the protective factors of his wife. prosocial friends and regular employment were of limited effectiveness in preventing the influence of the accused’s antisocial peer group and his elder brother which led to his current drug related offences, including some which would committed while on court bail. The various Societies Act charges show that he continued to maintain his anti-social peer links despite his previous PD terms.

113    Given that he had previously served 2 PD terms which involved drug offences and taken with the escalation of the accused’s conduct to drug trafficking for the current offences, I found that there was a need to protect the public and it was expedient that a third PD term be imposed. I made this finding on the basis of the accused’s history of criminal behaviour, the circumstances and nature of the present offences as well as the risk of re-offending.

114    Section 304(2) CPC provides that once the court is satisfied that it is expedient for the protection of the public to impose a term of PD, it should do so unless it has special reasons for not doing so. Special reasons would generally refer to an accused’s physical and mental suitability for PD, and in any event, must be exceptional: PP v Perumal s/o Suppiah [2000] 2 SLR(R) 145 at [41]. As the accused has been medically assessed to be fit to undergo PD, I did not find any special reasons for not imposing PD.

Duration of the PD term

115    I next considered the length of the PD term to be imposed on the accused.

116    In this regard, in Ravindran Hoong J held at [92] that the duration of the term of PD was guided by the extent to which the public requires protection from the offender.

117    In Sim Yeow Kee v PP & another appeal [2016] 5 SLR 938 (“Sim Yeow Kee”) the High Court observed at [97] as follows:

In the context of PD [Preventive Detention], general deterrence and the social value in keeping a hardened criminal out of circulation provide a legitimate basis and operative justification for the application of this regime. Thus, we consider that considerations of proportionality would not apply rigorously in such circumstances. Here too, these considerations would have limited scope for displacing the imposition of a term of PD where such a sentence would otherwise be warranted. However, we reject the notion that it has no application whatsoever. We note that in Tan Ngin Hai v PP [2001] 2 SLR(R) 152, the offender was sentenced to eight years’ PD for stealing coins amounting to $1.10 from a van. The court considered it appropriate to do so because it was satisfied that it was “dealing with” an offender who was “deeply” entrenched in the criminal way of life, as could be seen from his past convictions. With great respect, we find ourselves unable to endorse the view that the principle of proportionality is so wholly to be disregarded that incarceration for such duration can fairly be justified for the theft of a few coins.

118     Sim Yeow Kee stands for the proposition that considerations of proportionality would not be applied rigorously in terms of a PD term. In other words, a PD term may be considerably longer than the imprisonment term which the court would have been minded to impose if it was expedient for the protection of the public, having regard to the circumstances of the case. That said, proportionality should not be wholly disregarded when imposing a PD term and the court in Sim Yeow Kee expressed concern in a situation such as that in Tan Ngin Hai v PP [2001] 2 SLR(R) 152 where an 8 year PD term was imposed for the theft of a few coins.

119    Conversely, in calibrating the duration of the PD, in my judgment the accused should not be receiving a PD term which was less than the regular imprisonment term which the court would have imposed in the absence of a PD term, as that would detract from the purpose of PD which was to protect the public. In PP v Kamis bin Basir [2022] SGDC 297 similar observations were articulated by the District Judge at [39]:

The next factor that persuaded me that incarceration for seven to eight years would be insufficient has to do with the likely length of imprisonment, had the accused been sentenced to regular imprisonment. In the present case, I accepted the Prosecution’s submission that the likely length of sentence, had the accused been sentenced to a term of regular imprisonment, would have been around nine years. This would comprise the mandatory seven years’ imprisonment for the 2 Charge (and an argument may reasonably be made that the sentence for this charge should be higher than the mandatory minimum) and a term of two years’ imprisonment for the 1 Charge (bearing in mind the relevant offence-specific and offender-specific factors as well as the principle of escalation). This broadly suggested that the appropriate length of preventive detention should not be less than nine years in any event. In the present case, incarceration for a period longer than the likely imprisonment term that would have been imposed was, in my judgment, justified because preventive detention, as established earlier, operates on a different penological basis. The imposition of a term of imprisonment would generally incorporate the consideration of proportionality. However, such a principle would not apply rigorously where preventive detention was concerned as the overriding consideration must be the need to protect the public: Kuah Teck Hin (at [21]).

[emphasis added]

120    As such, I proceeded to assess the likely imprisonment term which the accused would have received.

121    For the trafficking charge, having regard to Loo Pei Xiang Alan at [17] trafficking in one gram of diamorphine was, all other things being equal, as culpable as one who trafficked 16.7g of methamphetamine. In the present case, trafficking in 166.99g of methamphetamine was equivalent to trafficking in 9.99 g of diamorphine.

123    Given that 166.99g of methamphetamine was equivalent to trafficking in 9.99 g of diamorphine, the starting pointing would be 15 years and 11 strokes. The accused was not liable for caning as he was above 50 years of age and the Prosecution did not make a submission of an imprisonment term in lieu of caning in calibrating the likely term of imprisonment.

124    There would then a need to then account for the accused’s culpability as well as aggravating and mitigating factors. Based on the Statement of Facts, there was no evidence to suggest that the accused was part of a syndicate. That said, the Defence’s position (which the Prosecution accepted) was that he had resorted to trafficking to fund his own addiction.

125    At the same time, I took into account his plea of guilt as a mitigating factor. All things considered, I was of the view that there should be a slight discount from the starting point of 15 years’ imprisonment and in my view, a sentence of 13 years’ imprisonment would be appropriate for the trafficking charge. The enhanced possession and consumption charges each carried minimum sentences of 2 and 3 years’ imprisonment respectively. The Societies Act charge would have carried an imprisonment term of 3 months, see PP v Muhamad Naqiuddin Khan Bin Jhangir Khan and ors [2021] SGDC 269 at [71].

126    Pursuant to s 307(1) CPC, at least 2 sentences of imprisonment would have to run consecutively and as such, the range of the imprisonment term which the accused was likely to have received would range of 13 years and 3 months’ imprisonment (if the imprisonment terms in the trafficking and Societies Act charges were to run consecutively) and 16 years’ imprisonment (if the imprisonment terms in the trafficking and enhanced consumption charges were to run consecutively). Given that the enhanced possession charge was committed on a different date from the trafficking charge while the accused was on court bail, I would have been minded to run the 2 years’ imprisonment term for the enhanced possession charge with the trafficking charge which would lead to global term of 15 years’ imprisonment.

127    In the circumstances, I was of the view that the PD term to be imposed should not be less than 15 years and that 15 years’ PD was sufficient for the protection of the public and not wholly disproportional given the accused’s history of offending, the circumstances and nature of the present offences as well as the accused’s risk of re-offending. I accordingly imposed term of a term of 15 years’ PD.

Backdating of the PD term

128    In Rosli the Court of Appeal observed at [20] that there was no statutory provision which conferred the court with an express power to backdate a sentence of PD in light of the fact that section 223 [now 318] CPC was not, stricto sensu, applicable to sentences of PD.

129    Section 318 CPC has since been amended (vide the Criminal Justice Reform Act 2018 with effect from 31 October 2018) to specifically allow for the backdating of sentences of corrective training or preventive detention:

318.—(1)    Subject to this Code and any other written law, a sentence of imprisonment, corrective training or preventive detention takes effect beginning on the date it was passed, unless the court passing the sentence or, when there has been an appeal, the appellate court, otherwise directs.

….

(3)     To avoid doubt, a court may under subsection (1) direct that a sentence of imprisonment, corrective training or preventive detention is to take effect on a date earlier than the date the sentence is passed.

4)     Where an offender has been remanded in custody, or remanded in a psychiatric institution (whether for observation or otherwise) under Division 5 of Part 13, for an offence, a court must consider directing that a sentence of imprisonment, corrective training or preventive detention, which is to be imposed for that offence, is to take effect on a date earlier than the date the sentence is passed.

(5)     Before directing the date on which a sentence of imprisonment, corrective training or preventive detention, which is to be imposed for an offence, is to take effect, a court must consider all the circumstances of the case, including the following matters:

(a)     the date on which the offender was arrested for the offence;

(b)     the length of the period (if any) during which the offender was remanded in custody in relation to the offence;

(c)    the length of the period (if any) during which the offender was remanded in a psychiatric institution (whether for observation or otherwise) under Division 5 of Part 13 in relation to the offence;

(d)     the length of the period (if any), after the offender was arrested for the offence, during which the offender was not in custody.

[emphasis added]

130    Notably, section 318(4) CPC provides that a court must consider the offender’s remand in custody when imposing PD to take place earlier than a date from which the sentence is passed and section 318(5)(a), (b) and (d) CPC enjoins that the court must consider the date on which the offender was arrested and the length of his remand in custody as well as the date for which he was not in custody (in other words, on bail).

131    The PSR noted that during the last two years in remand, the accused was observed to not display any of these violent behaviours or charged for such institutional offences for which he had been charged with or dealt with in the past (for instance, during his first PD sentence in 2000, he committed the offence of voluntarily causing hurt while in prison). The Prosecution did not press the point that there should be no backdating in the present case. In this regard, I noted that the offender in Ravindran s/o Kumarasamy v PP [2022] SGHC 197 had his PD term backdated to the date of the offender’s arrest. As such. I was of the view that there was no reason not to backdate the accused’s PD sentence in the present case.

132    I accordingly ordered that the accused’s PD term be backdated to 3 October 2019 which was the date of his arrest where he was in police custody and to take into account the remand period but to exclude the bail period. The accused was on bail from 4 July 2020 to 18 Nov 2020.

133    The accused is currently serving sentence.
