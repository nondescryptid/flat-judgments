Deputy Registrar Lewis Tan:

Introduction

1       This is a claim emanating from Mr K Kawshigan’s (“the Claimant”) frustration with Ms Tan Shu Mei, Nora (“the Defendant”) due to her unwillingness to accommodate his demands to deepen their “relationship”. It follows from almost one and the half years of counselling sessions, which began in 2020 after the Claimant had threatened legal action for the emotional trauma that he allegedly suffered upon finding out that the Defendant only regarded him as a friend.

2       While the Defendant had hoped that the counselling sessions would help the Claimant come to terms with her decision to not pursue a romantic relationship with him, this was not the result. Faced with repeated requests and demands for more frequent meetings and deeper conversations, the Defendant decided in May 2022 to cease all contact with the Claimant. An Expedited Protection Order has since been obtained against him, but the Defendant remains faced with the task of defending lawsuits brought by the Claimant.

3       Prior to bringing this action, he had initiated a claim against her in the General Division of the High Court (“High Court”) for damages in excess of $3 million. This $3 million, he claims, would serve as damages for among others, indefinite loss of potential investments, rehabilitation and therapy programs to overcome the sustained trauma, as well as reductions in his earning capacity.

4       After the Defendant proved her resolve to defend the High Court action, he commenced this action in the Magistrate’s Court, seeking $22,000 in special damages for “lost income arising from [his] affected earning capacity” stemming from the Defendant’s breach of an agreement, which he says arose from an “offer” that she had made on 24 April 2022.

Striking out application

Applicable principles

5       By this application, the Defendant seeks to strike out his claim for disclosing no reasonable cause of action, being an abuse of process, or pursuant to the inherent powers of the Court. These heads are consistent with O 9 r 16(1) of the Rules of Court 2021 (“ROC 2021”), which provides:

16.—(1)    The Court may order any or part of any pleading to be struck out or amended, on the ground that —

(a)it discloses no reasonable cause of action or defence;

(b)it is an abuse of process of the Court; or

(c)it is in the interests of justice to do so,

and may order the action to be stayed or dismissed or judgment to be entered accordingly.

6       In Iskandar bin Rahmat and others v Attorney-General and another [2022] SGCA 58 at [17]–[19], the Court of Appeal provided guidance on each of the above three grounds:

17    Under O 9 r 16(1)(a) [of] ROC [2021], the test is whether the action has some chance of success when only the allegations in the pleadings are concerned: Gabriel Peter & Partners (suing as a firm) v Wee Chong Jin and others [1997] 3 SLR(R) 649 (“Gabriel Peter”) at [21]. If that is found to be the case, then the action will not be struck out.

18    Order 9 r 16(1)(b) allows the court to strike out pleadings which constitute an abuse of process of the court. The inquiry here includes considerations of public policy and the interests of justice, and signifies that the process of the court must be used bona fide and properly and must not be abused; the court will prevent improper use of its machinery and the judicial process from being used as a means of vexation and oppression in the process of litigation: Gabriel Peter at [22].

19    In addition, Order 9 r 16(1)(c) allows the Court to strike out pleadings when it is in the interests of justice to do so. … [T]his gives effect to the court’s inherent jurisdiction to prevent injustice, such as where the claim is plainly or obviously unsustainable: The “Bunga Melati 5” [2012] 4 SLR 546 at [33].

7       Further, it is accepted that authorities pertaining to O 18 r 19(1) of the Rules of Court 2014, the predecessor provision of O 9 r 16(1) of ROC 2021, remain relevant in assessing the merits of a striking out application: see Leong Quee Ching Karen v Lim Soon Huat and others [2022] SGHC 309 at [23] and Eurogreen Building Products Private Limited v Savourer Pte Ltd [2022] SGMC 53 at [13].

8       With these overarching principles, I proceed to consider the application.

No reasonable cause of action

9       The first ground relied on by the Defendant is that the Statement of Claim (“SOC”) discloses no reasonable cause of action against her. Reference is made to the chain of messages between the parties as exhibited in her affidavit in support of this application. It is submitted that such messages were exchanged in a social context, and when read holistically, reveal that there was no intention to create legal relations. Accordingly, an essential requirement for contractual formation is not satisfied, and so the claim in breach of contract is unsustainable and must fail.

10     However, as the Claimant rightly submitted, O 9 r 16(2) of ROC 2021 provides that “[n]o evidence is admissible on an application under [O 9 r 16](1)(a) of ROC 2021]”. Without reference to any evidence, and in particular to the chain of messages in the Defendant’s affidavit, I am unable to conclude that the Claimant’s claim, premised on a breach of contract, is wholly devoid of merit or that it is “plain and obvious” that the claim discloses no reasonable cause of action (Gabriel Peter & Partners (suing as a firm) v Wee Chong Jin and others [1997] 3 SLR(R) 649 (“Gabriel Peter”) at [18]). This is because the Claimant has pleaded all the requirements necessary for a binding agreement, namely an offer, acceptance, intention to create legal relations, and consideration. He has also pleaded the alleged breach of such agreement, namely her act of “wantonly ceas[ing]” contact with him to “evade” her obligations under the agreement.

Abuse of process

11     I turn therefore to consider the alternate ground of whether the claim is an abuse of process of the Court. The abuse of process ground found in O 9 r 16(1)(b) of ROC 2021 seeks to ensure that the court’s process is used only for proper and bona fide purposes (Chia Kok Kee v Tan Wah and others [2012] 2 SLR 352 (“Chia Kok Wee”) at [29]). This allows the court to “prevent the improper use of its machinery” and prevents “the judicial process from being used as a means of vexation and oppression in the process of litigation” (Gabriel Peter at [22]). Before exercising such power, the court must look at all the circumstances of the case (Goh Nellie v Goh Lian Teck [2007] 1 SLR(R) 453 at [53]).

12     A common instance of the courts exercising their discretion to strike out a pleading to stem an abuse of process is when there is a re-litigation of former claims that have already been adjudicated upon (see, eg, Chia Kok Wee). However, the categories of conduct rendering a claim an abuse of process are not closed and will depend on the relevant circumstances of each case, and another judicially recognised type of conduct that constitutes an abuse of process is the bringing of an action for a collateral purpose (Gabriel Peter at [22]). The abuse of process doctrine was described by Sir Jack Jacob in “The Inherent Jurisdiction of the Court” (1970) 23 CLP 23 at 40–41 (cited with approval in Chee Siok Chin and others v Minister for Home Affairs and another [2006] 1 SLR(R) 582 (“Chee Siok Chin”) at [32]):

It connotes that the process of the court must be used properly, honestly and in good faith, and must not be abused. It means that the court will not allow its function as a court of law to be misused, and it will summarily prevent its machinery from being used as a means of vexation or oppression in the process of litigation. Unless the court had power to intervene summarily to prevent the misuse of legal machinery, the nature and function of the court would be transformed from a court dispensing justice into an instrument of injustice. It follows that where an abuse of process has taken place, the intervention of the court by stay or even dismissal of proceedings may often be required by the very essence of justice to be done, and so to prevent parties being harassed and put to expense by frivolous, vexatious or groundless litigation. [emphasis in original removed, emphasis added in bold]

13     Abuse of process can thus be classified into four categories, namely (Chee Siok Chin at [34]):

(a)    proceedings which involve a deception on the court, or are fictitious or constitute a mere sham;

(b)    proceedings where the process of the court is not being fairly or honestly used but is employed for some ulterior or improper purpose or in an improper way;

(c)    proceedings which are manifestly groundless or without foundation or which serve no useful purpose;

(d)    multiple or successive proceedings which cause or are likely to cause improper vexation or oppression.

[emphasis in original]

14     Here, the Defendant submits that this claim amounts to an abuse of process as it is without foundation and has been brought for an ulterior purpose, namely to ensure that she will comply with his demands to, among other things, resume communications with him. This is clear from paragraph 22 of his SOC, where he has claimed that he has had to expend significant time and energy to connect with her network to obtain insights on her conduct.[note: 1] Before making an assessment, a surgical examination of the parties’ relationship leading up to this action is required.

Circumstances leading up to the proceedings

(1)   The first threatened legal action

15     The Defendant first met the Claimant in a social setting in 2016. Over time, their friendship developed, but problems began to arise on or about September 2020, when they became misaligned about how they saw their relationship: while the Defendant only regarded the Claimant as a “friend”, he considered her to be his “closest friend”.[note: 2] In the discussions that followed, the Defendant requested for their interactions to be reduced. This caused displeasure to the Claimant, who felt that this would constitute “taking a step back in the[ir] relationship”. (2)   Counselling sessions

19     About two weeks later, one Ms Irene, who claimed to be the Claimant’s counsellor, reached out to the Defendant to request for her participation in counselling sessions. The Defendant agreed to participate as she “thought it would help the Claimant come to terms [with her] decision to, among others, not pursue a romantic relationship with him”.[note: 8]

20     With her compliance, the threatened legal proceedings were kept at bay. However, the counselling sessions bore little fruit. About one a half years after they had begun, the Defendant began to feel that the counselling sessions had become “meaningless and futile” as, among others, the Claimant appeared unable to accept her reasons for not wanting any relationship or association with him. She had also grown unable to deal with his “unreasonable requests”, and as such, she stopped participating in the sessions.[note: 9]

(3)   WhatsApp messages

21     At around this time, the Claimant and the Defendant remained in communication via the instant messaging application, WhatsApp.[note: 10] The WhatsApp messages exhibited in the Defendant’s affidavit spanning the period 23 April 2022 to 14 May 2022 readily bolster her assertion that the Claimant had been making “unreasonable requests” which she grew unable to accommodate with time. It was also within such messages that the Defendant allegedly made an “offer” that the Claimant “accepted”, thus giving rise to the “agreement” which is at the heart of this matter. Let me elaborate.

32     This Offer to Settle was again met with no reply, and as such, the Claimant resorted to initiating the High Court action against her on 7 July 2022, claiming damages from her for allegedly defamatory remarks and negligent conduct. Such remarks, he averred, suggested that he had harassed and spied on her, and this caused him to suffer “damage to his stellar reputation” as well as “trauma, depression and impacts” to his life. For this, he claimed damages in excess of $3 million, constituting among others “indefinite loss of potential investments” amounting to $1.2 million.

33     Again, this was insufficient to engage the Defendant, who obtained an Expedited Protection Order against him before proceeding to file her Defence and Counterclaim to the High Court action. There, she averred that she was a victim of the Claimant’s harassment, with the common theme in all his threatening messages being that she would have to maintain communications with him or face civil proceedings and/or police action. Thereafter, he initiated the present action in the Magistrate’s Court, claiming damages for the breach of the List of Improvements.

Claim without foundation

34     Reviewing the parties’ objective intentions as gleaned from their correspondence detailed above, it is clear that no binding contract was entered into between them. Even if one can regard the Defendant’s sending of the List of Improvements as an “offer”, there was no communication of acceptance of such offer on the Claimant’s part (see R1 International Pte Ltd v Lonstroff AG [2015] 1 SLR 521 at [53]). Contrarily, he consistently sought to add to the List of Improvements, and such proposed additions were all rejected by her (see [26] above). Also, when she sought to suggest that there had been an agreement between parties, he quickly rejected this, and reiterated that he had needs which had not been reflected in her list, most significantly the desire to become more involved in her life.[note: 20] On 8 May 2022, the Claimant insisted that any perceived “agreement” bearing the terms of the List of Improvements proposed by the Defendant was “void”.[note: 21] Seen thus, parties were at best exchanging offers and counteroffers without any acceptance ever being communicated. In other words, there is “insufficient evidence of a consensus ad idem on the terms of” the List of Improvements (Alphire Group Pte Ltd v Law Chau Loon and another matter [2020] SGCA 50 at [14]).

35     More significantly, even if there had been an acceptance of the Defendant’s “offer”, there was no intention to create legal relations.

36     In the context of social and domestic arrangements, there is a presumption that parties do not intend to create legal relations (Gay Choon Ing v Loh Sze Ti Terence Peter and another appeal [2009] 2 SLR(R) 332 at [72]). This presumption is not displaced, and is in fact entrenched when reviewed against the evidence, which demonstrates that the List of Improvements had been sent amidst discussions about the steps that could be taken to improve their relationship. The commitments made by the Defendant included “[n]ot trivialising [the Claimant’s] pain and hurt”, providing “weekly sharing[s] of her work and volunteer”, and to meet up based on their mutual availability, beyond coffee settings.[note: 22] Such commitments, which are legally difficult to enforce, and which were communicated on an instant messaging platform without the benefit of legal advice, can scarcely be said to be made with an intention to create legal relations.

37     This is especially so when one considers the relevant background in which the List of Improvements was sent. As mentioned, the parties had begun counselling sessions after the Claimant had threatened legal proceedings against the Defendant due to their misaligned views about their relationship. In this light, the List of Improvements was meant to set out the concessions or commitments that the Defendant was willing to make to better align their relationship. This list was then to be sent to Ms Irene, but it appears that the Defendant never sent that list to Ms Irene because parties could not come to an agreement on the proper scope of the list.[note: 23] The List of Improvements was therefore drafted for the purpose of their counselling session,[note: 24] and was to be a step towards better regulating their interpersonal relations. There is nothing to suggest that when such commitments were made, the Defendant intended that they would be legally binding against her.

38     The Claimant’s bare denial of the above is plainly insufficient to displace the presumption that there was no intention to create legal relations. In sum, he is seeking by this action to unilaterally impose a contract along the terms of the List of Improvements. However, the lack of an intention to create legal relations and lack of an unequivocal acceptance of any offer are fatal to his claim.

Ulterior purpose

39     The body of evidence just detailed also collectively reveal the Claimant’s proclivity to raise threats or suggestions of litigation to compel compliance from the Defendant (see [16]–[18], [22], and [30]–[31] above). The compliance which he seeks from her is essentially to maintain communications and interactions at a level satisfactory to him. Any perceived shortfall on her part has repeatedly become a source of conflict, first in 2020, and subsequently as revealed in their messages in 2022. Counselling sessions and the Defendant’s constant attempts to reason with him have borne little fruit, with the Claimant insisting on his “needs” and that compromises have already been made on his part. In my judgment, he is not, by the present action, seeking monetary relief. Rather, as the Defendant has surmised, he is primarily utilising the action as a tool to punish her for refusing to comply with his demands to, among other things, resume their interactions and communications.[note: 25]

40     The terms of his Offer to Settle, sent after the Defendant had left him in the cold, is most telling of this underlying motive to re-engage with her at all costs. That offer preceded his initiation of the High Court proceedings, wherein he has sought in excess of $3 million in damages. This is by no means a small sum, and yet the Offer to Settle, sent in a bid to avoid the commencement of the High Court action, merely stipulates for them to work together to repair the “sizeable harm” that had been caused by her “recent conduct”, presumably of setting up barriers and ignoring him entirely (see [31] above). There is no mention therein of any monetary compensation payable by her, strongly suggesting that his true motivation for threatening and bringing legal proceedings against her is not to seek payment for “damages”, but to tear down her barriers and to compel her into submission. As he had warned in 2020, she could either accede to his demands or suffer “irrevocable damages [sic] to [her] personal and professional endeavours”.[note: 26]

41     Additionally, I give heed to the following sequence of events that happened in August 2022, one month after the High Court action was initiated. First, on 3 August 2022, the Defendant obtained an Expedited Protection Order against the Claimant, prohibiting him from using any threatening, abusive or insulting words or behaviour that would cause her harassment, alarm, or distress.[note: 27] A week later, on 10 August 2022, the Defendant showed that she would not bend to his demands by engaging lawyers and filing her Defence and Counterclaim in the High Court action. On 26 August 2022, the Claimant filed his Defence to Counterclaim, and the very next day, on 27 August 2022, he initiated the present action in the Magistrate’s Court, allegedly claiming for the breach of the List of Improvements.

42     In my view, the proximity in time of the above events add further weight to the Defendant’s suspicion that the present action has been initiated as another attempt to break her will after the High Court action had failed to do so. This is especially so as the claims raised in both actions are so closely related that any party exercising reasonable diligence could properly have raised and argued the points there (Goldbell Engineering Pte Ltd v Etiqa Insurance Pte Ltd (Range Construction Pte Ltd, third party) and another matter [2022] SGHC 1 at [90(c)]):

(a)     In the High Court action, the Claimant avers that due to certain defamatory remarks (eg, “I am feeling very harassed by his actions”) and negligence on the Defendant’s part, he has suffered, among others, a loss in earning capacity and business partnerships amounting to $510,000, as well as $120,000 in general damages “[f]or costs incurred and to be incurred in rehabilitation and therapy programs to wholly overcome sustained trauma”.[note: 28]

(b)     In this action, he claims that because of the Defendant’s breach of the List of Improvements, his earning capacity as an “active high - capital trader by night and a busy CEO by day” has been negatively affected. In addition, he has had to “expend time and energies to scramble and source for deeper psychological assistance”. For such harm, he claims $22,000 in “special damages”.

43     Some latitude would ordinarily have been granted to the Claimant, who is unrepresented, for improperly commencing multiple closely related claims before different forums. However, the present circumstances do not merit such latitude because the initiation of this action after the High Court action was clearly not a result of the lack of reasonable diligence. Instead, it is entirely consistent with his threat of exhausting her resources through legal action so as to pressure her to comply.

44     Borrowing the Defendant’s words, the Claimant has commenced this action “simply because [she] no longer want[s] to have contact with him in any way and he is refusing to accept this fact.”[note: 29] This is well supported by his course of conduct and correspondence with her over the years, which I have detailed above. The body of evidence collectively reveal his tendency to resort to legalese and raise threats of litigation to compel compliance from the Defendant. While he had been successful before, it appears that the Defendant is now resolved to stand up against his threats. Faced with the mounting evidence against him, the Claimant has not provided any evidence in his bare affidavit to shine a different light on the picture painted.

45     Considered in totality, I find that the present action was intentionally initiated by the Claimant with the ulterior motive of vexing or oppressing the Defendant by requiring her to defend various claims that fundamentally stem from the same factual matrix in different forums.

Conclusion

46     This court will not be an accessory to his calculated attempt to compel engagement from the Defendant who, after years of massaging the Claimant’s unhappiness, has finally decided to stand up to his threats rather than cower and give in to his demands. This is in particular as the claim brought is one which is manifestly groundless and without foundation. In all the circumstances, I strike out his claim against the Defendant for being an abuse of process, pursuant to O 9 r 16(1)(b) of ROC 2021.

47     For completeness, given the circumstances in which the claim has been brought, and further given that it is plainly or obviously unsustainable, I consider that it would also be in the interests of justice to strike out the claim pursuant to O 9 r 16(1)(c) of ROC 2021.

48     I will hear parties on costs on a date to be fixed.
