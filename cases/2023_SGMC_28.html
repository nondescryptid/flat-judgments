Magistrate Lewis Tan:

Introduction

1       On three separate incidents in May and December 2021, the male accused person entered into female toilets and utilised his mobile phone camera to observe and/or record his victims using the toilet.

2       After the accused person pleaded guilty to three counts of voyeurism and trespass charges, I directed for a Mandatory Treatment Order suitability report as his psychiatrist had opined that he had an adjustment order that significantly contributed to his offending conduct. While the suitability report was being prepared, the High Court’s decision in Nicholas Tan Siew Chye v Public Prosecutor [2023] SGHC 35 (“Nicholas Tan”) was rendered. Nicholas Tan provides the authoritative framework for voyeurism offences under s 377BB(4) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) (“PC”) while leaving open the question of its applicability to other voyeurism offences under s 377BB to a suitable future case.

3       In this decision, I set out my reasons for declining to make a Mandatory Treatment Order, and explain why the Nicholas Tan framework is applicable in sentencing the accused person for his voyeurism offences under s 377BB(2) PC.

Background facts

4       On 30 May 2021, Mr Muhammad Faiz Bin Mohamed Yasin (“the Accused”), then a 22-year-old male food delivery rider, entered the female toilet at a community centre with the intent of recording the First Victim using the toilet without her consent. To this end, he activated the camera function of his mobile phone and placed it at the bottom of the cubicle door. However, before he could start recording, the victim noticed his actions and shouted at him, causing him to panic and to leave the female toilet to hide in the male toilet next door.

5       When he exited the male toilet, the First Victim recognised him and he was subsequently arrested by the police on the same day. The next day, he was released on police bail.

6       Some months later, on 13 December 2021, and while on police bail, the Accused entered into another female toilet, where he held his camera phone on top of the cubicle door that was being used by the Second Victim. He did so to observe her using the toilet without her consent, but he fled after she stomped her feet. He was only arrested after a review of the CCTV footage.

7       After his re-arrest, a forensic examination of the Accused’s mobile phone led to the recovery of a 43-second-long video of the Third Victim utilising the toilet on 14 December 2021. The video had been captured without the victim’s consent.

8       By virtue of the above, the Accused faced six charges, with three counts of voyeurism and criminal trespass under ss 377BB(2) and 447 PC respectively. He pleaded guilty to four charges and consented to the two remaining charges to be taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing (“the TIC Charges”). The charges are as follows:

Proceed



Mandatory Treatment Order

Psychiatric Reports

9       In mitigation, the Accused tendered a report by Dr Tommy Tan Kay Seng (“Dr Tan”) of Novena Psychiatry Clinic. Dr Tan opined that the Accused had adjustment disorder with depressed mood since the beginning of 2021 which was caused by the discovery of his father having a second family after many years of financial difficulties for the family. This caused him to feel stressed as he was caught in the middle of quarrels between his parents about his father’s second family. In addition to his chronic low mood, his low average intelligence and inability to cope with his mood was, in Dr Tan’s opinion, a “substantial contributory factor” in the commission of the offences. Dr Tan also expressed the view that “with the appropriate treatment, [the Accused’s] risk of reoffending can be significantly reduced”.[note: 1]

10     Dr Tan was not the first to provide a report on the Accused’s psychiatric condition when he committed the offences. In December 2021, after the Accused had been arrested for the offences against the Second and Third Victims, Dr Lee Kim Huat Jason (“Dr Lee”) from the Institute of Mental Health (“IMH”) examined the Accused. In his report, Dr Lee opined that the Accused’s “clinical presentation during the period of the alleged offences is not indicative of the presence of any mental illness, although it is notable that he has a significant history of deviant sexual behaviours and contact sexual offending in his teenage years” [emphasis in original omitted].[note: 2]

Calling of Mandatory Treatment Order suitability report

11     Counsel for the Accused, Mr Dendroff Jason Peter (“Mr Dendroff”) relied on Dr Tan’s report to urge the Court to call for a Mandatory Treatment Order (“MTO”) suitability report. Deputy Public Prosecutor Yeo Zhen Xiong (“Mr Yeo”) objected to the calling of the MTO suitability report as specific deterrence was the key sentencing consideration, and further because Dr Lee, an independent doctor from IMH, had opined that the Accused was not operating under any mental illness at the material time.

12     To this, I noted that See Kee Oon J (“See J”) had observed in GCX v Public Prosecutor [2019] 3 SLR 1325 (“GCX”) that the calling of a MTO suitability report is the first or preliminary stage in the assessment of whether MTO should be considered (GCX at [39]). The report serves to give the court a better sense of whether the offender is potentially suitable for rehabilitation. It also addresses the necessary question of whether the offender suffered from a psychiatric disorder that contributed to the commission of the offence, and whether the offender was susceptible to treatment for that disorder (GCX at [40], citing s 339(3) of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 68, 2012 Rev Ed) (“CPC”)).

13     Faced with the conflicting psychiatric reports, and in particular given Dr Tan’s opinion that the Accused had a treatable adjustment disorder that was a substantial contributory factor in the commission of his offences, I considered it prudent to call for the MTO suitability report. This would allow the court to be fully informed of the Accused’s condition, and in so doing discern the most appropriate sentence. As See J explained in GCX at [61]:

… the MTO suitability report itself will not be accepted without question, as it is still the role of the court to examine the internal consistency and cogency of the report once produced… More importantly, the court ultimately has to determine whether the MTO itself should be ordered. But it bears mentioning that calling for a MTO suitability report does not commit the court irrevocably to a decision that an MTO will be ordered. Instead, the report would help the court to better appreciate the [offender’s] true rehabilitative potential, if any exists. … [emphasis added]

Mandatory Treatment Order suitability report

14     Pursuant to my order, Dr Pamela Ng Mei Yuan (“Dr Ng”) was appointed to conduct a psychiatric evaluation of the Accused to ascertain his suitability for an MTO. In preparing her report, Dr Ng interviewed the Accused and his parents and conducted an in-depth review of the Accused’s psychiatric history.

15     Per her report, the Accused has a history of inappropriate sexual behaviour. In 2013, he committed an act of outrage of modesty by touching a female student’s buttock in a lift. The next year, he was involved in another case of outrage of modesty when he touched a different female student’s buttock inside a lift. He was also caught for trying to steal and smell a panty from a flat and for hiding in a girls’ school toilet. For his offences, he was sent to the Boys’ Home for two weeks and subsequently placed on probation. He was also referred to IMH where he underwent psychotherapy for his inappropriate sexualised behaviours until October 2015. He was not seen at IMH thereafter even though he had been given an open date appointment and was directed to continue interventions with the Positive Adolescent Sexuality Treatment Group.[note: 3]

16     As regards the present set of offences, the Accused explained that they were not premeditated. Rather, he had done so to relieve stress as his parents were arguing more around November and December 2021, the latter month being when he committed the offences against the Second and Third Victims. In addition, he was reportedly bullied by his army officer, and stressed by his father’s extramarital affair and second family. He also stated that he did not think he would have been caught in the act.[note: 4]

17     Based on the above, Dr Ng agreed with Dr Tan that the Accused was “likely suffering from adjustment disorder during the period of the offences”. However, unlike Dr Tan, she stated that “the adjustment disorder did not contribute to the offences, which was likely related to his maladaptive coping” [emphasis added]. Hence, Dr Ng did not consider him to be a suitable candidate for an MTO.[note: 5] Given Dr Ng’s opinion, the court cannot make an MTO: see ss 339(3)(b)–(c) and 339(4) of the CPC.

Appropriate sentence

18     Due to the unsuitability of an MTO, I directed parties to tender written submissions on the appropriate sentence, having regard to the recent decision of Nicholas Tan, where Sundaresh Menon CJ, Tay Yong Kwang JCA and Vincent Hoong J (delivering the judgment of the Court), sitting in the General Division of the High Court, set out a sentencing framework for voyeurism offences under s 377BB(4) of the PC punishable under s 377BB(7) of the same.

Sentence for voyeurism offences

Nicholas Tan framework

19     The Nicholas Tan framework contains five steps, as follows:

(a)     At the first step, the court considers the offence-specific factors and identifies the level of harm caused as well as the level of the offender’s culpability.

(i)       Factors going towards harm include, but are not limited to (Nicholas Tan at [65]–[75]):

(A)       Invasion of the victim’s privacy, which includes actual or potential invasions of privacy. This may arise where the offender observes the victim, retains a record of the victim’s image or disseminates a record of the victim’s image. The more intrusive the observation, the greater the actual loss of privacy.

(B)       Whether physical contact was made with the victim. The victim’s bodily integrity may be violated where the offender makes unwanted physical contact with the victim whilst committing the voyeurism offence. The level of harm in this regard will increase if the physical contact was prolonged, or with the victim’s private parts, or if violence was visited upon the victim.

(C)       Whether humiliation, alarm or distress was caused to the victim. The victim may suffer emotional and psychological harm (eg, post-traumatic stress disorder).

(ii)       Factors going towards culpability are as follows (Nicholas Tan at [76]):

(A)       whether the offender actually knew, or merely had reason to believe, that the victim had not consented to be observed;

(B)       degree of premeditation and planning;

(C)       stalking or following of the victim;

(D)       type and sophistication of equipment used and whether it was concealed;

(E)       breach of relationship of trust with the victim;

(F)       steps taken to evade detection;

(G)       motivation for the offence; and

(H)       persistence of the offending conduct that is the subject of the charge.

(b)     The second and third steps of the sentencing framework requires the court to identify the applicable indicative sentencing range before proceeding to identify the appropriate starting point within that range. Given that s 377BB(7) PC gives the court the discretion to impose a term of imprisonment of up to two years, a fine, caning, or any combination of such punishments, the following sentencing matrix applies (at [83]):

In this regard, regardless of where the offender is placed in the sentencing matrix, the option of imposing fines in addition to an imprisonment term should be considered if the offender had procured financial benefits from his offending conduct, such that it is necessary to disgorge his unlawful gains (at [85]). It may also be appropriate to impose fines without an accompanying imprisonment term provided that the offence lies at the less severe end of the low harm-low culpability spectrum, and where the level of deterrence warranted by the offence can be sufficiently met with the imposition of a fine (at [86]).

(c)     The fourth step requires the court to adjust the starting point sentence having regard the offender-specific aggravating and mitigating factors. A non-exhaustive list of such factors is as follows (at [87]):

Aggravating

Mitigating

(a) Offences taken into consideration for sentencing purpose

(b) Relevant antecedents

(c) Evident lack of remorse

(d) Offending while on bail or probation

(a) Guilty plea

(b) Cooperation with the authorities

(c) Offender’s apology

(d) Psychological factors with causal link to the commission of the offence



(d)     Finally, the fifth step of the framework is engaged where the offender is convicted of multiple charges. In such cases, the court has to make further adjustments to the sentence for the individual charges in keeping with the totality principle, which ensures that the aggregate sentence is sufficient and proportionate to the offender’s overall criminality. This is a two-limbed analysis, with the first limb examining whether the aggregate sentence is substantially above the normal level of sentences for the most serious of the individual offences committed, and the second limb considering whether the effect of the sentence on the offender is crushing and not in keeping with his past record and future prospects (at [88] and [89], citing Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998 at [54] and [57] and Public Prosecutor v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 799 at [73]).

20     Applying the Nicholas Tan framework, the Prosecution seeks a sentence of four months’ imprisonment for both voyeurism charges. While it is accepted that the harm and culpability in this case are on the low end of the scale, it is submitted that the culpability here was on the “upper band of [the] low” end as the Accused knew that the victims did not consent to being recorded, and there was a degree of premeditation as he deliberately trespassed into the female toilet to commit each offence. As such, a sentence of at least three months’ imprisonment is the appropriate starting point. To this starting point, a one-month uplift is necessitated because of the charges taken into consideration, and due to the strong need for deterrence as he had committed the second spate of offences after being released on police bail, and also given his 2014 conviction for outrage of modesty.[note: 6]

21     In response, Mr Dendroff accepted that the Nicholas Tan framework was applicable, although he highlighted that the indicative starting points therein were for “a first offender who is convicted after trial” [emphasis added] (Nicholas Tan at [83]). With that in mind, he submitted that the appropriate sentence ought to be three weeks’ imprisonment for each voyeurism charge as each episode was brief, the Accused had made an effort to seek psychiatric treatment, the actions were not premeditated, and as the Accused was remorseful and had pleaded guilty. Mr Dendroff also sought a sentence of two weeks’ imprisonment for the criminal trespass charges. Collectively, he sought a global sentence of at least two months’ imprisonment.

22     Before applying the Nicholas Tan framework, I note that the Accused is charged under s 377BB(2) PC, which is a different provision than that of the offender in Nicholas Tan (s 377BB(4) PC). Nonetheless, the court in Nicholas Tan explained at [62] that there is “no reason why the same sentencing framework should not apply to the other offences in ss 377BB(1), (2), (3), (5) and (6) PC should they also be punishable under s 377BB(7) PC”. While the Court left it to an appropriate future case to make a conclusive finding in this regard, no cogent reason has been proffered to dissuade me from applying that framework here.

23     Indeed, a consistent approach in sentencing for all manifestations of voyeurism under s 377BB PC would be in line with the legislative history of the provision.

Legislative history of voyeurism offences

24     Prior to the introduction of s 377BB PC, voyeurism offences were all “dealt with under the rubric of insulting the modesty of a woman” (Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (13 September 2021) vol 95 (Mr K Shanmugam, Minister for Home Affairs) (“2021 Debates”)). The relevant provision by which voyeurism was charged under was s 509 PC, which provided:

Words or gesture intended to insult the modesty of a woman

509.    Whoever, intending to insult the modesty of any woman, utters any word, makes any sound or gesture, or exhibits any object, intending that such word or sound shall be heard, or that such gesture or object shall be seen by such woman, or intrudes upon the privacy of such woman, shall be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to one year, or with fine, or with both.

25     Section 509 PC is substantially the same as s 509 of the Indian Penal Code 1860 (Act No 45 of 1860). This archaic provision was introduced during a time when modern technology differed significantly, and long before mobile phones with camera functions were invented. As such, the drafter of the PC could not have contemplated the myriad of ways by which voyeuristic activities tending to insult a woman’s modesty could be committed in today’s age, eg, by using a pin-hole spy camera. Nonetheless, s 509 PC was adapted for modern times, and in the then-authoritative decision of Public Prosecutor v Chong Hou En [2015] 3 SLR 222 (“Chong Hou En”), Chan Seng Onn J explained that general and specific deterrence were principles that should feature in determining the final sentence for voyeurism offences charged under s 509 PC (at [65]).

26     However, force-fitting voyeurism offences into the dated wording of s 509 PC and ss 29 and 30 of the Films Act 1981 (Cap 107, 1998 Rev Ed) (“Films Act”) (for the making and possession of obscene films respectively) had its limitations. In August 2018, the Penal Code Review Committee (“PCRC”) reported that “[t]he existing law is especially inadequate to deal with the surreptitious recording of others in circumstances of undress or intimacy and the dissemination of such recordings” (Penal Code Review Committee Report, 2018 (“PCRC Report”) at p 75, para 7). Another limitation of s 509 PC was that it was gender specific, and so in Public Prosecutor v Colin Teo Han Jern [2017] SGMC 74, the court observed that Parliament had not yet seen fit to render it an offence to insult the modesty of a man (PCRC Report at p 75, para 8).

27     In accordance with the PCRC’s recommendation, s 509 PC was repealed, and in its stead, ss 377BA to 377BN were introduced by the Criminal Law Reform Act 2019 (Act 15 of 2019). Specifically, s 377BB PC was introduced “to address technology-facilitated offences”, and also to double the maximum imprisonment term from one to two years as well as to provide for the possibility of caning (2021 Debates).

28     In particular, ss 377BB(1)–(6) PC (under the title “Voyeurism”) set out the entire range of contemplable voyeuristic offences. So, for example, s 377BB(1) PC provides generally that voyeurism occurs when a person “intentionally observes another person (B) doing a private act without B’s consent”. Section 377BB(2) PC descends into more particulars, and states that voyeurism can occur if any person (A) “operates equipment with the intention of enabling A or another person to observe a third person (B) doing a private act without B’s consent”. More particulars are provided, for example, in s 377B(4) PC, where it is stated that it is an offence for A to operate equipment without B’s consent “with the intention of enabling A or another person (C) to observe B’s genitals…”.

29     As explained during the Second Reading of the Criminal Law Reform Bill which introduced s 377BB PC, such particulars were intended to “define the offending behaviour” as the then-current laws (ie, s 509 PC and ss 29 and 30 Films Act) did not “adequately address the range of offences involving voyeuristic recordings” (Singapore Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (6 May 2019) vol 94 (Mr K Shanmugam, Minister for Home Affairs) (“Parliamentary Debates: Criminal Law Reform Bill”):

Voyeurism involves observation or recording of someone doing a private act without the person’s consent. Some of the more commonly known forms of voyeurism include “up-skirting” and “down-blousing”. …

The current laws, in my view, do not adequately address the range of offences involving voyeuristic recordings and the distribution of such material on the Internet. So, we propose to introduce specific offences involving “voyeurism” which will define the offending behaviour and provide for adequate punishments.

[emphasis added in italics and bold italics]

30     While the Minister for Home Affairs explained the need for a higher punishment of up to five years’ imprisonment for offences of distributing voyeuristic recordings (under s 377BC PC), no differentiation was made vis-à-vis the range of punishment for voyeurism offences captured under s 377BB PC: see Parliamentary Debates: Criminal Law Reform Bill; see also PCRC Report at p 79, para 27. Instead, per s 377BB(7) PC, “a person who is guilty of an offence under [s 377BB(1)–(6) PC] shall on conviction be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to 2 years, or with fine, or with caning, or with any combination of such punishments”. The range of sentencing options for voyeurism offences under s 377BB will only change if the offence is committed against a victim who is below 14 years of age; in such an instance, the offender “shall on conviction be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to 2 years” [emphasis added] (s 377BB(8) PC). In other words, where the victim is below 14 years of age, an imprisonment term becomes mandatory upon conviction.

31     Seen against this backdrop, it is evident that ss 377BB(1)–(6) PC served to update the law against voyeurism and to detail in various ways how such offences could be committed in the light of technological advancements which were not present when the predecessor provision, s 509 PC, was drafted. In addition, the new provision increased the maximum punishment for voyeurism offences. From the above, there is nothing to suggest that the legislators intended that each manifestation of voyeurism under s 377BB PC be dealt with under a different sentencing framework. If so, the legislators could have easily introduced each manifestation under a different provision, as was the case for ss 377BB (voyeurism) and 377BC (distribution of voyeuristic image or recording) PC, for example.

32     Also, while Parliament largely adopted the recommendations of the PCRC in respect of voyeurism offences, nowhere in the PCRC Report is it suggested that a different sentencing framework should apply for each manifestation of voyeurism under s 377BB PC (see PCRC Report at pp 73 to 80). Rather, “[u]nder the PCRC’s recommended scheme of punishment, the maximum imprisonment terms for the offences of (a) observance; (b) accessing and possession; and (c) making of a ‘voyeuristic’ recording will be the same” period of up to two years imprisonment (PCRC Report at p 79, para 30). This is reflected in ss 377BB and 377BD PC, which deal with observance and the making of a voyeuristic recording, as well as accessing and possessing such a recording.

33     Accordingly, I see no reason to apply a different sentencing framework for each manifestation of voyeurism under ss 377BB(1)–(6) PC that is punishable under s 377BB(7), in particular as the same mitigating and aggravating factors remain applicable regardless of how the offence of voyeurism under s 377BB is carried out.

Application of Nicholas Tan framework

34     I turn therefore to consider the appropriate sentence for the Accused’s voyeurism offences, having regard to the Nicholas Tan framework set out at [19] above.

(1)   Degree of harm

35     In Nicholas Tan, the offender took an up-skirt video of the first victim, a 20-year-old female student while they were at the lift lobby. He subsequently deleted the video. A few months after he was released on police bail for the first offence, he followed the second victim, a 17-year-old female into a lift where he took an upskirt video of her. In so doing, he touched the second victim’s thigh. The second victim called her father about the incident and started crying, and a police report was lodged the next day. On those facts, the Court concluded that both offences were in the low harm category as the video recordings were of the victims’ covered genitals for a brief period of time, and the videos were deleted shortly after the offences were committed. There was no evidence that he had viewed or disseminated the videos prior to disseminating them. Nonetheless, the harm caused to the second victim was higher as he had touched her thigh and she was also considerably more traumatised by the incident.

36     Here, the Statement of Facts which the Accused pleaded guilty to provides that he had turned on the camera function of his mobile phone and held it above the cubicle door to observe the Second Victim. There is nothing to suggest that he managed to observe her private regions as a result, or that he took photos or videos of the Second Victim using the toilet. There was also no circulation of any compromising image or video of the Second Victim, nor was there any physical contact made with her. The intrusion appears at best to have been brief, and the Second Victim does not appear to have suffered long-term emotional or psychological harm as a result of the episode.

37     Similarly, the Accused did not make physical contact with the Third Victim, and as with the Second Victim, he had utilised his mobile phone’s camera to observe the victim using the toilet without her consent. However, the degree of harm in respect of the Third Victim is heightened because a 43-second-long video of her using the toilet was recovered, although it is unclear from the Statement of Facts whether the video captured the victim’s private regions. There is also no evidence that the Third Victim has suffered emotional or psychological harm as a result.

38     Having regard to the above, I accept that the degree of harm for both voyeurism offences committed against the Second and Third Victims fall within the low harm category. The intrusion of privacy in both instances was brief, and there was no physical contact made with either victim, both of whom do not appear to have suffered from emotional or psychological harm as a result. With that said, the harm caused by the offence committed against the Third Victim is undoubtedly higher as the Accused had a video recording of her using the toilet. Nonetheless, Mr Yeo accepted that there is no evidence that he disseminated the video prior to deleting it, and as such I find that the harm caused remains on the low end.

(2)   Degree of culpability

39     The degree of culpability is also on the low end. In this regard, Mr Yeo submitted that there was a degree of premeditation as the Accused deliberately trespassed into the female toilet on each occasion to commit his offences. This demonstrated intentionality and was more severe than cases whereby an offender uses his phone to record someone on a train, for example. However, as See J explained in GCO v Public Prosecutor [2019] 3 SLR 1402 at [101], the mere act of trespassing does not necessitate that the offence was premeditated:

The key point then is whether the offence was premeditated. It is certainly true that the appellant should not have entered the female toilet. But that is the subject of the s 447 charge [for trespass] that has been taken into consideration for purposes of sentencing. Otherwise, there is nothing to suggest that the appellant had applied a great deal of foresight or planning prior to committing the offence. All he had to do was walk into the toilet, and find the relevant cubicle. The facts as described in the statement of facts do not suggest that he had given a great deal of thought to planning this entry; instead, the facts appear to suggest that he acted on a whim “when he heard someone showering inside the toilet”.

40     Similarly, the facts here are not indicative of any significant pre-planning or premeditation prior to the commission of the offences. Rather, the Accused’s actions were opportunistic: after forming the intention to record a victim, he would enter the female toilet and use his phone to record and/or observe his female victims using his mobile phone. On each occasion, no prior preparation was required, nor did he appear to take any steps to conceal his mobile phone.

41     Also, while the Accused was not caught with a recording of the Second Victim, his level of culpability is raised because he had utilised his mobile phone with an in-built camera. This is “strongly indicative” of his “intent to make a record of the victim”, and he was in fact caught with a 43-second-long video of the Third Victim utilising the toilet. This “is a factor raising the culpability of the offender in situations where the offender intended to make a record of the victim’s image but had not in fact done so” (Nicholas Tan at [78]).

42     Furthermore, the Accused knew that the victims did not consent to his conduct. In this regard, he has pleaded guilty to the charges without qualification. Each voyeurism charge stipulates that he committed the offences “knowing that the victim did not consent” [emphasis added] to him operating his mobile phone to observe them (see [8] above). Indeed, without any prior interaction with any of his victims, there would have been no sound basis for the Accused to think, let alone know, that any of the victims consented to his conduct. This is particularly so as he committed the offences against the Second and Third Victims while he was on police bail after having been caught for strikingly similar conduct against the First Victim. On each occasion, the Accused also attempted to observe or record his victims doing a highly private act of using the toilet, in circumstances whereby privacy is expected. The fact that the Accused fled after the Second Victim stomped her feet further demonstrates that he knew that his victims did not consent to his conduct of recording them doing a highly private act. Such actual knowledge, as opposed to an offender who merely had reason to believe that the victim had not consented to the offending conduct, increases the Accused’s culpability: see Nicholas Tan at [77], citing Huang Ying-Chun v Public Prosecutor [2019] 3 SLR 606 at [74].

43     Nonetheless, the Statement of Facts does not suggest that the Accused actively stalked or followed the Second and Third Victims, nor does it show that he abused any relationship of trust with the victims. In addition, the offences were not motivated by spite, commercial exploitation, or malice, and there is nothing to show that he disseminated any recording to embarrass the victims. Considering all of the above, I find that the degree of culpability remains on the low end.

44     Given the low degree of harm and culpability, the indicative starting sentence is a fine or up to four months’ imprisonment. In this regard, I find that the custodial threshold is clearly crossed as the Accused knowingly intruded into a private space that was not designated for males like himself. On each instance, he observed the victims doing their highly private activity of using the toilet, while they were very likely to be in a state of undress. As such, the starting point of six weeks’ and eights weeks’ imprisonment for the voyeurism offences committed against the Second and Third Victim respectively is appropriate. The slight uplift for the offence against the Third Victim is justified as he successfully recorded her for a not-inconsiderable period of 43 seconds. The indicative starting points in this case are also slightly higher than the starting point of three weeks’ and four weeks’ imprisonment in Nicholas Tan (at [98]), but they are in my view amply justified given the higher degree of intrusion here.

45     I also observe that the starting points are lower than the three months’ imprisonment that was sought by the Prosecution, which in my view is attributable to undue weight having been given to the act of trespass on each occasion. Contrary to Mr Yeo’s submission, and as explained at [39]–[40] above, I do not agree that each act of trespass, in and of itself, is sufficient to demonstrate that the offences were premeditated.

(3)   Offender-specific factors

46     Turning to the offender-specific factors, I give due regard to the Accused’s guilty plea as well as his cooperation during the investigations.[note: 7] I also give heed to his voluntary admission for treatment at IMH, and note that, as recommended by the psychiatrists, he is taking antidepressants to regulate his depressed mood.[note: 8]

47     It is further submitted that the Accused’s adjustment disorder which existed during the period of the offences ought to serve as a mitigating factor. I note in this regard that while Dr Tan and Dr Ng both diagnosed him with adjustment disorder during the relevant period, only Dr Tan opined that such a disorder had a causal link with his offences. On the contrary, Dr Ng’s opinion was that the offences were “likely related to his maladaptive coping.”

48     According to Dr Ng, the Accused shared that touching female buttocks in the past had helped him relieve stress, and as such he thought that he would feel the same if he recorded someone using the toilet.[note: 9] Even after he had been caught by the First and Second Victims, he decided to record the Third Victim a day after he had been chased away by the Second Victim. He did not feel disappointed about failing to make a recording the day before, nor did he think that he would have been caught again.[note: 10]

49     As explained in Chong Hou En at [55], voyeurism is the “result of conscious choices … to satisfy a deviant desire” [emphasis added]. The above account by Dr Ng is entirely consistent with this, namely that the Accused chose to commit his voyeuristic offences in an attempt to relieve his stress.

50     While Dr Tan opined that the adjustment disorder had a significant contributory link to the Accused’s offending conduct, Dr Tan did not explain how the adjustment disorder caused the Accused to lose control of his decision-making ability before committing his acts of voyeurism. Instead, Dr Tan appears to have listed the various stressors contributing to the Accused’s adjustment disorder, before leaping to the conclusion that such stressors had a significant contributory link to his voyeuristic tendencies. No clear explanation is given for why this contributory link exists. Without more, I am unable to agree with Dr Tan’s conclusion, and I accordingly prefer Dr Ng’s opinion on the lack of a causal link between the Accused’s adjustment disorder and his offending conduct. Hence, I do not give any mitigating weight to the Accused’s adjustment disorder.

51     Turning to the aggravating factors, I note that the TIC Charge for voyeurism pertains to an earlier episode that closely mirrors the offences against the Second and Third Victim. On all three occasions, he entered a female toilet without permission and used his mobile phone camera to observe and/or record the victims using the toilet. Furthermore, the offences against the Second and Third Victim were committed while he was on police bail for his offence against the First Victim. Despite this, he brazenly committed the offences against the latter set of victims in consecutive days, speaking volumes about his lack of remorse and blatant disregard of the law (Nicholas Tan at [100]).

52     Additionally, in December 2014, while he was still a youth, the Accused was sentenced to 18 months’ probation and two weeks’ detention in a juvenile home for outraging the modesty of two female victims, and also for stealing a panty and entering a girls’ toilet. While this prior probation is not a “conviction” pursuant to s 11(1) of the Probation of Offenders Act 1951 (2020 Rev Ed), such a similar antecedent remains relevant for the purposes of sentencing as it shows that “this [is] not the first time the [Accused has] had a run-in with the law” (Ng Jun Xian v Public Prosecutor [2017] 3 SLR 933 at [51]). The importance of specific deterrence is thus heightened in this case.

53     Balancing the offender-specific mitigating and aggravating factors, I find that an upward adjustment is warranted, and accordingly sentence the Accused to eight weeks’ and ten weeks’ imprisonment for his voyeurism offences against the Second and Third Victim respectively. These sentences are in keeping with the precedents (see, eg, Public Prosecutor v Mark Fritz Tanel [2022] SGMC 26), and cannot be said to be crushing on the Accused, who committed the latter set of voyeurism offences while he was on police bail, and notwithstanding his prior probation for sexually deviant conduct.

Sentence for Trespass Offences

54     I turn to the offences of trespass. Referring to the reported decisions of Public Prosecutor v Lee Chee Hong [2017] SGDC 242 (“Lee Chee Hong”), Public Prosecutor v Tan Yiwen Raymond [2015] SGDC 269 (“Raymond Tan”) and Public Prosecutor v Ong Wai Hoong [2011] SGDC 251 (“Ong Wai Hoong”), Mr Yeo submits that sentences of between two and six weeks’ imprisonment have ordinarily been imposed in cases involving criminal trespass for the purposes of committing voyeurism.[note: 11] With this, the Prosecution seeks a sentence of two weeks’ imprisonment for the offence of trespass against the First Victim, and a sentence of three weeks’ imprisonment for the offence of trespass against the Second Victim as the latter offence was committed while the Accused was on police bail.

55     Of the authorities cited above, only the offender in Raymond Tan was untraced. There, the offender was sentenced to a term of one month imprisonment for each charge of criminal trespass. In arriving at the sentence, the learned District Judge observed that his act of utilising his iPhone to film the female victims relieving themselves caused “anxiety to our female citizens and causes them to become cautious whenever they enter a public toilet”. As such, the deterrent sentence was required to “convey the message that this sort of conduct is not tolerated” (at [27]).

56     The offender in Lee Chee Hong had two prior convictions for criminal trespass, but was only sentenced to two weeks’ imprisonment for the offence. Meanwhile, the offender in Ong Wai Hoong committed his later offence barely four months after his prior conviction wherein he was sentenced to two weeks’ imprisonment. Given his antecedent and as he adopted “a similar modus operandi in his criminal pursuits”, the court sentenced the offender to six weeks’ imprisonment (at [39] and [42]).

57     The Accused here has a prior conviction for outrage of modesty. Despite this, he committed the offences of criminal trespass on three occasions, with the latter occasions committed within a short span of time (on 13 and 14 December 2021) and while he was on police bail. Such conduct, as observed in Raymond Tan, causes distress and anxiety to users of public toilets, and merit a sufficiently deterrent sentence. This is particularly so as mobile recording devices become increasingly accessible, and especially as the quality of such recordings continue to improve and can be easily transmitted through several online platforms and applications, causing irreparable harm to potential victims. In the circumstances, I sentence the Accused to four weeks’ and five weeks’ imprisonment for his trespass offences against the First and Second Victims respectively. The uplift for the latter offence is warranted as it was committed while the Accused was on police bail, demonstrating his wilful disdain of the law.

Conclusion

58     Applying the one-transaction rule and the totality principle, the sentences for the trespass offence against the First Victim and the voyeurism offence against the Third Victim are to run consecutively, for a total of 14 weeks’ imprisonment (see Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998 at [27]–[46]). The sentence for the remaining voyeurism and criminal trespass offences shall run concurrently with the above.

59     The cumulative sentence of 14 weeks’ imprisonment will not, in my view, be unduly crushing on the Accused, especially once regard is had to his past record. It also gives sufficient weight to the needs of general and specific deterrence, which are the dominant sentencing considerations here.

60     The sentence shall be backdated to the Accused’s date of arrest and to take into account the periods of remand and to exclude the bail period (from 4 January 2022 to 9 May 2023). The Accused is to begin serving his sentence immediately.
