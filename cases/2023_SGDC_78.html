27 April 2023

District Judge Kok Shu-en:

Introduction

1       The accused is a 29-year-old Singaporean male who claimed trial to 23 charges. These charges relate to the excise duty, Goods and Services Tax (“GST”) and Additional Registration Fee (“ARF”) payable on nine vehicles that were imported into Singapore:

(a)     6 charges under Section 128D, punishable under Section 128L(2) of the Customs Act, which relate to being concerned in the fraudulent evasion of excise duty leviable on the 8 vehicles;

(b)     6 charges under Section 128D of the Customs Act, read with Sections 26 and 77 of GST Act, punishable under Section 128L(2) of the Customs Act, which relate to being concerned in the fraudulent evasion of GST leviable on the 8 vehicles;

(c)     1 charge under Section 128(1)(a), punishable under Section 128L(1) of the Customs Act, which relates to causing a Penanshin Air Express Pte Ltd (“Penanshin Air”) to incorrectly declare the value of a vehicle in a cargo clearance permit, resulting in a shortfall in the excise duty payable;

(d)     1 charge under Section 128(1), read with Sections 26 and 77 of GST Act, punishable under Section 128(2) of the Customs Act, which relates to causing a Penanshin Air to incorrectly declare the value of a vehicle in a cargo clearance permit, resulting in a shortfall in the GST payable; and

(e)     9 charges under Section 11(9) of the Road Traffic Act (“RTA”), which relate to the giving of incorrect information in relation to the value of the same 9 vehicles, resulting in a shortfall in the ARF chargeable on each of the nine vehicles.

2       At the conclusion of the trial, I convicted the accused on all 23 charges and sentenced him to a global sentence of 4 weeks imprisonment and a fine of $465,033.96 (in default 30 weeks’ imprisonment). In addition, I made an order for the undercharged ARF amounts for the sum of $219,162 under section 11(9) of the RTA.

3       The accused has filed an appeal against both the convictions and the sentences imposed.

Facts

The Prosecution’s case

4       The Prosecution led evidence from a total of 8 witnesses. Their evidence was largely unchallenged by the Defence. The accused who acted in person throughout the trial had few to no questions for the witnesses, even though it was explained to him by the Court that cross-examination was his opportunity to ask questions of the witnesses, particularly if he disagreed with their evidence or had a different version of events that he wished to put forth.[note: 1]

5       Eight of the nine vehicles in question were imported a company named 1 Genesis Pte Ltd (“1 Genesis”). There was no dispute that the accused was the sole director and shareholder of this company[note: 2], and that he was in-charge of the management and operations of 1 Genesis.

6       The named importer of the remaining vehicle was one Justin Chua Yong Chao (PW7), but he denied being the importer of the said vehicle. He testified that he did not know why his name appeared as the importer of this vehicle[note: 3] and said that the accused was the actual importer[note: 4]. He also testified that the accused had acted on the instructions of the accused to transfer a sum of money that the accused had provided to him, from his personal bank account to the bank account of a company based in the United Kingdom, Easy Drive Ltd (“Easy Drive”), which had supplied the vehicle.[note: 5]

7       A witness from Penanshin Air that had been engaged to oversee the importation of this vehicle (PW5) and another witness from its parent company Penanshin Shipping Pte Ltd (PW6) also testified that instructions concerning this vehicle imported under PW7’s name were given by the accused.[note: 6] The evidence of PW5, PW6 and PW7 was not challenged by the accused.

8       Based on documents presented in court, all nine vehicles appear to have been purchased from the company Easy Drive. The director of Easy Drive was apparently one Mr Mohammad Asif, whose name appeared in some of the documents bearing the Easy Drive letterhead. Again, none of this was challenged by the Defence during the trial.

9       The Prosecution’s case was that the accused had submitted fraudulent declarations as to the cost, insurance and freight (“CIF”) value in respect of eight vehicles when taking up the cargo clearance permit, and in the case of the vehicle that was imported in PW7’s name, that he caused Penanshin Air to make an incorrect declaration to Customs as the CIF value of the vehicle.

10     An officer from the Singapore Customs Tariffs and Trade Services Branch (PW1) gave testimony on how Customs assesses the value of motor vehicles imported into Singapore, which is a process that involves the evaluation of information provided by the importer in a Declaration of Facts and supporting documents.[note: 7] He explained that Customs conducts an evaluation of the declared transaction value of imported vehicles for the purposes of determining the amount of excise duty and GST that is payable for the vehicle – excise duty is leviable at 20% of the CIF value of the imported car, while GST is leviable at 7% of the total of the excise duty and CIF value of the car.[note: 8]

11     In the Declaration of Facts, the importer provides information about the vehicle it intends to import, including the transaction value, which is accompanied by supporting documents such as the bill of lading and invoice that are submitted with the Declaration of Facts. This evaluation process can involve obtaining clarifications from the importer, for instance, by seeking an explanation if discrepancies or suspicious circumstances are noted in the documents that have been submitted.[note: 9] At the end of the evaluation process, Customs informs the importer the approved value at which they may import the vehicle, which the importer will then rely on to take up the cargo clearance permit and also make the necessary payment of the excise duty and GST leviable.

12     PW1 also gave evidence on the assessment carried out on the Declaration of Facts and supporting documents that had been submitted for the importation of the nine vehicles that form the subject matter of the charges against the accused, including correspondence that Customs officers had with the accused via the email address nicholas@1genesis.com.sg, and the values that Customs eventually approved for the importer to obtain the cargo clearance permit taken up for the payment of excise duty and GST.

13     The main Customs investigation officer (PW2) gave evidence of the investigations that he conducted, and how he came to the investigative conclusion that the invoices and sales contracts that were retrieved from the accused’s laptop by an officer of the Customs Intelligence Branch, Digital Forensic Unit (PW4) and WhatsApp messages found on the accused’s handphone reflected the actual value of the nine vehicles that were imported, i.e. the transaction price that was actually paid for the vehicle, and not the values that were stated in the respective Declaration of Facts and supporting documents. Based on what he had determined to be the actual values of the vehicles imported by the accused were, PW2 explained how he calculated the shortfall in the amount of excise duty and GST on each of the nine vehicles.[note: 10]

14     A total of 10 statements that were recorded from the accused by PW2, and another Customs investigation officer (PW3) were admitted into evidence.[note: 11] The accused did not raise any issues concerning their admissibility, and while PW2 and PW3 were on the stand he did not have any questions for them concerning the recording of these statements.

15     In the statements, the accused admitted that he had declared lower values of the vehicles that he imported in the hope of paying less taxes. In the first statement that was recorded from him on 22 August 2017, he stated that “I declare the lower price because I hope to pay less taxes. I tried before declaring a higher price and Singapore Customs will just counter-offer a high price. I know if I lower enough then I can get a lower accepted value which is lower than the one I have paid. I can earn more profit that way.”[note: 12]

16     The accused was also asked in his statements to provide explanations for the different versions of the invoices and other supporting documents that were found in his laptop, and why these differed from the values declared in the Declarations of Fact. The accused then produced a further set of invoices to the investigation officer and claimed that these represented the actual value of some of the vehicles. The accused claimed in the statements that the invoices and sales contracts that had been forensically retrieved from his laptop were fabrications that he created because he wanted to show them to one ‘Yang Fan’, who he told the investigators was his business investor. In the statements, the accused said that he wanted to deceive Yang Fan into believing that the vehicles cost more than they did.[note: 13]

17     In court, PW2 explained why he did not think that the additional set of invoices that the accused produced to the investigators during investigations represented the actual value of the vehicles. He also testified that Customs did not have any leads to follow-up on the person of Yang Fan, as they had been informed by the accused that Yang Fan was in the UK and uncontactable.[note: 14]

18     The Prosecution contended that the same incorrect information as to the CIF value in the submissions to Customs were transmitted to the LTA, which then calculated the ARF chargeable on each vehicle.

19     An officer from the Land Transport Authority (LTA) Vehicle Quota and Registration Division (PW8) gave evidence on how the ARF chargeable on each vehicle is calculated based on the declared open market value of the vehicle. PW8 explained that LTA obtained the information about the declared open market value from Customs, which information was transmitted from Customs to LTA via an external interface twice daily. PW8 also explained that LTA would round up the value obtained from Customs to the nearest dollar before calculating the ARF that is payable. [note: 15]

20     PW8 also gave evidence on how the ARF for each of the nine vehicles that form the subject matter of the charges against the accused was calculated, including offsets of rebates that some of the vehicles were entitled to under the Carbon Emission-based Vehicles Scheme (CEVS).[note: 16]

Close of the Prosecution’s case

21     The Defence did not make any submission of no case to answer at the close of the Prosecution’s case. Having the heard the evidence led by the Prosecution, I was satisfied that a prima facie case had been made out on the charges and accordingly I called upon the accused to enter his defence.

The Defence’s case

22     The accused elected to give evidence in his own defence.

23     When he first took the stand, the accused said that the figures declared to Customs were “only the Customs figures”[note: 17]. He also testified that he had a friend from the UK, one Yang Fan, who was supposed to be in the business of car importation with him. He claimed that Yang Fan’s father had apparently given him a large sum of money, but before Yang Fan could even go into business with the accused, Yang Fan spent all the money. To appease his father, Yang Fan apparently claimed that he had placed the money in Singapore and in his bid to help his friend, the accused prepared an “IOU” letter stating that 1 Genesis owed money to Yang Fan, which Yang Fan could show his father. The accused claimed that this “IOU” letter had been seized by the Customs investigators, along with some Chinese contraband cigarettes, which he said was proof of Yang Fan’s existence.[note: 18]

24     At this point, the accused indicated that he had finished giving his evidence, but the Prosecution suggested that he be given the benefit of additional time to consider what he wished to say, given that he was acting in person. The accused was given about 3.5 hours before resuming his evidence. It was also explained to the accused that his examination-in-chief was his opportunity to provide his version of events and why he was not admitting to the charges.[note: 19]

25     When he returned to the stand to resume his evidence, the accused testified that he had not actually submitted any papers that the vehicles he imported had been deregistered in the country of export, which he said was a breach of the importation procedures in the first place. He said that he did not know if the vehicles had in fact been deregistered and thought that he may have been able to purchase them cheaply because they had not been deregistered. As a result of this impropriety in the importation process, the accused said that there should not have been a case against him since the cars ought not to have been imported in the first place.[note: 20]

26     The accused also raised certain allegations about the recording of the statements by the Customs investigation officers. He first claimed that the statements did not fully capture all that had been said between him and the investigators, and that the investigator had tried to “trick” him and pressured him into putting in a statement.[note: 21] The Prosecution objected to this line of evidence on the basis that the accused had not raised any of these allegations when PW2 and PW3 were on the stand, and he should thus not be allowed to belatedly raise these allegations. This objection was explained to the accused, and he did not pursue this line of evidence any further, though he later revisited some of these allegations in his closing submissions.[note: 22]

27     In his written closing and reply submissions, the accused reiterated the points that he previously made about the deregistration status of the vehicles in the UK, and about his friend Yang Fan. He also elaborated on his allegations against the Customs investigators, particularly the recorder of his statement on 22 August 2017. He claimed that PW2, the recorder of the statement, placed him under pressure during the recording and forced him into the confession that he made. He claimed that the recorder kept probing him with questions and when he was not satisfied with the answers that were forthcoming, he “gave his own set of assumptions”.

28     No other witnesses were called by the Defence.

Analysis of the evidence

29     For the charges to be established in this case, the Prosecution would have to prove beyond reasonable doubt the following elements:

(a)     In relation to the charges under section 128D of the Customs Act:

(i)       The accused person was concerned in the fraudulent evasion of excise duty by fraudulently declaring the CIF value of the vehicles imported;

(ii)       The accused knew that the actual CIF value of the vehicles was in each case a different figure as specified in each charge; and

(iii)       This fraudulent declaration resulted in the evasion of excise duty and GST for each of the vehicles amounting to the sum as specified in the charges.

(b)     In relation to the charges under section 128(1)(a) of the Customs Act:

(i)       The accused caused Penanshin Air to make an incorrect declaration to Customs as to the value of a vehicle for the purpose of obtaining a cargo clearance permit;

(ii)       The accused knew that actual value of the said vehicle was a different amount; and

(iii)       This resulted in a short payment of excise duty and GST for the vehicle amounting to the sum as specified in the charges.

(c)     In relation to the charges under section 11(9) of the RTA:

(i)       The accused gave incorrect information by incorrectly declaring the value of each of the vehicles imported; and

(ii)       The said information is in relation to any matter affecting the amount of tax chargeable under section 11 of the RTA.

30     In analysing the evidence before me, I identified the following issues to be determined:

(a)     What was the actual CIF value of each of the nine vehicles;

(b)     Did the accused fraudulently or incorrectly declare the CIF value of the vehicles; and

(c)     Did the fraudulent or incorrect declaration of the CIF values result in the evasion of excise duty, GST and ARF chargeable for each vehicle.

31     I consider each of these issues in turn.

What were the actual CIF values of the vehicles?

32     Section D of the Declaration of Facts for the importation of motor vehicles contains various fields that a declarant may fill in to indicate relevant information for the computation of the Customs value of the vehicle. The fields of information include the invoice price, cost of accessories, registration and de-registration charges, insurance, freight charges and other charges. As PW1 explained, the information that Customs was seeking through the Declaration of Facts was the transaction value, or the value that was paid or payable for the vehicle.[note: 23]

33     Evidence was led during the trial of four possible actual CIF values of the vehicles, based on the following:

(a)     The declared values of the vehicles as set out in the respective Declaration of Facts and supported by invoices on the letterhead of Easy Drive that were submitted to Customs (referred to collectively as the “DOF values”);

(b)     The values of the vehicles that Customs approved after they had evaluated the DOF values, which was then submitted by the accused (or his agent) in order to obtain the cargo clearance permit and for the calculation and payment of excise duties, GST and ARF (referred to collectively as the “Customs approved values”);

(c)     Documents and information retrieved from the accused’s electronic devices during investigations (referred to collectively as the “retrieved values”). There were invoices on the Easy Drive letterhead in PDF format that were retrieved from the accused’s laptop in respect of seven of the nine imported vehicles. As for the remaining two vehicles, the investigators uncovered (i) a sales contract on the Easy Drive letterhead, and corresponding WhatsApp messages between the accused and one ‘Asif’ in relation to one of the vehicles, and (ii) evidence of fund transfers to Easy Drive with corresponding messages between the accused and ‘Asif’ in relation to the other vehicle.

(d)     Invoices on the letterhead of Easy Drive provided by the accused to the Custom investigators during investigations (referred to collectively as the “additional values”). Additional values were provided by the accused for six of the nine vehicles that were imported.

34     There was no serious contention by the Defence that the DOF values were not the actual values of the vehicles. In the statement that was recorded from the accused on 29 September 2019, he admitted that he used an “invoice template” in Excel format that Easy Drive had sent to him accidentally once to fill in a lower value for purposes of the Declaration of Facts value.[note: 24] There was no attempt by the accused to disavow that position at the trial.

35     Having considered PW2’s explanations in court and the alternative version that was put forth by the accused in his statement, I accepted PW2’s evidence that the retrieved values were the actual values of the vehicles. The reasons for my finding are as follows.

36     First, the invoices that the accused had provided in support of the DOF values and additional values were riddled with tell-tale signs that these were poorly made facsimiles of Easy Drive invoices. In his evidence, PW2 pointed out parts of the invoices supporting the DOF values and the additional values that were purported issued by Easy Drive that suggested that these documents were not genuine, which included glaring errors such as wrong chassis numbers and erroneous or missing descriptions of the vehicle. It was obvious that these documents provided by the accused to support the DOF and additional values were the erroneous documents, since they did not match the details of the vehicles in other documents such as the bills of lading or the vehicle registration documents.

37     A set of communications between the accused and officers of the Tariffs and Trade Services Branch of the Singapore Customs was particularly telling of how the accused was prepared to play fast and loose in generating and submitting documents to Customs, as well as his tendency for his documents to contain errors. PW1 testified that a Declaration of Facts submitted by the accused was received by Customs on 25 November 2016 with a declared value of GBP 25,500 for a Ford Mustang.[note: 25]

38     Officers reviewing the Declaration of Facts and the supporting invoice noticed that the chassis number of the vehicle in the invoice did not tally with that in the Declaration of Facts, and that it was missing an engine number.[note: 26] Customs officers emailed the clearing agent, Regent Express, who was appointed by the accused to act on his behalf, to obtain the invoice with the correct chassis and engine numbers.[note: 27] In response, they were emailed a copy of an invoice with the correct chassis and engine numbers.[note: 28]

39     At this point, Customs officers requested further documentation from the supplier indicating whether there were any custom packages for this vehicle on 30 November 2016.[note: 29] They received the following replies:

(a)     First, a reply from the accused (using email address nicholas@1genesis.com) at 1.47am on 5 December 2016 with an attached invoice in Excel format, with an additional description of the good stating “Custom Package”.[note: 30] PW1 explained that it was highly unusual to receive a commercial invoice in Excel format, since the Excel format is editable, and it was doubtful that a supplier would use an editable document to request payment from a client.[note: 31] The Excel format invoice also contained the same errors in the chassis and engine numbers that was present in the very first invoice received.

(b)     On 6 December 2016 at 12.25pm, a staff from Regent Express forwarded a letter purportedly issued by one Asif Adam from Easy Drive confirming that the car came only with a custom package, with no optional package.[note: 32] As there was no signature or designation of this ‘Asif Adam’ indicated on the letter, Customs officers responded at 10.48am on 7 December 2016 to request that the letter from the supplier include these details.[note: 33] At 12.59pm on the same day, the accused emailed Customs with a copy of the same letter – but this time, it included a designation of ‘Asif Adam’ and a signature.[note: 34] PW1 noted in his testimony that 12.59pm in Singapore would have been around 5am in the United Kingdom, where Easy Drive was supposed to be based.[note: 35]

40     On 8 December 2016 at 10.21am, Customs received another email from the accused urging that Customs expedite its process. Attached was yet another copy of the invoice for the vehicle, again in Excel format.[note: 36] PW1 noted that this was the fourth invoice that had been received by Customs for this vehicle, and that when Customs officers checked the document data on the Excel invoice, it appeared to have been last edited by ‘Nicholas Ng’ at 9.58am on that same day.[note: 37]

41     Customs officers noted that the engine number in the fourth invoice did not tally with the engine number reflected in the bill of lading and registration documents that had also been submitted and sought an explanation from the accused. This email was sent at 11.27am on 8 December 2016.[note: 38] They received a reply from the accused shortly thereafter at 12.43pm the same day with the following reply: “Theres a typo error for the engine numbers, its edited here the new one thanks”. Attached to the email was yet another invoice in Excel format, this time with the engine number correctly reflected.[note: 39] Again, the document data on this Excel invoice indicated that it had been last edited by ‘Nicholas Ng’ at 12.40pm on that same day.[note: 40]

42     Quite apart from the entirely farcical way different versions of invoices and documents kept being produced and sent to Customs by the accused, it showed his lack of attention to detail and the obvious errors that he was prone to making in these documents. The Defence did not challenge PW2’s evidence on the issues found with the invoices that had been submitted by the accused in support of the DOF and additional values.

43     In stark contrast to the invoices that were submitted in support of the DOF and additional values by the accused, the invoices that were retrieved from the accused’s laptop did not contain the same obvious errors and discrepancies in details of the vehicles. The invoices that were retrieved from the laptop were also in PDF version. These were features of a document that one would expect to receive in a legitimate commercial transaction. Where there were no corresponding invoices retrieved from the accused’s laptop, PW2 explained how the sales contract, fund transfers and WhatsApp messages with ‘Asif’ were indicative of the actual amounts that were paid to Easy Drive for the vehicles.

44     Second, the explanations that the accused provided were lacking in logic and internal consistency. It should be noted that the accused did not fully flesh out these explanations in his cross-examination of the Prosecution’s witnesses or in his own testimony as a witness. That said, I was prepared to give him some leeway given that he was acting without the benefit of counsel and considered the explanations that he proffered in his defence during the recording of his statements.

45     Central to the accused’s version of events was a person called Yang Fan. The accused was not able to produce any information about Yang Fan that enabled the investigators to contact him, and Yang Fan was not produced as a witness in this trial, though the accused indicated in his testimony that a document seized by Customs authorities and some contraband cigarettes from China are indicators that there is indeed such a person. Ultimately, none of this was placed before the court, and thus all that was before me was a bare assertion by the accused as to his existence.

46     Even on the assumption that Yang Fan was indeed an actual person, the accused was not able to keep an internally consistent account as to who he was, his involvement with 1 Genesis, and why the accused was fabricating invoices. In his statements to the investigators, he said that he had created invoices to deceive Yang Fan, who he said was his investor, into believing that the vehicles cost more than they did.[note: 41] In court, he claimed that Yang Fan was “supposed to be in this business” with him, but that he had spent the sum of money that his father had given him “before he even started this business” with the accused.[note: 42]

47     Taking these considerations together, I found the accused’s claims regarding Yang Fan and his creation of the documents to be lacking in credibility and accordingly I rejected his version of events involving Yang Fan.

48     In addition to his claims in relation to Yang Fan, the accused raised two other arguments in connection with the actual values of the vehicles:

(a)     In his evidence, the accused claimed that the vehicles had not in fact been deregistered in the UK and that this may have been a reason why he was able to bring the vehicles into Singapore at a cheap price. In his closing submissions, he annexed a set of documents that he said indicate that the vehicles were in fact still registered in the UK.

(b)     In a further set of written submissions, the accused made another claim that the retrieved values of the vehicles were higher than the ‘Recommended Retail Price’ taken from a publicly listed website.

49     What I gathered the accused was trying to establish with these arguments was the existence of other fair and reasonable valuations of the vehicles. Putting aside the fact that none of these points were seriously pursued with the Customs officers who came to testify in court, I could not see how any of these points assisted the accused in his defence.

50     The question of the actual value of the vehicles is essentially a question of what the transaction value of the vehicles is, i.e. what had actually been paid by the importer for the vehicles. By suggesting that the price of the vehicles may have been affected by a failure to have them deregistered in the UK, or that the Customs approved values were higher than the Recommended Retail Price did not ultimately assist me in determining what the actual values of the vehicles were.

51     For these reasons, I was satisfied that the retrieved values represented the actual values of the vehicles. Given that the sources of the retrieved values were the accused’s laptop and messages on his phone, I was also satisfied that the accused knew that the retrieved values represented the actual values of the vehicles.

Did the accused fraudulently or incorrectly declare the CIF values of the vehicles?

52     Next, I deal with the question of whether the accused had fraudulently or incorrectly declared the CIF values of the vehicles.

53     I first recap some of the undisputed facts concerning the accused’s direct involvement in the declarations of the vehicles’ values:

(a)     The accused was the sole director and shareholder of 1 Genesis, the company named as the importer of eight of the nine vehicles in question. He was the person responsible for the information that was set out in the Declaration of Facts for these vehicles, and the supporting documents that accompanied them.

(b)     As for the remaining vehicle that was imported under the name of PW7, PW7 had acted under the instruction and direction of the accused. The accused was in effect also the importer for this vehicle.

54     As I had noted earlier, there was no dispute that the DOF values were not the actual values of the vehicles. It was therefore clear to me that the accused was, at the very least, the source of the incorrectly declared values of the vehicles in the Declaration of Facts.

55     However, the charges framed by the Prosecution specified the fraudulent or incorrect declaration of the CIF values as the declaration made when the cargo clearance permit is obtained, and not the Declaration of Facts. The values that were submitted for this purpose were the Customs approved values. The Defence’s position was that there was no false declaration because the Customs approved values, having been evaluated and approved by Customs, could not be fraudulent or incorrect.[note: 43] In other words, if all the accused had done was to submit a value that Customs had deemed to be an acceptable value of the vehicle, then it could not be said that the accused had fraudulently or incorrectly declared the values of the vehicles to Customs.

56     The Prosecution’s position was that while Customs did carry out an assessment of the information and materials submitted by an importer, the assessment was premised on the accuracy of the information provided by the importer. Thus, if inaccurate or false information had been provided to Customs, the false information would taint the outcome of the assessment. In that sense, the value derived from Customs’ assessment cannot be considered the correct value of the vehicle. It argued that the accused should not be allowed to hide behind the fact that Customs conducted its own assessment of the value of the vehicle to sidestep the fact that he had provided the incorrect values that formed the basis of that assessment.

57     In my view, the Defence’s argument – while having a gloss of logic at first blush – could not stand. The accused in submitting the Customs approved values was not innocently relying on these values, in the sense that he did so knowing full well that the provenance of the Customs approved values were the incorrect information and materials that had been submitted when the Declaration of Facts was submitted for each vehicle. In the accused’s words as recorded in his statement of 11 September 2017, “I know I can declare a low value to make sure their proposed value will be lower than the one I have paid.”[note: 44]

58     He also made clear in his statements that the reason that he declared a lower value than what he had actually paid was to pay less taxes, and thereby improve his own profit margin.

59     Taken together, I cannot see how the accused’s submissions of the Customs approved values can be considered correct or honest. They were plainly incorrect – because they were premised on incorrect information that the accused himself had first provided to Customs. They were also plainly fraudulent – because the accused’s intention in so doing was to deceive Customs in order to gain an advantage for himself in the form of smaller amounts of tax and duties.

60     As I have referred to the statements that were given by the accused during investigations in my analysis of the issues, I briefly address the attempts that the accused had made to cast doubt on these statements.

61     I start by noting that the statements given by the accused were admitted into evidence without any challenge during the Prosecution’s case. The accused made no attempt at questioning the recorders of the statements while they were on the stand, despite the court reminding him that cross-examination was his opportunity to raise any disagreements or challenges he had with the witnesses’ evidence.[note: 45]

62     While I appreciated that the accused did not have the benefit of legal counsel during the trial, I found the attempts to cast doubt on the statements to be lacking in credibility. Putting aside that these attempts came at a belated stage of proceedings, even on a close review of the allegations that the accused eventually raised, what the investigator was alleged to have done seemed nothing more than a robust interview with accused and informing the accused that a thorough investigation of his business would be carried out.[note: 46] I cannot see how any of these circumstances would amount to a threat, inducement, promise or oppression.

63     Even if there was some truth in the accused’s claims of impropriety in the recording of his statement (which I do not accept there was), it was never made clear to the court exactly what about the statements the accused was seeking to impugn. It was not clear which specific portions of the statements he was claiming to be the recorder’s “own set of assumptions and answers”, and if so, what was to be regarded as his ‘actual’ answers and version of events.

64     If anything at all, the statement recorders appear to have given the accused significant latitude in presenting his version of events in the statements. The 10 statements from the accused that were admitted into evidence were recorded over the course of nearly 2 years. The statements covered his explanations regarding the actual values of the vehicles, and how the documents containing the retrieved values were fabrications to deceive his investor Yang Fan.

65     Accordingly, I was satisfied that the accused did make fraudulent declarations and gave incorrect information as to the value of the nine vehicles.

Did the fraudulent or incorrect declaration of the CIF values result in the evasion of excise duty, GST and ARF chargeable for each vehicle

66     Evidence was led from PW2, the lead investigator from Customs, and PW8, an officer from LTA, as to the amounts of excise duty, GST and ARF that would have been payable based on the retrieved values being the actual CIF values of the vehicles. Based on those figures, they explained what the resulting shortfall in the excise duty, GST and ARF was. The tabulations of these calculations were set out in exhibits P35 and P57.

67     Having found that the retrieved values were the actual values of the vehicles, I was satisfied that the shortfalls in the excise duty, GST and ARF chargeable as calculated by PW2 and PW8 were the amounts of excise duty, GST and ARF that had been evaded on each of the vehicles.

Decision on the charges

68     For the reasons I have set out above, I was satisfied that the Prosecution proved the charge beyond reasonable doubt, and I convicted the accused on the charge.

Antecedents

69     The accused was traced with a previous conviction under section 14(1)(B)(1) Moneylenders Act, for which he was sentenced to 6 weeks imprisonment and a fine of $30,000.

Sentencing

Punishment provisions

70     Under section 128L(2) of the Customs Act, the charges under section 128D of the Customs are punishable only with a fine of:

(a)     not less than 10 times the amount of the customs duty, excise duty or tax the payment of which would have been evaded by the commission of the offence or $5,000, whichever is the lesser amount, subject to a minimum of $1,000 where the specified offence involves goods consisting wholly or partly of relevant tobacco products; and

(b)     not more than 20 times the amount of the customs duty, excise duty or tax the payment of which would have been so evaded or $5,000, whichever is the greater amount, except that where the amount of customs duty or excise duty cannot be ascertained, the penalty may amount to a fine not exceeding $5,000, subject to a minimum of $1,000 where the specified offence involves goods consisting wholly or partly of relevant tobacco products.

71     Under section 128L(1) of the Customs Act, the charges under section 128(1)(a) of the Customs Act are punishable by a fine not exceeding $10,000, or the equivalent of the amount of the customs duty, excise duty or tax payable, whichever is the greater amount, or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months, or to both.

72     As for the charges under section 11(9) of the RTA, these are punishable by a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 6 months. In addition, it is provided that the court shall order the person to also pay the amount of tax which has been undercharged.

Prosecution’s submissions

73     The Prosecution sought a global sentence of at least 4 weeks’ imprisonment and a fine of $581,292.45 (in default 15 months and 4 weeks’ imprisonment to 18 months’ imprisonment). In addition, an order under s 11(9) of the RTA was sought for the payment of the shortfall of $219,162 in ARF payable to the Registrar of Vehicles.

74     The case of Public Prosecutor v Ang Wee Tat Vida [2016] SGDC 163 (“Vida Ang”) was cited as a relevant precedent, as this case similarly involved the evasion of excise duties, GST and ARF in the context of the importation of vehicles. The offender in Vida Ang pleaded guilty to 28 charges under the Customs Act and 15 charges under the RTA, with another 84 charges taken into consideration for the purposes of sentencing. The sentencing court applied a multiplier of 10 times the amount of excise duty and GST evaded to derive the fine to be imposed on the Customs Act charges and imposed a sentence of 2 weeks’ imprisonment on each of the RTA charges.

75     The Prosecution argued that for the present case, a multiplier of 15 times the amount of excise duty and GST evaded – an uplift from the multiplier of 10 times that was applied in Vida Ang – would be the appropriate fine to be imposed for the Customs Act charges, and for 2 weeks’ imprisonment to be imposed for each of the RTA charges. The following reasons were cited in support of this position:

(a)     The accused’s lack of remorse, as demonstrated by maintaining the correctness of his position defiantly despite the overwhelming evidence to the contrary.

(b)     The offences were premeditated, as seen in the accused’s fabrication of invoices in his submissions to Customs and in using PW7’s particulars to import one of the vehicles.

(c)     The shortfall in excise duties, GST and ARF remained unrecovered, as the accused had made no restitution.

(d)     The accused had demonstrated a disregard for authority and a lack of remorse by absconding while on bail during pre-trial proceedings. A warrant for his arrest was issued on 25 May 2021, which was executed on 27 August 2021.

(e)     Unlike the offender in Vida Ang who was untraced, the accused in this case had previously been convicted for offences under the Moneylenders Act in 2018.

76     Prior to the commencement of the trial, the accused has spent a period in remand from 27 August 2021 to 9 February 2022. This period of remand arose from his arrest pursuant to a warrant that was issued on 25 May 2021 when he failed to attend court, after which no bail was offered. Bail was subsequently offered following a bail review, and the accused was released on bail on 9 February 2022.

77     In court, the Prosecution took the position that this period of remand should be given little to no weight in the calibration of the sentence to be imposed, arguing that the accused’s remand in this case was a result of his abscondment during proceedings and ought to be distinguished from situations where an offender has spent a period in remand due to an inability to be bailed out despite bail being offered.

Defence’s submissions

78     Following the conviction of the accused on the charges, the accused sought time to engage representation for the purposes of mitigation and sentencing.

79     In the written mitigation plea prepared by Defence Counsel, the Defence indicated that a global sentence of not more than 12 days’ imprisonment and a fine of $ 465,033.96 (in default 5 months 14 weeks to 6 months 16 weeks imprisonment) was appropriate. In court, Counsel argued that the imprisonment term and in-default imprisonment term should be reduced to either none or nominal, on account of the following reasons:

(a)     The substantial period that the accused had spent in remand should be accounted for in the calibration of the sentences.

(b)     The accused was impecunious and would not be able to pay the fines imposed. Counsel informed that the accused was a bankrupt and tendered a copy of a bankruptcy order dated 2 September 2021 in support of this. The case of Tan Yan Qi Chelsea v Public Prosecutor [2022] SGHC 275 (“Chelsea Tan”) was cited as support for the argument that the accused’s impecuniosity should affect the sentence to be imposed in this case in two ways: first, by resulting in an overall reduction in the quantum of the fine to be imposed, and second, by reducing the in-default sentences for these fines. While Counsel readily acknowledged that Chelsea Tan was concerned with offences where the court had the ability to consider both sentences of fines and imprisonment terms, whereas under section 128L(2) of the Customs Act, there was no option for a sentence of imprisonment, he argued that the principles discussed in Chelsea Tan could be extrapolated and applied in the present case.

80     As for the quantum of the fine that should be imposed, the Defence accepted the Prosecution’s position that an uplift from the multiplier of 10 times the amount of excise duty and GST evaded that was applied in Vida Ang would be appropriate but argued that a multiplier of 12 times instead of 15 times would suffice. It also made counterarguments against some of the points that the Prosecution had raised:

(a)     The mere fact that the accused had elected to claim trial was not an aggravating circumstance. As the Prosecution had not put forth evidence that the accused had unnecessarily prolonged his trial, they could not argue that he had conducted the trial in a manner that ought to be considered an aggravating factor.

(b)     The lack of restitution by the accused in this case was similar to the lack of restitution made by the offender in Vida Ang.

(c)     The accused’s antecedents under the Moneylenders Act were dissimilar to the charges that he faced in the present matter, and little weight out to be placed on this as a sentencing factor.

(d)      Vida Ang featured many similar charges that were taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing, which is absent in this case.

Reasons for the sentences imposed

Sentencing factors

81     Considering the accused’s entire scheme of offending, I agreed with the Prosecution that the main offence-specific factor was the premeditation and planning that was involved in the offences. The accused carried out a scheme to under-declare the values of vehicles for importation that involved the fabrication of supporting documents to falsely represent to Customs and LTA the actual value of the vehicles. All this was done with a view to paying less excise duties, GST and ARF for the vehicles.

82     That said, while clearly premeditated and blatant, the scheme was not particularly sophisticated. As a comparison, the offender in Vida Ang used three different methods to under-declare the value of his imported vehicles, by enlisting the assistance of his exporters to either split the invoice for each vehicle into two or issue an invoice for a lesser amount than the actual price. The accused in this case did not employ a scheme of quite the same level of complexity. There is no indication that he had enlisted the assistance of the exporter in this case, but instead chose to fabricate the invoices on his own. As I had indicated in my trial findings, those fabricated invoices contained many errors and were not particularly convincing documents.

83     As for the offender-specific factors, I agreed with the Defence that some of the factors that the Prosecution had raised in their submissions ought not to be considered as aggravating factors. These were:

(a)     The fact that the accused had elected to claim trial. While I accepted that the manner an accused conducts his defence can be considered an aggravating factor, I did not think this was a consideration that was engaged in the present case. The trial itself had not been unnecessarily prolonged by the accused, and the only time he sought adjournments was for time to engage counsel for mitigation and sentencing. The lack of a guilty plea meant that he could not expect any discount to the sentence that he might have otherwise received, but it was not an aggravating factor that weighed against him.

(b)     The lack of restitution by the accused. Had the accused made restitution to the authorities, this might have been considered a mitigating factor to his credit. However, the failure to do so, in my view ought to be considered a neutral factor.

(c)     The accused’s antecedents for Moneylender Act offences. As these antecedents were dissimilar to the offences at hand, I did not think that they ought to weigh significantly against him as an offender-specific factor.

84     As for the issue of the accused’s impecuniosity, I was prepared to accept that the accused was indeed not in a financial position to pay any fines imposed due to his bankruptcy. The case of Chelsea Tan that the Defence had cited in support of its arguments on this front was concerned with a situation where a court might impose a custodial sentence where a fine would otherwise be appropriate due to the impecuniosity of the offender, but that position must be premised on the court being able to impose a sentence of a fine or imprisonment. In contrast, most of the charges of concern in this case were punishable under section 128L(2) of the Customs Act, which provides only for a punishment of fine. I could not agree with the Defence’s extrapolation of the High Court’s decision in Chelsea Tan to justify a reduction in the quantum of the fine to be imposed on these charges.

85     That said, I was conscious that the purpose of an in-default term of imprisonment is to deter the non-payment of fines, as opposed to being the punishment for an offence itself: Chelsea Tan at [21]. As such, I did give some consideration to the accused’s lack of financial ability to pay the fine in determining the in-default imprisonment terms to be imposed.

Customs Act charges

86     Taking these factors into consideration, I was of the view that while some uplift from the 10 times multiplier that had been applied by the court in Vida Ang was appropriate, considering that the offender in Vida Ang had pleaded guilty. However, apart from this distinguishing factor, I did not think that the present case was so much more aggravated that justified a 15 times multiplier, which the Prosecution had sought. In my view, a 12 times multiplier of the excise duties and GST amounts evaded was the appropriate quantum of fines to be imposed for the offences punishable under section 128L(2) of the Customs Act.

87     As for the in-default imprisonment terms to be imposed for each of the fines, the Prosecution had cited the case of Public Prosecutor v Ho Chee Wai [2021] SGDC 174 (“Ho Chee Wai”) as a reference point for the calibration of the in-default terms. Ho Chee Wai similarly involved offences punishable under section 128L(2) of the Customs Act, where the offender was sentenced to a global fine of $340,776.60. The Prosecution in that case had proposed a banded approach to converting fines to default sentences, taking into account that section 119 of the Customs Act provides that the maximum default sentence that can be imposed per charge is 6 years’ imprisonment.

88     I noted that the District Court in Ho Chee Wai did not expressly endorse the Prosecution’s proposed approach to the calibration of the in-default sentence, and that I was in any event not bound by its decision or approach. I did nevertheless find the suggested approach to be a helpful reference point, and more importantly, a reminder of the need to ensure that the cumulative in-default sentence was not overly crushing.

89     I was also of the view that it was appropriate to take into account the time that the accused had spent in remand prior to his conviction, which came up to a period of about 23 weeks. There is ample case precedent for the backdating of imprisonment sentences to take into account periods of remand, though in this case the period of remand was much longer than the imprisonment term that the Prosecution was seeking on the RTA charges and it was not possible for in default imprisonment terms to be backdated. As such, I considered it appropriate to make downward calibrations to the in default terms to be imposed for the charges punishable under section 128L(2) of the Customs Act to account for the time spent in remand.

90     The Prosecution had argued that little to no weight should be given to the remand period in calibrating the sentence to be imposed, as the remand period had arisen out of his abscondment and subsequent arrest, which they argued was qualitatively different from remand periods arising out of an accused who has been offered bail but was not able to be bailed out. No case authority was cited in support of the distinction that they sought to make, and I was not aware of any such authority. I could not see any good reason to disregard or give little weight to the accused’s time in remand, which was substantial. If I had done so, the effect of that would be to punish the accused for his abscondment with the entire period of his remand, which in my view would be disproportionate.

91     For these reasons, I imposed the following fines at a multiplier of 12 times the excise duty or GST evaded, and corresponding in-default terms, which I calibrated downward to take in account the period that he had spent in remand:

92     In writing these grounds of decision, I have noted that I had made an error in respect of the sentence imposed on the charge DAC 922466-2019. Unlike the rest of the charges for the offences under Customs Act in this case, the charges DAC 922466-2019 and DAC 922467-2019 were for offences under section 128(1)(a) Customs Act, which is correspondingly punishable under section 128L(1) of the Customs Act. Section 128L(1) provides for a maximum fine of up to $10,000, or the equivalent of the amount of duty or tax payable. Accordingly, the fine of $18,903 (in default 2 weeks’ imprisonment) that I had imposed in respect of DAC 922466-2019 was in excess of the maximum fine that I had the jurisdiction to impose for this charge, and I invite the appellate court to correct the sentence imposed for this charge.

RTA charges

93     As for the sentences to be imposed on the RTA charges, parties were not far apart in their positions. The Defence agreed that the Prosecution’s suggested sentence of 2 weeks’ imprisonment per charge was appropriate but suggested that this should be adjusted downward to a global imprisonment term of 12 days’ imprisonment.

94     As with the Customs Act charges, the case of Vida Ang was the main point of reference that parties relied upon. In that case, the court had sentenced the offender to 2 weeks’ imprisonment on each proceeded charge under the RTA. The main differences between Vida Ang and the present case were (i) the larger number of charges and amount of ARF avoided in Vida Ang compared to the present case, and (ii) the offender in Vida Ang pleaded guilty, while the accused in this case claimed trial.

95     Balancing these factors, I agreed with the Prosecution that a sentence of 2 weeks’ imprisonment per charge, with two of the sentences to run consecutively for a global imprisonment term of 4 weeks’ imprisonment was appropriate.

96     In addition, I made an order for the undercharged ARF amounts for the sum of $219,162 under section 11(9) of the RTA.

Conclusion

97     For the reasons set out above, the accused was convicted on the charges and sentenced to 4 weeks’ imprisonment, a fine of $465,033.96 (in default 30 weeks’ imprisonment). An order for the undercharged ARF amounts of $219,162 was also made under section 11(9) of the RTA.

98     The accused has appealed against the conviction and sentences and is on bail pending appeal.
