Introduction

1       The Accused pleaded guilty to two charges:

DAC 905065/2022

You, are charged, that you, on 20 March 2022, at about 10.45pm, along Keng Cheow Street, Singapore, together with Mohamad Amirul Shafi Bin Abdullah (male/ then 22 years old) and Dani Zulastri Bin Mohamad Salim (male/ then 22 years old) and in furtherance of the common intention of you all, did intend to cause hurt to Chua Yong Jie Justin (male/ 30 years old), to wit, by using a glass bottle to hit his head, punching and kicking him, causing him to suffer a right parietal scalp laceration measuring 10cm by 5cm, a right temporal scalp laceration measuring 8cm, and post-concussion syndrome with giddiness and memory loss, and you have thereby committed an offence of voluntarily causing hurt punishable under section 323 read with section 34 of the Penal Code 1871.

DAC 910632/2022

You, are charged, that you, on or before 29 March 2022, in Singapore, did consume a specified drug listed in the Fourth Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs Act 1973, to wit, methamphetamine, without authorisation under the Misuse of Drugs Act 1973 or the Regulations made thereunder, and you have thereby committed an offence under section 8(b) (ii) of the said Act,

and further, that you, before the commission of the said offence, were on 19 September 2018, pursuant to an order made by the Director of the Central Narcotics Bureau in Singapore under section 34(2)(b) of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed), admitted to an approved institution, namely the Drug Rehabilitation Centre, for the consumption of a specified drug, to wit, methamphetamine, and you were on 26 October 2020, in District Court No. 18C, vide DAC900868/2020, convicted of an offence of consumption of a specified drug, to wit, methamphetamine, under section 8(b)(ii) of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed) and sentenced to undergo reformative training, which conviction and punishment have not been set aside to date, and you shall now be liable to be punished under section 33A(1) of the Misuse of Drugs Act 1973.

2       Two other charges were taken into consideration (TIC) in sentencing. These other two charges were an offence under section 174(1)(a) of the Penal Code for intentionally omitting to attend at Selarang Park Community Supervision Centre and an offence under section 8(1)(a) of the Computer Misuse Act 1993 for disclosing the user identification and password of his Singpass account to an unknown person.

The RT report found the Accused physically and mentally suitable for reformative training at Level Two intensity i.e. a minimum of 12 months detention in an RT centre. Notwithstanding the contents in the RT report, upon a consideration of the facts and circumstances in the case, I imposed the following sentences:

Statement of Facts

6       Key portions of the Statement of Facts is reproduced in the following paragraphs. The Accused admitted to the Statement of Facts without qualification.

Facts pertaining to DAC 905065/2022 – Voluntarily Causing Hurt

7       The co-accused persons are:

a)     Dani Zulastri Bin Mohamad Salim (“Dani”), a 23-year-old Singaporean male. At the time of the offence, Dani was 22 years old; and

b)     Mohamad Amirul Shafi Bin Abdullah (“Amirul”), a 23-year-old Singaporean male. At the time of the offence, Amirul was 22 years old.

8       The Accused is friends with the co-accused persons and got to know them during the terms they each spent at the Reformative Training Centre (RTC). The victim is Chua Yong Jie Justin, a 30-year-old male (“the victim”). The Accused and co-accused persons do not know the victim.

9       On 20 March 2022 at about 11.05pm, a “Mr Nel” called the police to report “Someone is bleeding from his head. And say someone pushed him”. The incident location was given as Block 11 Keng Cheow Street, The Riverside Piazza Singapore 059608.

10     On 20 March 2022 at around 7pm, the Accused met up with Amirul, Dani and one Ryzqa (another friend) at The Mask Restaurant and Bar, located at Circular Road, Singapore, to have alcohol. They ordered one bottle of Chivas whiskey (“the glass bottle”) to share. While they were drinking, Amirul showed Dani he had a black foldable knife with him. At about 10.30pm, the group of four left the bar. The Accused held onto the glass bottle and consumed the remaining whiskey while they walked around. The group walked around and ended up near Riverside Point, along Merchant Road. They were intoxicated. As they wanted to look for a convenience store to buy cigarettes, Dani and the said Ryzka approached the security officer for directions. The security officer pointed them to the location of the nearest 7-Eleven, along Keng Cheow Street.

11     The victim had been drinking with his friends at Stickies Bar, located near 7-Eleven, along Keng Cheow Street. At about 10.45pm, the victim walked to 7-Eleven alone. The Accused and Amirul attacked the victim from behind, as Amirul felt that the victim had stared at him. The Accused used the glass bottle to hit the victim’s head a few times, before dropping it on the floor, causing it to smash. A patch of the victim’s hair fell off. The victim fell onto the ground. The Accused and Amirul punched the victim. Dani heard the commotion and ran towards them to join in. Amirul and Dani punched and kicked the victim. Ryzqa watched them from a distance.

12     After attacking the victim, the Accused, Amirul, Dani and Ryzqa ran off, towards the State Courts, near Havelock Road. Dani had the victim’s blood on his palm. Amirul told the Accused and Dani in Malay that he “cukur” the Chinese guy, which means “shave” in English. They shared a taxi to leave the area. After the incident, Amirul called the Accused to tell him that he had used a karambit to attack the victim and he had slashed him three times.

13     After being attacked, the victim walked back to Stickies Bar to ask his friend for help. The victim was bleeding at his head and the victim’s friend called for the paramedics. The victim was conveyed to the Emergency Department of Singapore General Hospital (“SGH”).

14     The victim suffered a right parietal scalp laceration measuring 10cm by 5cm and a right temporal scalp laceration measuring 8cm[note: 1]. He was diagnosed with post-concussion syndrome with giddiness and memory loss. The victim was hospitalised and discharged on 23 March 2022. The victim’s medical leave was for 17 days, from 21 March 2022 to 6 April 2022. The victim went for two follow-up visits with the SGH Neurology Department on 13 June 2022 and 31 October 2022. After the assault, the victim suffered giddiness and headaches, especially when bending down, until around May 2022. He would occasionally still suffer from giddiness and headaches, had bad memory, and was fearful of going out at night.

Facts pertaining to DAC 910632/2022 – Consumption of Methamphetamine

15     On 29 March 2022 at about 6.10pm, the Accused was arrested by the police in relation to offences of voluntarily causing hurt and failure to attend in obedience to an order from a public servant and on suspicion of consumption of controlled drugs. The Accused was escorted to Central Police Division for further investigation.

16     On the day of his arrest, the Accused provided two bottles of his urine samples, which were sent to the Health Sciences Authority (“HSA”) on 30 March 2022 for analysis. Upon analysis, both the urine samples were found to contain methamphetamine. The presence of methamphetamine in the Accused’s urine was due to him having consumed methamphetamine on or before 29 March 2022. He had purchased methamphetamine and a drug utensil from a Telegram user “Skittle” via the Telegram messaging application for $100 for his consumption.

17     Methamphetamine is a specified drug listed in the Fourth Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs Act 1973 (“MDA”). The Accused was not authorised to consume methamphetamine under the said Act or the Regulations thereunder.

18     The Accused further admitted his antecedents. On 19 September 2018, he was admitted to the Drug Rehabilitation Centre (DRC) for the consumption of methamphetamine. On 26 October 2020, the Accused was convicted of an offence of consumption of methamphetamine, a specified drug, under section 8(b)(ii) of the Misuse of Drugs Act (Cap 185, 2008 Rev Ed) for which he was sentenced to undergo reformative training.

19     The Accused was released on a Supervision Order on 2 March 2022, which was in effect until 15 November 2024. Under the order, the Accused was not to re-offend, and was to undergo urine tests as an when directed. The Accused failed to abide by the conditions of the order that required him to report and remain in contact with his Reintegration Officer. The Accused removed his electronic tag so that his location could not be tracked. He became uncontactable from 23 March 2022, and did not report as required or ordered[note: 2].

20     The Accused was eventually arrested on 29 March 2022. At the time of his plea of guilt and sentence, he was serving the recall period in reformative training.

Prosecution’s Sentencing Position[note: 3]

21     The Prosecution sought imprisonment terms.

22     The Prosecution was of the view that deterrence was the key sentencing consideration[note: 4]. In her written submissions, the learned DPP highlighted the Accused’s poor conduct whilst he was undergoing the first order of reformative training and his persistent association with drugs. The Accused had failed to be rehabilitated during the previous stint of reformative training.

Defence’s Mitigation

23     Defence Counsel urged the Court to impose a term of reformative training, without an elaboration on the reasons why. He informed the court that he had spoken to the Accused’s mother and “[they] agreed on RTC”[note: 5].

24     I gave an opportunity to the Accused himself to mitigate. He stated: “I only hope that I will be sentenced to RTC, because it’s only 1 year and I’m the only child to support my family, Your Honour.”[note: 6]

The Approach to Sentencing Young Offenders

25     Where young offenders are concerned, the approach to sentencing is well-established. It involves a two-step approach as set out in Public Prosecutor v Mohammad Al-Ansari bin Basri [2008] 1 SLR(R) 449 (“Al-Ansari”). From [77] of Al- Ansari:

“First, the court must ask itself whether rehabilitation can remain a predominant consideration. If the offence was particularly heinous or the offender has a long history of offending, then reform and rehabilitation may not even be possible or relevant, notwithstanding the youth of the offender. In this case, the statutorily prescribed punishment (in most cases a term of imprisonment) will be appropriate.

However, if the principle of rehabilitation is considered to be relevant as a dominant consideration, the next question is how to give effect to this….”

26     The Al-Ansari framework was elaborated upon in Public Prosecutor v Koh Wen Jie Boaz (“Boaz”) [2016] 1 SLR 334 and affirmed by the Court of Appeal in Public Prosecutor v ASR [2019] SLR 941. As stated by the Court of Appeal in See Li Quan Mendel v Public Prosecutor [2020] SGCA 61 (“Mendel”), at [15]:

“We reiterate the point that where young offenders are concerned, the sentencing framework remains the two-step approach set out in Al-Ansari ([7] supra) and Boaz Koh as affirmed and explained in ASR. The inquiry proceeds from the foremost question of whether rehabilitation remains the dominant sentencing principle, before considering whether probation or reformative training or some other type or combination of community-based sentences would be the correct way to achieve this. At the first stage, the court should not be asking whether the offender showed enough rehabilitative potential for probation, as opposed to reformative training. Rather, at that stage of the inquiry, the question is whether in all the circumstances, the presumptive emphasis on rehabilitation has been displaced. If it has, then rehabilitative options such as probation or reformative training would typically not be available; and if it has not been displaced, then such options may be considered.” 

27     Hence, in determining the appropriate sentence for the Accused, the first and key question for the Court was whether rehabilitation remained the dominant sentencing consideration in this case. When this question in the first step of the Al Ansari framework is answered in the affirmative, the court may then proceed to consider what type of rehabilitative sentence would be suitable for the Accused.

Reasons for the Sentence

Rehabilitation had been Displaced as the Dominant Sentencing Principle

28     On a careful consideration of the facts and circumstances of this case, I found that the presumptive emphasis on rehabilitation had been displaced as the dominant sentencing consideration by the need for deterrence.

Gravity of the Offence

29     The offences committed by the Accused were not trivial in nature. For the offence of voluntarily causing hurt under section 323 of the Penal Code, the Accused had used a glass bottle to hit the victim’s head a few times, before punching him[note: 7]. The photographs[note: 8] show the extent of injury suffered by the victim as a result of being set upon by the Accused and his friends. As stated in the Statement of Facts[note: 9], the victim still bore the residual effects of the hurt caused to him by the senseless attack.

30     The other charge of consumption of methamphetamine carried a minimum punishment of five years’ imprisonment and three strokes of the cane, and was an offence which reflected the Accused’s persistent association with drugs. He had returned to consuming methamphetamine within a month of being released on supervision order on 2 March 2022.

Level of Culpability

31     The Accused’s overall level of culpability was high. As pointed out by the Prosecution[note: 10], his drug consumption began from 2018 when he was first sent to DRC. Subsequently, in October 2020, the Accused was sent to RTC for a series of drug-related offences, which included four counts of drug consumption. However, efforts to wean the Accused off drugs and to rehabilitate him appeared futile. As mentioned, within a month of being released on supervision order on 2 March 2022, the Accused returned to his old ways and again tested positive for consumption of methamphetamine after his arrest on 29 March 2022.

32     Prior to his arrest on 29 March 2022, while on supervision order, the Accused committed the offence of voluntarily causing hurt, failed to attend at Selarang Park Community Supervision Centre as required[note: 11] and also illegally disclosed the personal particulars of his Singpass account to unknown persons for a fee[note: 12].

Accused’s Recalcitrance

33     The Accused constantly demonstrated himself to be obstinately uncooperative towards authority or discipline. As set out in the RT report, he showed defiance towards authority figures in school[note: 13] and when he was undergoing reformative training in November 2020, he committed “five minor and two major institutional offences”[note: 14]. When he was in remand in September 2022 (in the recall period), he was involved in a fight as well[note: 15].

34     Under the supervision order, the Accused was not to re-offend and was to undergo urine tests as and when directed. However, instead of abiding by the conditions of the supervision order and remaining in contact with his Reintegration Officer, the Accused removed his electronic tag so that his location could not be tracked. He became uncontactable from 23 March 2022 and failed to report as required[note: 16].

Overall Assessment

35     I should make it clear that the fact that the Accused had undergone a stint of reformative training before was not a factor which militated against the imposition on another term of reformative training. There was nothing to bar the court from ordering a second term of reformative training in respect of the Accused, if rehabilitation remained the key sentencing objective.

36     However, on the reasons elaborated above, I found that deterrence had come to the fore as the dominant sentencing consideration in this case. The fact that the Accused had been through the regime of reformative training where help and intervention in the rehabilitation process were rendered over 12 months or more, but with seemingly little success in the Accused’s reform, merely served to satisfy this court that a greater measure of deterrence was necessary for this young person.

37     The Accused's capacity for rehabilitation was not so demonstrably high that it would not be overshadowed by the need for deterrence. On the evidence before me, there was nothing exceptional about the surrounding circumstances of this case, the degree of remorse shown by the Accused or the level of familial support that would be available to the Accused, so as to justify maintaining an overriding focus on the Accused’s rehabilitation despite the circumstances of the case. Reformative training, which primarily has its focus on rehabilitation, was therefore not a viable sentencing option for the Court.

The Appropriate Sentence

DAC 905065/2022 – Voluntarily Causing Hurt

38     The offence carried an imprisonment term which may extend to three years, or fine, or both. Prosecution submitted for a sentence of eight months’ imprisonment, in parity with the sentence of the co-accused, Dani (even though Dani’s role in the offence was less egregious). In my mind, that was a fair sentence, bearing in mind the age of the Accused at the time of the offence. I imposed the same.

DAC 910632/22 – Consumption of Methamphetamine

39     The minimum punishment prescribed for this offence was five years’ imprisonment and 3 strokes of the cane. I did not see the need to impose a sentence higher than the minimum sentence.

Total Sentence

40     I ordered both imprisonment terms to run consecutively.

41     These offences were distinct and involved different legally protected interests; they were also committed on separate occasions[note: 17]. The total sentence was five years and eight months’ imprisonment, and three strokes of the cane. Taking a last look, I was satisfied that the sentence was not manifestly excessive but proportionate to the Accused’s criminality and culpability, notwithstanding his young age.

42     As the Accused was on RTC recall prior to sentencing, the imprisonment terms were ordered to run with effect from the date when they were passed i.e. 27 February 2023. The Accused was also ordered to make compensation to the victim in DAC 905065/2022, in the sum of $269.65 – the amount having been arrived at after dividing the total cost of the victim’s hospital bill by the three assailants[note: 18] involved.

43     The Accused was 21 years old at the time of the sentence and is currently serving the term of imprisonment.
