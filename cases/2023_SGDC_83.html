2 May 2023

Senior District Judge Ong Hian Sun:

Introduction

1       The accused, Muhamad Saifullah Bin Matdafi, pleaded guilty to the following charge as set out below:

You…are charged that you, on 3 January 2022 at about 3.20pm, at 182 Pandan Loop, Singapore 128373, did attempt to possess a “Class A” controlled drug listed in the First Schedule to the Misuse of Drugs Act 1973 (“MDA”), to wit, by attempting to collect twenty two (22) bundles of vegetable matter weighing not less than 5,843 grams, which were analysed and found to contain mitragynine, from one Muhammad Saiful Bin Ramle (NRIC No.: SXXX) at the said location, without any authorization under the MDA or the Regulations made thereunder, and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 8(a) read with Section 12 of the MDA and punishable under Section 33(1) of the MDA.

2       The accused admitted to and consented to one charge of consumption of a “Class A” controlled drug, to wit, mitragynine, an offence under Section 8(b)(i) and punishable under Section 33(3A) of the MDA to be taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing.

3       I sentenced the accused to an imprisonment term of four years and six months’ imprisonment.

4       The accused has appealed against the sentence imposed. He is currently placed on bail pending his appeal.

5       The accused has admitted to the following Statement of Facts without qualification below:

Statement of Facts

INTRODUCTION

6       The accused is Muhamad Saifullah bin Matdafi, a 29-year-old male Malaysian. At the material time, he was working as a delivery attendant in Singapore.

7       The co-accused persons are:

(a)     Mohamad Shafuan bin Borhan, a 32-year-old male (“Shafuan”). At the material time, he was working as a technician in Singapore. Shafuan is the accused’s friend. The accused knows Shafuan as “Wan”.

(b)     Muhammad bin Hut (“Muhammad”), a 33-year-old male Malaysian. At the material time, he was working as a delivery driver. His job required him to drive a motor lorry from Johor Bahru to Singapore regularly to deliver empty plastic bottles to a factory at Pandan Loop, Singapore (“the Pandan Loop factory”). Muhammad is Shafuan’s friend.

FACTS RELATING TO THE CHARGE – ATTEMPTED POSSESSION OF A CLASS A CONTROLLED DRUG (DAC-907488-2022)

8       Sometime on or about 31 December 2021, Shafuan ordered about 6kg of “ketum” leaves from one “Amar”, a supplier of “ketum” leaves based in Malaysia. The “ketum” leaves were meant to be delivered to the accused in Singapore at the Pandan Loop factory.

9       On or about 2 January 2022, as the accused was unable to collect the “ketum” leaves, he sought the assistance of his friend, Muhamamd Saiful bin Ramle (“Saiful”), to collect them on his behalf. Saiful would collect the “ketum” leaves from the Pandan Loop factory and deliver them to the accused.

10     The accused intended to consume about 1kg of the “ketum” leaves by boiling the leaves in water and drinking it, and deliver the remaining “ketum” leaves, or “kratom” tea made from boiling the leaves, to Shafuan, Saiful and another friend in Singapore. The accused had provided “ketum” leaves and “kratom” tea to Saiful on previous occasions.

11     On 3 January 2022, Muhammad drove to Singapore in one Malaysian-registered lorry bearing registration number JSA7199 (“the Lorry”), which carried a black plastic bag containing the “ketum” leaves (“the Bag”), which “ketum” leaves were meant to be delivered to the accused.

12     At about 8.15am, at 21 Woodlands Crossing, Woodlands Checkpoint Arrival Cargo, Singapore 738203, officers from Immigration and Checkpoints Authority (“ICA”) stopped the Lorry for checks. During the search, the Bag was recovered from the battery compartment of the Lorry and 22 packets of leafy substance were recovered from the Bag. The Bag and the 22 packets of leafy substance were seized as case exhibits as marked as “MH-A” and “MH-A1” respectively.

13     Muhammad was subsequently arrested by officers from the Central Narcotics Bureau (“CNB”) on suspicion that he had committed offences under the Misuse of Drugs Act 1973 (“MDA”).

14     On 3 January 2022, at about 3.20pm, at 182 Pandan Loop, Singapore 128373, the accused was arrested by officers from the CNB on suspicion that he had committed offences under the MDA. Prior to his arrest, the accused was waiting at the said location intending to collect the “ketum” leaves from Saiful.

15     On 5 January 2022, the exhibit marked “MH-A1” was submitted to the Health Sciences Authority for analysis.

16     On 16 February 2022, Lee Yan Kai, an analyst with the Illicit Drugs Laboratory of the HSA, issued a certificate under s 16 of the MDA bearing Lab No. 2200232-ID-001, stating that the exhibit marked “MH-A1” was found to be twenty-two (22) bundles of vegetable matter weighing not less than 5,843g. The vegetable matter in all the bundles was analysed individually and found to contain mitragynine.

17     Mitragynine is a Class A controlled drug listed in the First Schedule to the MDA.

18     The accused is not authorised under the MDA or regulations thereunder to be in possession of mitragynine.

19     The accused did attempt to be in possession of 5,843g of vegetable matter found to contain mitragynine, and the accused has thereby committed an offence under s 8(a) read with s 12 of the MDA and punishable under s 33(1) of the MDA.

20     Muhammad has been dealt with. Shafuan will be dealt with separately.

Antecedents

21     The accused is untraced.

Prescribed Punishment

22     The prescribed punishment for an offence under Section 8(a) read with Section 12 of the MDA and punishable under Section 33(1) of the MDA is 10 years’ imprisonment or fine of $20,000 or both.

The Prosecution’s submission on sentence

23     The accused together with his co-accused, Muhammad, are the first to be prosecuted involving mitragynine, which is a Class A controlled drug listed in the First Schedule to the MDA which include other common hard drugs such as diamorphine, cannabis and methamphetamine.

24     The Prosecution submitted that deterrence should be the dominating consideration in the present case due to the harmful effects and growing prevalence of mitragynine by citing an article written by the Health Sciences Authority dated 13 September 2022 (HSA Write-up).

25     Mitragynine is the psychoactive component found in the leaves of Mitragyna speciaso, a tropical tree native to Southeast Asia and commonly known as “kratom” or “ketum”. Depending on the dose, the consumption of kratom leaves (typically by chewing the fresh leaves, ingesting the dried leaves in powdered form or drinking kratom tea made from boiling kratom leaves in water) produces opioid and/or stimulant effects. In low doses (<5g), kratom leaves produce “cocaine-like” stimulant effects, e.g., increased alertness, physical energy and talkativeness, while high doses (5-15g) bring about more “morphine-like” sedative-narcotic effects.[note: 1] As such, kratom leaves have been used both as a stimulant to counter fatigue and enhance stamina as well as an opium substitute, being often marketed as a legal alternative to controlled opiates such as morphine and heroin.[note: 2] They have also been used as part of traditional medicine to treat a variety of ailments, especially pain. However, kratom leaves, or its constituents (including mitragynine), have not been approved as safe and effective medicines for therapeutic use.[note: 3]

26     Consumption of kratom leaves may cause users to suffer a variety of adverse effects such as nausea, itching, constipation, vomiting, drowsiness, loss of appetite, even seizures and hallucinations. A long-term user may suffer from dependence, anorexia, insomnia and hepatoxicity (liver damage), and may experience withdrawal symptoms when they cease regular kratom use, though these symptoms are likely to be mild or moderate.[note: 4] The paucity of data on the effects of kratom’s interaction with other substances such as alcohol and drugs, further increases the risks of kratom use.[note: 5]

27     Internationally, there have been several reported fatalities associated with the use of kratom leaves. A 2019 study by the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention on drug overdose attributed 91 deaths to kratom over the period of 1.5 years, though it should be pointed out that in 84 of the 91 deaths, kratom was found to be used in combination with other substances.[note: 6]

28     The dangers of mitragynine did not go unnoticed in Singapore. In 2013, mitragynine and other new psychoactive substances (“NPS”) were temporarily listed in the then-Fifth Schedule to the MDA to bring them under legal control, and the Central Narcotics Bureau was empowered to seize these substances so as to restrict their circulation in Singapore.[note: 7] After studying its harmful effects and possible legitimate uses,[note: 8]mitragynine was included in the First Schedule to the MDA as a Class A controlled drug in 2014,[note: 9] ranking together with hard drugs such as heroin, cannabis and methamphetamine, thereby reflecting Parliament’s recognition of the dangerousness of the mitragynine as well as Parliament’s intention that drug offences involving mitragynine should not be treated less seriously than those concerning other Class A controlled drugs.

29     Further, in view of its ill effects, the prevalence and proliferation of mitragynine in Singapore is particularly disconcerting. The occurrence of mitragynine in Singapore has risen over the years. Mitragynine was the sixth-most common NPS identified in drugs samples analysed in Singapore in 2018 and 2019, and became the most common NPS detected in Singapore in 2020.

30     The Prosecution urged this Court to impose a deterrent sentence to stem the tide of the growing abuse of mitragynine, and the potential deleterious effects it brings. Considering the substantial quantity of mitragynine involved and the fact that the accused’s attempted possession of the drugs was for a purpose connected to its illicit circulation in Singapore, the Prosecution submitted that a sentence of five to six years’ imprisonment is necessary to meet the need for deterrence.

Mitigation

31     The Counsel for the accused stated that in or around November 2021, accused came to be acquainted with an individual known to him as “Wan”. Wan represented to accused that drinking tea made from “ketum” will help improve his health and alleviate body aches from work. Wan provided accused with “ketum” which accused found to be effective in relieving body aches.

32     Accused shared his “ketum” with his friend Shaiful. On or about 3 January 2022, accused asked Shaiful to collect “ketum” from Wan. Wan had directed accused to collect the “ketum” at the place and around the time stated the Charge (i.e. on 3 January 2022 at about 3.20pm, at 182 Pandan Loop, Singapore 128373).

33     Counsel for accused relying on the High Court decision in Liew Zheng Yang v Public Prosecutor [2017] SGHC 257 and the High Court decision in Dinesh Singh Bhatia s/o Amarjeet Singh v PP [2005] 3 SLR(R) 1 submitted that a sentence between the sentencing range of six to 18 months’ imprisonment would suffice for the accused as he is a first offender, and a casual consumer and he has pleaded guilty to the charge.

The Court’s Decision

34     The prescribed punishment for an offence of attempted possession of a controlled drug for a first offender may range from a sentence of up to 10 years’ imprisonment or a $20,000 fine or both.[note: 10] In Ramesh a/l Perumal v PP & another appeal [2019] 1 SLR 1003[note: 11] (at [117]-[118]), the Court of Appeal considered two factors in determining the appropriate sentence for the offence of possessing 29.96g of diamorphine, namely, the purpose for possession and the quantity of drugs involved.

35     With regards to the purpose for possession, the Court of Appeal in Ramesh endorsed the High Court’s holding in Liew Zheng Yang v PP [2017] 5 SLR 1160[note: 12] that offenders who possess drugs for a purpose connected to the illicit circulation of the drugs should be punished more severely than those who possess drugs for their own consumption. This is because offenders in the former group harm other persons, as opposed to consumers who primarily harm themselves. As the offender in Ramesh must have known that he was committing some act that was connected to the illicit circulation of the drugs by safekeeping the drugs for one Chander with the intention of returning the same to the latter, the Court of Appeal held that it would be appropriate to impose a penalty at the higher end of the sentencing range.

36     In relation to the quantity of drugs involved, the Court of Appeal in Ramesh had regard to the fact the quantity of diamorphine involved was 29.96g, which is almost double that which would attract capital punishment in the context of a trafficking charge and found that a sentence of ten years’ imprisonment was appropriate.

37     I agree with the Prosecution’s submission that notwithstanding Ramesh was a case involving the possession of diamorphine, the two considerations identified by the Court of Appeal are equally relevant to the present case. Applying these principles to the present case, the accused intended to keep 1 kg of kratom for himself and to deliver at least 4,843g of kratom to others either in the form of leaves or tea made by him. His attempted possession of the drugs was therefore largely for the purpose of facilitating its circulation, which is more serious than mere possession of the same for consumption. Had the accused been charged for attempted possession of the drugs for the purpose of trafficking, he would be facing an imprisonment term of at least 5 years and 5 strokes of the cane.

38     The dominant sentencing principle in the sentencing of drug offences is deterrence. In the present case, the amount of mitragynine involved is substantial and accused intended possession of the drugs was linked to illicit distribution in Singapore after the drugs were brought in from Malaysia.

39     Besides the principle of deterrence, the principle of consistency is also a relevant consideration in the present case.

40     As highlighted by Sundaresh Menon CJ in the case of Azu v Public Prsosecutor [2016] SGHC 25 (at [1]): “Consistency in sentencing is a key principle in our criminal justice system. This is rooted in the notion that all are equal before the law (Public Prosecutor v Ng Sae Kiat and other appeals [2015] 5 SLR 167 (“Ng Sae Kiat”) at [76], citing Green v R (2011) 283 ALR 1 at [30]). The principle of parity in sentencing between co-offenders urges that sentences meted out to co-offenders who are party to a common criminal enterprise should not be unduly disparate from each other. To put it simply, those of similar culpability should receive similar sentences, while those of greater culpability should generally be more severely punished.”

41     The co-accused, Muhammad bin Hut, the courier that brought the drugs from Malaysia to Singapore pleaded guilty and he was sentenced to 5 years imprisonment for a possession charge of same amount of vegetable matter as the accused. Muhammad bin Hut did not appeal against his sentence.

42     Considering the huge amount of the drugs involved, and that accused’s attempted possession was for the purpose of distribution of the drugs to his friends, and the accused having one charge of consumption taken into consideration for purpose of sentence, and the sentence meted out against the co-accused who together with the accused was involved in a common criminal enterprise, this court is of the view that a sentence that is not too disparate from the sentence of 5 years imprisonment imposed on the co-accused, would be appropriate.

Conclusion

43     Having considered accused’s plea of guilt and his charge of attempted possession of the drugs and his cooperation with the authority and the circumstances of this case, this court is of the view that a sentence of 4 years and six months imprisonment term would be commensurate with his culpability.
