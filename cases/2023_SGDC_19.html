District Judge Lim Wee Ming:

Introduction

1       The accused pleaded guilty to three charges, arising from the accused and two co-offenders acting with common intention in:

(a)     attacking and causing grievous hurt to the victim, while the accused pinned him to the ground in a chokehold,

(b)     forcibly stripping the victim, and recording a video of his exposed genitals, which was sent to the victim’s contacts on his mobile phone, and

(c)     behaving in a disorderly manner in a public place by shouting derogatory insults.

2       The accused pleaded guilty to a further charge, arising from a subsequent incident where he hosted a poker game comprising more than eight individuals, in contravention of Regulation 6(1) of the COVID-19 (Temporary Measures) (Control Order) Regulations 2020.

3       The accused was sentenced to a total of 30 months’ imprisonment, four strokes of the cane and a fine of $5,500 (in default 14 days’ imprisonment) for the above offences. He has appealed against the sentence.

4       The accused pleaded guilty after the General Division of the High Court sentenced the co-accused, GEH, in Public Prosecutor v GED and other appeals [2022] SGHC 301 (“GED”). For ease of reference, the redaction references to the persons in the GED decision are used in the grounds of decision herein. In the GED decision, the accused was referred to as E.

Charges

5       The four charges that the accused pleaded guilty to are set out below.

(a)     1st Charge (Amended) (“VCGH Charge”): “You … are charged that you, on the 7th of February 2020, at or about 11.30pm, along Woodlands Centre Road, Singapore, together with one [GEH] and one [D], in furtherance of the common intention of you all, did an act with the intention of voluntarily causing grievous hurt to one [V2] (“the victim”), to wit, you pinned the victim in a chokehold while the said [GEH] and [D] punched and kicked the victim, thereby causing grievous hurt to him, and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 325 read with Section 34 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).”

(b)     3rd Charge (Amended) (“Insulting Modesty Charge”): “You … are charged that you, on the 7th of February 2020, at or about 11.30pm, along Woodlands Centre Road, Singapore, together with one [GEH] and one [D], in furtherance of the common intention of you all to insult the modesty of one V2 (“the victim”), did an act which intruded upon the victim’s privacy, to wit, by pinning the victim down while [GEH] and [D] took off his clothes and took videos of his exposed penis without his consent, and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 377BA of the Penal Code read with Section 34 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

(c)     5th Charge (Amended) (“Public Order Charge”): “You … are charged that you, on the 7th of February 2020, at or about 11.30pm, along Woodlands Centre Road, Singapore, a public place, together with one [GEH] and one [D], in furtherance of the common intention of you all, did behave in a disorderly manner in a public place, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 20 of the Miscellaneous Offences (Public Order and Nuisance) Act (Cap 184, 1997 Rev Ed) [“MOA”] read with Section 34 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).”

(d)     6th Charge (Amended) (“Covid Charge”): “You … are charged that you, on 1 April 2021, at or about 2.50 am, at … Barbershop, …, without reasonable excuse, did attend a gathering made up of other individuals for a common purpose at the said location which is a place outside your ordinary place of residence, and you were not in a cohort permitted under Regulation 6(1) of the COVID-19 (Temporary Measures) (Control Order) Regulations 2020, to wit, you organized a poker game which comprised more than 8 individuals, and you have thereby contravened Reg 6(1) of the COVID-19 (Temporary Measures) (Control Order) Regulations 2020, which is an offence punishable under s 34(7)(a) of the COVID-19 (Temporary Measures) Act 2020 (Act 14 of 2020).”

6       The accused agreed to two other charges relating to the 7 February 2020 incident, to be taken into consideration for sentencing.

Defence’s submissions

37     The mitigation plea filed by the defence relied largely on the young age of the accused and that he went along with D (his father) and GEH.[note: 9] The accused had turned 21 years old, less than two months before he committed the VCGH, Insulting Modesty and Public Order offences.[note: 10] The mitigation plea submitted that the only reason the accused had joined D and GEH that day was because D, his father, had asked him to join them in following B, to obtain photographic evidence of her movements.[note: 11]

38     The mitigation plea also relied on the following two testimonials:

(a)     A testimonial from the form teacher of the accused at the independent school that he previously attended, stating that the offence that the accused committed was “very unexpected and completely out of character” and “that in his eagerness to protect his love ones, [the accused] may have accidently caused hurt … without any ill intent.”

(b)     A testimonial from the commander of the unit in which the accused had served during his national service that the involvement of the accused in the offence “was unexpected and is completely out of character” and that he was “a promising young man who cares about protecting the weak”.

39     For the VCGH Charge, the defence submitted that a sentence of 15 months’ imprisonment without caning would suffice, in view of:

(a)     The young age of the accused.

(b)     The accused was influenced by others older than him.

(c)     The grievous hurt was inflicted by GEH, rather than the accused.[note: 12]

40     For the Insulting Modesty Charge, the defence submitted that 6 months’ imprisonment would suffice, as he was not involved in GEH sending the messages to others.[note: 13]

41     For the other two charges, where the prosecution sought a fine, the defence submitted that as the accused might not be able to pay a fine and may have to serve the default sentence, the court should consider the total sentence that the accused would have to serve, considering his role and background.[note: 14]

The court’s decision on sentence

42     I agree with the prosecution’s submission that the accused was as culpable as the co-accused, GEH. I disagree with the arguments raised by counsel for the accused in his mitigation plea, where he sought to downplay the culpability of the accused.

VCGH Charge

43     In GED, the General Division of the High Court (at [157]) applied the two-step sentencing framework for VCGH set out by the Court of Appeal in Public Prosecutor v BDB [2018] 1 SLR 127 as follows:

(a)     First, the court should determine an indicative starting point for sentencing, based on the seriousness of the injury caused to the victim as an indicator of the gravity of the offence. This should be assessed along a spectrum, having regard to considerations such as the nature and permanence of the injury. The indicative starting point should also reflect the full breath of the permitted sentencing range.

(b)     Second, after the indicative starting point has been identified, the court should consider the necessary adjustments upwards or downwards based on an assessment of the offender’s culpability and the presence of relevant aggravating and/or mitigating factors.

44     In relation to the first step, the General Division of the High Court in GED considered the physical injuries suffered by the victim (at [161]), as well as the psychological harm that he suffered (at [162]). Having regard to the degree and nature of the injuries caused to the victim, the court in GED considered that an indicative starting point of 16 months’ imprisonment would be appropriate (at [163], [168]).

45     In the present case, as we are looking at the same injuries and harm suffered by the victim, the indicative starting point of 16 months’ imprisonment would be the same as well. I note that the sentence sought in the mitigation plea of 15 months’ imprisonment,[note: 15] is one month lower than the aforesaid indicative starting point.

46     No explanation was given in the mitigation plea on how the 15 months was derived from the BDB framework. A sentence that is one month lower than the indicative starting point can only be justified if in the second step of the BDB framework, the mitigating factors outweigh the aggravating factors. For the reasons set out below, I am of the view that this cannot be the case.

47     In relation to the second step of the BDB framework, where the indicative starting point is to be adjusted based on the offender’s culpability and the relevant aggravating and mitigating factors, the General Division of the High Court in GED took into account the following two aggravating factors:

(a)     The “group attack on V2 by GEH, D and E [the accused] heightened the fear he experienced, encouraged the continuation and escalation of the attack, and resulted in a higher degree of actual and potential harm to him” (at [171]).

(b)     The “manner and duration of the attack, which … lasted for about half an hour, with GEH and his co-offenders punching and kicking V2’s face and body numerous times while V2 was pinned to the ground in a chokehold and thus helpless to fight back or defend himself. The attack against V2 also persisted notwithstanding B pleading with GEH to stop and V2 saying at several points that he could not breathe” (at [172]).

48     The aforesaid first aggravating factor of a group attack would apply equally to the accused.

49     As for the second aggravating factor in relation to the manner and duration of the attack, there are two aspects from the aforesaid decision in GED which apply significantly to the accused:

(a)     It was the accused who pinned the victim to the ground in a chokehold and rendered the victim “helpless to fight back or defend himself”.

(b)     The accused continued to hold the victim in a chokehold, notwithstanding the victim “saying at several points that he could not breathe”.

50     In relation to the argument in the mitigation plea that the grievous hurt was inflicted by the co-accused, GEH, rather than the accused, I would point out the following:

(a)     The statement of facts states that “the accused tackled [the victim] and pinned him to the ground. He held [the victim] in a chokehold, with his arms squeezing [the victim’s] neck.”[note: 16] It is clear from this that the accused played a key role in subduing the victim and ensuring that the victim could not escape or defend himself.

(b)     The following extracts from the statement of facts makes it clear that the accused did not merely hold down the victim and had himself inflicted punches, kicks and slaps on the victim, poked the victim’s eyes and had held the victim to a chokehold until he could not breathe:

(i)       “While [the victim] was pinned, both [GEH] and the accused punched and kicked [the victim’s] face and body numerous times”.[note: 17]

(ii)       “The accused … poked [the victim’s] eyes with his fingers and choked him repeatedly to the point that he could not breathe, belittling [the victim] and punching and slapping his face when the latter begged him to release his grip.”[note: 18]

(c)     The statement of facts further states “This attack lasted about half an hour, with [the victim] being pinned to the ground and choked by the accused for at least 23 minutes, and with GEH and D alternating punches and kicks against [the victim’s] head and body. Towards the end of the incident, GEH also kicked [the victim’s] bare genitals while the accused held the [the victim] down.”[note: 19] It is clear from the statement of facts that the attack on the victim was prolonged. From the video of the attack that was played in court, it could be seen that the multiple blows struck on the victim were closely visible to the accused who was holding the victim down in a chokehold. There is nothing to suggest that the accused was merely a passive or reluctant participant in the attack on the victim or that the accused felt any concern upon seeing the blows suffered and the injuries sustained by the victim. The role of the accused in holding the victim to the ground in a chokehold was critical in enabling the grievous hurt that was caused to the victim.

51     In relation to the argument raised in the mitigation that the accused was influenced by others older than him, I would point out that the accused was above 21 years old at the time he committed the offence. He had already completed his national service in the Singapore Civil Defence Force as a section commander. In the mitigation plea, it is stated that “At the time of the offence, he was running his own hair salon.”[note: 20] It did not appear that the accused was so young and immature, that it should significantly reduce his culpability for the attack on the victim.

52     Furthermore, it is important to highlight that the protracted attack on the victim was enabled by the accused holding the victim in a chokehold, causing the victim to be defenceless. This was not a case of a young offender acting out of impulse or momentary loss of self-control. The accused held the victim in a chokehold on the ground for at least 23 minutes,[note: 21] and would have close sight of the repeated kicks and punches inflicted upon the victim. Despite this, the accused continued to hold the victim in a chokehold, notwithstanding the victim repeatedly pleading that he could not breathe. As the accused played a crucial role in the brutal, savage and prolonged attack on the victim, any mitigating value from the age of the accused would be minimal.

53     The mitigation plea states that the accused cooperated fully during investigations, apologises to the victim, and submits that this is a sign of his remorse.[note: 22] The prosecution submits that unlike GEH, the accused did not offer compensation and instead re-offended while court proceedings for this offence was ongoing.[note: 23] In relation to the absence of an offer of compensation, counsel for the accused explained that no offer was made as the victim had already turned down an offer of compensation from GEH.

54     I am of the view that what is critical in weighing any remorse of the accused, is the offence the accused committed giving rise to the Covid Charge, while the accused was on bail for the VCGH Charge and related offences. The accused hosted the poker game where he invited nine friends, advertised the game on public telegram channels and intended to create the misleading appearance that there were fewer people present by allowing four of the ten persons present to flee.[note: 24] One of the co-accused he invited was only 15 years old.[note: 25] The accused would have been aware of the gravity of the VCGH Charge that he was facing, yet he committed the offence relating to the Covid Charge, where he was the organiser of the poker game breaching the regulations, rather than a mere participant. In the premises, I put little weight on the plea by the accused that he was remorseful.

55     I note that the accused committed the Covid offence in April 2021, after the former commander of the accused furnished a testimonial dated 2 October 2020 stating that the charge for hurt that the accused was facing was “completely out of character”. The accused would have been aware that his commander had taken the effort to write a lengthy testimonial to vouch for the character of the accused. It is troubling that the accused appeared indifferent to his commander having staked his credibility to help the accused, by going on to commit the Covid offence even after that testimonial was provided.

56     As for the testimonial that was provided by the former form teacher of the accused, that testimonial is undated. Nevertheless, I note that the testimonial described that the accused “may have accidentally caused hurt during the unexpected fray without any ill intent”. In the light of the VCGH Charge that the accused has pleaded guilty to and the agreed statement of facts, it appears that the impression that was given to his former teacher on the role of the accused in the offence was wrong and misleading. Little weight can be placed on this testimonial.

57     In the light of the aforesaid reasons, I agree with the prosecution that the accused was as culpable as GEH in the attack. In view of the aggravating factors set out earlier, the uplift of 5 months applied by the General Division of the High Court in GED (at [177]) at the second step, should similarly apply in this case. This lifts the imprisonment term after the second step to 21 months.

58     As for caning, I am unable to agree with the mitigation plea that caning should not be imposed because of the youth of the accused. In the light of the severe injuries suffered by the victim and the aggravating factors that I have highlighted earlier, the prosecution’s submission of four strokes which is the same as that imposed by the General Division of the High Court in GED was reasonable.

59     Accordingly, the individual sentence to be imposed on the accused for the VCGH Charge is 21 months’ imprisonment and four strokes of the cane, the same sentence imposed by the General Division of the High Court on GEH for the corresponding charge, before the reduction in that case for the totality principle (at [177], [178]).

Insulting Modesty Charge

60     In relation to the Insulting Modesty Charge, I am unable to agree with the submission in the mitigation plea that as the accused was not involved with GEH sending the messages to others, the accused was less culpable.

61     The statement of facts state that “The accused continued to restrain [the victim] while [GEH] and [D] pulled down his pants and underwear to expose his genitals. When [the victim] attempted to resist, [D] punched his stomach, the accused poked his eyes a second time, and [GEH] kicked his crotch.”[note: 26] It is clear from the aforesaid extract that the accused was very much involved in the Insulting Modesty offence that was committed against the victim, to the extent that the accused poked the victim’s eyes when the victim attempted to resist having his pants and underwear pulled down.

62     The court in GED also highlighted that GEH “and his co-offenders forcibly stripped V2 and held his legs open, while V2 was pinned down, so that pictures of V2’s exposed penis – and thereafter the Video – could be taken. … V2 was utterly helpless to prevent either the taking of the Video or its subsequent distribution” (at [145]). Again, the accused was not a mere passive or reluctant participant. The accused pinning down the victim in a chokehold ensured that the victim could be stripped, his genitals exposed, the video taken and distributed, with the victim being unable to offer any resistance against the violent depravity that was being inflicted upon him.

63     While GEH was recording the victim’s injured face and exposed penis, the accused mocked the victim’s penis, encouraged his co-offenders to expose it and continued holding the victim in a chokehold despite the victim being unable to breathe. 64     The aforesaid extract shows that the accused was actively mocking the victim and encouraging GEH and D to open up the victim’s legs to expose the victim’s penis. In GED, the court made the following observations on the exchange that was captured in the video evidence:

(a)     “It is clear from the exchange … that GEH and his co-offenders acted with cruelty, and appeared to take a savage form of pleasure in mocking V2 in the dehumanising manner that they did. We regard this as substantially aggravating GEH’s culpability” (at [147]).

(b)     “GEH appears to have been spurred on by D and E [the accused], as the confrontation escalated, to film V2’s exposed penis in the Video and send the Video on to V2’s contacts” (at [148]).

65     The aforesaid observations would apply to the accused as well, in particular that GEH was “spurred on by D and E [the accused] … to film V2’s exposed penis in the Video and send the Video on to V2’s contacts”.

66     In the light of the aforesaid aggravating factors, I am unable to agree with the submission of six months’ imprisonment in the mitigation plea, which amounts to only half the maximum term of imprisonment of one year for the Insulting Modesty Charge. In view of these aggravating factors, the prosecution’s proposed sentence of nine months’ imprisonment for the Insulting Modesty Charge is entirely justified. Accordingly, the individual sentence for the Insulting Modesty Charge ought to be nine months’ imprisonment.

Public Order Charge

67     For the Public Order Charge, I note that the court in GED imposed a fine of $1,500 for a similar charge against GEH in that case. As the culpability of the accused for this offence is similar to GEH, I saw no reason to depart from the fine imposed in that case. Accordingly, I sentenced the accused to a fine of $1,500 (in default, 4 days’ imprisonment).

Covid Charge

68     For the Covid Charge, in the light of the accused having organised the poker game and committing the offence while he was on bail, I agreed with the prosecution’s submission on sentence and fined the accused $4,000 (in default, 10 days’ imprisonment).

Totality principle

69     The General Division of the High Court in GED (at [182], 183]), reduced the total term of imprisonment in its sentence against GEH, from 57 months to 51 months, in the light of the totality principle. The court reasoned that although the provisional aggregate sentence was not substantially above the normal level of sentences for the most serious of the individual offences committed, the effect on GEH of the provisional aggregate imprisonment term of 57 months’ imprisonment might be crushing, bearing in mind that he was a first-time offender and the remorse that he demonstrated.

70     In the present case, I am of the view that the totality principle should not give rise to a need for a reduction. Firstly, the provisional aggregate sentence is not substantially above the normal level of sentences for the most serious of the individual offences committed. Secondly, the provisional aggregate term of imprisonment of 30 months for the VCGH Charge and the Insulting Modesty Charge would not be crushing on the accused, bearing in mind that this is more than 40% lower than the total term of imprisonment of 51 months imposed on GEH and that both are first time offenders.

71     Furthermore, the General Division of the High Court in GED took into consideration the remorse of GEH, in reducing the sentence imposed in view of the totality principle (at [182]). In the present case, as I have explained earlier, there is little weight to be placed on any purported remorse by the accused, in view of his key role as the organiser in the offence relating to the Covid Charge, while he was on bail for the VCGH Charge and related offences.

Conclusion

72     In conclusion, I sentenced the accused to a total of 30 months’ imprisonment, four strokes of the cane and a fine of $5,500 (in default, 14 days’ imprisonment).
