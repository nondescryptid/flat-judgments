District Judge Kaur Jasvender:

1       This is a labour trafficking case in the entertainment sector at a dance bar called Jaiho Club located at the second floor of 46A Dunlop Street. The labour trafficking took the form of harbouring by means of coercion for the purpose of exploitation, i.e. to place them under servitude to work on terms dictated by accused, which was prosecuted under s 3(1) and punishable under s 4(1)(a) of the Prevention of Human Trafficking Act 2014 (‘PHTA’).

2       The three charges to which the accused claimed trial related to three adult females from India who worked as dancers and resided in a room at the third floor of the Club. Their identities are protected by a gag order. For the charge in DAC-922624-2016, the victim will be referred to as TA, for DAC-908828-2017 as KR and in relation to DAC-908830-2017 as CV. The particulars of the three charges are identical and as such only one charge is reproduced below:

DAC 922624-2016

… are charged that you, between 17 February 2016 and 15 June 2016, at unit 46A Dunlop Street, Singapore 209375, did harbour one [TA], by means of coercion, for the purpose of exploitation, to wit, servitude of the said TA in Singapore, and you have thereby committed an offence under section 3(1) of the Prevention of Human Trafficking Act 2014 (Act No. 45 of 2014), and punishable under section 4(1)(a) of the same Act.

Particulars

(1)    “Coercion” in relation to [TA] includes a combination of the following:

a.    Curtailment of her freedom of movement;

b.    Threat to frame her for theft;

c.    Threat of physical harm to her if she returned to India without your approval;

d.    Threat to send her family videos of her interacting with customers.

(2)    “Servitude” in relation to [TA] includes a combination of the following:

a.    Imposition of onerous financial demands for premature termination of employment;

b.    Requiring her to allow customers to dance with and touch her in the course of her work;

c.    Withholding monthly salary for the duration of employment.

3       The accused was convicted after a lengthy trial on the three charges which I was satisfied were proven beyond a reasonable doubt. He was sentenced to a total imprisonment term of 41 months’ and a fine of $27,365, in default 20 weeks’ imprisonment. He was ordered to pay compensation totalling $2,722. The accused has brought this appeal against the conviction and sentence and the compensation order. A stay of execution was granted on the sentence and the order.

Case for the Prosecution

4       The accused was in charge of the Club. He had a partner, one Velappan Krishna Kumar (‘Krishna’). The accused managed the daily running of the Club and all affairs relating to the three victims. The accused resided at the Club.

5       The accused made arrangements for the three dancers to work at the Club. TA arrived in Singapore on 17 February 2016, CV on 11 March 2016 and KR on 8 April 2016. Apart from the three victims, Muthu Vijayan Baskar (‘Baskar’) also worked there as a deejay and bartender and stayed at the club. He too is an Indian national. In addition, Danaraj s/o Pichanayagam (‘Raj’) worked as a cleaner and helped at the bar. He also resided at the Club.

6       The salient evidence of the witnesses called by the prosecution is summarised below.

Evidence of PW3 – Velloor Krishna Kumar (‘Velloor’)

7       The RR Group of Companies Pte Ltd (‘RR Group’) were the shareholders of the Club. Velloor became a director of RR Group around the fourth quarter of 2014.

8       On 23 April 2015, the RR Group entered into an agreement (see P17) with Velappan Krishna Kumar (‘Krishna’) for Krishna to operate the Club between 1 May 2015 to 30 April 2016 for a monthly fee of $11,000 for the first three months and $13,000 thereafter. The agreement was signed in the presence of the accused. In March 2016, the revenue of the Club dropped drastically. Consequently, Krishna and the accused were unable to pay the monthly fee in one lump sum. The accused and Krishna requested for a two or three month extension of the agreement to August 2016.

9       Velloor testified that the accused was in charge of the day to day management of the club. He said the accused would approach him to make online applications for work permits for the dancers. The accused gave him the code to key in their occupation as ‘performing artiste’ and informed him to enter their monthly salary as $800. He confirmed that he made the online work permit application in P10 for TA, in P15 for CV and in P18 for KR, at the request of the accused.

10     He said the dancers had complained to him that their phones were being withheld. The accused told him the girls would sometimes call the clients directly and he was trying to discourage that. With respect to the girls going out, he was asked in cross-examination[note: 1]:

Did you specifically tell Bala that the girls are not to be allowed to go off to wander on their own after the club closes?

He answered:

I have not specifically told him that, but I know that he never use to allow that.

Evidence of PW5 – Muthu Vijayan Baskar (‘Baskar’)

11     Baskar is an Indian national. He worked at the Club between 6 March 2016 to 15 June 2016.

12     Prior to 2016, Baskar had worked in Singapore. In 2010, he came to know the accused. In February 2016, whilst he was in India, he called the accused to arrange for a job for him. The accused told him that he is running a club and offered him a job to serve liquor and to clean the tables and club premises for a monthly salary of $1,000. Baskar agreed to do the work and the accused made the arrangements for him to work at the Club.

13     On 5 March 2016, he arrived in Singapore. The accused told him what his duties were and taught him how to do the work. He only reported to the accused. He was aware the accused had a partner named Krishna. Baskar stayed in the Club. He said the accused, the dancers and one Raj also stayed in the Club. TA was already working as a dancer together with four others when he came to the Club. CV and KR started work subsequently. He stated the accused generally did the marketing. Sometimes, he (Baskar) was asked to buy vegetables. The accused would do the cooking for the employees.

14     After the daily dance show ended at 3am, the accused would tally the amount received from the sale of liquor and the tips given by the customers to the dancers. This process would take about half an hour or so. He said the dancers and himself would be standing during this period near to the accused. He said the accused was abusive towards him when he suspected he had pocketed money although he (accused) had miscounted the money. He said the accused would talk to the dancers about their dancing, the amount of tips earned and their daily targets when he was checking the accounts. The accused would scold and abuse the dancers with vulgarities when the tips were low.

15     Baskar said that everyone would eat after the show at around 3.30am. He would go to sleep between 4.00am to 5.00am and get up at 5pm. He said if the dancers were hungry, they would inform the accused and the accused would in turn wake him up to buy food. He said this happened about four times in a week.

16     Asked how many entrances and exits there were to the Club, he said that there was only one entrance cum exit. He said the rear door was always secured with a padlock. He would only open it to bring the rubbish out. Asked if the street door and Club door would be open when the club is closed, he said both doors will be locked. However, the doors could be opened from within by twisting the door knob. He said he had seen the accused using the padlock to lock the Club door from the outside only when there was no one else in the Club and all of them were going out for a meal.

17     Asked if the dancers could leave the club on their own, he answered that they could go out and there were no restrictions. However, it was also his evidence that he had not seen the dancers going out on their own.

18     He stated that the mobile phones of the dancers which they brought from India were kept in a locked drawer at the bar counter. He said they were given another mobile phone with a local SIM card.

19     He confirmed that there were CCTV cameras in the Club which were recording 24/7. He said the accused used the CCTVs to monitor the activities in the Club and the staff, which included the dancers, himself and Raj.

20     The learned DPP was given leave to cross-examine Baskar on his prior statement (P23) which he made on 16 June 2016 between 9 to 11pm at the premises of the Ministry of Manpower (‘MOM’). The statement was recorded by MOM officer Teng Yi Liang Derrick (‘Derrick’) with the assistance of a Tamil interpreter, K Kalidas (‘Kalidas’). He was referred to [7] in which it was recorded that the accused had instructed him on numerous occasions that the dancers are not allowed to go out of the accommodation; that the accused did not want to risk the dancers to get pregnant by allowing them to go out with the customers; and that he (Baskar) was given the responsibility to ensure that the doors were always locked when the accused and himself were not in the Club. Baskar claimed he was misunderstood and denied that he said the dancers were not allowed to go out. He said the doors could be opened from the inside by twisting the knob. He stated that he said the accused told him that he should lock up when he (Baskar) went out. He said he did not know whether the accused allowed the dancers to go out. However, he agreed that he told the recorder that the accused told him that he does not want to risk the dancers to get pregnant by allowing them to go out with customers of the club. He said he only came to know of this reason when the accused told the police in June 2016 when the police came to the club.

21     With respect to the last sentence in [7] that the dancers were not able to leave the premises, he claimed the recorder and interpreter repeatedly insisted in a loud voice that if the accused asked him to lock the doors it meant that the girls could not go out. He accepted the statement was interpreted to him before he signed. However, he said the interpreter raised his voice and spoke forcefully to emphasise the point that the girls were unable to go out of the premises. He said he was frightened and signed the statement as there was no way out.

22     He went on to say that he was threatened that if he spoke in a manner that is not supportive, he would face a problem because it was illegal to start work before he obtained the work permit. The officer added that he will see to it that he does not face any problem if he cooperated. He confirmed that his work permit was issued on 23 March 2016.

23     He agreed that he gave the underlined contents at page 3 relating to photograph 9 which shows the interior of the Club door. He was asked who would be able to open the door, and he claimed that he meant by the answer that nobody could open the door from the outside, except for himself and the accused. He said the reason for locking the door so that no one could open it from the outside was misunderstood.

24     He agreed that the underlined contents at page 5 that he saw the accused slapping ‘Devi’ once and in relation to CV that he saw the accused raising his hand to slap her but did not know if he actually did so were accurate.

25     Under cross-examination, he said that all the dancers were “kind of angry” at TA because she received a lot of tips and the accused was ‘especially nice’ to her. He said that CV did not earn much tips as she could not dance. He initially stated that CV had asked the accused on two occasions if she could go out with a customer who was offering $1,000. Subsequently, he said she asked ‘many times’ to go out because customers were willing to pay $1,000. However, the accused rejected her requests. As a result, CV cried and made a lot of noise. He said the accused prohibited the dancers from going out with customers and said he was not running a brothel. He stated that a customer, George, had hit the accused with a bottle when he did not permit TA to go out with him, and on another occasion, another customer, Naidu, had slapped the accused for not allowing the dancers to go out with customers. He said there were two other occasions when customers caused physical damage when TA was not allowed to go out with them.

26     He said the accused had daily meetings with the dancers and he told them to earn tips from customers but not to get deeply involved with them like talking about marriage because they will be cheated. He said the accused had heard the same customers making marriage proposals to different dancers.

27     As regards his statement P23, he said another Chinese officer was present throughout the recording and he did the bulk of the questioning and raised his voice and scolded him. He said that Derrick spoke softly.

Evidence of PW1 – TA

28     TA was 38 years old when she came to Singapore from India on 16 February 2016. She was a dancer in India. She was in debt in India and came here to work as a dancer at the Club. She was introduced to the job by one Vetri and the accused’s younger brother, Chandru, whilst she was in India. She was not told how much she would earn but was informed that she would get ‘collections’.

29     She was told she would be given an advance of 70,000 Rupees (around S$1,400) in India, and out of this amount, she received 60,000 Rupees and the remainder was given to an agent in India. She was told she had to collect S$12,500 for the amount she had received, failing which she will not be able to return to India. After she settled the amount, she will have to continue to work until her visa expired. If she earned further sums of S$12,500, she will be given another 70,000 Rupees for each target achieved. However, the monies will be accumulated and given to her when she returned to India. In addition, if her daily earnings were more than $500 per night, she would be entitled to a daily bonus of $50 but it would also be kept and handed to her when she returned to India.

30     Upon arrival, the accused fetched her from the airport. She was taken to her place of work at the Club. She was asked by the accused to surrender her personal handphone. She said the accused kept the handphones of the dancers as he did not want them to contact the customers privately. However, she did not hand over her personal phone. About one week later, the accused demanded for her handphone. She refused and created a scene. About five to six days later, she surrendered her handphone as she realised that there was ‘no way out’. The accused then kept it.

31     She was in turn given the company’s handphone and told to use it to contact the customers to invite them to come to the Club. She was told not to make any phone calls to India with the phone. However, she did make calls to India with the phone. She had to surrender the company’s handphone at 8pm before the show started. She had to earn collections of at least $100 for the night in order to obtain the company’s handphone and retain it until 8pm the next day.

32     On 27 February 2016, she went for a medical check-up. On 8 March 2016, the accused brought her to apply for a work permit at MOM.

33     On 30 May 2016, there was a police raid. TA and the other dancers were brought to the police station. TA chose to return to the Club the following day but the other dancers did not. He explanation for returning to the Club was that the accused had told her that she would not be paid if it became a police case. She said that she wanted to continue working and to leave with the money she had earned. She continued working until there was a raid by MOM on 15 June 2016. She said the accused kept on saying that he will give her the money when she goes back to India but he did not.

34     After the MOM raid, she met the accused once at a bus-stop. Under cross-examination she said that the accused told her that the girls had got him into trouble and said a lot of bad things about him whereas he had actually taken good care of them. He asked her to follow her conscience.

Curtailment of freedom

35     She said she was not allowed to keep even $1 dollar from her daily collections. She could not purchase her own necessities. She and the other dancers had to ask the accused to purchase things and he would buy as he deemed fit. She gave the following evidence in this regard[note: 2]:

We should only listen to what he instructs. We cannot say anything, we cannot talk back, we cannot ask for anything that we desire on our own. Any little thing that we wanted to buy, even the toothpaste or brush, it has to be done only with his permission. Even our sanitary pad, we had to ask him and he would buy it for us and he would not---he would buy those of sub quality.

36     She stated the accused had brought her and the dancers out but he would always decide where to go and he would always be with them. According to her, he brought them for an outing on two occasions – first, to the Marina Bay area on the day when there was a power outage at the Club, and second, to the zoo with his relatives. She said they were not allowed to bring their handphones and were not allowed to speak to anyone outside.

37     She stated the dancers were not allowed to go out on their own. The doors to the premises were kept locked. She said the keys were kept by the accused and Baskar. She added that she also did not have her passport, work permit and any money. Under cross-examination, she agreed the lock on the inside of the Club door had a twist lock which should open the door by twisting the lock. For the rear exit door, she said it was padlocked from the inside. As for the door leading to the laundry area, she said it had a chain with a padlock. The accused will leave it open but lock it when he was angry. As for the street door of the Club, she said that she had never tried opening it. She only noticed that it was sometimes locked and sometimes left unlocked when she went out with the accused or Baskar.

38     As for the Club door, it was her evidence that the accused would lock it from the outside when he went out. She said that she had tried to open it once from the inside when the accused and Baskar were not in the club but was unable to. She said that Baskar would open the Club door at 7.30pm for the customers. She placed flowers at the staircase during the daily evening prayers between 8 to 9pm.

39     Under further cross-examination, she said that she tried to open the Club door once during the daytime by turning the handle but was unable to as there was “something small attached there”. She agreed that she did not turn the twist lock. Later, she said that before CV came, there “was not much locking going on around”. However, after CV came, the accused started to lock all the doors as he could not keep constant watch over CV who wanted to go out. She agreed that CV wanted to go out to prostitute herself. She stated that after CV came, a padlock was sometimes secured to the hook on the inside of the Club door.

40     With respect to not being allowed to go out, she agreed that the accused was being overprotective but said that he could have allowed them one day leave to go out. She agreed that she had gone with the accused to the market, to Mustafa and Sheng Siong on a regular basis when he did the shopping. She agreed that the accused had sometimes brought her and the other dancers to a nearby Chinese restaurant to eat. However, she said there was no freedom for her as the accused would be with them and he would bring them to places where he wanted to bring them. She did not agree that the accused always went out with them for their own good.

41     In re-examination, she said that when only the dancers were in the club and the accused went out, he would lock the door from the outside. She assumed the accused used the padlock to lock. She said they would then be unable to open the door from the inside. As for placing flowers during prayer time, she said the accused, Baskar and all the dancers would be aware when she did that.

Withholding monthly salary for the duration of employment

42     She said her declared salary was $800 to obtain the work permit. However, the accused did not pay her any salary. She was told that all the collections that she earned must be placed immediately into the collection box and she was not allowed to hold onto any cash. According to her, she collected $12,500 in one and a half months.

43     In cross-examination, she agreed that there was no issue of salary because the arrangement was for her to be paid from the collections that she earned.

Threat to frame for theft

44     She testified that the accused told the dancers that if they talked back or did not listen, he may put things in their bags and frame them for theft. She said that during the arguments, the accused would threaten that he would put a ‘dollar or two’ into their bags and make a police report stating that they have stolen the money. She said that he would use this sort of blackmail during arguments because he expected them to listen and abide by what he said.

45     In cross-examination, she said the accused did not threaten to frame her for theft but he made the threat to the other girls because she did not give the accused problems. She said the main problem was that the accused did not permit the girls to go out. She agreed that the threat would apply to any dancer who wanted to go out.

46     In re-examination, she said the threat to frame for theft was made every now and then to all the girls. She said the accused did not tell her specifically that he would put things in her bag.

Threat of physical harm

47     She testified that the accused told her that she could not go to India against his will, and if she did so, he will get someone to ‘finish you off’. She said that when she wanted to return to India within one week of arrival, the accused asked the bouncers to speak to her. The bouncers asked her why she wanted to go back after spending money to come here. As a result, she was frightened that the accused was asking people to speak to her.

48     In cross-examination, asked if she was threatened with physical harm if she were to return to India without approval, she answered: “I only know that I cannot leave without his permission. But as for this other things, I do not know.” She was then asked[note: 3]:

Q    So, no---“do not know” is one thing. You are in this Court testifying. And so it’s important you tell the truth. So, I’m asking this question. You agree with me there was no physical threat to say, for example, you will be killed, you will be beaten up. Correct?

A    Yes. Never threatened me with any harm if I were to return to India without his approval. But in other circumstances, he has spoken angrily.

49     She agreed that there was no physical threat or abuse to force her to stay and agreed that the accused had not threatened physical harm to her if she returned to India without approval. However, she said that she was unable to return without the permission of the accused as her passport and work permit were held by him. She said the accused also asked her to pay 200,000 Rupees instead of the 70,000 advance if she wanted to return, which she was unable to raise.

Threat to send videos of interactions with customers

50     Asked if the accused threatened to send videos of her with customers to his family, she said that during the fights he has remarked if she wanted him to show the videos of what she had done. Asked to elaborate, she said that she and the girls would hug the customers and dance. She said there were cameras in the Club and when there was a fight, the accused would say “You want me to play? Play and show what you have done? You want to see your colours?” She agreed that this referred to showing to her and the other girls to confront them. However, she later agreed that the threat was not directed to her but to the other girls.

Requiring her to allow customers to dance with and touch her in the course of her work

51     She testified in evidence-in-chief that she was expected to hold the hands of customers for solo performances in order to earn collections. She said the customers will hold her hands and hug her whilst dancing during solo performances. She said the accused told her to dance to the extent that she wanted to see to the collections coming in. She said it was apparent that the collections will only come in if she allowed the customers to touch her and she accordingly allowed them to do so.

52     Under cross-examination, she said the accused was very strict about having no physical contact with customers. She said the accused did not tell her to hold the hands of customers. She was asked[note: 4]:

Q    Isn’t it true, that when the learned deputy asked you what happens at---on the dance floor, when you say, men would hug you or touch you, Bala would be very angry if he had seen any of that happening, isn’t it? He would intervene, correct?

A    Yes, previously, he had gotten angry then later when the collections dropped, he said, “Try your very best to somehow dance and bring in the collections.”

53     Asked to explain the contradiction in re-examination, she said that she saw the other dancers holding the hands of customers and dancing. She told the accused that she did not want to dance like that. The accused told her that ‘Shalini’ was able to earn her collections because she danced like that and has left for India. He told her that she may dance like that, if she wanted to earn more money. However, he told her to be careful because if she were to be fined, the company would have to close down. She claimed she was left to do whatever she wanted to bring in the collections. As she was not able to bring in the collections without the customers touching and kissing her, she decided to allow them to do so. She said that she had danced at the VIP area where five solo songs will be placed continuously and the customer would dance with her. She said the customer would hold her hands, hug her and touch her on various parts of the body.

Premature termination of employment

54     She testified that the accused told her that she has received 70,000 Rupees, and that she must return him that amount if she wanted to go back. However, when she said she was prepared to return the amount, he changed the amount to 200,000 Rupees. She said that she then decided to stay on because she was bringing in collections and wanted to earn money for the sake of her children. In cross-examination, she was asked[note: 5]:

Q    In any event, your evidence - and correct me if I’m wrong - is that after that incident, you continued to stay there because you were making a lot of money.

A    Yes.

Q    So the fact is that this issue of 200,000, et cetera never really had an effect on your mind once you started seeing how much your revenue was.

A    Yes, that was all to it. That’s all.

Q    Yes, in fact, even when initially when he said 200,000, truth be told, that’s not the reason why you stayed on. You stayed on because you wanted to earn.

A    Yes.

Impeachment of credit

55     TA was referred to her statement recorded on 16 June 2016 at the MOM. At [21] she stated that the accused told her to tell the authorities that she received advance salary of six months totalling 300,000 Rupees in India. She complied with his request, and on 15 June 2016, she lied to a MOM officer that she had received 300,000 Rupees in India as advance salary. She agreed that she was afraid of the accused and Baskar and hence told the untruth.

56     She agreed that on 16 June 2016, she told the MOM officer that she will tell the truth as the accused had been arrested and she was no longer staying with Baskar. She was referred to [6] of her statement, in which she said:

On the same day, Bala told me the company rules such as I cannot go out on my own and I am only allowed to be in the club and my rest place upstairs. The entire exit doors are locked at the day and I and the other girls is not allowed to leave; only Bala and DJ Baska has the keys.

She agreed that what she said in her statement is the truth, i.e. that she and the other dancers were not allowed to leave on their own without permission. She agreed that the accused said that was the company’s rule. She stated in her statement that the exit doors were locked during the day because she had assumed the accused would use the padlock to secure the door as she had seen the padlock outside. She agreed the accused told her to tell the police that the door could be unlocked from the inside in the event of an emergency.

57     She was referred to [8] of her statement of 16 June 2016 where she said that it was accused who told her to let the customers touch and kiss her to earn tips. It was recorded as:

I saw the other dancers dancing sexily with the customers. The customers were hugging and kissing the other dancers while dancing. Bala told me that I have to let the customers touch and kiss so as to earn the tips, if the customers tip me for S$50/- I have to let them touch me and kiss me for 5 minutes. I thought that I have no choice but to follow his instruction as that is the only way I can collect enough tips for club to call home and earn a decent salary during my employment in Singapore.

She testified that she allowed only one customer whom she liked to dance and touch her. She said the other customers were angry and threatened to withhold their contributions to the collection. She said the accused scolded her for only dancing with one customer, and said “Why don’t you do the same with the others? You have come here to find your love or to earn your money?” She said the accused also told her that he would stop the customer from coming. She said it was making the accused angry because the others were not tipping her. She then started to allow all the customers to hug and touch her whilst dancing with her. She was referred to her statement which she gave on 2 September 2016 (P14) in which she said: Bala did not like me to entertain only one customer and only allow this one customer to touch. Bala then scolded me and told me to let other customers touch me also. He had scolded me in a threatening voice. Say that I am not that decent woman, and ask me not to act decent. Bala then threaten to ban my favourite customer who tip me a lot from coming to club. After sometime, I gave in and allowed the other customers to touch me.” She was asked by the DPP[note: 6]: “And we come back to paragraph 8 of 16th June. The truth of the matter, Ms TA, is in that last sentence which is underlined, because it says there, “I thought I have no choice but to follow his instructions”. That is the truth, isn’t it, Ms TA; you had no choice? She replied, “Yes, that’s the truth.”

58     Under further cross-examination, TA was referred to answer 10 of her police statement which was recorded on 31 May 2016 (D65) in which she stated that the “door can be unlocked from the inside in the event of an emergency”. She stated that she said that because she was able to open the door by pressing the door handle when she was doing the evening prayers.

59     She was next referred to answer 17 of her police statement (D65) where she said the accused “is a good person”. She said that she did not tell the truth then because she did not want to say anything against the accused as he was furious when the police came. She testified[note: 7]:

After Bala was arrested, I got frightened so I did not want to get involved in this. So I thought I would just give such a report and to leave for India. Furthermore, my---all my money was with him, so if I made a complaint against him, I’ll not be able to get my money. So that is why I said I’ll just give such a statement and then collect my passport and money and my phone that was with him and to leave for India. So I wouldn’t say that he’s a good person.

…

Yes, because if I were to say something else, then maybe I will not be able to leave and that’s why I said nice, positive things. And he is from Madurai and so am I, so I would not be able to stay there either.

Determination – statement J

191    The Prosecution bore the burden of proving beyond a reasonable doubt that statement J was made voluntarily. In Sulaiman bin Jumari v Public Prosecutor [2021] 1 SLR 557, the Court of Appeal stated the test of voluntariness in the following terms at [39]:

The first stage of the inquiry is whether there was any inducement, threat or promise having reference to the charge against the accused person. If any of these was present, the next stage examines whether the said inducement, threat or promise was such that it would be reasonable for the accused person to think that he would gain some advantage or avoid any “evil” (meaning adverse consequences) in relation to the proceedings against him. These constitute the twin limbs of the voluntariness test (Chai Chien Wei Kelvin v Public Prosecutor [1998] 3 SLR(R) 619 at [53]). The first stage considers objectively whether any inducement, threat or promise was made. This entails a consideration of what might be gained or lost as well as the degree of assurance (see, for example, Poh Kay Keong and Ismail bin Abdul Rahman v Public Prosecutor [2004] 2 SLR(R) 74). The second stage, which is the subjective limb, considers the effect of the inducement, threat or promise on the mind of the accused person.

Explanation 1 to s 258(3) of the Criminal Procedure Code 2010 states that “if a person in authority who had acted in such a manner that his acts tend to sap and have in fact sapped the free will of the maker of the statement… such acts will amount to a threat, inducement or promise.” The litmus test for oppression is whether the investigation was, by its nature, duration or other attendant circumstances, such as to affect the accused’s mind and will such that he speaks when he otherwise would have remained silent” (Tey Tsun Hang v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 1189 at [113]).

192    Taking first the threat to kill which the accused alleged was made to him in the van on the way back to MOM. The accused had two versions on who made the alleged threat. During the questioning of Gerald and Seva, the version which was put to them was that the threat was made by Derrick. On the other hand, during Elwin’s testimony and when the accused gave evidence, he claimed the threat was made by Elwin. Findings

212    Labour trafficking can take multiple forms depending on the economic conditions and the state of labour regulation and enforcement in a country. In a country like ours, where workers enter legitimately on a work pass, labour trafficking takes on a more subtle form. The means set out in s 3 of the PHTA go beyond the traditional practices of forced labour, such as slave-like practices. Its intent is to bring it in line with international standards on trafficking in persons.

213    It is undisputed that the three dancers were harboured at the Club. They stayed in a room which had a signage ‘storeroom’ at the third floor. Apart from accommodation, they depended on the accused for their daily meals and necessities. They worked daily without any off-day and their passports and work permits were kept by the accused. The issue is whether they were harboured by means of coercion and for the purpose of exploitation, i.e. to place them under servitude to work for the accused on the terms he dictated.

214    Section 2 of the PHTA defines ‘coercion’. The definition makes it clear that the use of a threat can be “violent or otherwise”. I accepted the prosecution’s submission that ‘physical restraint’ should not be restricted to the application of manual means to restrain a person but encompass rules to restrain a person’s freedom of movement. The prosecution’s case was that in order to keep the dancers in his employment, the accused had rendered them physically and psychologically unable to leave their employment by four means of subjugation, which were particularised as:

(a)     Curtailment of freedom of movement such that the dancers were not allowed to go out of the premises on their own.

(b)     Threat to frame for theft, putting the dancers in fear of disobeying the accused.

(c)     Threat of physical harm if they returned to India without approval, putting the dancers in fear of leaving their employment without the accused’s approval.

(d)     Threat to send videos to the dancers’ families of their interaction with customers, putting the dancers in fear of disobeying the accused.

215    As regards exploitation, section 2 of the PHTA defines ‘servitude’. In the brief remarks issued by the High Court in Public Prosecutor v Khema Bhatta (MA No. 9343 of 2020/01), the following observations were made in relation the definition of ‘servitude’:

(a)     Servitude involves deprivation of personal autonomy, not confined to just physical control, namely the conditions are such that one cannot escape or not be free to change.

(b)     There is no requirement that the condition is permanent but it must be for a period of time that is more than negligible, but need not have a permanent character. There can thus be servitude for a specific or limited time.

(c)     The condition need not be illegal as such, but is enough that it is not specifically permitted by any written law. Obligations expressed in contract can give rise to servitude, if that is in effect in reality.

216    The prosecution’s case is that there was servitude in that the three dancers were “bound to work for the accused for the six-month contractual period and were not free to leave their employment whenever they wanted and could not change this obligation” and relied on three particulars:

(a)     Imposition of onerous financial demands for premature termination of employment.

(b)     Requiring the dancers to allow customers to touch them in the course of solo dances.

(c)     Withholding monthly salary for the duration of their employment.

217    The fact that the dancers continued to stay at the Club and continued with their employment is irrelevant. In this regard, section 3(3) of the PHTA provides:

In determining whether an offence has been committed under this section, the following shall be irrelevant:

…

(b)    in any other case, whether the alleged victim of the offence consented to the actual or intended exploitation.

218    I shall first deal with the evidence of the two employees of the club, Baskar and Raj.

If indeed, the focus on locking was to prevent outsiders, then it is odd that he made no reference to this alleged claim in the statement. Also, if the dancers were able to open the door from within if there was no one else in the club, then it is odd that he made no mention of thist alleged fact. Baskar disputed that he gave this sentence. He alleged that he was threatened by an unknown male Chinese officer who was present throughout the recording that he had worked illegally before he obtained his work permit. I rejected this allegation. It is not in dispute that Baskar had an in-principle approval (‘IPA’) issued to him which enabled him to travel and work here before the physical copy of the work permit was issued. Further, Baskar had worked here previously and knew that he could start work the day after he arrived on an IPA. It is therefore indeed absurd that he would be threatened with a spurious claim which he would have known was false.

He paid attention to the interpretation and had no fear in pointing inaccuracies in the statement. At [8], he pointed out it should be in the month of May and not March as was recorded. For photograph 17, it was recorded that the tips are for the ‘company’ and it was changed to ‘Bala’ when Baskar said the accused collected and held onto the tips. For photographs 23 to 25, the words “sometimes the padlock” were deleted, at his request.

228    Accordingly, I found that Baskar was the author of statement P23 and he had failed to satisfactorily explain the contradictions between his evidence and the prior statement. I found that his credit was impeached and substituted Baskar’s testimony with the inconsistent underlined contents of his previous statement as substantive evidence under s 147(3) of the Evidence Act.

229    As regards Baskar’s evidence that he did not hear the accused threatening the dancers whilst he was present near to where the accused had his meetings with the dancers after the daily shows, Baskar was not a participant at the meetings but was “going over the sales of the liquor, counting the money that I received from the customers”. It would indeed be surprising that he paid attention to every detail of what was said at the meetings for him to testify that no threats were made at the meetings. In any case, it was not the evidence of the dancers that the threats were only made during such meetings. Accordingly, I did not find Baskar’s evidence to be of any assistance on what transpired between the accused and the dancers.

238    In addition to the previous inconsistent MOM statements being used for the impeachment of credit, previous inconsistent statements are also admissible as evidence of any facts stated therein (see s 147(3) Evidence Act 1893). It is undisputed that CV did not receive the two alleged payments as stated in the payment vouchers P11. It is also not in dispute that CV was not paid any monthly salary. She had received an advance payment of 50,000 Rupees (about SGD $1,000) in India. It is obvious that the two vouchers P11 were false documents. As CV was an employee, she was entitled to a monthly salary.

282    Further, the accused’s arrangement was that CV had to earn SGD8,000 for the 50,000 rupees. This was from the tips which she received from customers for the dance performance. Instead of the tips belonging to her, the accused treated the tips as belonging to the Club and required her to earn about seven times more than what she had received. This was clear exploitation of CV.

Charge relating to KR

283    The thrust of the defence case was that KR gave adverse evidence because she was angry that the accused had sent audio clips of messages from a customer to her husband on 15 June 2016, and that her credit ought to be impeached because her evidence was contrary to her police statement made on 31 July 2016 (D69) and her conversation with one Anitha in June 2016. I was satisfied that KR gave a satisfactory explanation for the contradictions between her police statement and what she told Anitha and found that her credit was not impeached.

284    KR was candid that her relationship with the accused was good for about one month. In her words[note: 50], “Yes, from the time I came, for about 1 month, yes he treated me well. Then after that, he showed his true colours.” She described his erratic behaviour as akin to a ‘psycho’. In similar vein to TA, she testified that the accused simply wanted to assert his authority and make the dancers do his bidding.

285    With respect to her police statement, I accepted her evidence that her encounter with the police was something “new” and the “foremost thing” that she was feeling when she answered the questions was fear. She said the accused had handed TA a handphone and she suspected that she was communicating with the accused. The following evidence on what was operating on her mind had a clear impress of truth[note: 51]:

The first time when I was giving the statement, I didn’t know anything about this, I was frightened of saying anything against him and I was afraid that I maybe stopped from going back to India. I didn’t know the police here will give so much protection. I was in a totally fearful state of mind. I was totally afraid of saying anything against him.

286    Learned counsel questioned her on Answer 3 where she said “We do not ask to go out.” and answer 16 where in response to the question if she felt that she was prevented from leaving the ‘house’, she answered, “No, not really.”, whereas her evidence in court was that she and the other dancers were denied permission to go to the temple. I did not find anything to turn on this discrepancy. In the same answer (Answer 3), she stated that the “The rules was as such, that we had to stay in the house…” unless the accused brought them out. Her evidence that the dancers wanted to go to the temple was consistent with TA and CV’s evidence.

287    As regards answer 4 where she said “We are not allowed to go down from the stage.” and answer 16 that the accused “does not let us talk to the customer”, it was TA and CV’s evidence that they had to shake hands with the customers and make calls to customers to invite them to the club. The accused confirmed that dancers wrote their phone number on pieces of paper for customers (see P4) or gave it to customers to write their numbers. He said that if the dancers were to “…do such things, then the customers would come back, it would improve our business.”[note: 52] Answers 4 and 16 were therefore untrue statements as testified to by KR. I accepted her explanation that she “was afraid of telling police some things and getting caught. Only I did not know the police provided such safety.[note: 53]”

288    Learned counsel highlighted answer 8 where KR had said that if the dancers do not do well, they were punished by confiscation of phones by the accused to suggest that KR had complained against the accused. I did not agree that this was a complaint, or that it suggested that KR was not fearful of speaking against the accused. In question 8, she was asked about the rules and stated how the dancers were rewarded for doing well. She naturally had to state the corollary of not doing well. It is also significant that the answer was consistent with the fact that KR did not have a phone with her when the police came and the phone was retrieved from the accused with the assistance of the police.

289    As regards answer 9 where she said there was not much issue and she felt protected by the accused, which she said was untrue, it is clear that KR was not simply recanting her statement. She was candid in testifying that the accused would sometimes speak nicely to her and had not hit her. With regard to answer 10, it was claimed by the defence that KR had in fact complained to the police that the accused locked the door from the outside and the main door was locked from the inside at night. I accepted KR’s evidence that this was stated matter-of-factly. In fact, what is significant is that she did not reveal her true feelings and instead said, “I did not feel that I was confined or anything.”. The statement that she did not feel confined although she did not have the freedom to step out alone cannot be a normal reaction. This clearly supported KR’s evidence that she was in fear in of speaking against the accused.

290    In answer 11, she stated that she did not see the accused hit the other dancers. Although the defence had placed emphasis on the fact that the question referred to the dancers being non-compliant, the fact of the matter was that both CV and ‘Devi’ were hit and despite that KR stated that she did not see anything. This again showed her reluctance to state anything adverse against the accused due to her fear as she testified.

291    Turning now to the conversation which KR had with Anitha and a third unknown person after the accused’s arrest by MOM. The conversation took place at the hostel where KR was housed by MOM and it was recorded by Anitha. It is not in dispute that the accused knows Anitha. He initially referred to Anitha as a relative and later said she is a friend. KR knew that Anitha was recording their conversation at the behest of the accused. She said[note: 54]:

And MOM has sent us to a place, a safe place. There, Bala can send a spy. When she kept on talking about Bala incessantly, I understood this one was an arrow shot by Bala. When she came to me asking about why we came out, what happened and asking things about Bala itself, I understood that she was Bala’s person.

I accepted KR’s evidence that “In that situation, where [Anitha] was speaking favourably about Bala, I saw it fit that I also spoke favourably. If I had not done that, I feared that I may then face some problems.”[note: 55]

Coercion

(a)    Curtailment of freedom of movement

292    With respect to coercion, I accepted there was curtailment of freedom of movement. KR was not allowed to leave the premises without permission and could only do so accompanied by the accused or the other men in the club. She testified that the EZ link card was taken away after it was tapped. KR disagreed that the restriction was in her best interest and she appreciated it. She disagreed that she did not want to go out on her own. Her answers in this regard are set out below[note: 56]:

Q    Yes. Because you didn’t want to do that also, correct?

A    Cannot go. It’s not that did not want to go, it cannot go. That’s the rules---that’s the rules that I’ve been told. Even to the toilet, we have been told we can only go so many times.

Q    No, the question is this, have you---have you wanted to go on your own? Yes or no?

A    Yes, I had---

Q    Okay.

A    wanted to go.

(b)    Threat to frame for theft

293    I found KR’s evidence that the accused threatened to frame her for theft by telling her that he would remove his ring and bracelet and put them in a bag and take a photograph and send to the police to be convincing. She did not exaggerate and said that he made this threat only once. She also confirmed that the accused did not take the photograph but had only threatened her. I found that this was not an allegation which she had trumped up. She was able to explain that he said this because she was pestering him to go back to India and he accused her of ganging up with all the girls.

(c)    Threat of physical harm if she returned without permission

294    I accepted KR’s evidence that the accused said[note: 57] “I see how you go back to India. I will send people to---I will send people to abduct [you] or to assault [you] at the airport.” When it was put to her that her evidence was untrue, she answered wryly[note: 58], “He did---he did threaten me, but, ho---how could I---could I have that kind of recording that he was doing.” I was satisfied that KR’s evidence that the accused threatened physical harm if she returned to India without his approval was true.

(d)    Threat to send videos of interactions with customers

295    KR testified that the accused did not want the dancers to talk back. I accepted KR’s evidence that the accused had threatened to put recordings of her dancing with customers on Facebook and she was afraid that her family would see it. In particular, I accepted the following evidence:[note: 59]

A    He has---he has also threatened to put it on Facebook. So that’s why I---I became very frightened. Everywhere, there is camera. Even the places where you walk, there are cameras.

Q    What did he threaten to put on Facebook?

A    We’ll be dancing with the customer and all that. We must just be agreeable to---just agree with whatever he says, not talk back. That’s what he expects.

296    KR’s evidence is supported by the fact that the accused did make good the threat by sending two audio recordings of KR’s interaction with a customer to KR’s husband after the police raid. I rejected the accused’s evidence that the audio recordings were sent to him by Anitha. I found that the accused had obtained the recordings from the company’s phone that KR was using.

Servitude

(a)    Imposition of onerous financial demand for premature termination of employment

297    KR testified that she was asked to pay 100,000 Rupees if she wanted to go back to India. She was unable to repay the amount. She asked him to calculate the collections that she has received so that she could pay the balance. However, the accused did not do the calculation. She also asked if she could repay him after she has been sent back. The accused did not agree. As she was unable to repay the amount, she was unable to return.

(b)    Requiring dancers to allow customers to touch in the course of solo dances

298    The accused accepted that he required the dancers to sustain the Club’s business. He agreed that if a dancer was able to draw in the crowd, the collections from the tips and liquor sales would increase. From the audio-recording (see transcript P9), it is apparent that the accused was checking the phones of dancers to ascertain if they were calling customers. KR told the accused, “I am talking to the customers, sir.” (pg 2). The accused then remarked to her “Let us see. If this continues I will give you only company phone.” He next asked another dancer, ‘Abi’, and said “No problems. Made many calls.” He then asked where another dancer ‘Devi’ was. He said to her “Come here how many call you make today? You did not call anybody. You made calls for namesake. You just dial cancel the call and put down the phone.” He then turned to CV and said there were still a lot of missed calls. He continued ‘There are many calls. Just like ‘Devi’ 0 minutes. We are checking so make calls for namesake. Just made calls then cut the line.” The accused said “Show some interest. We are going down. I need to pay $15,000 rent in 15 day time. How to pay?” In his statement P33, at [8], the accused said, “I also asked them to keep contact numbers of customers of the club. They are to message or call the customers to invite them to our club. I checked their company phones every day to ensure the dancers bring in customers.”

299    I accepted KR’s evidence that when the customer paid $50, the customer had a choice to come up to the stage and dance with her. She said that some customers would hold her hands and ask her to twirl, some would hug her and some would place their hands on her chest. I accepted her candid evidence that she was compelled to allow the customers to touch during solo dances because if she did not, she will not be able to earn $50 for each solo dance, as the opportunity will go to another dancer, like TA, and she will also not be able to receive the company phone if she did not earn $100 per day. I accepted the prosecution’s submission at [120] that [t]he accused created the environment such that the victims would have no choice but to allow the customers to touch them.

(c)    Withholding monthly salary for the duration of employment

300    It is undisputed that KR was not paid any salary. She was asked to sign the payment voucher in P11 dated 3 May 2016 for the amount of $585 and was told that it was payment to the government for her. I accepted her evidence that she was told that for an advance payment of 100,000 Rupees in India, she would have to earn SGD12,800 within six months. However, she received only 70,000 Rupees. She was to be entitled to 10 per cent of her collections only after she had earned the target sum. This clearly reflected a gross underpayment and exploitation.

Conclusion

301    With regard to the photographs of outings and a birthday celebration tendered by the defence, I accepted the prosecution’s submissions at [133]:

The Defence also tendered photographs (Exhibit D1-D43) depicting the victims looking happy on various outings and a birthday celebration. Just because the victims looked happy in the photographs did not mean that the conditions at work and the Club were good. There were clearly other instances which showed otherwise – as evident in Exhibit P9 and P9T. Just because the victims were sometimes treated well by the accused did not mean that they were not also subject to unreasonable and unlawful demands and various kinds of abuses. It certainly does not detract from the fact that the offences detailed above were committed. Further, it is also clear that any benefits were purely at the whim of the accused and the victims could just as easily incur the wrath of the accused over any small misstep. Pertinently, there is also no legal requirement that labour trafficking must involve an unremitting and unchanging hellscape of suffering for its victims. To hold thus would be a great disservice to the victims, as it would give them justice only when they are faced with unrelenting torture, when in reality, human traffickers can sometimes be nice to their victims for various reasons.

302    Looking at the evidence in totality, the cumulative effect of the scheme was that the victims were trapped in the situation they found themselves in on the terms decided by the accused, and had no alternative but to continue working and complying with the accused. I found it safe to conclude beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused had used coercive means for the purpose of ensuring that the dancers had no choice but to continue to work for him on the terms dictated by him. Accordingly, the accused was found guilty and convicted on the three charges.

Address on Sentence

303    The prosecution sought a global imprisonment term of five years and $31,200 fine. In addition the prosecution sought a compensation order for the sum of $1,675.40 in respect of TA and $1,046.60 in respect of CV.

304    The prosecution submitted that the relevant sentencing objective is deterrence “to send an unequivocal message to strongly deter such acts as part of the wider aim to combat and eliminate human trafficking in Singapore”. Reference was made to two decisions. In Public Prosecutor v Bhattacharya Priyanka Rajesh and anor [2020] SGDC 124 (‘BPR’), the offenders were convicted after trial of three charges under s 3(1)(d) p/u s 4(1)(a) of the PHTA r/w s 34 of the Penal Code in respect of three victims. They were also convicted of Women’s Charter offences relating to one of the victims. There were eight charges under s 3(1) PHTA and 10 Women’s Charter offences which were taken into consideration for sentence. The PHTA charges related to harbouring the three victims by means of abuse of power and the nature of exploitation was servitude. They were each sentenced to an aggregate of five years and six months’ imprisonment and with total fines of $7,500 fine.

305    In Public Prosecutor v BSR [2020] 4 SLR 335 (‘BSR’), the offender faced, inter alia, one charge of recruiting his wife for the purpose of sexual exploitation, by placing her in fear of being physically assaulted if she refused to prostitute herself, an offence under s 3(1)(a) and punishable under s 4(1)(a) PHTA and another charge of knowingly receiving payments in connection with the sexual exploitation of his wife, an offence under s 6(1) and punishable under s 6(2)(a) of the PHTA. The offending took place over less than three months. She earned an estimated $10,930 through prostitution. He pleaded guilty. Having regard to the high level of harm and culpability, the accused was sentenced to six years’ imprisonment, three strokes of the cane, and $6,000 fine for the s 3(1)(a) PHTA offence. He also faced other charges under the Penal Code. The sentence was upheld by the Court of Appeal.

306    The prosecution highlighted that the facts in BSR were more egregious but noted that there was only one victim. In the instant case, there are three trafficked victims and the accused committed the offences over a longer period of time. It was submitted that the accused had exploited the vulnerability of the victims who are lowly educated and depended on the accused for every aspect of their daily lives. They had no choice but to continue with their employment as their requests to be sent back were rejected.

307    With respect to the mandatory fines, the prosecution referred to the High Court decision in Public Prosecutor v Su Jiqing Joel [2021] 3 SLR 1232 (‘Joel Su’) at [37] where it was held that where the court imposes a fine in addition to imprisonment, the primary purpose would be to disgorge profits which the offender may have made from his illegal behaviour, subject to the maximum limit of the fine prescribed by statute. As a starting point, the court may presume that the entire revenue received by the offender represents his profits. From the revenue, the court should only permit a deduction for necessary expenses, i.e. expenses the sole purpose of which was to enable the offender to commit the offence: Joel Su at [61] and [63]. The burden of proving such necessary expenses lies with the accused: Joel Su at [76].

308    Turning now to the revenue which represented the profits. The prosecution submitted that the alcohol sales and the tips earned by the dancers represented the total revenue. The prosecution, however, used only the tips earned by the victims to derive the amount of fines to disgorge the accused’s profits as a result of his offending. The amounts of the tips for each victim were extracted from the daily tips collection as recorded in Exhibit P7. The record showed the following:

a.     TA (period of employment between 17 February 2016 to 15 June 2016) – $20,0346.

b.     KR (period of employment between 8 April 2016 and 29 May 2016) – $6,5107.

c.     CV (period of employment between 11 March 2016 and 29 May 2016) – $4,7458.

309    Next, in respect of the deduction of the necessary expenses which should be permitted, the prosecution disputed the amount of $13,725 proposed by the defence. It submitted that the amount should instead be $3,420. The prosecution accepted that food expenses were necessary expenses but disputed the sum of $50 per day for each victim. It was pointed out that in the cross examination of CV, it was put that the accused spent “$50 a day on food for everyone”[note: 60], as opposed to $50 for each victim. The prosecution submitted that the accused was likely to have spent closer to $10 per day for each victim. The prosecution accepted that cosmetic expenses were necessary expenses given the nature of the victims’ job and did not dispute the amount of $300 for each victim proposed by the defence. The prosecution disputed that the accused incurred phone top-up charges. Reference was made to the evidence of CV and KR that the accused did not top up the credit in the phones. The accused also did not produce any evidence to show that he topped up the credit.

310    With respect to the compensation order under s 359 CPC, the prosecution sought an order based on the declared monthly salary of $800. For TA, she received 70,000 Rupees in India, which is about $1,489 based on the exchange rate of 47 Rupees to one SGD in 2016. Her period of employment was from 17 February 2016 to 15 June 2016, which is about 120 days. It was submitted that the salary she should have received less the advance payment would amount to $1,675.40.14.

311    For KR, she also received 70,000 Rupees in India, which is about $1,489. Her period of employment was from 8 April 2016 to 29 May 2016, which is about 52 days. Her advance payment exceeded the salary she should have received by $117.76. Accordingly, no compensation was sought for her.

312    For CV, she received 50,000 Rupees in India, which is about $1,063. Her period of employment was from 11 March 2016 to 29 May 2016, which is about 80 days. The salary she should have received less the advance payment would amount to $1,046.

Mitigation

313    The accused is a 46 year old Indian national. His health issues include diabetes, high blood pressure and a deep wound on his right leg. He has no antecedents. He is no longer involved in the entertainment industry and his risk of reoffending is low.

314    The defence sought a sentence of not more than two years’ imprisonment and a fine of $2,500 for TA’s charge and not more than one year’s imprisonment and a fine of $2,500 for each of the charges relating to KR and CV. The total sentence sought was three years’ imprisonment and a fine of $7,500.

315    For BSR, the defence highlighted that the level of harm and culpability was found to be high by the High Court. As regards BPR, it was highlighted that the sentences were lower than that in BSR as the court found that the charges in BPR were significantly different from the factual matrix in BSR. Learned counsel submitted that the factual matrix in BPR was more egregious than the instant case. First, in BPR, the accused persons had applied significant pressure for the victims to offer sexual services and one of the victims did so. In contrast, the accused did not allow the victims to provide such services. Second, in BPR, the victim’s personal communication with the outside world was cut-off by confiscation of their personal phones. In contrast, the victims in the instant case were issued with the company’s phone which they used to contact their customers and families. The accused did not require the victims to speak to their families in his presence.

316    With respect to the fines, the defence submitted that the accused’s ability to pay the fine may be a relevant consideration, especially when there is a combined sentence of imprisonment and fine to ensure that the sentence does not become excessive or disproportionate (Koo Kah Yee v PP [2021] 3 SLR 1440 (‘Koo Kah Yee’). It was submitted that the tips did not represent the actual profits earned by the accused as he received a monthly salary of $3,000 and he earned $12,000 between February 2016 to June 2016. It was submitted that the accused would be unable to pay a high fine as he had been receiving financial assistance from the Department of Medical Social Work from the National University Hospital in order to pay his medical bills.

317    The defence proposed a total deduction of $13,725 based on $50 per day for food expenses for each dancer, $300 cosmetic expenses for each dancer and $25 monthly phone top-up charges for each victim.

Sentence

318    The charges carry mandatory imprisonment and fine. The maximum sentence is up to ten years’ imprisonment, a fine up to $100,0000 and discretionary caning up to six strokes. The accused was sentenced as follows:

TA

(DAC-922624-2016)

26 months’ imprisonment and fine of $18,294, in default 14 weeks’ imprisonment

KR

(DAC-908828-2016)

15 months’ imprisonment and fine of $5,586, in default 4 weeks’ imprisonment

CV

(DAC-908830-2016)

21 months’ imprisonment and fine of $3,485, in default two weeks’ imprisonment

The 26-month term and 15-month terms were ordered to run consecutively, making a total of 41 months’ imprisonment, and a total fine of $27,365, in default 20 weeks’ imprisonment.



319    In addition, a compensation order was made in respect of TA for the sum of $1,675.40, in default one week’s imprisonment. In respect of CV, the order is for a sum of $1,046.60, in default one week’s imprisonment. The total compensation ordered was $2,722, in default two weeks’ imprisonment.

320    The relevant sentencing objective is general deterrence to deter such offences and to offer protection to victims. It is trite that the court ought to consider the full spectrum of sentences enacted by Parliament in determining the appropriate sentence (Poh Boon Kiat v Public Prosecutor [2014] 4 SLR 892 at [60]). In calibrating the sentence, the following set of factors are relevant to assess the seriousness of the offending: the period of harbouring, the level of vulnerability of the victims, the number of victims, the nature and degree of coercion exercised upon the victims to maintain control over them, the nature and degree of exploitation, the level of sophistication and planning of the scheme, and the accused’s role.

321    The sentences imposed in BPR and BSR which have been upheld on appeal are useful indicators. BSR is a far more egregious case where both harm and culpability were found to be high. BPR is broadly analogous to the instant case but it has more aggravating factors. First, there was deception in that the victims were promised jobs as PAs. Second, the victims were expected to provide sexual services. Third, they did not have any means of communication with their families. Fourth, the number of PHTA charges was higher; 11 in total, three proceeded with and the remaining charges were TIC-ed. In BPR, the individual sentence for V1 who was harboured for about five months was four years’ imprisonment and fine of $2,500, for V2 who was harboured for two weeks’ and V3 who was harboured for close to two months, two years’ imprisonment and $2,500 fine each. The four-year sentence for V1 was ordered to run consecutively with a Women’s Charter charge. The total sentence was 5 years and 6 months’ imprisonment with a fine of $7,500, in default six weeks’ imprisonment.

322    In the present case, the prosecution sought a total sentence of 5 years’ imprisonment. In comparison to BPR, I found the level of harm and culpability to be lower. Accordingly, I did not agree with the sentences proposed by the prosecution. I noted the period of harbouring and the exploitative conditions under which the victims were held. Their poor economic conditions in India were exploited by paying them an advance payment in return for which they had to earn a ‘target’ sum which was about seven times more for the Club. They were not allowed to go out on their own even to buy necessities and were totally dependent on the accused. Their personal handphones were taken away. They were required to earn $100 per day to be entitled to use the company’s phones. They were required to permit customers to touch them during solo dance performances for which the customers paid $50. Threats were made to ensure compliance and they were unable to terminate their employment to return back as they were unable to pay the amounts demanded. Their passports were confiscated and their work permits were never provided to them. They were thus trapped to continue working every night with no off day and with non-payment of salary. The accused played the primary role in the entire scheme, exercising control and authority over the victims.

323    In calibrating the individual sentences, I made adjustments for the custody period from 15th June 2016 to 5th July 2016 when the accused was released on bail and the totality principle taking into account the in default sentences for the mandatory fines. Accordingly, I arrived at individual sentences of 26 months’ imprisonment for TA, 15 months’ imprisonment for KR and 21 months’ imprisonment for CV. The 26-month and the 15-month terms were ordered to run consecutively, making a total sentence of 41 months’ imprisonment.

324    Turning now to the imposition of the fines. Whilst there was a financial assistance memo dated 14 December 2020 tendered in respect of the accused’s hospital bills, I was not satisfied that by itself established that the accused is impecunious. The accused stated in his evidence that he is employed as a chef. He said he had a good reputation in Little India because he has several businesses there. Specifically, he mentioned he had a ‘side business’ of exporting laptops and of electronic appliances whilst he was running the Club. In any case, this is not a case where a fine is discretionary. As a fine is mandatory, the primary purpose was to disgorge the financial gains to ensure that the accused is not better off by reason of having committed the offences.

325    The recorded tips were earned by the victims during their dance performances. They were directly related to the offending. In fact, these amounts were given to the victims by the customers and rightfully belonged to them instead of the Club. The accused agreed that he required dancers to draw in the customers. This resulted in increased sales of liquor. However, there was no reliable way of calculating the monthly revenue earned from the sale of liquor which was attributable to the dancers. Accordingly, the tips earned by the victims was a very conservative approach in determining the benefits derived from the offending.

326    I disagreed that the accused’s so-called monthly ‘salary’ of $3,000 should represent the ceiling for the calculation of the profits earned. The accused was not an employee. He was a partner of the business with Krishna and he had a stake in the revenue of the Club.

327    In respect of the deduction for necessary expenditure, it is undisputed that the food expenditure for the three victims should be permitted to be deducted. The dispute related to the quantum of the expenditure per day that should be allowed. I found the daily figure of $50 per victim suggested by the defence to be excessive. The prosecution suggested $10 per day for each victim. In my view, an amount of $12 per day for each victim is reasonable. Accordingly, for TA, the figure was worked out to be $1,440, in respect of KR, $624 and for CV, $960. In respect of the cosmetic expenses, I gave the accused credit for the proposed sum of $300 per victim. In respect of the phone top-ups, the burden was on the accused to provide the evidence. No such evidence was provided and in fact the evidence indicated otherwise. Accordingly, I disallowed this expenditure.

328    The amount of the tips recorded in P7 totalled $20,0346 for TA, $6,5107 for KR and $4,7458 for CV. After deducting the expenditure, I imposed a fine of $18,294 in respect of TA, $5,586 in respect of KR and $3,485 in respect of CV. The total in default sentence of 20 weeks’ was calculated to ensure that the overall sentence combined with the imprisonment term would not be disproportionate.

329    With respect to the compensation order, s 359 CPC imposes an obligation upon the court to consider whether or not to order compensation and to make such an order if it considers it appropriate to do so (Tay Wee Kiat v Public Prosecutor [2018] 5 SLR 438 1 (‘Tay Wee Kiat’). TA, KR and CV received an advance payment. No other payments were made to them. Considering the principles in Tay Wee Kiat, I found the imposition of a compensation order to be appropriate. The victims were from a poor financial background. They belong to a class of victims for whom it is impractical to commence a civil suit to recover unpaid salary. The amount of unpaid salary is based on the declaration by the accused in the work permit applications and is therefore easily ascertainable. Accordingly, I made an order in respect of the unpaid salary of TA and CV. The amount ordered is not a large sum as to be oppressive. No order was made in respect of KR as her advance payment exceeded the salary she should have received.
