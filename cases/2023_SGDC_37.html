District Judge Christopher Goh Eng Chiang:

Introduction

1       The accused, Alfonso Low Eng Choon (“the accused”), claimed trial to a single charge for abetting, by intentionally aiding one Tan Cheng Hock (“the co-accused”) to dishonestly receive a sum of SGD555,277.66, an offence under s 411(1) of the Penal Code (Cap 224) (“PC”) read with s 109 of the PC.

2       At the conclusion of the trial, I convicted the accused and sentenced him to a total of 25 months’ imprisonment. The co-accused was convicted for dishonestly receiving the same property and was sentenced to 40 months’ imprisonment.

3       The accused, being dissatisfied the conviction and the sentence imposed, has now appealed against both his conviction and sentence. The Prosecution, being dissatisfied with the sentence imposed, has appealed against the sentence imposed.

The Charges

4       The particulars of the charge are as follows:

DAC-924210-2019

“… are charged that you, on or about 29 October 2013, in Singapore, did abet by intentionally aiding one Tan Cheng Hock, a Director of Metal Concept Engineering Pte Ltd (“Metal Concept”) to dishonestly receive stolen property, namely, a sum of S$555,277.66 from one Fayez Saeed Mohamed Ibbini, having reason to believe the same to be stolen property, to wit, by communicating on behalf of the said Tan Cheng Hock with one “Lilian Alves” and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 411(1) of the Penal Code, Chapter 224 read with Section 109 of the Penal Code, Chapter 224.”

The Prosecution’s case

Background: how the monies were stolen

5       The victim is one Fayez Saeed Mohamed Ibbini (“Ibbini”), a Jordanian National living in the United Arab Emirates (“UAE”) and a client of NBAD Private Bank (Suisse) SA (“NBAD”), a bank based in Geneva.

6       In October 2013, an unknown person managed to convince NBAD that he was Ibbini. Pursuant to his instructions, on 21 October 2013, NBAD transferred USD200,000 to a Development Bank of Singapore (“DBS”) account belonging to one S Kabaleesan and on 23 October 2013, a second sum of USD450,000 was transferred to the Overseas Chinese Banking Corporation (“OCBC”) account belonging to one Metal Concept Engineering Pte Ltd (“MCE”).

7       On 7 November 2013, Ibbini informed NBAD that he had not given any authorisation for the transfer of any monies. On 15 November 2013, NBAD authorised Clifford Chance LLC to act on their behalf in Singapore, and, on the same day, Clifford Chance LLC filed a police report setting out what happened.[note: 1]

Receipt of the stolen monies by MCE

8       The accused was an employee of Aqua Mart Pte Limited (“Aqua Mart”). He was its project manager. The co-accused is the director of both MCE and Aqua Mart. The case against the accused in main, is based on the three statements[note: 2] that he had given to the Commercial Affairs Department (“CAD”) on 20 January 2014, 11 July 2014 (two statements), the various SMS messages and e-mail communications with one “Lilian Alves” (“Lilian”). The accused did not challenge the accuracy of the three statements.

9       Sometime in May 2013, the accused became acquainted with Lilian through Tagged.com, an online dating website. Lilian supposedly owned a gem and jewellery business. In an e-mail dated 20 July 2013 (timed 12:41 AM),[note: 3] Lilian tried to borrow money from the accused, ostensibly, for her gem business. The accused told PW3, Commercial Affairs Officer Lin Kian Siang (“CAO Lin”), in his statement that he felt that Lilian was trying to get money from him. Further, he did not trust her because she had not shown him any documents and in fact, he had some doubts as to whether Lilian actually owned a gem business. The accused did not provide Lilian with any loan.

10     Subsequently, Lilian told the accused that she had just closed her gem business and she needed his bank account number so that the client could transfer monies into his account.[note: 4] The accused stated that he found it strange that Lilian was running a business without a bank account.[note: 5] Nonetheless he provided his personal account particulars vide an e-mail to Lilian dated 31 July 2013 (timed 6:24 PM)[note: 6] because he wanted to help her out. However, Lilian told the accused that the account he provided could not be used to receive monies, but she didn’t explain why it could not. Therefore, no monies were transferred into this account.

11     In his statement, the accused conceded that he found it suspicious that, even though Lilian seemed in need of an account to transfer monies into urgently, she nevertheless failed to provide him with any details as to, for example, who would be transferring monies into the account. In fact, Lilian did not contact him for about one and a half months from mid-August 2013 until the end of September 2013. The accused further conceded that he thought that Lilian was trying to make use of his account for suspicious and illegal activities,[note: 7] though he did not know what these were. He claimed that he provided Lilian with the account number because he wanted to help her and not because Lilian had promised him a commission for helping her.

12     On or before 23 October 2013, Lilian asked the accused to provide her with a corporate account to receive her client’s monies. The explanation given by the accused as to why Lilian required a corporate account appeared to differ in his statements:

(a)     In Exhibit P12, the accused agreed that this request to provide her with a corporate account was essentially the same request as was made earlier, ie. to receive monies from the closure of her gem business, as earlier, as his personal account had been unable to receive the monies[note: 8];

(b)     In Exhibit P11, the accused stated that the reason was because Lilian had asked the accused whether his company was interested in a renovation project in Malaysia worth SGD500,000[note: 9] for a house that she had bought.[note: 10]

13     On 23 October 2013, Lilian reminded the accused to provide her with the details of the corporate account.[note: 11] As the accused did not have a corporate account, he referred Lilian to his boss, the co-accused. This was despite his suspicions as to the authenticity of Lilian’s claims.

14     On 23 October 2013 at about 4.00 PM[note: 12], the details of MCE’s OCBC account were sent, by the co-accused, to Lilian via Aqua Mart’s email address.

15     On 25 October 2013, Lilian told the accused via SMS to withdraw SGD300,000 from the money that was going to be deposited in MCE’s OCBC account. This was even before any sum had been transferred into MCE’s account.[note: 13]

16     By 28 October 2013, the funds had still not been transferred into MCE’s OCBC account and Lilian was asking about the monies. The reason for the delay was because OCBC required additional verification before the releasing the funds into MCE’s OCBC account. As such, the co-accused prepared a quotation for the construction project of a purported property in Malaysia. However, both the accused and co-accused were aware that there was no such project in Malaysia. The quotation was made with the sole purpose of deceiving OCBC Bank to release the monies. This quotation was sent to Lilian who signed off on it. Thereafter, a total of USD450,000 (equivalent to SGD555,277.66) was transferred into MCE’s OCBC account.[note: 14] It should be noted that this was less than the amount that Lilian had said that was going to be deposited into the account.

Removal of stolen monies from MCE’s account

17     Once the monies were received into MCE’s OCBC account, Lilian proceeded to ask the accused via SMS, on 30 October 2013, to withdraw as much money as he could.[note: 15] The monies were supposedly meant for her business. The accused was to pass the money to an associate of Lilian in Singapore. The accused informed the co-accused of these messages. The co-accused was not happy with this turn of events as he wanted the money to roll over his businesses. However, after a discussion with the accused, the co-accused decided to withdraw SGD150,000 instead. Both of them proceeded OCBC Bank in Marine Parade to withdraw the amount and subsequently, the co-accused handed over the monies to Lilian’s associate, one Mary, in Woodlands.

18     Thereafter, the co-accused refused to hand over any further monies to Lilian. This caused some ire on Lilian’s part, and this could be seen from the tone of her subsequent SMS and e-mail messages demanding to the co-accused demanding the return of the “rest” of her monies.[note: 16]

19     By this time, the co-accused had realised that the monies were tainted monies, however, he did not inform the authorities as to what had happened. Instead, the remaining money in MCE’s account was subsequently used by the co-accused to ease MCE and Aqua Mart’s cashflow problems. Eventually, the co-accused spent the remaining amount of money left in the account.[note: 17]

Submissions at the close of the Prosecution’s case

20     No submissions were made by either the accused or co-accused. I therefore called upon both accused persons to enter their defences to their respective charges and administered the standard allocution.

The Case for the Defence

21     The co-accused elected to give evidence. However, the accused, Low, elected to remain silent.

Analysis of the evidence

The charge

22     The co-accused, Tan, was charged for an offence under s 411(1) of the PC prior to its amendment by the Criminal Law Reform Act of 2019 and took effect in January 2020. The charge against the he co-accused is as follows:

“Whoever dishonestly receives or retains any stolen property, knowing or having reason to believe the property to be stolen property, shall be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to 5 years, or with fine, or with both.” (emphasis in bold, the limbs that Tan was charged with)

23     The accused, Low, was charged with abetting the co-accused, by intentionally aiding him in communicating with Lilian on behalf of the co-accused under s 107(c) of the PC.

24     Further, the definition of “stolen property” can be found in s 410 of the PC. It is defined as follows:

“(1) Property the possession whereof has been transferred by theft, or by extortion, or by robbery, and property which has been criminally misappropriated, or in respect of which criminal breach of trust or cheating has been committed, is designated as “stolen property”, whether the transfer has been made or the misappropriation or breach of trust or cheating has been committed within or without Singapore…..”

25     Having set out the charge above, the Prosecution would therefore have to prove that

(a)     the co-accused received the said monies;

(b)     the monies received by the co-accused were, objectively, stolen monies;

(c)     the accused had reason to believe that the monies were stolen property; and

(d)     the accused had abetted the co-accused, as particularised in the charge, in this case, that he intentionally aided the co-accused in communicating with the said Lilian on his behalf.

Whether the monies were received by the co-accused

26     There is no doubt that the monies were received into the MCE’s OCBC account on 29 October 2013. This can be seen in Exhibit P4 (the SWIFT fund transfer message) and Exhibit P22 (MCE’s OCBC bank statement, entry for 29 October 2013 showing the receipt of the monies).

27     The co-accused, being the sole director of MCE for the relevant period had exclusive control over the account.[note: 18] The co-accused was aware that the money had been credited to MCE’s OCBC account and had conveyed this fact to the accused.

Whether the monies received were, objectively, stolen property

28     From the PW1’s conditioned statement,[note: 19] it is clear that the monies were stolen property as defined in s 410 of the PC.

29     In a case of what appeared to be identity theft, NBAD had been deceived into believing that the real account holder had given the instructions to transfer the amount into MCE’s OCBC account, when this was in fact false. Operating under this deception, NBAD transferred the funds. Thereafter, OCBC released the monies into MCE’s account after the co-accused had provided documentary evidence that the monies were meant for a renovation project.

Whether the accused had reason to believe that the monies received were stolen property

The law

30     The term “reason to believe” was considered by the High Court in Koh Hak Boon and others v PP [1993] SGHC 198 (“Koh Hak Boon”). At [10], Yong Pung How CJ cited with approval the decision of Rattigan J in Gulbad Shah v The Empress (1888) PR No 37 of 1888 at p92 where that court held that it was not sufficient to show that the accused was careless, or that he had reason to suspect that the property was stolen, or that he did not make sufficient inquiry to ascertain whether it had been honestly acquired.

31     At [12] – [13], the High Court went on to hold that:

“12    …. “reason to believe” involves a lesser degree of conviction than certainty and a higher one than speculation. The matters which contribute towards a person having reason to believe something are not those which a court would require as proof of that thing, since “reason to believe” is not itself a standard proof, as opposed to that which must be discharged by the prosecution in persuading the court of the existence of such reason.

13    Whether or not a person had “reason to believe” certain property was stolen property for the purposes of ss 411 and 414 is a test to be applied by the court, but from the perspective of the accused person. It must relate to the standards of belief of a reasonable man and not to those of any particular accused person, since the Legislature could hardly have intended that, ceteris paribus, a conviction might depend on whether the accused reasons like a cretin or a genius. However, some element of subjectivity must be involved, because what might be apparent to a person with specialised knowledge of a certain field might not be apparent to a layman of even very high intelligence ….”

32     Subsequently, in Ow Yew Beng v PP [2003] 1 SLR(R) 536 (“Ow Yew Beng”) the High Court held that the test to be applied in determining whether an accused person had “reason to believe” in a s 411 PC offence was an objective one from the vantage point of someone with an offender’s knowledge and experience. This involved a lesser degree of conviction than certainty but a higher one than speculation. In Abdul Ghani bin Tahir v PP [2017] 4 SLR 1153 (“Abdul Ghani”), at [126] and [127], the High Court explained that mere carelessness, failure to inquire and ‘reason to suspect’ would not be considered a “reason to believe”. The presence of suspicious circumstances would also be insufficient.

The Defence’s submission

33     Whilst the Prosecution submitted that there were sufficient red flags for the accused reach the conclusion, the Defence submitted that he had no reason to believe that the monies were stolen monies. Two cases were cited to support the Defence’s proposition. These were PP v Soon Beng Kim [2015] SGDC[note: 20] (“Soon Kim Beng”) and PP v Hergobind s/o Arjandas Goklani [2014] SGDC 398 (“Hergobind”).

34     In Soon Beng Kim, the offender claimed trial to three charges, including one for dishonestly receiving stolen property under s 411 of the PC. The offender was the boss of a company named Oceanpac. Oceanpac’s business development manager, one Pantelis, had entered into an online romantic relationship with one “Regina”, with whom he had never met. “Regina” claimed that she did not have a bank account in Malaysia to receive monies that her company wanted to send her and that she wanted the money to set up an office in Singapore. She tried to persuade Pantelis to provide his bank account number to her. As one of Pantelis’s responsibilities was to get business for the offender’s company, he told the offender about “Regina’s” request and also about his online relationship with her.

35     The offender in turn agreed to help the “Regina” hoping that she would bring business to the company. A sum of USD200,000 was subsequently credited into the Oceanpac’s account of which SGD189,000 was handed to one “Waitie” on the same day. The company kept 20% for “taxation and company expenses”, whilst Pantelis was given SGD3,000. It should be noted that Pantelis had earlier pleaded guilty and had been sentenced to 14 months’ imprisonment for conspiring with the offender to receive the said amount. During the trial, Pantelis claimed, however, that he had pleaded guilty out of convenience as he had already been remanded for more than six months as he had been unable to raise bail.

36     The offender in Soon Beng Kim also elected to remain silent when his defence was called. He was acquitted of all three charges. The Defence highlighted that the district judge in Soon Beng Kim found, inter alia, at [60], that there was insufficient evidence to show the offender’s true motive in agreeing to receive the monies and at [82], that at worst, the offender was being careless. The district judge also accepted Pantelis’s evidence that he had pleaded guilty to his charges out of convenience and at [66] of his grounds, held that there was more than a reasonable doubt that Pantelis did not have reason to believe that the USD200,000 was stolen property.

37     In Hergobind, the offender claimed trial to six charges including two charges for dishonestly receiving stolen property under s 411 of the PC. The offender, who had martial problems at the material time, befriended one “Rose” online. He soon began an online romantic relationship with Rose. Soon, he agreed to provide her with his company’s bank account number to receive monies, ostensibly from “Rose’s” mother. The offender received monies into his company’s account and subsequently transferred them to other accounts provided by “Rose”.

38     Similar to Sim Beng Soon, the issue faced by the court in Hergobind was whether the offender believed that the sums of money he received were stolen properties. The district court found that the offender to be a credible witness. He had adequately explained why he had no reason to believe that the monies received were stolen properties, including the fact (at [79]), that he was smitten by “Rose”, and that she was open to having a relationship with him. The offender was ultimately acquitted of all charges.

39     The Defence submitted that facts in Soon Kim Beng were similar to that of the present case. The accused, like the offenders in both Soon Kim Beng and Hergobind, was at worst guilty of being careless in ignoring the red flags which might have raised his suspicions as to the legitimacy of the source of Lilian’s funds[note: 21].

40     I will first deal with Hergobind. I note that the prosecution eventually withdrew their appeal against the district judge’s order of acquittal. The main difference between the offender in Hergobind and the accused was that the offender had elected to give evidence. His version of events was thus tested under cross examination. He provided an explanation as to his relationship with “Rose” and why he had agreed to provide ‘Rose” with his account number. In particular, the district judge, at [91], found the offender to be a “candid and truthful witness”. He had, in the district judge’s view, provided an adequate explanation as to the “red flags” that were raised

41     Next, I would agree that the facts in Soon Kim Beng, are very similar to the facts in the present case. However, that was as far as I would go. As highlighted by the Prosecution, the Defence appeared to be unaware that the district judge’s order of acquittal was overturned by the High Court vide MA 9031/2015.[note: 22] The offender was sentenced to a global sentence of 17 months’ imprisonment with 16 months’ imprisonment being for the s 411 PC charge.

42     In Soon Kim Beng, Tay Yong Kwang J (as he then was)[note: 23] held that there were various aspects the case which ought to have put a reasonable man with honest intentions on high alert, not to mention that the offender was an experienced businessman. There were various red flags that should have put the offender on notice. This included the fact that there was “a dearth of information concerning her (ie “Regina”) and her alleged plans”, why the sender was unable to come to Singapore to open a bank account, the purpose of the funds transfer seemed to be changing frequently, the amount of money remitted increasing suddenly and substantially, the sudden urgency to withdraw the money once it was transferred, the manner in which it was transferred (ie in cash) and how the receiver of the monies was to be identified (ie with a number).

43     Tay J was also held“ … that the DJ was wrong to have attributed the respondent’s acts and attitude to mere carelessness. Further, the DJ ought to have drawn adverse inferences against the respondent for his refusal to testify on matters to which only he had the answers.” Tay J was of the view that the offender’s refusal to be cross-examined in these circumstances pointed clearly to a guilty mind, one that had ample reasons to believe that the money was “stolen” but chose to receive and deal with the money anyway obviously because of the very attractive reward.

The relationship between the accused and Lilian

44     I first point out some observations as to the nature of the accused’s relationship with Lilian. Counsel for the accused submitted that the messages suggested that the accused and Lilian were in some form of romantic relationship with each other.[note: 24] This was based on the WhatsApp and e-mail messages they had sent to each other.

45     However, as the accused had chosen to remain silent, the only evidence as to the nature of his relationship rests with the three statements he provided to the CAD which suggested otherwise. In his statements, the accused had, on numerous occasions, when questioned, replied that they were nothing more than friends[note: 25]. He maintained this position even when it was pointed out by the recorder as to how he had addressed her (and vice versa) when communicating with her.[note: 26]

46     The accused described Lilian as a friend whom he did not wholly trust. He explained why:

(a)     In the beginning, it was because she was unwilling to tell him about her background;[note: 27]

(b)     When Lilian tried to borrow money from him for her gem business, he felt that she was trying to get money from him and that he did not trust her as she had not provided him with any documentation;

47     Despite not trusting Lilian, he nonetheless continued communicating with her and eventually provided her something as important as his personal bank account particulars vide an e-mail to her dated 31 July 2013 (timed 6:24 PM)[note: 28] because, according to the accused, he simply wanted to help her. Even though the accused’s account was not used to receive any monies, the accused conceded that he thought that Lilian was trying to make use of his account for suspicious and illegal activities[note: 29]

The “Red Flags” raised

48     In addition to the not trusting Lilian, there also were many other red flags in the present case:

(a)      Why Lilian needed an account. Ostensibly, the reason why Lilian needed a bank account was because she needed an “international bank account” for her client to transfer money to.[note: 30] It extremely suspicious that Lilian was running a business without having a bank account. The accused acknowledged as much in his statement to CAD.[note: 31] Further, as per Tay J in Soon Kim Beng, there appeared to be no reason why the protagonist could not have come down to Singapore from Malaysia to open an account. Despite the suspicion, the accused nonetheless provided something as important as his personal bank account particulars vide an e-mail to Lilian dated 31 July 2013 (timed 6:24 PM)[note: 32] because he simply wanted to help her. I found this explanation difficult to accept;

(b)      Why Lilian subsequently needed a corporate account. On or before 23 October 2013, Lilian asked the accused to provide her with a corporate account to receive her client’s monies. At this point it should be noted that the reason(s) why she made this request is not clear. the accused gave different reasons in his statements (see [12], above), namely, this was either because his personal account, particulars of which he had already forwarded to Lilian earlier, had been unable to receive monies from Lilian’s client after the sale of her gem business, or for a construction / renovation project in Malaysia. Also, in his first statement, the reason given by the accused was that Lilian told him that the monies was meant as a “deposit” monies for the construction project.[note: 33] It seems clear that there were several reasons why Lilian claimed that she needed a corporate account, yet, the accused, despite not trusting her, did not see it fit to clarify or inquire further. Likewise, it seems absurd that Lilian was running a business without a corporate account;

(c)      The “project” in Malaysia. For a person that the accused claimed was simply a friend, with whom he did not totally trust, he nonetheless referred Lilian to his boss, ie. the co-accused, in order to provide a corporate bank account for monies to be transferred into. Stranger still, even if we were to take the accused’s explanation at face value, and these monies was meant to be a deposit for a construction project, it should be noted that the accused had referred her request to the co-accused even before the accused was aware of the nature of the project or the value. This is bearing in mind that the accused had then been a project manager for Aqua Mart for about three years and his job scope included looking for new projects and managing existing projects, and yet, there were no details of Lilian’s plans in Malaysia.[note: 34] The accused agreed that both he and the co-accused were aware that there was no project whatsoever in Malaysia[note: 35] and that the corporate account was to help Lilian receive monies in Singapore.

(d)      The amount of money remitted. The monies to be transferred was not determined. Lilian said that she was transferring USD500,000 but eventually transferred only USD450,000. No reason(s) were given. The accused conceded that this was suspicious.[note: 36] Furthermore, if we were to take at face value, that the money was for a construction / renovation project in Malaysia, no negotiations had occurred at this point as to the value of the project. In fact, the quotation for the project was not prepared by the co-accused until after 23 October 2013;

(e)      The need to withdraw the money. On 25 October 2013, Lilian asked the accused to withdraw SGD300,000.[note: 37] This was even before any money had been transferred into MCE’s OCBC account. Again, the accused conceded that his was suspicious by this request and suspected that “Lilian was using someone else’s money for her business”[note: 38]. It seems incredulous that the accused (and the co-accused) was not alive to the fact that something was amiss;

49     The accused had expressed suspicions in many aspects of what Lilian had done. Taken individually, these suspicions may not amount to the accused having “reason to believe” that the monies that Lilian intended to transfer into the corporate account were stolen monies. however, taken in totality, the facts suggested that any reasonable person, let alone an experienced project manager such as the accused, and considering the circumstances of his relationship with Lilian, would certainly have reason to more than speculate (per Koh Hak Boon at [12]) that the monies were stolen properties.

50     Hence, I adjudged that, due to the lack of any other evidence and explanation to the contrary, that the accused had reason to believe that the moneys that were being transferred by Lilian, were stolen property. I also find further that this belief existed even before the accused broached the subject of opening a corporate account with the co-accused.

Whether the accused had intentionally aided the co-accused

51     The accused was charged with abetting, by intentionally aiding the co-accused to commit the offence. Explanation 2 of s 107 of the PC defines “aid”. It states that “whoever, either prior to or at the time of the commission of an act, does anything in order to facilitate the commission of that act, and thereby facilitates the commission thereof, is said to aid the doing of that act.”

52     I agreed with the Prosecution that the act that the accused facilitated was the actus reus of the co-accused’s offence under s 411 of the PC. As highlighted by the Prosecution, the evidence showed that the accused:

(a)     was a “necessary precursor” to the receipt of the monies by the co-accused;

(b)     was aware that MCE and Aqua Mart were in financial difficulties and the co-accused needed money to roll the business;[note: 39]

(c)     played a major part in the subsequent communications between the co-accused and with Lilian after the co-accused had agreed to Lilian’s plan;

(d)     helped the co-accused obtain the release of the monies that was being withheld by OCBC by communicating with Lilian, on the co-accused’s behalf, OCBC’s request for documentary evidence as to the purpose of the monies.

53     I therefore find that the Prosecution has proven that the accused had abetted, by intentionally adding the co-accused to commit the offence by communicating with Lilian on behalf of the said co-accused, Tan.

54     I am further fortified in my conclusion when I considered the fact that the accused continued to assist the co-accused in the retention of the monies. For example, it was the accused who handed over the SGD150,000 to ‘Mary”. In addition, as earlier mentioned, independent of the co-accused, I am of the view that the accused had reason to believe that the monies were stolen monies even before the co-accused had entered the picture.

Whether an adverse inference should be drawn by the accused’s election to remain silent

55     The accused had elected to remain silent when his defence was called, the Prosecution submitted that the court should draw an adverse inference by his refusal to testify.

56     In PP v Kong Hoo (Pte) Ltd and another appeal [2017] SGHC 65 (“Kong Hoo”), See J, at [53], set out the following guidelines which he distilled from the authorities:

(a)     an adverse inference would properly be drawn where the facts clearly call for an explanation which the accused ought to be in a position to give;

(b)     An adverse inference may justifiably be drawn where the circumstantial evidence is such as to demand that the accused proffer some explanation, even if the objective evidence does not itself establish guilt;

(c)     In appropriate cases, the proper inference to be drawn is that of guilt itself. In other cases, an adverse inference is an “additional factor to consider in assessing whether the appellant’s guilt had been established beyond reasonable doubt” which can lend weight to an assessment of the accused’s culpability and, when considered cumulatively with the other evidence, be sufficient to establish guilt;

(d)     An adverse inference cannot be drawn solely for the purpose of bolstering a weak case; there must be basis for a drawing of an inference and it “cannot fill in any gaps in the prosecution’s case; it cannot be used as a makeweight;

(e)     An adverse inference cannot be drawn if it appears to the court that the accused’s “physical or mental condition makes it undesirable for him to be called on to give evidence”

57     This position was accepted by Aedit Abdullah J in PP v Mohd Zaini Bin Zainutdin and others [2020] SGHC 76 (“Mohd Zaini”) at [34].

58     The Defence argument was simply that no adverse inference should be drawn against the accused because “there was very little else that Low could have added by giving oral evidence since his statements are consistent with [the Defence’s] legal case. Low was fully entitled to elect not to suffer the ignominy of prolonged trial proceedings …..”.[note: 40]

59     I noted that in Kong Hoo, at [56], See J disagreed with a similar submission, namely that the offender in that case had elected to remain silent because there was “ …. “nothing material that [the offender] may add” or that there was “no utility for [the offender] to add to the combined strength of the evidence”….”. At [55], he held that this was so that:

“ ……. The evidence of the witness can then be tested and a comparison can be made between what is said on the stand and what was disclosed in statements he made in the course of investigations. Should there be unexplained discrepancies, the witness’s credit can be impeached, or submissions can then be made that adverse inferences be drawn (see ss 258 and 261 of the CPC).”

60     In the present case, I was of the view that the objective evidence available to the court was sufficient to establish the accused’s guilt. There are inconsistencies in the accused’s statements that only he, the accused, could explain. For example, why he provided his bank account number to Lilian and later referred her to the co-accused when he himself had said that he did not trust her, or why Lilian wanted to transfer the monies, or why he continued communicating with Lilian when he was suspicious of her motives. It appeared that the accused wanted the court to accept his statements without challenge or explanation and say no more about the matter.

61     For the above reasons, I adjudged that an adverse inference should be drawn as a consequence of the accused’s decision not to testify in court.

Decision the end of the trial

62     In summary, I found that the Prosecution had proven their case against the accused beyond a reasonable doubt.

63     On the evidence before me, I found that the Prosecution had proven that:

(a)     the monies were received by the co-accused;

(b)     the monies received were, objectively, stolen property;

(c)     the accused had reasonable cause to believe that the monies received were stolen property transferred. In addition, I found that this belief existed even before the accused communicated to the co-accused of Lilian’s request for a corporate account to be used to transfer monies; and

(d)     the accused had abetted the co-accused, by intentionally aiding him in committing the offence by communicating on behalf of the co-accused with Lilian.

64     In addition to the above, I also drew an adverse inference against the accused for refusing to give evidence at the trial. Accordingly, I adjudged that the Prosecution had proven their case against the accused and that the accused had failed to raise any reasonable doubt. I therefore found the accused guilty and convicted him of one charge under s 411(1) read with s 109 of the PC.

Submissions on Sentence

65     Having convicted the accused, I next dealt with the issue of sentence. The following is an expansion of my Oral Grounds (on Sentence) that were delivered on 22 September 2022.

Antecedents

66     The accused had no antecedents.

Prosecution’s submission on sentence

67     The Prosecution sought a sentence of 42 months’ imprisonment for the co-accused and 36 months’ imprisonment for the accused.

68     It was submitted that the primary sentencing consideration for offences under s 411 of the PC was deterrence.[note: 41] For the accused, the following aggravating factors were highlighted.

(a)      The seriousness of the predicate offence. The monies were transferred out of NBAD by means of a fraud. Offences involving banking and commercial fraud are undoubtedly serious offences. The accused aided the co-accused to use MCE’s OCBC bank account to receive the illicit proceeds of the fraud;

(b)      The amount of money involved. The amount of money involved was large. A sum of SGD555,277.66 was transferred into MCE’s account;

(c)      Loss to Victim. The Victim only received the equivalent of approximately SGD100,00 from NBAD;

(d)      Personal benefit. Although it was the co-accused who had retained, and eventually used almost all of the remainder of the monies in MCE’s OCBC account, the Prosecution nonetheless submitted that the accused did derive personal benefit, being three partial salary payments and a company loan amounting to a total of about SGD14,400. The court was urged to infer that the accused knew that the amount he received was derived in whole, or in part, from the monies he helped the co-accused receive from Lilian;

(e)      Mitigating factors. There were none.

Precedents cited by the Prosecution

69     The Prosecution cited three cases as precedents. These were PP v Osborne Yap [2019] 2 SLR 319 (“Osborne Yap”), PP v Ong Kim Chuan [2016] SGDC 58 (“Ong Kim Chuan”) and PP v Mohammed Haikal bin Abdullah [2020] SGDC 196 (“Haikal”). It was submitted that, based on a comparison of the facts disclosed in the cases above, the proposed sentence of 36 months’ imprisonment sought for the accused was not excessive.[note: 42] A summary of the cases are set out at [74] of these grounds.

Defence’s submission on sentence / plea in mitigation

70     Unsurprisingly, the Defence sought a much lower sentence. The Defence submitted that the appropriate sentence in the present case was 17 months’ and two weeks’ imprisonment. This took into account the period that the accused had already spent in remand.[note: 43]

71     The main reasons or this submission was that the accused did not derive any personal profit and that his role was limited. Further, the Defence submitted that the accused’s relationship with Lilian as a mitigating factor. The Defence also submitted that “…. the correspondences depict a deeply infatuated Alfonso, who was being taken advantage of by Lilian in a highly calculated and deliberate manner.”[note: 44] Further, the Defence highlighted the delay between the commission of the offence in October 2013 and when the accused was finally charge din court in July 2019 as a factor to be considered.

Precedents cited by the Defence

72     The Defence cited two cases as precedents to support their submissions. These were PP v Ambrose Dionysius [2018] SGDC 35 (“Ambrose”) and PP v Lim Chih Ming John [2018] SGDC 103 (“John Lim”). A summary of the cases are likewise set out at [74] of these grounds.

The starting point within this range

77     The value of stolen property received in the present case is SGD555,277.66. Hence, the indicative starting sentence would be in the range of 24 to 36 months’ imprisonment. I assessed that the indicative starting sentence should be at the upper end of this range, namely at about 32 to 33 months’ imprisonment.

The adjustments to the indicative starting sentence

78     I next considered the offence-specific aggravating and mitigating factors in order to make adjustments to the indicative starting point. I note that, as the accused (and the co-accused, for that matter) were convicted after trial, they were not entitled to a plead guilty discount.

79     I found that the accused was the less culpable of the two accused persons. In particular, I was unable to accept several factors that the Prosecution had listed as being aggravating factors. These included:

(a)      Role played by the accused. In my view, the accused played a limited role in the commission of the offence. While the accused was the one that had referred Lilian to the co-accused, and provided a conduit between the co-accused and Lilian, it was clear that the co-accused was thereafter the lead protagonist. For example, the co-accused:

(i)       had sole control over MCE’s OCBC account;[note: 45]

(ii)       created the “fake” invoice that was used to deceive OCBC into releasing the withheld funds into MCE’s account;

(iii)       determined that only SGD150,000 (and not SGD300,00) would be given to Lilian’s associate.

(b)      Personal benefit. The Prosecution had submitted[note: 46] that the accused had received a total of SGD14,400 in the form of a company loan (amounting to SGD10,000) and three partial salary payments.[note: 47] With regard to the salary payments, I accepted the Defence’s submission that the accused was entitled to be paid his salary and he should be treated the same as every other employee who were paid their salary from the tainted funds.[note: 48] I was also prepared to accept that, as an employee or the company, the accused was entitled to take a company loan. It was not free. The accused would be required to repay the loan to the company. If there was any personal benefit at all, this was an indirect benefit. The accused’s actions, in all likelihood, allowed the company to stay afloat for longer than it would have had it been not for the infusion of the monies. This in turn allowed the accused to remain employed for a longer period of time.

(c)      The accused’s relationship with Lilian. Earlier in these grounds, I had explained that, as the accused had chosen to remain silent, I determined his relationship based on the evidence before me ie. his statements, in particular, the answers therein. However, I am prepared to accept that what the accused had done, he did so as he wanted to gain favour or a potential romantic relationship from a female friend, notwithstanding one that he did not fully trust.

80     I was therefore of the view that a downward adjustment to the indicative starting sentence would be appropriate in the present case. I adjudged that this downward adjustment should be in the region of seven to eight months’ imprisonment, giving a final sentence of 25 months’ imprisonment.

Last look

81     I next proceeded to take a last look at the final sentence had I had reached in order to determine whether there were any further adjustments to be made to the final sentence. This would include looking once at the cases that I had reviewed.

82     I concluded that there were done. The final sentence I had reached is in line with the sentences imposed in cases with a more or less similar factual matrix and is proportionate to the amount of stolen monies received. This sentence also reflected the appropriate culpability of the accused vis-à-vis the co-accused. The co-accused, Tan Cheng Hock was sentenced to 40 months imprisonment, being more culpable, and had benefited the most personally (ie. about SGD400,000) from the monies received.

The Final Sentence Imposed

83     Accordingly, I imposed a sentence of 25 months’ imprisonment on the accused, Alfonso Low, for his role in this matter. I further ordered that this sentence was to commence with effect from 12 July 2019, the date the accused was first charged in court but excluding the period that the accused was out on bail, namely from 27 September 2019 until 2 September 2022, when he was sentenced.

84     The accused, being dissatisfied with his conviction and the sentence imposed, has now appealed against both his conviction and sentence. The Prosecution has appealed against the sentence imposed. The accused is presently on bail pending the outcome of this appeal.

85     The co-accused did not appeal against his conviction or sentence and is presently serving sentence.
