58     The applicable statutory provision is s 230(1)(j) of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 68, 2012 Rev Ed) (“CPC”). Section 231(1)(j) provides that the court must call on the accused to give his defence if it is of the view that “there is some evidence which is not inherently incredible and which satisfies each and every element of the charge as framed by the prosecutor or as altered or framed by the court”.

59     In applying the said provision, I referred to the case of Public Prosecutor v Wong Wee Kong [2016] 3 SLR 965 at [32] – [34].

33    As explained by Chan Sek Keong CJ in Re Nalpon Zero GeraldoMario [2012] 3 SLR 440 (“Re Nalpon”) at [26], this is a statutory codification of the test which was set out in the seminal decision of the Privy Council in Haw Tua Tau v PP [1981–1982] SLR(R) 133 (“Haw Tua Tau”). The question, Chan CJ explained, was not whether the evidence as it presently stood had already established the guilt of the accused beyond a reasonable doubt but whether the evidence – if it were all accepted as accurate – would do so (at [26]). At a minimum, the evidence put forward has to cover every constituent element of the offence in question; if it did not, then it would plainly be impossible for a conviction to be lawfully sustained. In deciding whether to call on the accused to enter his defence, regard should be had to the following guiding propositions:

(a)    All evidence of primary fact should be accepted as being true, unless it is so inherently incredible that no reasonable person would be able to accept it as being true or if it has been discredited or shown to be wholly unreliable. This may happen, for example, during the course of cross-examination (see Haw Tua Tau at [15]; Re Nalpon at [25]).

(b)    Inferences may be drawn, but only if they are reasonable – it is not enough that the inference may be credible or not inherently incredible. In this regard, there is a different standard which applies to primary facts and inferences insofar as the former should be accepted as true unless it is inherently incredible whereas inferences can only be accepted if they can reasonably be drawn (see Re Nalpon at [25]). It is not necessary that the inference be irresistible or that it must be only possible inference that may be drawn from the facts.

(c)    The totality of the evidence has to be considered when determining whether evidence is so inherently incredible that it can be accepted or if the inferences sought to be drawn are reasonable enough to pass muster. The court cannot only look to those parts of the evidence which are favourable to the prosecution’s case and ignore those which are detrimental: ie, it cannot pick out only the plums and leave the duff behind (see Public Prosecutor v IC Automation (S) Pte Ltd [1996] 2 SLR(R) 799 at [17]).

34    As the Privy Council explained in Haw Tua Tau at [16] and [17], calling for the defence only brought proceedings to the next stage. The evaluation of the evidence was a task that still fell to be performed at the close of the trial. Until then, a Judge must keep an “open mind” as to the accuracy of the evidence of any of the witnesses until both sides have had a chance to present their case and to advance such submissions as they might wish. It was entirely possible, Chan CJ later explained in Re Nalpon at [25] that a trial court which had earlier found that there was a case to answer could – even if no evidence had been called for the defendant – eventually decide that the charge had not been proved beyond a reasonable doubt and that the accused should be acquitted.

Assessment of the Evidence and Findings

Approach to assessing the evidence

103    There are numerous case authorities that have discussed the approach a trial court should take when assessing evidence emerging from a trial involving sexual offences. It is well-established that in a case where no other evidence is available, a victim’s testimony can constitute proof beyond reasonable doubt (see s 136 of the Evidence Act (Cap 97, 1997 Rev Ed) (“EA”)) – but only when it is so “unusually convincing” as to overcome any doubts that might arise from the lack of corroboration: AOF v Public Prosecutor [2012] 3 SLR 34 at [111] and followed in Public Prosecutor v GCK [2020] 1 SLR 486 (“GCK”) at [89].

104    Simply put, the uncorroborated testimony of a victim can sustain a conviction only if it is “unusually convincing”. The Court of Appeal (“the CA”) in GCK provided more insight to the “unusually convincing” standard:

90    Put simply, the “unusually convincing” standard entails that the witness’s testimony alone is sufficient to prove the Prosecution’s case beyond a reasonable doubt: see Teo Keng Pong v Public Prosecutor [1996] 2 SLR(R) 890 at [73]. The overwhelming consideration that triggers the application of the standard is the amount and availability of evidence: see also Kwan Peng Hong ([72] supra) at [29].

91    In a sense, the “unusually convincing” standard is not a “test” at all but rather, a heuristic tool. It is a cautionary reminder to the court of the high threshold that the Prosecution must meet in order to secure a conviction, and of the anxious scrutiny that is required because of the severe consequences that will follow from a conviction. That scrutiny is directed towards the sufficiency of a witness’s testimony, which is inextricably linked to the ultimate inquiry of whether the case against the accused person has been proved by the Prosecution beyond a reasonable doubt: see Mohammed Liton at [39].

105    In summarising key legal principles, the CA in GCK further held:

(e)    The principle of proof beyond a reasonable doubt can be conceptualised in two ways. First, a reasonable doubt may arise from within the case mounted by the Prosecution. As part of its own case, the Prosecution must adduce sufficient evidence to establish the accused person’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt on at least a prima facie basis. Failure to do so may lead to a finding that the Prosecution has failed to mount a case to answer, or to an acquittal. In those situations, the court must nevertheless particularise the specific weakness in the Prosecution’s own evidence that irrevocably lowers it below the threshold of proof beyond a reasonable doubt.

(f)    Once the court has identified the flaw internal to the Prosecution’s case, weaknesses in the Defence’s case cannot ordinarily shore up what is lacking in the Prosecution’s case to begin with because the Prosecution has simply not been able to discharge its overall legal burden.

(g)    The second way in which a reasonable doubt may arise is on an assessment of the totality of the evidence. The inquiry here is intimately connected with the “unusually convincing” standard, which arises in the context of mutually exclusive and competing testimonies. The “unusually convincing” standard sets the threshold for a witness’s testimony to be preferred over the evidence put forth by the accused person where it is a case of one person’s word against another’s.

(h)    The assessment of the Prosecution’s evidence under the “unusually convincing” standard must be made with regard to the totality of the evidence, which logically includes the case mounted by the Defence. The evaluative task is not just internal to the Prosecution’s case, but also comparative in nature. Where the evidential burden lies on the Defence and this has not been discharged, the court may find that the Prosecution has discharged its burden of proving its case against the accused person beyond a reasonable doubt. At this stage, regard may be had to weaknesses in the Defence’s case.

106    Bearing in mind the above principles, I turn now to my assessment and evaluation of the evidence.

The Defence has failed to raise a reasonable doubt in the Prosecution’s case

120    The incidents occurred in August 2019. A time gap of more than a year had passed before the First Victim testified on 25 January 2021 and the Second Victim testified on 29 September 2020. In Sandz Solution (Singapore) Pte Ltd and others v Strategic Worldwide Assets Ltd and others [2014] 3 SLR 562 at [56], the CA made the following observation:

… Conversely, a witness should not be found to be less credible merely because of gaps in his memory, particularly where a long period of time has passed since the occurrence of the events in question. We reiterate that ultimately, the trial judge has to consider the totality of the evidence in determining the veracity, reliability and credibility of a particular witness’s evidence. This includes contemporaneous objective documentary evidence.

121    In Tang Kin Seng v Public Prosecutor [1996] 3 SLR 444 at [39], the High Court held:

It emphasises that in a trial for sexual offences, what is important is that the evidence is weighed carefully, always bearing in mind the relevant aspects of human nature and behaviour.

122    Given the passage of time between an event and a witness’s testimony in court of law, it is unavoidable that there may be some discrepancies and inconsistencies in any witnesses’ testimony. Adequate allowance must be given for human fallibility in observation, retention and recollection: Tay Wee Kiat v Public Prosecutor [2018] 4 SLR 1315.

123    The Accused pointed to several inconsistencies in the victims’ accounts (see Exhibit E[note: 40] and Exhibit K[note: 41]). In my assessment, the Accused had merely cherry-picked the evidence and had failed to consider the totality of the evidence. I found that these inconsistencies were neither material nor fatal to the Prosecution’s case. I also found that the inconsistencies highlighted in the Accused’s submissions touched on peripheral issues and did not alter the substance of the victims’ evidence.

124    The Prosecution submitted that the victims had testified in a straightforward manner, without any embellishment. They provided a clear account of the sequence of events before and after each incident of outrage, the layout of the incident location and the manner of the touching by the Accused. They were able to provide logical and rational explanations for the apparent inconsistencies suggested by the Accused as well as giving clear and rational responses to show why the Accused’s allegations of falsehood on their part were unbelievable. I agreed with the Prosecution’s submission.

125    Having regard to all of the above, I found that the Accused had failed to raise a reasonable doubt within the prosecution’s case in respect of each of the four charges.

126    I also found the Accused to be an unreliable witness. I rejected his defence. My reasons are set out below.

127    First, the Accused alleged that the victims had a motive to frame him and to give false evidence against him. The Accused had failed to support his allegations with credible evidence other than his own suppositions and inferences.

128    Second, the Accused alleged that there were many factual inconsistencies within the victims’ evidence. For the Court to assess the evidence, it is important for the Accused to put his version to the victims, and then state them again during his evidence-in-chief. This would provide internal consistency of the Accused’s case. However, under cross-examination by the Prosecution, the Accused conceded that:

A    I get what you are saying. If the prosecution witnesses’ evidence was unchallenged by him, and that evidence is not illogical or inherently incredible, then the Court would have no grounds to reject the prosecution witnesses’ evidence. The Accused claimed that the victims were lying. At some point in time, he felt that it was pointless to continue to question them. The Accused has only himself to blame for his own tardiness and omission to put his version of the events to the prosecution witnesses.

130    Third, I found the Accused to be an evasive witness. There were numerous occasions during the trial where he did not answer the Prosecution’s questions. The Accused denied committing the offence as set out the charges. Hence, it was important for him to narrate his versions of the event. This was explained to him several times during the trial. Despite the explanation, the Accused maintained his defiance. At one stage of his cross-examination, the Accused had the audacity to say:

Q    So, even after it was specifically told to you, it’s not about the charge, it’s about what happened that night, you didn’t mention it.

A    I didn’t tell you about the weather that night.[note: 43]

131    Fourth, I found the Accused giving different accounts at different stages. The version he gave in Exhibit P16 differed materially from his oral testimony in court (see [73] to [79] above). The Prosecution thus sought to his impeach his credit.[note: 44] I found the Accused to be proficient with English, both in the spoken and written form. I therefore did not believe his testimony that PW8 did not record accurately the things he had told her in Exhibit P16. I also did not believe his testimony that PW8 had omitted to record some of the things he had told her in Exhibit P16. Exhibit P16 was read to him on completion and he was then given the opportunity to make amendments but he chose not to. This indicated that he had accepted the contents of the statement. I have studied the explanations he gave in court. I was not persuaded by his explanations. I found the credibility of the Accused impeached.

Conviction of the Accused

132    In the final analysis, I found that the Prosecution had proven the actus reus for the four charges. I also found that the Accused had the requisite mens rea, that is, knowing it to be likely that he would thereby outrage the victims’ modesty. All the essential elements of each of the four charges had been proved. I therefore found that the Prosecution had established beyond a reasonable doubt the four charges against the Accused. The Sentencing Approach

133    The “two-step sentencing bands” approach applies to sentencing an offence under s 354(1) of the PC: Kunasekaran s/o Kalimuthu Somasundara v Public Prosecutor [2018] 4 SLR 580 (“Kunasekaran”).

134    The first step is to consider offence-specific factors such as the degree of sexual exploitation, the circumstances of the offence and the harm caused to the victim. Based on the consideration of the foregoing offence-specific factors, the court should ascertain the gravity of the offence and then place the offence within any of the following three bands:

Bands

Description[note: 45]

Sentencing Range

Band 1

This includes cases that do not present any, or at most one, of the offence-specific factors, and typically involves cases that involve a fleeting touch or no skin-to-skin contact, and no intrusion into the victim’s private parts.

Less than five months’ imprisonment

Band 2

This includes cases where two or more of the offence-specific factors present themselves. The lower end of the band involves cases where the private parts of the victim are intruded, but there is no skin-to-skin contact. The higher end of the band involves cases where there is skin-to-skin contact with the victim’s private parts. It would also involve cases where there was the use of deception.

Five to 15 months’ imprisonment

Band 3

This includes cases where numerous offence-specific factors present themselves, especially factors such as the exploitation of a particularly vulnerable victim, a serious abuse of a position of trust, and/or the use of violence or force on the victim.

15 to 24 months’ imprisonment



135    The second step is to consider the aggravating and mitigating factors that relate to the offender generally but which are not offence-specific.

136    In their respective submissions on sentence, the Prosecution and the Defence had correctly cited Kunasekaran. However, they differed in their respective assessments of the sentencing factors involved. This led to different sentencing outcomes.

General and Specific Deterrence

138    In its arguments, the Prosecution submitted that general and specific deterrence are key sentencing considerations. General deterrence is applicable where there is a pressing need to educate and deter other like-minded members of the general public by making an example of the particular offender: Public Prosecutor v Law Aik Meng [2007] 2 SLR(R) 814 (“Law Aik Meng”) at [24]. General deterrence is applicable when the offender commits sexual exploitation of his victims in a setting where the victims are taken advantage of in the course of their work such as in the present case. Furthermore, the public disquiet about sexual offences and the difficulty of detection of the offender’s offences are key factors that calls for general deterrence: Law Aik Meng at [25(c)] and [25(d)].

139    Specific deterrence is applicable in the present case as the Accused was a brazen offender who committed the sexual offences against two victims in quick succession. The Accused was unremorseful for his transgressions. Specific deterrence dictates that unremorseful offenders are to be punished more severely: Thong Sing Hock v Public Prosecutor [2009] 3 SLR(R) 47 (“Thong Sing Hock”) at [59].

Offence-specific and Offender-specific factors

140    The Prosecution highlighted the following offence-specific factors:

(a)     The Accused used his penis to touch the victims in respect of the 1st to 3rd Charges. The touches were more than fleeting. In respect of these three charges, the victims reported that the touches lasted a few seconds each time. In respect of the 4th Charge, the Accused used his hand to touch the Second Victim’s buttocks. The contact was a forceful one. The Prosecution thus submitted that there was a moderate degree of sexual exploitation.

(b)     The victims were at the Accused’s place to look after his elderly and bedridden grandmother. The Accused had exploited the victims whilst they were performing their duties. The Accused had therefore abused the victims’ trust when he outraged their modesty. The Accused’s offending conduct forced the victims into making a difficult decision of either staying to see through their professional commitment to look after the Accused’s grandmother or to the leave the premises for their personal safety. The First Victim chose to remain. The Second Victim decided to leave immediately. The different decisions of the victims demonstrated the dilemma the Accused’s transgression had put them through. The Prosecution submitted that a deterrent sentence is necessary to send a message that such exploitative behaviour is intolerable. A deterrent sentence is also needed to protect private nurses like the victims from suffering such harm.

(c)     The victims were defenceless when the Accused perpetrated the offending acts against them in his home where there was no one to witness the offending or to help them. The victims had to fend for themselves.

(d)     The Accused grew more emboldened in his offending. For the First Victim, the Accused started with touching her arm in a circular motion before touching her thigh with his penis through his boxer shorts and then again with his penis through his triangular underwear. For the Second Victim, the Accused used his penis to touch the Second Victim’s buttocks on the first incident. The Accused then used his hand to touch the Second Victim’s buttocks during the second incident. On both occasions, there was a pattern of escalation.

141    Turning to the offender-specific factors, the Prosecution submitted that no offender-specific mitigating factors were present in this case. The Prosecution characterised the Accused as a brazen offender who had committed similar acts of outrage of modesty against two victims over s short span of three days. The Prosecution submitted that the Accused had displayed a lack of remorse by his conduct at the trial. The Prosecution informed that the Accused had made baseless allegations of professional incompetence against the victims, in order to substantiate his speculation that they had motives to frame him. An offender’s lack of remorse has been recognised as an aggravating factor: Thong Sing Hock at [56] to [59] and Wan Kim Hock v Public Prosecutor [2003] 1 SLR(R) 410 at [30].

142    Lastly, the Prosecution submitted that an aggregate sentence of seven to eight months’ imprisonment is not a crushing sentence: Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998 (“Mohamed Shouffee”) at [54] and [57]).

Court’s Decision on Sentence

Applying Kunasekaran

149    I agreed with both the Prosecution and Defence that the offending acts fell into Band 1 of Kunasekaran sentencing framework.

150    I agreed with the Prosecution’s submission that the sentencing principles of general and specific deterrence were applicable to this case. I also agreed with all but one of the Prosecution’s submissions on the offence-specific and offender-specific factors applicable to this. I disagreed with the Prosecution’s submission regarding the weight to be placed on the duration of the physical contact. The evidence, as pointed out by the Defence, showed that the duration of the contact was not more than five seconds on each occasion. In my assessment, the Prosecution has put excessive weight on this factor.

151    Turning to the Defence’s plea-in-mitigation, I found that the Accused was not a first-time offender given that he had committed three more offences after the 1st Charge. The fact that he had claimed trial was a neutral factor. A sentencing discount that is often given to an offender who has elected to plead guilty would not apply to the Accused. I also found that the Defence had placed excessive weight to the Accused’s personal and familial circumstances. It is well settled that, except in most exceptional circumstances, hardship to the offender’s family has very little, if any mitigating value: Lai Oei Mui Jenny v Public Prosecutor [1993] 2 SLR(R) 406. I did not find the Accused’s personal and family circumstances to be any different from other cases. This was plainly not an exceptional case.

152    In my judgment, the appropriate individual sentence for 1st, 2nd and 3rd Charges lies somewhere between the sentences proposed by the Prosecution and the Defence. It was my assessment that a sentence of 12 weeks’ imprisonment each for the 1st, 2nd and 3rd Charges would be adequate punishment on the facts of this case.

153    In my assessment, the 4th Charge is less egregious than the 1st, 2nd and 3rd Charges. This was also the respective positions of the Prosecution and Defence for they have each set a lower sentence in respect of the 4th Charge. In my judgment, a sentence of eight weeks’ imprisonment would be adequate punishment for the 4th Charge.

Global Sentence and Totality Principle

154    Having established the individual sentence for each charge, I then proceeded to consider the appropriate total sentence for this case. In this regard, the Accused’s overall criminality should be reflected in the global sentence. I was also mindful of the applicability of s 307(1) of the Criminal Procedure Code (2010 Rev Ed.)

155    It was clear to the Court that the sentence of imprisonment involving the two victims should run consecutively. First, these were separate and unrelated offences involving different victims. Second, the two offences were committed on different occasions. The general rule of consecutive sentencing therefore applied in this case: Public Prosecutor v Raveen Balakrishnan [2018] 5 SLR 799 at [98(b)] (“Raveen”).

156    The Prosecution and the Defence submitted for only two out of the four sentences of imprisonment to run consecutively. I agreed.

157    The Prosecution and the Defence have submitted for the sentences of imprisonment on the 1st and 4th Charges to run consecutively. With respect, I disagreed. I ordered the sentences of imprisonment for the 1st and 3rd Charges to run consecutively for two reasons.

158    First, the 1st and 3rd Charges were clearly distinct offences. The two offences were committed on different dates and against different victims. As such, it would be proper to run the individual sentences consecutively. This would also be in line with the one-transaction rule and the totality principle (see Mohamed Shouffee bin Adam v Public Prosecutor [2014] 2 SLR 998 at [25]).

159    I shall illustrate my second reason with the aid of a table:

(a)     The 3rd Charge and the 4th Charge involved the Second Victim

(b)     With regard to (B) above, the net effect would result in the Accused receiving a lesser punishment for the transgressions he has committed against the Second Victim. It is tantamount to an unwarranted discount on sentence. In my judgment, this cannot be correct.

(c)     With regard to (A) above, in my judgment, the aggregate sentence of 24 weeks’ imprisonment would not amount to a crushing sentence.

160    In the final analysis, I ordered the individual sentences of imprisonment for the 1st and 3rd Charges to run consecutively. The sentences of imprisonment for the 2nd and 4th Charges were ordered to run concurrently with the aforesaid two sentences. The aggregate sentence imposed was therefore 24 weeks’ imprisonment.

161    In my judgment, the aggregate sentence of 24 weeks’ imprisonment was on the whole proportionate to the number of offences, the gravity of the offences and the Accused’s culpability.
