Evidence – Expert evidence – Effect of alcohol intoxication on ability to form criminal intention

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in MA 9024/2020/01 and MA 9025/2020/01.]

9 January 2023

District Judge Kessler Soh:

Introduction

1       On 9 January 2020, Michael Marcus Liew (“Michael”) and Cheo Lye Choon (“Cheo”) and three other co-accused persons were convicted, after a trial, of a charge of rioting under s 147 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) (“Penal Code”).[note: 1] Michael also pleaded guilty to a charge of disorderly behaviour under s 20 of the Miscellaneous Offences (Public Order and Nuisance) Act (Cap 184, 1997 Rev Ed) (“MOA”). On 31 January 2020, Michael, Cheo and the three co-accused persons were sentenced to imprisonment terms ranging from 12 to 18 months. They each filed an appeal against their conviction on the rioting charge as well as the sentence. Grounds of Decision were issued on 29 April 2020 in PP v Michael Marcus Liew and 4 Ors [2020] SGDC 104 (the “GD”).

2       In the course of their appeal at the High Court, Michael and Cheo filed three criminal motions to adduce additional evidence:

(a)     CM 61 of 2021 – application by Michael to adduce a medical report dated 16 February 2021 issued by Dr Tan Sheng Neng (“Dr Tan”);

(b)     CM 62 of 2021 – application by Cheo to adduce a medical report dated 28 March 2021 issued by Dr Tan; and

(c)     CM 67 of 2021 – application by Michael to adduce an HSA report dated 15 May 2017 relating to his blood alcohol level.

3       On 11 August 2021, the High Court allowed the criminal motions with the condition that the prosecution be allowed to cross-examine Dr Tan on his medical reports and to adduce a medical report from the Institute of Mental Health (“IMH”) if required. The case was then remitted back to this court for the additional evidence to be taken.

4       At the remittal hearing before me, which was held from 3 to 5 August 2022, the medical reports and oral testimony of Dr Tan were considered, together with the expert reports and oral testimony of two forensic psychiatrists from the IMH: Dr Charles Mak Chia Meng and Dr Guo Song. Submissions from parties were subsequently heard on 10 October 2022.

5       Set out below are the key points of the additional evidence taken. The crux of Dr Tan’s opinion was that due to the severe alcohol intoxication of Michael and Cheo at the material time of the incident, they were unable to form the requisite criminal intention for an offence of rioting. This opinion was refuted by Dr Mak and Dr Guo. Having evaluated the additional evidence, I found the opinions expressed by Dr Mak and Dr Guo to be more cogent, and to be preferred over that of Dr Tan; and the additional evidence would have no effect on my earlier verdict in finding both Michael and Cheo guilty of the charge of rioting under s 147 of the Penal Code.

Additional evidence by the Defence

Expert opinion of Dr Tan Sheng Neng

6       Dr Tan (DW8) was the expert witness called by the defence to render an opinion on the effects of the alcohol intoxication of Michael and Cheo on their ability to from the requisite criminal intention for an offence of rioting. Dr Tan was a senior consultant psychiatrist at Winslow Clinic. He had a Master of Medicine (Psychiatry). He had professional experience dealing with alcohol-related cases. He obtained a Master of Clinical Investigation (MCI) in 2011, which qualified him as a research scientist and equipped him in performing translational studies and looking at clinical papers.[note: 2]

Michael incapable of forming requisite criminal intention

7       Dr Tan prepared a medical report for Michael, dated 16 February 2021 (“Michael’s Medical Report”)[note: 3]. Dr Tan’s opinion may be summed up as follows:[note: 4]

(a)     Based on Michael’s account of his symptoms, he had “altered perception of the environment, ataxia, impaired judgment, lack of coordination, behavioural changes and amnesia” at the material time of the offence.

(b)     It was “highly possible” that Michael’s blood alcohol level lay “near the range of BAC = 200mg/100ml”.

(c)     Michael suffered from Alcohol Intoxication, and was not capable of forming “any requisite criminal intention” at the material time.

8       Dr Tan elaborated on the report in his oral testimony.

(a)     Michael had exhibited “altered perception of the environment”, which was characterised by: (a) he remembering (despite the objective facts showing otherwise) that he had walked to his car alone, that he was walking unassisted, that there was a fat Malay lady cursing that she was in pain; (b) he perceiving that he was in the lock-up for two to three nights when that was not the case; (c) he being able to remember that a blood test was taken but was unaware of the blood test’s reasons.

(b)     Michael had exhibited “ataxia” (wobbly walking, inability to balance) and “lack of coordination”, characterised by his stumbling to his car and having to be physically supported by his friend.

(c)     Michael had exhibited “impaired judgement”, characterised by his refusing to take a taxi or a lift from a friend, and not being able to consent to a blood test until 10 hours later.

(d)     As for his “behavioural changes”, considering that Michael did not have any criminal charges or fights or anything like that before, he would not have done what he did if not for the alcohol consumed.

(e)     Michael had suffered alcohol-induced “amnesia”, as there were many pockets where he could not remember, and he did not have a clear recollection of what had occurred. [note: 5]

9       Dr Tan explained that he adopted a symptoms-based approach in back-estimating Michael’s blood alcohol concentration (BAC) level at the material time to be lying near 200 mg/100 ml.[note: 6] This symptoms-based approach was based on scientific literature.[note: 7]

10     In coming to his conclusion that Michael was not capable of forming any requisite criminal intention, Dr Tan said that he was looking at the mens rea of unlawful assembly under s 142 Penal Code (“being aware of facts which render any assembly an unlawful assembly”). Dr Tan elaborated that given Michael’s symptoms, his ability to perceive and receive information about his surroundings was very badly impaired at the material time due to alcohol intoxication. He was not even aware that he was being assisted; and his sense of sight, hearing and touch were impaired quite badly. Dr Tan was “very sure” that Michael was “not even aware within the first few metres of what’s around him”.[note: 8] It was “actually quite difficult or almost impossible” for Michael to be aware of what was going on at a distance about 8 metres away at that time [where a co-accused, Regina, assaulted a victim, Maureen]; and if Michael did not have “the relevant information”, then he could not form the judgement or “informed decision about the particular events”.[note: 9] The “possibility was pretty low” that Michael was aware that he was part of a wider assembly of at least five people, and of what the other people were doing at that time.[note: 10]

Cheo incapable of forming requisite criminal intention

11     Dr Tan similarly prepared a medical report for Cheo, dated 28 March 2021 (“Cheo’s Medical Report”).[note: 11] Dr Tan’s opinion may be summed up as follows:[note: 12]

(a)     Based on Cheo’s account of his symptoms, he had “altered perception of the environment, ataxia, lack of coordination, and amnesia” at the material time.

(b)     It was “highly possible” that Cheo’s blood alcohol level lay “well beyond the BAC range = 200mg/100ml”; his BAC was 123mg/100ml at 12.35 pm that day (about 10 hours after the incident).

(c)     Cheo suffered from Alcohol Intoxication, and was not capable of forming “any requisite criminal intention” at the material time.

12     Dr Tan elaborated on the report in his oral testimony.

(a)     Cheo had exhibited “altered perception of the environment”, characterised by: (a) he being unaware of the topics of discussion at the gathering with his friends; (b) he remembering that the police had asked him to follow them to the police station on foot, remembering making the trip with “Benny” and “Ah Chong” [Meng Chong, one of the co-accused] who were helping him, but being unsure if he was walking on the pavement or roadside, and unsure of how long the trip took.

(b)     Cheo had exhibited “ataxia” and “lack of coordination”, characterised by having to be supported by Benny and Meng Chong in order to manage the trip to the police station on foot.

(c)     Cheo had suffered alcohol-induced “amnesia”, as there were “a lot of memory lapses”. [note: 13]

13     As in Michael’s case, Dr Tan similarly adopted a symptoms-based approach in back-estimating Cheo’s BAC level at the material time to be lying well beyond 200 mg / 100 ml, based on scientific literature.[note: 14]

14     In coming to his conclusion that Cheo was not capable of forming any requisite criminal intention, Dr Tan likewise said that he was looking at the mens rea of unlawful assembly under s 142 Penal Code (“being aware of facts which render any assembly an unlawful assembly”). Dr Tan elaborated that Cheo was worse off than Michael. His awareness of the environment was “really, really much reduced”, and it would not be surprising if he was “not aware of people around him”.[note: 15] He did recall Benny and Meng Chong helping him; however, he was unsure whether he was walking on the pavement or on the road, which was about two metres away. His sensory functions at the material time were “bad”, and his awareness of the environment seemed to have stopped around the time he was drinking (at the restaurant).[note: 16] His spatial awareness was very limited. He would not have been aware of what other people were doing at a distance about 8 metres away, or that he was part of a wider assembly of at least five people. [note: 17]

Rebuttal evidence by the Prosecution

Expert opinion of Dr Charles Mak (for Michael)

15     The prosecution called Dr Charles Mak Chia Meng (PW13, “Dr Mak”), a Consultant Psychiatrist at the IMH, to give an expert opinion on Michael’s condition. Dr Mak assessed Michael on 7 January 2022 and prepared a medical report dated 6 February 2022 (“Michael’s IMH Report”).[note: 18] He had also written an earlier report dated 21 May 2021 providing his opinion on Dr Tan’s approach in back calculating blood alcohol concentration (“Michael’s BAC Report”).[note: 19]

16     Dr Mak’s assessment was that Michael was in a state of Acute Alcohol Intoxication at the material time. This may have contributed to his offences in the sense that his judgment and decision-making may have been impaired. But it did not amount to a direct causal link to his offences:

17.     He was in a state of Acute Alcohol Intoxication at the material time. In simple parlance, he was drunk on alcohol, which he willingly consumed. This may have contributed to his offences, in the sense that his judgement and decision-making may have been impaired.

18.     However, this does not amount to a direct causal link to his offences.

One of the victims was similar in appearance to his ex-business partner, whom he resented. The accused offered this as a possible explanation for his actions toward the group. He was also able to navigate his way back to his car, which he had earlier parked by himself. This information suggests that he had awareness of his surroundings at the material time.

There was also voluntary de-escalation of violence as per Paragraph 16 of the court’s decision which stated that “the defendants stopped attacking their victims’ upon being approached by two men ‘saying they were the police’”. This suggests that he had the presence of mind to know the difference between wrong and right.[note: 20]

(Emphasis added)

17     In his oral testimony, Dr Mak explained that in determining aspects of causal link or significant contributory link, there were three criteria: (a) awareness of actions; (b) control, or ability to control the actions; (c) an understanding that these actions were against the law.[note: 21]

(a)     In Michael’s case, there was evidence of awareness at the material time. Michael was able to navigate his way back to where his car was parked. This indicated an awareness of his surrounding environment and an ability to select and perform actions which were appropriate. As such, Michael’s awareness was not significantly impaired. Michael’s ability to draw a sketch plan[note: 22] which marked out where his car was parked, the buildings around him, the spot where he saw a commotion and the spot where he was arrested, also indicated the presence of awareness (although Michael probably did not have complete awareness of his surroundings and there was some impairment of his awareness).

(b)     In terms of control, there was an ability to voluntarily de-escalate the level of violence. With reference to the GD at [67], which set out the facts relating to the disorderly conduct offence which Michael pleaded guilty to, there were no violent acts that were continued after the police officers arrived and there was no violence enacted upon the police officers either, which indicated that there was control over actions.

(c)     Michael did not engage in violence or continue his violence after the police officers arrived, which indicated an understanding of the difference between wrong and right, and that he understood that it would be a matter of consequence and against the law.

18     Dr Mak accepted the contention of the defence that Michael had altered perception; and his senses of hearing, sight and touch had possibly been impaired. But the altered perception was “mild to moderate”, and not nearing the point of significance. There was still some semblance of recall by Michael, such as walking back to the car and seeing certain individuals, so there was some semblance to the actual facts that had occurred.[note: 23] Dr Mak accepted that given the amount of alcohol that Michael had consumed, there would be some degree of impairment of coordination; but it was not significantly impaired as he was able to stand and deliver an effective kick with force that was sufficient to topple a grown man over (GD at [12]).[note: 24] As was explained by Dr Mak in his earlier report:

Delivering a kick with such force would have required intact balance, coordination and gross motor control. This detail could provide insight into the degree of alcohol intoxication at the material time.[note: 25]

Thus, while Dr Mak acknowledged that there was some degree of impairment in Michael’s ability to control his actions due to the alcohol that he had consumed, it did not reach the “threshold of significance”.[note: 26]

19     Dr Mak did not agree with the conclusion of Dr Tan that Michael was unable to form requisite criminal intention. He explained:

An individual may have difficulty recalling events which have transpired, however, this does not necessarily equate to a lack of awareness and understanding of the difference between wrong and right at the material time.[note: 27]

Expert opinion of Dr Guo Song (for Cheo)

20     The prosecution called Dr Guo Song (PW14, “Dr Guo”), a Senior Consultant Psychiatrist at the IMH, to give an expert opinion on Cheo’s condition. Dr Guo interviewed Cheo on 2 and 23 December 2021 and prepared a medical report dated 31 January 2022 (“Cheo’s IMH Report”),[note: 28] with some reference material in support of his opinion.[note: 29]

21     Dr Guo agreed with Dr Tan that Cheo was in a state of Alcohol Intoxication. However, he disagreed with Dr Tan’s conclusion that Cheo was not capable of forming any requisite criminal intention. Dr Guo’s opinion was that although Cheo was in a state of “en-bloc amnesia” at the material time, he was still conscious of what he was doing, or he would have had clear intentions for his actions:

22.    The accused is likely to have been in a state of Alcohol Induced Amnesia (blackout) during the course of the alleged incident as he stated that he could not remember the exact sequence of events despite the fact that he was seen to be moving around consciously, joining the fight and even hitting the victims with his bare hand.

23.    Alcohol induced blackout is defined as a transient form of cognitive impairment where the memory consolidation (transformation from short term memory to long term memory) is affected by a high level of alcohol in the blood stream. A person with alcohol induced amnesia experiences a lapse of memory where they are unable to remember the events after the onset of the amnesia (anterograde amnesia) while sustaining other memories. The memory loss can be partial (segmental amnesia) or complete (en-bloc amnesia). Although a person in alcohol induced amnesia cannot remember what they have done, they will still be conscious of their behaviours, be able to understand their actions, be aware of the happenings around them, and be able to respond relevantly. The person may also be capable of carrying out more complex actions such as driving a vehicle. However, as the brain function is under the influence of alcohol, people experiencing amnesia may exhibit disinhibition and poor judgement in decision making which is commonly seen in alcohol intoxication and so they are likely to commit offences which they might otherwise not commit in a sober state (Reference 3).

24.    Based on the accused person’s subjective account, his behaviours observed by the witnesses and the objective BAC evidence confirming his alcohol intoxication, my opinion is that he was in a state of en-bloc amnesia at the time of the alleged incident. This means that he should have been still conscious of what he was doing or he would have had clear intentions for his actions, in other words, he was not of unsound mind at the time of alleged incident. However, his decision making might have been imprudent and so he could have behaved irresponsibly due to the dis-inhibitory effects of alcohol. Hence, I am not fully agreeable with Dr Tan’s conclusion.[note: 30]

[Emphasis in italics added]

22     Among Dr Guo’s references was an extract from Ethanol Forensic Toxicology, J Am Acad Psychiatry Law 45:429–38, 2017 (pp 435-6), which made the point that a person suffering an alcoholic “blackout” could still have the ability to form the specific intent needed to commit crimes:[note: 31]

It has been stated that “[an alcoholic blackout] is not forgetting, rather it is not remembering,” implying that, at a high BAC, new memories cannot be formed. The individual may or may not appear intoxicated, but will still be able to maintain a coherent and interactive conversation (short-term memory), recall past events (recall from long-term memory), and perform normal activities (procedural memory). This is a critically important problem from a legal standpoint, because, during a blackout, individuals with intact immediate and short-term memory have the ability to form the specific intent needed to commit crimes that require this ability, despite their inability to retain their involvement in the crime in their long-term memory.

(Emphasis added)

23     In his oral testimony, Dr Guo reiterated his opinion. He said that the theory of amnesia was well established; during amnesia, the person is conscious and would have the ability to form intention.[note: 32] Cheo was conscious, though his awareness about what was happening would have been affected to some extent by the alcohol.[note: 33] When asked by counsel if Cheo would have been aware of things happening 8 metres away, Dr Guo replied that Cheo “should be” aware as he was conscious; though it was also possible that his focus could be on the person (in front of him that he was beating) and he was neglecting what was happening 8 metres away. Dr Guo accepted that Cheo’s awareness could have been affected; but pointed out that, on the other hand, because of alcohol, some sensory functions could be “even sharper” than the time when the person was not under the influence of alcohol.[note: 34]

No effect on earlier verdict

24     Having taken the additional evidence, this court “must also state what effect (if any) the additional evidence taken has on its earlier verdict”: s 392(4) of the Criminal Procedure Code. In my judgment, the additional evidence would have no effect on my earlier verdict, which was that Michael and Cheo, together with three other co-accused persons, were guilty of an offence of rioting under s 147 of the Penal Code. My reasons are explained below.

Defence’s argument

25     In seeking to overturn the conviction of Michael and Cheo, the defence submitted that their state of intoxication had to be taken into account in determining whether they had the requisite criminal intention for the offence. Under s 86(2) of the Penal Code:

Intoxication shall be taken into account for the purpose of determining whether the person charged had formed any intention, specific or otherwise, in the absence of which he would not be guilty of the offence.

26     For an offence of rioting, the offender must be aware of the facts which render the assembly an unlawful one, and the offender intentionally joined or continued in that assembly, as provided in s 142 of the Penal Code:

Being a member of an unlawful assembly

142.  Whoever, being aware of facts which render any assembly an unlawful assembly, intentionally joins that assembly, or continues in it, is said to be a member of an unlawful assembly.

27     The object should be common to the persons who constitute the assembly, and the requisite mens rea is that they should be aware of it and concur in it: Lim Thiam Hor and another v PP [1996] 1 SLR(R) 758 at [13].

28     The court had found that the riot comprised five persons: Michael, Cheo, Meng Chong, Wan Seng and Regina. One of the victims (Maureen) testified that Regina had assaulted her about 8 metres away from where another victim (George) was being assaulted (by Michael, Cheo and the other two).

29     The crux of the defence’s argument was that both Michael and Cheo were intoxicated, and they were unaware of where Regina was or what she was doing, unaware of there being an assembly of at least five persons, and unaware of any object common to all five persons to even concur in any such common object. Accordingly, the defence submitted that the charges of rioting against Michael and Cheo (as well as the other three co-accused persons) had not been made out.[note: 35]

Dr Tan’s expert opinion not cogent

30     The defence’s argument rested on the expert opinion of Dr Tan, in particular his conclusions that Michael and Cheo, in their state of intoxication at the material time, were incapable of forming the requisite criminal intention. I did not find Dr Tan’s expert opinion to be cogent, for two main reasons:

(a)     First, Dr Tan relied almost entirely on the self-reported accounts of Michael and Cheo, and omitted to take into account other relevant objective evidence.

(b)     Second, it was unclear how Dr Tan arrived at his conclusion that Michael and Cheo were not able to form “any requisite criminal intention”, as such a conclusion appeared inconsistent with the objective evidence.

31     Further, in his oral testimony, Dr Tan came across as being partisan and lacking in objectivity.

Reliance on self-reported accounts

32     In Sakthivel Punithavathi v PP [2007] 2 SLR(R) 983, the High Court emphasised (at [76]) the need to evaluate the evidence relied on by an expert in assessing the expert’s opinion:

[…] Evidence must invariably be sifted, weighed and evaluated in the context of the factual matrix and in particular, the objective facts. An expert’s opinion “should not fly in the face of proven extrinsic facts relevant to the matter” per Yong Pung How CJ in Khoo James v Gunapathy d/o Muniandy [2002] 2 SLR(R) 414 at [65]. In reality, substantially the same rules apply to the evaluation of expert testimony as they would to other categories of witness testimony. Content credibility, evidence of partiality, coherence and a need to analyse the evidence in the context of established facts remain vital considerations […].

(Emphasis added)

33     More recently, in Teo Ghim Heng v PP [2022] 1 SLR 1240, the Court of Appeal highlighted (at [39]) the risks where an expert’s medical opinion was based in whole or in part on an accused person’s self-reported symptoms:

Where the expert’s medical opinion is based, whether in whole or in part, on an accused person’s self-reported symptoms, the application of the above principles requires the court to carefully assess the accused person’s self-reported symptoms in the light of “[a]dditional information from people who would ordinarily interact with the [accused person]”, where available: Ong Pang Siew at [43]. One reason for this, as Dr Phang explained during his examination-in-chief, is that it is not uncommon for accused persons to exaggerate or malinger symptoms in order to escape liability. Compounding this risk is the fact that the diagnostic criteria of many diseases and disorders are readily available through the Internet. Conversely, it is also possible that accused persons may downplay their symptoms or be in a state of denial: see Ong Pang Siew at [43]. Whatever the case may be, it is clear that an accused person may not always be the best source of information about his own physical and mental state.

(Emphasis added)

34     Dr Tan’s reports on Michael and Cheo were based on their self-reported accounts. He saw them in December 2020 and January 2021, which was more than 3½ years after the offence (which occurred on 1 May 2017).

35     Dr Tan did not appear to have compared their self-reported accounts with independent and objective evidence which could have shed more light on their condition. When asked during cross-examination about the materials he had reviewed in preparing the medical reports, his responses were unclear and seemingly incoherent at times.

(a)     In both reports, Dr Tan stated that in arriving at his opinion, he had read various documents. One of the documents listed in both reports was titled “Magistrate’s Appeal Number 9024/2020/01”.[note: 36] When asked by the prosecution what this document was, Dr Tan said he “guess” that it referred to “the black bundle” before him (which was the Record of Proceedings (ROP) of the trial which parties made reference to at the remittal hearing); but he was not sure because the document was “what’s given to [him] as a stack in PDF” which he “just screened through”.[note: 37] Dr Tan was given time over a lunch break to confirm what the document was. After the lunch break, Dr Tan continued to give a series of unclear responses, before eventually confirming that he was referring to the 1,462-page ROP. He added that, as there were a lot of pages, he “would skim through, try to pick out whatever that’s important that I feel that is relevant to, I guess, the case itself” and he tried his best to be “as comprehensive as possible”.[note: 38] However, Dr Tan’s medical reports of Michael and Cheo made no reference to any specific material in the ROP.

(b)     Specifically, Dr Tan was asked if he had reviewed the GD found in the ROP.[note: 39] His initial reply was that he “cannot remember [as this] is some time ago.”[note: 40] He later said that “if I had read it, what is important also is who is in front of me and the history I have taken.”[note: 41] When probed about the importance of the GD compared with the self-reported accounts of Michael and Cheo, Dr Tan did not give a straight answer and said the question was “very unfair”; he eventually made a vague, general statement that “every factor must be taken into consideration” without giving a direct answer to the question posed.[note: 42]

36     There were evident difficulties in Dr Tan’s reliance on the self-reported accounts of Michael and Cheo. When interviewing Michael, for example, Dr Tan said that he was “a bit frustrated”, because Michael “tends to offer more information than is needed”, which included “retrospective thoughts” about events leading to his offence (such as what others had told him about the incident), “and sometimes that confuses things”.[note: 43] As for Cheo, Dr Tan said that there were “really a lot of gaps in the memory” when he interviewed him.[note: 44]

37     Clearly, these difficulties could have been overcome if Dr Tan had considered other relevant sources of information. For example, Dr Tan stated in Michael’s report that Michael had issues remembering events from the point he departed from the bar until after he was arrested.[note: 45] However, in a statement recorded from Michael on the day of the incident, Michael had stated that while he was walking back to his car, he heard some cursing from a fat Malay lady, cursing that she was in pain. [note: 46] Dr Tan conceded that if he had more evidence about the details that Michael could remember, he “certainly would be able to prepare a better report”.[note: 47]

Conclusions not consistent with objective evidence

38     Apart from evaluating the evidence relied on by an expert, it is also necessary to examine the analytical process by which the expert’s conclusions are reached. In Teo Ghim Heng v PP [2022] 1 SLR 1240, the Court of Appeal explained at [38]:

Secondly, we agree with the Judge that, in assessing and weighing expert medical evidence in general, it is necessary for the court to examine the underlying evidence and the analytical process by which the experts’ conclusions are reached (see Judgment […] at [89] and Kanagaratnam Nicholas Jens v Public Prosecutor [2019] 5 SLR 887 at [2]). In undertaking this analysis, the court must consider “the cogency and the limits of the medical evidence complemented by, where appropriate, an understanding of human experience and common sense”: Ilechukwu Uchechukwu Chukwudi v Public Prosecutor [2021] 1 SLR 67 at [95].

39     In Dr Tan’s reports on Michael and Cheo, he stated their symptoms of Alcohol Intoxication based on their self-reported accounts, then derived from the symptoms an estimate of their possible blood alcohol levels at the material time, and concluded that they were both incapable of forming any requisite criminal intention. Dr Tan gave no explanation, however, as to how he came to such conclusion.

40     If Dr Tan’s conclusions were based on the various symptoms of Alcohol Intoxication exhibited by Michael and Cheo, then his conclusions were undermined by concessions that he made on a number of the symptoms. These concessions were made after he was referred to some of the objective evidence in the course of cross-examination by the prosecution.

(a)     For Michael, Dr Tan conceded that at the material time, Michael had situational awareness;[note: 48] intact balance, coordination and gross motor control when he kicked one of the victims;[note: 49] and awareness, control over his actions and rational thought as he voluntarily de-escalated the level of violence (after the arrival of the police officers).[note: 50] Dr Tan also accepted that amnesia due to Alcohol Intoxication did not necessarily mean that there was no awareness and understanding of what was happening.[note: 51] While he maintained that there should be “at least some lack in awareness […] resulting in an issue [with] forming an intent”, he accepted that Michael’s capacity to form a requisite criminal intent was “not a complete zero”.[note: 52]

(b)     For Cheo, Dr Tan conceded that the gaps in Cheo’s account could possibly be attributed to Cheo simply not remembering.[note: 53] When Dr Tan was referred to the GD where it was found that Cheo had rushed towards the commotion and assaulted the victim George together with three others (Wan Seng, Meng Chong and Michael), he accepted that Cheo “should be aware” that there were others with him attacking the victim, and it demonstrated that Cheo not only had a “certain level of awareness” but also a “certain level of intention” when he joined in the assault.[note: 54] Dr Tan then rephrased his opinion as, “I do not believe that [Cheo] was capable of forming the full requisite criminal intent” at the material time.[note: 55]

41     Thus, once the objective evidence was considered, there was simply no basis for Dr Tan’s sweeping conclusion in his medical reports that Michael and Cheo were not capable of forming “any requisite criminal intention” at the material time. Such a conclusion was at best speculative, and clearly inconsistent with the objective evidence which pointed to Michael and Cheo being aware and having intent for their actions despite their intoxication.

Partisan and lacking in objectivity

42     In the course of his oral testimony, Dr Tan came across as being somewhat partisan and lacking in objectivity. He willingly gave evidence favourable to the defence; but when confronted with unfavourable objective evidence that challenged his expert opinion, his responses tended to be hesitant and incoherent, and even evasive.

43     The following exchange during cross-examination is one example:

Q    [DPP Yang]: So, let me unpack that a bit. You are saying that even if you had read the grounds of decision, what is more important to you is the patient sitting in front of you and the history that the patient provides to you. Is that correct?

A    [Dr Tan]: You see, what---what is interesting, I guess, about this, is that this is appeal. So, I mean, I understand that there is information inside also, but my opinion is also something that is fresh. Okay, let me put it---in another sense is that my opinion is being sought, because is there a possibility of another consideration, if you understand what I’m saying?

Q:    I need you to answer my question first. Is---are you saying that what is more important to you is the testimony or the account given by the patient before you?

A:    Okay, that is very unfair question, because to me, I find that when you see a patient, there is information you gather. Yes, there’s information here also. But what I am also deeply aware is that there’s also opinion that’s---that people sought out or---he lawyer wrote to me and they seek out a diff---maybe is there another possibility, another opinion that might change or that might, you know---or should have been taken into consideration? And this is where my stand is. So---so, everything would be taken into consideration as much as possible. But I also want to give an idea that, you know, is there something else that’s not considered?[note: 56]

(Emphasis added)

44     Dr Tan appeared to view his role as expert witness as simply putting forth a fresh and alternative opinion based on what he was told by his patients. His partisan approach was illustrated in the following exchange during cross-examination:

Q:    But earlier, you told us that the reason why you have two sessions with Mr Liew is because you want to check out the consistency. So---

A:    Yeah, of course. I mean, I understand what you’re saying. Okay, the consistency, we have to make sure is we get a consistent answer from him. Okay. So, consistency is not to check and confront. Consistency is what he’s telling me at the point in time, there’s variation. Because if there is variation, it gives me this idea, eh, is lying to me, because he cannot remember. A lot of times after maybe a week or two weeks, if it happens in a conversation and all that, sometimes people won’t and they will come up with something new, especially if they have gone back and talk to someone, if you understand what I’m saying. So, what I am saying here is that I’m not here to double check, I’m not here to confront him on everything that he said before, okay. My job is to understand from him what he believe happened and to form a conclusion based on what he’s telling me. So, I think that’s important because obviously there’s a difference. And to me, this can’t be helped. I guess it will be in the---it will be up to the Court to decide what really is going on. But from there, I formed my opinion and from there, I decide what I think it should be and I will inform this to Court.[note: 57]

(Emphasis added)

45     Dr Tan appeared to have misconstrued his role as an expert witness. As was explained by the Court of Appeal in PP v Chia Kee Chen and another appeal [2018] 2 SLR 249 at [5]:

Experts are duty-bound to be neutral and independent, and to assist the court rather than to advocate for a cause in a partisan manner. This remains the case regardless of how sincerely the expert may sympathise with the cause of his client.

Expert opinions of Dr Mak and Dr Guo preferred

46     I found the expert opinions of Dr Mak and Dr Guo to be cogent, and to be preferred over that of Dr Tan. Both Dr Mak and Dr Guo possessed relevant specialist qualifications and ample experience in alcohol intoxication and forensic psychiatry; and their opinions were based on relevant objective evidence and a sound analytical process.

Relevant qualifications and experience

47     The credentials and relevant experience of an expert would have a bearing on the credibility and reliability of the expert evidence presented. In Sakthivel Punithavathi v PP [2007] 2 SLR(R) 983, the High Court explained at [75]:

[…] Generally speaking, the court should also scrutinise the credentials and relevant experience of the experts in their professed and acknowledged areas of expertise. Not all experts are of equal authority and/or reliability. In so far as medical evidence is concerned, an expert with greater relevant clinical experience may often prove to be more credible and reliable on “hands-on” issues although this is not an inevitable rule of thumb. Having said that, there is no precise pecking order or hierarchy relating to expert evidence. Experts may sometimes be abundantly eminent while lacking credibility in a particular matter.

48     Both Dr Mak and Dr Guo possessed more relevant qualifications and had considerably more experience than Dr Tan in the areas of alcohol intoxication and forensic psychiatry.

49     Dr Mak was employed as a Consultant at the National Addictions Management Service (NAMS) at the IMH. In addition to his qualifications which were a prerequisite for practice as a psychiatrist (MBChB, MRCPsych), he obtained a Masters in Addiction Studies (Distinction) from King’s College, London in 2017. He ran an addiction clinic every Monday and Friday, and around 30% of the patients seen at the NAMS were alcohol patients. He had completed two postings in forensic psychiatry during his traineeship, and he had been doing forensic reports ever since he became a registrar in 2012. He did forensic assessments on average around once every two months. He had testified in court numerous times, and he would attend court on several occasions each year.[note: 58]

50     Dr Guo was a Senior Consultant at the IMH. He had a Bachelor in Medicine from Beijing Medical College, China; and a Master Degree in Psychiatry from the Capital Medical University in Beijing, China. He also had a PhD in Psychopharmacology from Peking University, China in 2000. (Psychopharmacology is the study of substances, including alcohol, and how these substances affected a human being’s mood, thought and behaviours.) He had been dealing with forensic psychiatry for about 25 years. He had been with the IMH for 18 years, and had provided forensic medical reports to the courts in Singapore for more than 15 years.[note: 59]

51     Dr Tan, on the other hand, did not specialise in alcohol intoxication or forensic psychiatry during his postgraduate studies. His specialist qualification was a Master of Medicine (Psychiatry); and he had a Master of Clinical Investigation, which qualified him as a research scientist. He saw alcohol cases as a psychiatrist, and his most relevant experience was when he was previously with the Changi General Hospital as Director of Consultation Liaison, where he advised on the management of patients with alcohol intoxication.[note: 60] He had done a posting in forensic psychiatry some time ago, but had not received specialised training in forensic psychiatry; and addictions was not one of his subspecialties. He had testified once in court, “last year or the year before”; and his oral testimony had been from the clinical perspective (relating to the treatment of patients). Dr Tan said that while he tried his best to give his opinion on forensic matters, he accepted that he was “lesser than my forensic colleagues”.[note: 61]

Cogency in approach and opinions

52     Considering that Dr Mak and Dr Guo had more relevant qualifications and experience, it was reasonable that greater weight be accorded to their opinion. I found their opinions to be cogent, and more credible than Dr Tan’s.

53     Dr Mak and Dr Guo did not rely only on what was self-reported by Michael and Cheo. They also took into account objective facts that had been proven and established. In the case of Michael, as he was unable to recall details of events that occurred at the material time, Dr Mak had to rely heavily on other sources of evidence, such as the GD and the Statement of Facts (for Michael’s s 20 MOA offence) in providing his opinion.[note: 62] A similar approach was adopted by Dr Guo in respect of Cheo. As Cheo reported that he could not remember the exact details of the alleged incident, Dr Guo referred to the GD for more information about the incident.[note: 63]

54     Apart from relying on relevant sources of information, the analytical processes adopted by Dr Mak and Dr Guo were also cogent.

(a)     Dr Mak evaluated Michael’s ability to form a criminal intention based on the three criteria of awareness, control and understanding that his actions were against the law; Dr Mak referred to relevant evidence in support of each criterion, both in his report and in his oral testimony (above, at [16]-[17]).

(b)     Dr Guo’s opinion was that although Cheo was in a state of “en-bloc amnesia” at the material time, he was still conscious of what he was doing or he would have had clear intentions for his actions. This was supported by academic authority. In particular, during a “blackout”, individuals with intact immediate and short-term memory have the ability to form the specific intent needed to commit crimes despite their inability to retain their involvement in the crime in their long-term memory (above, at [21]-[22]). (This was a point with which Dr Tan also agreed as a general principle.[note: 64])

Intoxication offered no valid defence

55     Therefore, while it was common ground that both Michael and Cheo were in a state of Alcohol Intoxication at the material time, the expert opinion diverged on the question of whether they were able to form the specific intent for the offence of rioting. Dr Mak and Dr Guo disagreed with Dr Tan’s opinion that Michael and Cheo were unable to form the necessary criminal intention. As explained above, the opinions of Dr Mak and Dr Guo were more cogent and to be preferred; despite their intoxication, Michael and Cheo retained the ability to form the specific criminal intent for the offence of rioting.

56     In my judgement, from the evidence heard at the trial, Michael and Cheo did have the requisite mens rea. There was an unlawful assembly comprising Michael, Cheo and the three co-accused persons (Wan Seng, Meng Chong and Regina). They shared a common object to hurt the victims, and each one of them was an active participant in causing hurt to one or more of the victims.

57     In Lim Thiam Hor and another v PP [1996] 1 SLR(R) 758, the High Court explained (at [13] and [17]) that the common object of an assembly is an inference of fact to be deduced from the facts and circumstances of the case, and an unlawful object can develop suddenly, on the spot after the offenders gather at the scene of occurrence:

13    […] [A]ll that is required to constitute an unlawful assembly is that (a) the assembly must consist of five or more persons; and (b) the assembly should have as its common object one or more of the objects stated in s 141. […] It was not in dispute that the object here was to cause hurt to PW1 which fell within s 141(c) of the Penal Code. However, the assembly must be animated by a common object. This common object is an inference of fact to be deduced from the facts and circumstances of each case. Such an inference may be arrived at by considering the nature of the assembly, the weapons used by the offenders and the behaviour of the assembly at or before the scene of occurrence. But it is essential that the object should be common to the persons who constitute the assembly and they should be aware of it and concur in it.

[…]

17    […] [F]or there to be a common object, there is no need to even go so far to find a common intention. It is true that the two may overlap sometimes but in an offence involving unlawful assembly, the law requires only a common object. This means that there is no requirement of a prior meeting of the minds or preconcert from the offenders before the formation of the assembly. An unlawful object could develop suddenly, on the spot after the offenders gather at the scene of occurrence. Accordingly, an assembly lawful at its inception may, therefore, convert itself into an unlawful one by the actions of its members.

(Emphasis added)

58     At the remittal hearing, Michael and Cheo sought to pursue a new line of defence: because of their intoxication, their sensory perception was impaired, and they were not aware where Regina was or what she was doing, unaware of there being an assembly of at least five persons, and unaware of any object common to the assembly. At the trial, however, neither Michael nor Cheo gave any evidence in support of such an assertion, nor was such a point put to any of the witnesses. Their defence then was one of bare denial (GD at [34], [47]); and it was also asserted that it was the victims who first assaulted Michael, and the others including Cheo had tried to help Michael and got dragged into the fray (GD at [22]-[26]). I rejected these defences at the trial (GD at [62]-[63]).

59     The new line of defence was an afterthought. In any event, it was not supported by the objective evidence and accounts of the witnesses at the trial, and the expert opinions of Dr Mak and Dr Guo.

(a)     The testimony of the four victims was that the incident was initiated by Michael, who kicked the headlights of their car a few times, subsequently kicked the victim Daniel such that Daniel fell to the ground, and then subsequently assaulted the victim George together with the co-accused Wan Seng, Cheo and Meng Chong (GD at [11], [12], [14]).

(b)     Two of the co-accused persons (Meng Chong and Regina) said in their police statements that they saw Cheo walking towards or rushing towards the commotion (GD at [49]-[50]). This would indicate his awareness of what was happening some distance away. He was also seen by the victims to be beating George using his bare hands (GD at [14]).

These observations showed that, contrary to the defence’s claims, Michael’s and Cheo’s sensory perception and awareness of their surroundings had not been so severely impaired by their intoxication. As explained by Dr Mak and Dr Guo, they were not so intoxicated that they could not form the necessary criminal intent.

60     The assault on the victim George took place at carpark Lot 55. His wife Maureen saw that he was being surrounded and beaten up, and she ran towards him to stop the attack, but was intercepted and assaulted by Regina at Lot 57 or 58 (GD at [15]). It was a relatively short distance separating them – the width of two to three carpark lots, which Maureen estimated as about 8 metres. Both groups of assailants were within sight and earshot of each other. Their unlawful common object to cause hurt to the victims arose on the spot as events unfolded. Michael and Cheo were clearly aware of this common object and concurred in it, as they actively participated in physically assaulting George.

61     Accordingly, the additional evidence taken at the remittal hearing would have no effect on my earlier verdict that both Michael and Cheo were guilty of an offence of rioting under s 147 of the Penal Code.
