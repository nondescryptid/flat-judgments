Introduction

1       This is a landlord-tenant dispute.

2       The plaintiff is the sole owner and landlord (referred to where appropriate as “Landlord”) of the property known as 28 Jalan Keria Singapore 588549 (“the Property”). He is an airline pilot by occupation, and at the time of the trial, was employed by Turkish Airlines.[note: 1] His claim against the defendant in this action is for recovery of possession of the Property as well as for arrears in rental and double rent for holding over of the Property upon termination of the tenancy.

3       The defendant is a teacher. While he had represented himself in these proceedings, the defendant is not someone who is unlearned in the law as he holds an honours degree in law from the University of Nottingham[note: 2]. The defendant had rented the Property as a residence for his family comprising his wife and four children as well as their domestic helper[note: 3]. They have occupied the Property from October 2016 and continue to remain in possession of the Property even up to the date of the trial.

4       The defendant has resisted the plaintiff’s attempts to recover possession of the Property and has instead made a counterclaim for damages against the plaintiff for breaches of the tenancy agreement, fraudulent misrepresentation and conspiracy by unlawful means. The defendant had rented the Property through one Lim Leng Koi also known as Daniel Lim (“Lim”), a property agent with ERA Realty Network Pte Ltd (“ERA”) representing the plaintiff at all material times. Both Lim and ERA were named as the second and third defendants in the defendant’s counterclaim but they have not been properly brought into this action as required by the Rules of Court 2014. Hence, they did not appear, nor did they participate, in these proceedings.

Chronological background

October 2016 to September 2018

5       At the end of September 2016, the defendant had met Lim at the Property and negotiated the terms of the tenancy agreement which he eventually signed on 3 October 2016. The tenancy agreement bearing the letterhead of ERA stipulated that it was for a term of twenty-four months from 8 October 2016 to 7 October 2018 at the monthly rent of $3,400 payable in advance on the eighth day of each month. Payment was to be made direct into the bank account of the Landlord. At the time of signing of the tenancy agreement, the defendant paid the first month’s rental together with the security deposit of $6,800 being two months’ rental.

6       As requested by the defendant and incorporated into the signed tenancy agreement (“TA-1”) were inter alia:

(a)     the inclusion of a guaranteed option to renew the lease for a further period of one year with the renewal period commencing at the expiration of the initial lease term on 7 October 2018 at the same monthly rent of $3400[note: 4];

(b)     the deletion of the words “the Landlord shall be entitled to re-enter” the premises upon non-payment of rent or breach of any of the Tenant’s covenants[note: 5]; and

(c)     the reduction of the interest rate claimable by the Landlord on default in payment of rent from 10% per annum to 1.5% per annum[note: 6].

7       Although the defendant and his family moved into the Property on 3 October 2016, no further payment of rental was made until 1 August 2017. This was because the defendant was not given the plaintiff’s bank account number for the payments to be credited. According to the defendant, Lim did not answer his calls nor his email of 8 November 2016 in which he had requested for a hard copy of the signed tenancy agreement, the Landlord’s bank account number as well as various repairs to be carried out in the kitchen.

8       It was only on 31 July 2017 when Lim contacted the defendant by a WhatsApp message stating that the Landlord had not received his rental payments that the defendant was provided the bank account number[note: 7]. The defendant then made payment of all the outstanding rentals in two tranches as agreed with Lim. The first payment for the rentals from November 2016 to March 2017 totalling $17,000 was paid on 1 August 2017 into the plaintiff’s bank account. The second payment of $13,600 on 18 August 2017 for the rentals for April 2017 to July 2017 was made on 18 August 2017[note: 8].

9       On 15 August 2017, the defendant notified Lim that he would refuse to make further rental payments as Lim had not responded to his emails and requests for the kitchen repairs to be carried out. While there is no evidence as to whether all the requested repairs were carried out, the defendant did make sporadic payments of rental by internet bank transfers carried out on 7 February 2018, 8 March 2018, 11 April 2018, 10 June 2018, 9 August 2018 and 10 September 2018[note: 9].

10     On 2 May 2018, the defendant met the plaintiff for the first time when the plaintiff attended at the Property together with some workers to effect repairs to the kitchen[note: 10]. The defendant asked him for the physical copy of TA-1 but was told to get it from Lim.

The defendant’s defence and counterclaim

42     The defendant denies the plaintiff’s claim for payment of arrears of rent, double rent and denies that the plaintiff is entitled to an order for possession. He further denies that he is a tenant at will. In his defence and counterclaim spanning 129 paragraphs across 32 pages, he pleads that[note: 38]:

(a)     Sometime in the period from 28 September 2018 to 3 October 2018, Lim acting at all times as the plaintiff’s agent and upon the plaintiff’s instructions had negotiated, agreed and concluded a second tenancy agreement before the expiry of TA1 on 7 October 2018.

(b)     The salient terms of the second tenancy agreement (TA2) include rental at $3,300 per month, security deposit at $6,800, rental term of 24 months from 8 October 2018 to 7 October 2020, guaranteed option to renew for one year at $3,300 per month and a further option to renew for one additional year at $3,300 per month.

(c)     TA2 has been part performed by the payment and acceptance of a security deposit of $6,800 by the plaintiff and payment of monthly rent S$3,300 by the plaintiff. Further, the guaranteed option to renew (Clause 1) and the further option to renew (Clause 4g) had been exercised.

43     In his counterclaim, the defendant sought various categories of damages for fraudulent misrepresentation, conspiracy by unlawful means and breach of contract. The specific reliefs prayed for in his counterclaim included a declaration that TA2 is valid and binding, an order for specific performance of TA2 and payment by the plaintiff for various specific sums as well as an assessment of damages.

Fraudulent misrepresentation

44     The defendant alluded to the fact that the plaintiff was a Singapore permanent resident (“SPR”) who had received permission to purchase the Property for his own occupation and had been expressly prohibited by law from renting out the Property under the Residential Property Act 1976 (“RPA”). The plaintiff had ceased to be a SPR on 9 September 2013 and was required to dispose of his entire interest in the Property by 9 September 2015.

45     The defendant alleged that in respect of TA1, the plaintiff had knowingly, wilfully and fraudulently made the “following representations of fact in words oral and written, and by conduct” that[note: 39]:

(a)     he was at all material times a SPR;

(b)     he had obtained prior written consent for the lease of the Property;

(c)     he is the owner of the Property and is authorised to use the Property for the purpose of rental; and

(d)     the value of the rental contract, including the guaranteed option to renew, reflected the true market value of the Property.

46     The defendant pleaded further that the false statements of fact had been made knowingly to him with the intention that they would induce him to act upon them to agree to rent the said Property. The defendant had acted upon the false representations and agreed to conclude, sign and subsequently perform the contract.

47     The defendant claimed that as the plaintiff was explicitly prohibited by law, ie s 25(5) RPA, from renting out the Property, the true rental value of the Property was and remains at $0 since it could not be rented out. The plaintiff, by keeping the money from the unauthorised rental, had been unjustly enriched at the defendant’s expense. As the TA1 contract had been performed, the defendant claims the sum of $81,600 being the difference between the “true” rental market value of the Property and the contract value of TA1.

48     Because he had allegedly been defrauded into signing and performing TA1, the defendant further claims the sum of $326 which he had paid as stamp duty for TA1 on 9 January 2019 being a direct loss flowing from the misrepresentations.

49     The defendant further pleaded that he should be awarded exemplary damages on account of inter alia the plaintiff’s brazen misrepresentation and flagrant and calculated disregard of the law. The exemplary damages that the defendant claimed should be assessed with reference to the amount of money the plaintiff had received under all the unauthorised rental contracts prior to and including the defendant’s and with reference to the penalty for unauthorised rental under s 25B (1)(b) RPA.

50     Similar allegations of fraudulent misrepresentation were made with regard to TA2. As TA2 has been part-performed, and the plaintiff unjustly enriched by the unauthorised rental, the defendant claims damages which he calculated to be:

(a)     $79,200 being the value of the contract part-performed for the period from 8 October 2018 to 7 October 2020,

(b)     a further $3,300 per month after 7 October 2020 until the end of the guaranteed period of renewal calculated at $39,600, and

(c)     $3,300 for each subsequent monthly period after 7 October 2021 until the end of the period of further renewal and until the Property has been sold to an owner who has purchased the Property from the plaintiff subject to the tenancy as agreed between the plaintiff and the defendant. The sum for the entire period of further renewal is calculated at $3,300 x 12 and equals $39,600.

Unlawful means conspiracy

51     The defendant pleaded an alternative claim in conspiracy by unlawful means in which he alleged that the plaintiff, Lim and ERA (“Joint Tortfeasors”) had combined in the conspiracy in the following manner:

(a)     The plaintiff had provided the Property without authorisation to Lim and ERA who marketed the availability of the Property to the defendant, made false representations and solicited valuable consideration from him for the rental of the Property.

(b)     The Joint Tortfeasors committed an unlawful act by renting out premises which could not be lawfully rented out.

(c)     The Joint Tortfeasors had the intention to cause loss and damage to the defendant with the ultimate motive of enriching themselves. They had succeeded in unlawfully renting out the premises to the defendant and causing damage to the defendant.

52     He claims as damages from the Joint Tortfeasors jointly and severally the value of the contract part-performed being all the rentals he had paid under TA2 and the respective renewal periods after 7 October 2020.

Breach of contract

53     The defendant claims that the plaintiff had breached an implied warranty of the contract in TA1 when he refused to deliver the physical signed copy of TA1. This breach of contract had caused great anxiety and mental distress to the defendant as he had moved into the premises together with his entire family after 3 October 2016 and he worried over their security. He claimed to have suffered mental anguish and distress over the prospect that he and his family would be evicted by force and his security deposit stolen by the plaintiff.

54     On 9 January 2019, the defendant received a penalty notice from the Commissioner of Stamp Duties for unpaid stamp duty in respect of TA1. The stamp duty assessed was $326 and the penalty imposed was $326. Subsequently, the defendant received a further late stamping penalty notice from the Commissioner of Stamp Duties on or after 4 May 2019. The stamp duty assessed was $326 and the penalty imposed was $336.

55     In respect of the plaintiff’s breach of contract in unreasonably refusing to deliver the signed physical TA1 written instrument, the defendant claims as damages the late stamping penalty of $336 and $10 for each day of the 24-month period that the defendant allegedly suffered mental anguish and distress. The total sum claimed for the plaintiff’s “totally unreasonable behaviour” is pleaded at $7,300.

56     In relation to TA2, the defendant referred to the visit by the SLA officer on 30 October 2019 and his subsequent telephone conversation with her on 2 November 2019 as events that irrevocably breached his contractual right to peaceful enjoyment of the Property for which he had provided valuable consideration. He further pleaded that on or around 6 May 2018, the plaintiff had visited the Property and spoken to him. Amongst other things, the plaintiff had promised to deliver the signed TA2 written instrument to him by June 2019, to sell the Property subject to his tenancy and to instruct his newly appointed agent, Gafoor, on the same.

57     The plaintiff had on or around 7 May 2019 by email to the defendant directed that he vacates the Property. The plaintiff through his solicitors’ letter dated 28 June 2019 confirmed that he had sought to terminate the tenancy on 7 May 2019, and also threatened to exercise his right of re-entry and to evict the defendant from the Property. The defendant asserted that he had negotiated with the plaintiff to specifically exclude the plaintiff’s right of re-entry in TA1 and TA2. By threatening re-entry on 28 June 2019, the plaintiff had breached his contractual obligation which had caused mental anguish and distress.

58     The defendant tabulated his claim for damages including the exact dollar quantum for mental distress for the various breaches of contract allegedly committed by the plaintiff at paragraph 128 of his defence and counterclaim. In relation to the plaintiff’s breach of TA1, he seeks damages in the aggregate sum of $7,636 while the damages for breach of TA2 totalled $43,063.

Issues

59     From the parties’ cases as pleaded, the issues which the court has to decide are the following:

(a)     whether the defendant had failed to pay rent in the months of September 2017, October 2017, May 2018 and July 2018 and thereby breached clause 1 of TA1;

(b)     whether the defendant’s continued occupation of the Property after the expiry of TA1 on 7 October 2018 was pursuant to a new tenancy agreement, ie TA2, binding on both parties, or whether the defendant was only a tenant at will;

(c)     what is the effect of the plaintiff’s notice of termination dated 7 June 2019 and the notices to quit sent to the defendant dated 28 June 2019 and 15 July 2019;

(d)     whether the plaintiff is entitled to claim arrears of rental, interest and double rent, and if so, from which date and for how much;

(e)     whether the defendant has a cause of action in fraudulent misrepresentation against the plaintiff arising from the plaintiff’s unauthorised rental of the Property to him in breach of the RPA;

(f)     whether the defendant has a cause of action in conspiracy by unlawful means against the plaintiff arising from the plaintiff’s unauthorised rental of the Property to him in breach of the RPA; and

(g)     whether the plaintiff is in breach of his contractual obligations to the defendant pursuant to TA1 and TA2 (if TA2 is held valid and binding on both parties).

Whether the defendant had failed to pay rent for September and October 2017 and May and July 2018

60     The plaintiff pleaded specifically that in default of clause 1 of TA1, the defendant had failed to pay rent for the months of September and October 2017 and also May and July 2018. He has claimed these sums as arrears[note: 40].

61     The defendant denies the plaintiff’s claim. In respect of the rent for September 2017 and October 2017, he has pleaded in his defence and given evidence that payment was made by way of bank transfer into the plaintiff’s designated bank account as agreed in Clause 1 of TA1[note: 41].

62     While it is undisputed that the payments were made late, ie on 7 February 2018, they had nonetheless been accepted by the plaintiff without demur. It is also undisputed that it was the plaintiff who had delayed in giving the defendant his bank account number until ten months into the tenancy, and the plaintiff had accepted the defendant’s rental payments aggregated over several months. Notably, no action had been taken against the defendant during the currency of TA1, and the plaintiff had even instructed his agent, Lim, to proceed with the renewal of the tenancy to the defendant upon the expiry of TA1.

63     It is trite that he who asserts must prove. Since the defendant had given evidence of the details of the transfer made on 7 February 2018 into the plaintiff’s OCBC bank account, the evidentiary burden shifts to the plaintiff to prove that the defendant did not make the payment mentioned. The plaintiff could easily have done so by producing his OCBC bank account statement to show that the rental payments were not made but this was not done. As such, the plaintiff’s claim for payment of rental arrears for September 2017 and October 2017 fails.

64     As for the rentals for May and July 2018, the defendant has admitted to withholding payment of these sums. Based on the defendant’s admission of non-payment, there ought to be judgment against him for payment of these sums, subject to the defendant’s counterclaim succeeding which will be dealt with later in this judgment.

Whether the defendant’s continued occupation of the Property was pursuant to TA2, or as a tenant at will

The plaintiff’s case

65     The plaintiff’s position is that the defendant’s continued occupation of the Property after the expiry of TA1 was only as a tenant at will since he had not made a written request to extend the tenancy agreement two months before the expiry of TA1.

66     While there were negotiations between the plaintiff’s agent and the defendant, no new tenancy agreement was executed between parties[note: 42]. The following arguments were canvassed by counsel for the plaintiff, Mr Shafiq, in his written closing submissions[note: 43] :

(a)     He referred to Pure Group (Singapore) Pte Ltd v Siong Ann Engineering Pte Ltd [2021] SGDC 212 (“Pure Group”), a case which concerned the acceptance/execution of a contract through an agent. The court had found that no agreement was concluded on the basis that the agent did not possess any actual authority (express or implied) to enter into agreement on the plaintiff’s behalf and the plaintiff also did not represent that the agent had any such authority. Similarly, in the present case, there was a series of negotiations undertaken with the agent and the counterparty ie the defendant, was seeking to enforce the contract as concluded.

(b)     He then submits that putting aside all questions of whether the plaintiff’s agent had the authority to bind the plaintiff to the tenancy, the issue may be entirely disposed of by the application of s 6(d) Civil Law Act. This provision mandates that contracts for the disposition of an interest in land are unenforceable unless the agreement is “in writing and signed by the party to be charged therewith or some other person lawfully authorised by him.” On the present facts, the draft TA2 relied on by the defendant is unsigned and unstamped because the plaintiff’s agent had no actual, apparent or ostensible authority to bind or charge the plaintiff. Because there is indisputably no signed/executed TA2, the defendant is wrong to assert that the same was concluded and binding.

(c)     Further, the plaintiff’s conduct and correspondence with SLA and the defendant also indicates that there had never been any affirmation or acknowledgement of TA2 and instead supports the conclusion that it was not validly concluded or accepted.

The defendant’s case

67     The defendant is not relying on the guaranteed one-year renewal option in Clause 1 of TA1. As pleaded in his Defence and Counterclaim, his case is that a new tenancy agreement in the form of TA2 was negotiated and concluded with Lim, acting on behalf of the plaintiff and on the plaintiff’s instructions[note: 44].

68     In his closing written submissions[note: 45], the defendant has referred inter alia to the High Court decisions in SM Integrated Transware Pte Ltd v Schenker Singapore (Pte) Ltd [2005] 2 SLR(R) 651 (“SM Integrated”) and Midlink Development Pte Ltd v The Stansfield Group Pte Ltd [2004] 4 SLR(R) 258 (“Midlink Development”) both of which were disputes pertaining to lease agreements.

69     In SM Integrated, the High Court held (as succinctly stated in the headnote) that:

In order for there to be a concluded contract for a lease, the essential terms of that contract must have been agreed to unconditionally by both the prospective landlord and tenant. The essential terms were that the premises to be leased and the landlord and tenant had to be identified, the commencement and duration of the term had to be fixed, and the rent and other consideration to be paid (if any) had to be agreed. If any other terms were mentioned by one party, these also had to be unconditionally accepted by the other.

70     On the present factual matrix, all the essential terms of the second tenancy had been unconditionally accepted by both parties latest by 3 October 2018 in the form of WhatsApp messages between him and Lim (acting as the plaintiff’s agent). There was also unconditional acceptance of the other terms and conditions of the tenancy by both landlord and tenant as seen in the email correspondence between the defendant and Lim. The tenancy agreement attached to Lim’s email had included all the terms agreed between the parties including the guaranteed option to renew and the deletion of the tenant’s liability for ERA’s commission.

71     As for the requirements of s 6(d) Civil Law Act, the defendant submitted that the WhatsApp messages and email correspondence sufficed as the memorandum or note in writing electronically signed by Lim with his unique identification number as a registered CEA property agent authorised by the plaintiff.

72     The defendant further submitted that the court should take guidance from Midlink Development which was factually analogous. There, the defendant who was the tenant had refused to sign the new tenancy agreement after negotiations between parties had concluded and all essential terms had been accepted by both parties. The defendant paid the reduced rental which it had negotiated for in the new term of tenancy for several months before attempting to terminate the tenancy on the basis that there was only a tenancy at will in existence. The High Court held that agreement between parties had been reached and the conduct of the parties thereafter, including the payment and acceptance of the adjusted rent (as part performance) was consistent with the terms of the agreement. The Court further held (at [49] and [51]) that a “contract could be concluded on the terms of a draft agreement if the parties were perceived by their conduct to have acted on it”, even if a party remained “silent” by not signing the tenancy agreement provided that the factual matrix showed that “the silent inactivity... was tantamount to acceptance”.

73     The Court in Midlink Development had found the defendant’s omission to sign the tenancy agreements hardly accidental, and the defendant’s silence for some six months was a deliberately calculated attempt to exploit what they thought was a contractual ace – an unsigned lease. Similarly, it was submitted that the plaintiff in the present case had refused to sign or deliver TA2 as he knew that his rental of the Property was unauthorised and he simply wanted an excuse to disavow the tenancy when he was discovered by SLA. It was of crucial significance that the plaintiff had accepted as part performance the revised rent of $3,300 for four months without complaint and no objection was raised to the second term of tenancy until after the defendant had informed him of the SLA officer’s visit to the Property. Looking at the history of the negotiations and discussions, the inexorable conclusion is that TA2 had been accepted by parties, concluded and was therefore valid, binding and enforceable.

My decision

74     I accept the defendant’s submissions and find that an agreement for a second tenancy, TA2, had been reached between him and Lim who was acting at all material times as the plaintiff’s agent. The term of the tenancy which was for two years commenced on 8 October 2018 at the rental of $3,300 per month. The defendant would also be given a guaranteed option to renew the tenancy for another 12 months.

75     Mr Shafiq’s reliance on the district court’s decision in Pure Group is misplaced. That case is easily distinguishable on its facts which pertained to a contract for building works concluded by way of correspondence. The court dealt with the issue of whether a contract was formed when an email was sent by an individual (who was assisting in the project but not an employee) to the defendant accepting the latter’s quotation. The district court had found that there was no concluded agreement as the sender did not possess any actual authority to enter into the agreement on the plaintiff’s behalf. However, this finding was reversed on appeal to the High Court. See Pure Group (Singapore) Pte Ltd v Siong Ann Engineering Pte Ltd [2022] SGHC 73. The High Court had examined the correspondence and agreed with the defendant’s argument that the acceptance was supported by the preceding and subsequent exchange of emails and WhatsApp messages between the parties.

76     It is also not open to the plaintiff in the present case to assert that Lim as the plaintiff’s agent had no actual, apparent or ostensible authority to bind the plaintiff in the conclusion of TA2 given that the plaintiff had testified that he had given Lim permission to “manage” the Property. See excerpt below[note: 46]:

Q    Did you or did you not give him permission to act on your behalf in the negotiation and conclusion of TA2?

A    I gave him permission to manage the property.

Q    It’s a simple question, Mr Amirul. Did you or did you not give him permission to act on your behalf to negotiate to conclude TA2?

A    I gave him a general permission to manage the property.

77     While the plaintiff appeared reluctant to make any admission that TA2 had been concluded, he has not denied the WhatsApp exchange that he had with Lim which had been shared with the defendant. Of significance is the message that he sent on 3 October 2018 in response to the defendant’s request for a token reduction of rent by $200 instructing Lim that the “rent will be $3,300 for the next two years”, as reproduced below:

78     Clearly, Lim was acting on the plaintiff’s instructions in conveying the counterproposal of $3,300 rent which the defendant had accepted, and all that remained to be done was the formality of preparation and signing of the tenancy agreement. The preparation of TA2 was a follow-up action taken by Lim and what was required thereafter would be the signatures of both parties. Similar to the scenario in Midlink Development, as regards TA2, this is also a case[note: 47]:

where “execution of a lease by the tenant was no more than an incident of the performance of the agreement reached by the parties. … There was nothing more for the parties to do except to execute the lease”: per Lim Teong Qwee JC in Masa-Katsu Japanese Restaurant Pte Ltd v Amara Hotel Properties Pte Ltd [1998] 2 SLR(R) 662 at [11]–[12]. In other words, the sterile formality of a signature is not always necessary in law to breathe life into contractual undertakings. Incompleteness in form is not tantamount to legal inefficacy. Subject to any specific statutory requirements, the law almost invariably allows substance to take precedence over inadequacies in form.

79     Hence, it is my finding that as at 3 October 2018, the parties had agreed on TA2 – a tenancy for two years which would commence from 8 October 2018 to 7 October 2020 at the rent of $3,300 per month. As Rajah J had explained at [48] and [49] of Midlink Development,

48    Acceptance in a contractual setting must be ascertained objectively. Acceptance can be signified orally, in writing or by conduct. When there is a history of negotiations and discussions, the court will look at the whole continuum of facts in concluding whether a contract exists. The subjective intention of a party and silent mental conduct that may incorporate dissemblance is neither relevant nor legally admissible.

49    A contract may be concluded on the terms of even a draft agreement, if the parties are perceived by their conduct to have acted on it….

80     In another similarity to the Midlink Development, the plaintiff here also seeks to belatedly rely on the formality of s 6(d) Civil Law Act 1909 to deny the efficacy of TA2[note: 48]. The response to the plaintiff’s contention is also to be found in Rajah J’s judgment at [62] to [64] of Midlink Development. The plaintiff here has also failed to expressly plead non-compliance with s 6(d) Civil Law Act 1909 which thus precludes this court from even considering it.

81     As for the lack of stamping on TA2, I note that the tenancy agreements in Midlink Development were also not signed nor stamped. The lack of stamping did not preclude the High Court from recognising and enforcing them. The failure to comply with the formalities of signing and stamping does not undermine the enforceability of the agreement reached between the parties since “(e)quity looks on that as done which ought to be done.” (see Midlink Development at [56]).

82     Contrary to Mr Shafiq’s submission (at PCS [52]), the plaintiff’s subsequent conduct and his correspondence with SLA supports the conclusion that TA2 was validly concluded:

(a)     It was only after the defendant had informed him of the SLA officer’s visit to the Property that he raised issues with the validity of the second tenancy.

(b)     Yet in the plaintiff’s communication with SLA, he had informed them that the Property was being rented out at a monthly rent of $3,300 and not $3,400[note: 49]. Objectively considered, the plaintiff must have been referring to the terms of TA2 in which he had stipulated the rent at $3,300.

(c)     The plaintiff had also instructed his solicitors that the monthly rental after the expiry of TA1 was $3,300 and not $3,400[note: 50] which again necessarily refers to TA2.

(d)     The plaintiff had accepted several payments of rent at $3,300 per month.

83     The plaintiff’s attempt to portray his previous averment of rental owing at $3,300 per month as a mere mistake on his part was clearly a convenient afterthought[note: 51].

84     In conclusion, the defendant’s continued occupation of the Property after TA1 had expired on 7 October 2018 was not as a tenant at will but pursuant to an agreement for a lease on the terms of TA2.

What is the effect of the plaintiff’s notice of termination / notices to quit dated 7 June, 28 June and 15 July 2019?

The plaintiff’s case

85     The plaintiff’s solicitors had issued a total of three notices to the defendant dated 7 June 2019, 28 June 2019 and again on 15 July 2019. Each of these notices had referred to the defendant’ occupation of the Property as a tenancy at will as the plaintiff had refused to recognise the second tenancy as valid and subsisting.

86     According to the plaintiff’s pleaded case, owing to the defendant’s failure to pay rent, the notice of termination and eviction was served on 7 June 2019 to the Property and subsequently, notices to quit were sent to the defendant on 28 June 2019 and 15 July 2019.

87     The plaintiff claims that the tenancy was terminated on 7 June 2019, and the defendant’s holding over from that date onwards entitles him to double rent until possession of the Property is delivered up to him.

The defendant’s case

88     The defendant denies that a notice of termination and eviction was sent to him on 7 June 2019 although he did receive a “Notice of Termination of Tenancy” from the plaintiff’s then solicitors. He denies receiving a notice to quit from the plaintiff’s solicitors on 28 June 2019. He does, however, admit receiving a notice to quit by email on 15 July 2019.

89     The defendant denies that he was a tenant at will and that the tenancy was terminated on 7 June 2019. He also denies the plaintiff’s claim for double rent from that date.

90     The defendant asserts that the plaintiff is not entitled to rent by virtue of his repudiatory breach of contract as well as the fraud allegedly committed which forms the subject of his counterclaim.

My decision

91     The plaintiff’s pleaded position is that the tenancy was terminated on 7 June 2019 upon the service of his solicitor’s “Notice of Termination of Tenancy”. There was however a total of three notices to quit issued to the defendant. The language used in the notices appear contradictory to what the plaintiff had pleaded:

(a)     Presumably, the 7 June 2019 notice would have required the defendant to immediately surrender possession of the Property – the position is however unclear since the second page of the 7 June 2019 notice was not produced in evidence, whether in the plaintiff’s AEIC or in the core bundle of documents.

(b)     The subsequent notice of 28 June 2019 had stipulated to the defendant that he was to move out of the Property by 5 July 2019. He was also required to pay the arrears in rental in the sum of $30,100 comprising rental at $3,400 per month for four months prior to the expiry of TA1 and $16,500 being rental at $3,300 per month for five months after the expiry of TA1 ie December 2018, February 2019, April 2019, May 2019 and June 2019.

(c)     In a further notice to quit dated 15 July 2019, the plaintiff was notified that rent was outstanding for December 2018, February 2019, April 2019, May 2019, June 2019 and July 2019, and he had to vacate and deliver possession of the Property within 30 days, ie by 14 August 2019 failing which legal proceedings would follow.

92     Assuming that the last notice to quit dated 15 July 2019 had superseded the earlier two, the plaintiff’s position as stated therein is that the defendant was then still occupying the Property as a tenant at will for $3,300 per month on a monthly basis. Accordingly, the tenancy had not terminated as at 7 June 2019 contrary to the plaintiff’s pleaded case. The plaintiff’s case that the defendant was holding over the Property from 7 June 2019 and his claim for double rent from this date is not borne out by his own evidence.

93     Based on my earlier finding that upon the expiry of TA1, the parties had concluded an agreement for a second tenancy (TA2), this second tenancy being for a fixed term could only be terminated by effluxion of time or by the defendant’s repudiatory breach of its terms. While the notice to quit dated 28 June 2019 had alluded to the defendant’s breach of clause 4(a) by his failure to pay rent in a timely manner from December 2018, this is not the plaintiff’s pleaded case.

94     From the chronology of events set out, given the unusual circumstances that the defendant found himself to be in after the visit from the SLA officer to the Property within the same month that the second tenancy had been finalised, and being informed that the Property was not to be used for rental, the defendant’s refusal to pay rent from December 2018 without a proper explanation from the plaintiff was not entirely unreasonable nor surprising. By his email dated 5 November 2018[note: 52], the defendant had clearly stated his position on the second tenancy which was supported by the correspondence exchanged with the plaintiff’s agent, Lim. The plaintiff’s disavowal of the second tenancy was unjustified in the circumstances. The plaintiff had also taken a confusing position by the information he gave to SLA at end April 2019 that the Property was then rented out at a monthly rental of $3,300 and the defendant’s “tenancy term commenced in October 2018 and ends in October 2019”. The rental of $3,300 per month could only be referable to the agreed terms in TA2 which entitled the defendant to continue his occupation of the Property until 7 October 2020, and up to 7 October 2021, if he chose to exercise the guaranteed option to renew.

95     In my view, the three notices issued by the plaintiff did not suffice to terminate the defendant’s tenancy on 7 June 2019 as pleaded in his statement of claim.

Whether the plaintiff is entitled to claim arrears of rental, interest and double rent and if so, from which date and for how much

96     The plaintiff has tabulated his claims for arrears and double rent at paragraph 12 of his statement of claim. Based on my finding that upon the expiry of TA1, an agreement for TA2 had been concluded between the parties, subject to any counterclaim proven by the defendant, the plaintiff will be entitled to payment of rental in arrears for the entire term of TA2.

97     Pursuant to clause 1 of TA2 which provides that[note: 53]:

The Tenant shall have the guaranteed option to renew the lease for a further period of One (1) year with the renewal period commencing at the expiration of the initial lease term on 7th October 2020 at the monthly rent of S$3,300/= (inclusive of the Goods and Services Tax, if applicable).

the defendant had exercised his guaranteed option to renew on 4 August 2020[note: 54].

98     The defendant had on 3 August 2021 purportedly exercised a further option to renew for another year from 8 October 2021 to 7 October 2022 pursuant to clause 4g of TA2[note: 55] which provides that:

The Landlord shall on the written request of the Tenant made not less than TWO (2) months before the date of expiry of this tenancy, and if there shall not at the time of such request be any existing breach or any non-observance of any of the conditions, covenants or stipulations on the part of the Tenant herein contained, grant to the Tenant a tenancy of the said premises for a further term of ONE (1) year from the date of expiry of this tenancy at the same rent and containing the like conditions, covenants and stipulations as are herein contained with the exception of this option for renewal.

99     I do not agree that the defendant has a further option to renew after he had exercised his guaranteed one-year renewal upon the expiry of the term in TA2. Clause 4g, which was a conditional renewal for one year has to be read subject to the guaranteed renewal that the tenant had specifically negotiated and included in clause 1 of the agreement. TA2 was an agreement for a fixed-term of two years with a guaranteed option to the tenant to continue for one more year. Once exercised, the term of the tenancy would expire at the end of the third year. To construe otherwise would effectively mean that the tenancy could continue perpetually at the tenant’s option.

100    It was also the defendant’s position as seen in his email dated 5 November 2018 that he regarded the final date by which he could legally occupy the Property pursuant to TA2 as 7 October 2021 should he exercise his guaranteed option to renew. Accordingly, the defendant ought to have vacated the Property and delivered its possession to the plaintiff on or before 7 October 2021. Beyond that date, he would be regarded as holding over, and be liable for double rent according to s 28(4) Civil Law Act 1909 which provides:

Every tenant holding over after the determination of his tenancy shall be chargeable, at the option of his landlord, with double the amount of his rent until possession is given up by him or with double the value during the period of detention of the land or premises so detained, whether notice to that effect has been given or not.

101    In the premises, the defendant is liable to pay the plaintiff arrears in rental and double rent, less the security deposit of $6,800, as follows:

(a)     Arrears in rental for the months of May 2018 and July 2018 at $3,400 per month pursuant to TA1;

(b)     Interest on the arrears at 1.5% per annum calculated from the dates due to the date of actual payment pursuant to clause 4b of TA1;

(c)     Arrears in rental for the months of December 2018, February 2019, April 2019 to October 2021 (33 months) at $3,300 per month pursuant to clause 1 of TA2;

(d)     Interest on the arrears at 1.5% per annum calculated from the dates due to the date of actual payment pursuant to clause 4b of TA2; and

(e)     Double rent at $6,600 per month from 8 October 2021 until such time that possession of the Property is delivered to the plaintiff.

Whether the plaintiff’s claim is defeated by considerations of public policy on account of his contravention of the condition mentioned in s 25(7)(a) Residential Property Act 1976

102    The plaintiff’s cause of action in this suit is founded on TA1, a tenancy agreement for the Property, which had been entered into in contravention of the condition imposed on him, a foreign person, when he was granted approval by the Minister to purchase the Property under the RPA. The relevant condition in s 25(7)(a) RPA which was contravened is that the plaintiff “being a natural person, must use the residential property for his ... own occupation and that of his ... family as a dwelling house and not for any other purpose.”

103    At the close of the trial, I had requested parties to address the court on the issue of whether considerations of illegality and/or public policy could affect the enforceability of the plaintiff’s claim by virtue of the plaintiff’s breach of the condition imposed on his purchase of the Property. In this regard, Mr Shafiq, in his closing submissions, has helpfully referred the court to the specific regime under s 25B of the RPA which serves to regulate breaches committed by foreigners in relation to unauthorised rentals of properties[note: 56]. This section provides:

Penalty for contravening condition mentioned in section 25(7) (a)

25B.—(1)  Where an approved purchaser, by lease or agreement, grants to another the right to occupy any residential property (other than non-restricted residential property) in contravention of any condition mentioned in section 25(7)(a), the Controller may, by written notice, require the approved purchaser to pay, within a specified period, a financial penalty of such amount (as the Controller thinks fit) not exceeding the highest of one of the following amounts:

(a)    $10,000;

(b)    3 times the amount of rental payable and collected —

(i)    …

(ii)   … — during the period of contravention.

104    Suffice to say that I agree with Mr Shafiq’s analysis that on a plain reading of the relevant sections of the RPA together with the relevant background set out in the extract from Hansard, Parliament had decided to put in place a precise and separate statutory regime for dealing with cases of foreigners such as the plaintiff who are found to have rented out their property without approval. The imposition of a financial penalty of up to three times the rental income received was determined by the Legislature to be a more effective deterrent than the previous criminal sanctions.

105    The plaintiff has disclosed the financial penalties that he has had to bear thus far and may be further subjected to[note: 57] on account of the defendant’s extended tenancy.

106    Bearing in mind the relevant statutory framework in s 25B RPA, the court sees no impediment in allowing the plaintiff’s claim for arrears and double rent. As for delivery of possession, the plaintiff who holds the legal title to the Property is clearly entitled to such an order against the defendant.

Whether the defendant has a cause of action in fraudulent misrepresentation

107    This is one of the causes of action that the defendant has mounted in his counterclaim. In order to succeed in his claim for fraudulent misrepresentation, the defendant, as a claimant, must satisfy the essential elements of this cause of action as laid down by the Court of Appeal in Panatron Pte Ltd v Lee Cheow Lee [2001] 2 SLR(R) 435 (“Panatron”) at [14], namely the following:

(a)     a representation of fact made by words or conduct;

(b)     the representation must have been made with the intention that it should be acted upon by the claimant or by a class of persons which includes the claimant;

(c)     the claimant had acted upon the false representation;

(d)     the claimant suffered damage by acting upon the false representation; and

(e)     the representation must have been made with the knowledge that it was false, or at least made in the absence of any genuine belief that it was true.

108    In his counterclaim, the defendant has pleaded five specific statements as the alleged representations of fact made to him “in words oral and written, and by conduct” which he claims to be false and made with the intention to induce him to enter into the tenancy agreements TA1 and TA2:

(a)     The plaintiff was, at all material times, a Singapore Permanent Resident with his NRIC number as specified in TA1.

(b)     The plaintiff’s address was 28 Jalan Keria, Singapore 588549.

(c)     The plaintiff has obtained prior written consent for the lease of the said premises.

(d)     The plaintiff is the owner of the said premises and is authorised to use the premises for the purpose of rental.

(e)     The value of the rental contract, including the guaranteed option to renew, reflected the true market value of the said premises.

109    Bearing in mind that all communications prior to the signing of TA1 were done by Lim, for the statements set out in [108(a) and (b)], these were the Landlord’s particulars which cannot be said to be false statements. The plaintiff was a Singapore Permanent Resident with an NRIC number as shown in the Land Register maintained by SLA.

110    As to the statements set out in [108(c) and (d)], it would have been most unusual for an estate agent to specifically articulate that the owner of the Property being the Landlord has prior written consent to lease the Property and is authorised to use the Property for rental. Further, since the claim made here is in fraudulent misrepresentation, the law requires cogent evidence to establish such a serious allegation. The defendant’s self-serving assertions, in my view are not compelling enough to discharge his burden of proof.

111    Lastly, for the statement set out in [108 (e)] pertaining to the value of the rental contract, it is difficult to see how such a statement, even if it had been made by Lim to the defendant, would have induced him to enter into TA1. The defendant is a highly educated individual sufficiently well-versed with tenancy matters such that he could insist on deleting the Landlord’s express right of re-entry on breach of the tenant’s covenant to pay rent as well as to reduce the late payment interest rate to a mere 1.5% per annum. He had also extracted the plaintiff’s agreement (through Lim) for him and his family to move into the Property even before the stipulated commencement date of 8 October 2016. He was clearly satisfied with the rental of $3,400 per month that he had negotiated for and would have done his homework as to the rental market for similar properties in the vicinity before committing himself to a rental contract for two years with a one-year guaranteed extension at his option.

112    As elucidated in Panatron, an actionable misrepresentation requires there to be a false statement of an existing or past fact made by one party to the other party before or at the time of making the contract which had induced the other party to enter into the contract. The defendant has not explained in his AEIC or in his oral testimony how he had been induced to enter into TA1 by the alleged false statements.

113    A fatal flaw in the defendant’s claim in fraudulent misrepresentation is the fact that the defendant has suffered no loss or damage even if the alleged false statements had been made and they had induced him to enter into TA1. He has had exclusive possession of the Property since 3 October 2016 without making full rental payments. Other than the singular visit from the SLA officer on 30 October 2018, he has had quiet enjoyment of the Property with no disruption.

114    The extravagant claims that he has made against the plaintiff for the repayment, not just of the rentals that he had paid pursuant to TA1 and TA2, but for the entire value of the two rental contracts from October 2016 and some more, speaks of an opportunistic streak. As evident from his pleaded counterclaim, he is seeking damages for the alleged fraudulent misrepresentation in the total sum of $161,126 plus exemplary damages to be assessed[note: 58].

115    The defendant’s claim in misrepresentation is entirely without merit and has to be dismissed.

Whether the defendant has a cause of action in conspiracy by unlawful means

116    It is settled law that to prove conspiracy, a claimant must establish the following (see EFT Holdings Inc v Marinteknik Shipbuilders (S) Pte Ltd [2014] 1 SLR 860):

(a)     there was a combination of two or more persons to do certain acts;

(b)     the alleged conspirators had the intention to cause damage or injury to the claimant by those acts;

(c)     the acts were unlawful;

(d)     the acts were performed in furtherance of an agreement; and

(e)     the claimant suffered loss as a result of the conspiracy.

117    As the alleged co-conspirators, namely Lim and/or ERA, have not been properly included, this cause of action is non-starter since there is no evidence of a combination of two or more persons to do the acts complained of.

118    Similar to his misrepresentation claim, there is no evidence of how the defendant has suffered any loss as a result of the alleged conspiracy. The defendant’s excessive and extravagant claims for damages which he quantified at a total of $158,400 is entirely misconceived[note: 59].

119    Similar to his misrepresentation claim, the defendant’s claim in unlawful means conspiracy is without merit and has to be dismissed.

Whether the plaintiff is in breach of his contractual obligations to the defendant pursuant to TA1 and TA2

120    The breach of contract that the defendant claims is premised on an implied warranty that the plaintiff would deliver the signed TA1 so that he could have the document stamped. Since clause 4(m) of TA1 stipulated that the stamp duty on the original of the Agreement was to be borne by the Tenant, I accept that the plaintiff had an obligation to deliver the signed TA1 to the defendant for him to carry out the stamping. This is so notwithstanding the wording in clause 4(m) which requires the stamp duty to be paid on the date the Agreement was signed.

121    In the ordinary course of concluding a tenancy, one would have expected the estate agent to collect the stamp duty from the tenant, arrange for the landlord’s signature on the agreement, effect the stamping and returning a signed copy of the agreement to each party. The failure by the plaintiff and/or his agent, Lim, to complete these necessary steps had resulted in IRAS pursuing the defendant for the unpaid stamp duty and penalty for late stamping. The damage suffered by the defendant is the penalty that he had to bear. From the documentary evidence, the penalty imposed was $336[note: 60]. This sum should be repaid to the defendant.

122    The other claim made by the defendant for damages for mental distress due to the plaintiff’s failure to deliver the signed copy of TA1 is without any legal justification and has to be dismissed.

123    As for the plethora of other heads of damages touted in the defendant’s counterclaim – mental distress caused by the plaintiff’s failure to deliver the signed TA2, breach of covenant to give quiet enjoyment of the Property, wrongful termination of the TA2, wrongful threat to re-enter the Property, wrongful claim for interest, wrongful claim for indemnity, and wrongful claim for more than the contractual rent in TA2 – these claims have no legal basis and are in any event not proven. 124    In his closing submissions, the defendant had also sought to claim exemplary, aggravated and punitive damages against the plaintiff seemingly as punishment for his unauthorised rental of the Property and the conduct of these legal proceedings. These claims are misconceived. Punishment and deterrence are matters to be dealt with in a totally different forum as the defendant being a person with legal learning ought to appreciate. This court is concerned only with the enforcement of contractual obligations of the parties in accordance with their pleaded cases.

Summary of findings

125    In summary, my findings on the parties’ pleaded cases are as follows:

(a)     the plaintiff failed to prove his claim that the defendant failed to pay rental for September 2017 and October 2017;

(b)     the plaintiff has proven his claim that the defendant failed to pay rental for May 2018 and July 2018 based on the defendant’s admission;

(c)     the defendant’s continued occupation of the Property upon the expiry of TA1 on 7 October 2018 was not as a tenant at will but pursuant to an agreement for lease in accordance with the terms of TA2;

(d)     the three notices dated 7 June 2019, 28 June 2019 and 15 July 2019 issued by the plaintiff did not suffice to terminate the defendant’s tenancy on 7 June 2019;

(e)     the defendant’s right to occupy the Property pursuant to the terms of TA2 expired on 7 October 2021 after he had exercised his guaranteed option to renew for a further year from 8 October 2020;

(f)     the defendant is liable to pay the plaintiff arrears in rental together with interest less the security deposit of $6,800, for the period of his occupation of the Property up to 7 October 2021 pursuant to the terms of TA1 and TA2;

(g)     the defendant is liable to pay the plaintiff double rent from 8 October 2021 upon the expiry of TA2 until vacant possession of the Property is delivered to the plaintiff;

(h)     there was no fraudulent misrepresentation committed by the plaintiff and/or his agent, Lim, to the defendant to induce him to enter into the tenancies (TA1 and TA2) for the Property;

(i)     there is no basis for the defendant’s claim in unlawful means conspiracy; and

(j)     the plaintiff breached his contractual obligation to the defendant pursuant to TA1 and TA2 in failing to deliver a signed copy of the tenancy agreements to him causing the defendant to suffer a loss of $336 being the penalty for late payment of stamp duty.

Conclusion

126    In the result, there will be judgment on the plaintiff’s claim as follows:

(a)     The defendant shall deliver up vacant possession of the Property to the plaintiff forthwith;

(b)     The defendant shall pay the plaintiff arrears in rental in the sum of $6,800 for May 2018 and July 2018 under TA1, and $108,900 being arrears in rental under TA2 (being 33 months in total as set out in [101]) less the security deposit of $6,800;

(c)     The defendant shall pay the plaintiff interest on the arrears at 1.5% per annum calculated from the dates on which the rentals were due up to the date of payment; and

(d)     The defendant shall pay the plaintiff double rent for holding over of the Property at the rate of $6,600 per month from 8 October 2021 until possession is delivered to the plaintiff.

127    Save for the declaration that TA2 is valid and binding which is granted, and damages in the sum of $336 being the penalty imposed for the late stamping of TA1, the rest of the defendant’s counterclaim is dismissed. The sum of $336 is to be set off against the sums payable to the plaintiff.

128    The court will deal with the issue of costs on a date to be fixed. The parties have liberty to apply.
