District Judge Clement Seah Chi-Ling:

Introduction

1       This suit was brought by the Plaintiff in negligence and for breaches of the Workplace Health and Safety Act (“WSHA”) against the Defendant, claiming damages for personal injuries suffered by him in an industrial accident that occurred on 7 January 2020 at about 10.00 am at Temasek Polytechnic, Singapore.

Undisputed Facts

2       At the material time, the Plaintiff was employed by the Defendant as a Construction Worker under a work permit issued by the Controller of Work Permits.

3       The Defendant was an exempt private company limited by shares carrying on the business of “general contractors”, with “building construction including major upgrading works” as its primary business activity.

4       At the material time, the Defendant was the main contractor at Block 9, 21 Tampines Avenue, Temasek Polytechnic, Singapore 529757 (the “Worksite”), and was carrying out repair and redecoration works at various levels of the Worksite. The Defendant was also the occupier having control of the Worksite and the work carried out therein.

5       A scaffolding structure was built around the external façade of the building at the Worksite so that the Plaintiff and his co-workers could access the necessary parts of the exterior of the building. There were access openings on each level of the scaffold structure, with a monkey ladder installed at each opening, to provide a point of access and egress between the different levels of the structure.

6       On 7 January 2020, the Plaintiff was carrying out work at the Worksite when the Plaintiff sustained a fall, as a result of which, he suffered the following injuries (the “Accident”):

(a)     Comminuted intra-articular fracture of the left distal humerus with intra-articular fracture and traumatic ulnar neuritis; and

(b)     Surgical Scar – Left elbow revealed a 16cm x 0.5cm posterior longitudinal scar along the med-proximal forearm, elbow and the distal arm.

7       How the accident happened, and the manner in which the Plaintiff sustained the fall, were severely disputed between the parties.

The Disputed Facts

The Plaintiff’s case

Plaintiff’s testimony

8       The Plaintiff’s case was that on 7 January 2020, at about 10.00 am, he was instructed by his supervisor, one “Periyasamy”, an employee, agent or servant of the Defendant, to drill rain water drainage holes along the external façade on levels 5 and 6 of the building. The Plaintiff was instructed by his supervisor to mark spots along the said façade, approximately 1.2 metres apart, before beginning to drill the holes at the said spots. As he was walking along the curved scaffold structure on level 6 and marking the spots, he fell through an opening on the floor which was not covered or cordoned off. He landed on the platform of level 5 with great impact to his left side and back, and his left hand struck the scaffolding pipe.

9       The Plaintiff claimed that he was strictly instructed to move sideways along the structure to accurately measure the distance between the spots, making it all the more difficult for him to notice the said opening[note: 1].

10     The Plaintiff asserted that during the initial days of his employment with the Defendant, the Defendant used to install metal pipes on either side of the openings on each floor, built horizontally up to the waist level, so as to prevent the workers from falling over whilst working. The Defendant, however, gradually stopped installing such metal barricades/pipes. On the material date, there were no such metal pipes/barricades on either side of the opening from which he fell[note: 2].

11     The Plaintiff further stated that at a safety briefing conducted on the morning of the Accident prior to the commencement of works, he had requested for a co-worker to assist him with the instructed task. However, his request was denied. Instead, he was instructed to carry out the instructed task on his own and in an expeditious manner as the Defendant had to hand over the site soon.[note: 3]

Elavarasan’s testimony

12     The Plaintiff called one Govindasamy Elvarasan (“Elavarasan’) as his witness at trial. Elavarasan was employed by the Defendant as a general construction worker at the material time. Elavarasan did not personally witness the Accident. He, however, gave the following testimony which supported the Plaintiff’s case.

13     Elavanrasan testified that during the safety briefing conducted on 7 Janauary 2020, the Plaintiff had requested for a co-worker to assist him with the works. The request was denied and the Plaintiff was instructed to carry out the assigned work on his own.[note: 4]

14     Elavarasan also testified that the Site Engineer of the Defendant, one Muthuraja Manikandan (“Manikandan”), had informed him and his co-workers during lunch time at about 1.00 pm on the day of the Accident that the Plaintiff had fallen through an access opening on the scaffold structure as the opening was not barricaded or cordoned off, and advised them to be more careful whilst working on the scaffold structure. Elavarasan claimed that at the safety briefing meeting conducted the next morning however, they were told to disregard the instructions received at the briefing the day before, and to instead maintain that the Plaintiff had fallen whilst descending the monkey ladder as he had failed to anchor his safety harness[note: 5].

15     Elavarasan further corroborated the Plaintiff’s testimony that during the initial days of his employment, the access openings used to be barricaded by the Defendant using metal pipes similar to the description testified by the Plaintiff. The Defendant however gradually stopped installing these metal pipes, and that on the day of the Accident, there were no such pipes installed on either side of the opening where the Plaintiff allegedly fell[note: 6].

The Defendant’s case

16     The Defendant’s case was that the Plaintiff lost his footing, slipped and fell whilst he was descending from Level 7 to Level 6 of the external scaffold using the monkey ladder. The Plaintiff lost his footing as he failed to anchor his safety harness to the rungs of the ladder or other available structures whilst descending the ladder.

Kulangi’s testimony

17     The Defendant based their case on, inter alia, the testimonies of a number of Plaintiff’s co-workers, including one Mahalingam Kulangi (“Kulangi”). Kulangi was carrying out plastering touch-up works from the external scaffolding at Level 6 at the material time. Kulangi testified that at about 9.30am, he heard a shout at Level 6. He rushed to the location and saw the Plaintiff lying down on the metal decking of the external scaffold near the ladder at level 6[note: 7]. When he asked the Plaintiff how he fell, the Plaintiff stated that he was climbing down the ladder to level 6 when he lost his footing and slipped and fell, about 1 metre, from the monkey ladder. Kulangi further testified that he noticed that the Plaintiff was wearing his safety harness but it was not secured to the rungs of the ladder[note: 8].

Anbumani’s testimony

18     The Defendant’s Workplace Safety Health Officer (“WSHO”), Govindaraj Anbumani (“Anbumani”), testified to similar effect. Anbumani was not in the immediate vicinity of the Plaintiff when the Accident happened, and did not personally witness the Accident. He claimed, however, to have spoken to the Plaintiff after the Accident, and the Plaintiff told him he was climbing down the monkey ladder to Level 6 when he was careless and lost his footing. He fell about 1 metre from the ladder and landed on his left elbow. The Plaintiff also informed Anbumani that he was wearing his safety harness and that he had hooked both his lanyards, but had removed one of them to transfer the hook as he was descending. However, the other lanyard was not fully or properly hooked on, causing him to fall[note: 9].

19     Anbumani further testified that he carried out investigations for the purposes of preparing an Incident Investigation & Analysis Report[note: 10] (the “Investigation Report”). He interviewed the Plaintiff’s two co-workers, Kulangi and Halder Bimal (“Bimal”), who had attended to the Plaintiff at the scene immediately after the Accident. He prepared the Investigation Report on the same day based on what the Plaintiff, Kulangi and Bimal recounted to him.

20     The description of the incident in the Investigation Report noted as follows:

“[The Plaintiff] was assign [sic] by his work supervisor, Periyasamy to do silicone for the brickwall flashing. He finished his silicone work at Level [sic] and came own to Level 6. When he climbed down from the monkey ladder, suddenly slip his foot then lost his balance and fell down around 1 metre height to the platform. He was wear [sic] the safety harness and fail to anchor when he was descending from the ladder.”[note: 11]

21     Anbumani also noted that it was not the first time that the Plaintiff had removed his safety harness whilst working on an external scaffold. The Plaintiff had received a warning letter on 11 October 2019 for failing “to anchor safety harness with removed lanyard when working at height 22 m at Elevation – 1 level 7 and failing to wear goggles, no chin strap, no dust mask, during demolition bricks” [note: 12].

The Trial on Liability

22     The trial on liability took place over four days on 27 to 29 July 2022, and 3 October 2022. The following witnesses testified at trial:

Plaintiff’s witnesses

(a)       Rengaraj Krishnamoorthy (“PW1” or “Plaintiff”);

(b)       Yogesvaran S/O Suppiah (“PW2” or “Yogesvaran”); and

(c)       Govindasamy Elavarasan (“PW3” or “Elavarasan”).

Defendant’s witnesses

(a)       Mahalingam Kulangi (“DW1” or “Kulangi”);

(b)       Govindaraj Anbumani (“DW2” or “Anbumani”); and

(c)       Muthuraja Manikandan (“DW3” or “Manikandan”).

23     At the end of the trial on liability, I found that based on the Plaintiff’s pleaded case, the Plaintiff had not shown that the Defendant had breached its duty of care owed to it. I therefore dismissed the Plaintiff’s claim with costs. The Plaintiff is dissatisfied with my decision and has filed the present appeal. I set out my detailed grounds of decision below.

State of the Pleadings

24     I start by noting that the Plaintiff had pleaded a very specific version of events on how the Accident occurred. It is trite law that it is wrong for a judge to decide a matter on grounds which are not part of a party’s pleaded case. In Mookan Sadaiyakumar v Kim Hock Corp Pte Ltd [2019] SGHC 230 (“Mookan”), Dedar Singh JC (as he then was) issued the following reminder:

“A party is required by O 18 r 7(1) of the Rules of Court (Cap 322, R 5, 2014 Rev Ed) to set out in his pleadings all material facts on which he relies for his claim. The fundamental purpose of pleadings is to define the issues and inform the opponent in advance of the case he has to meet, so that he may take steps to deal with the case: Alwie Handoyo v Tjong Very Sumito and another and another appeal [2013] 4 SLR 308 at [168]; Farrell (formerly McLaughlin) v Secretary of State for Defence [1980] 1 WLR 172 at 180. This will prevent parties from being caught by surprise at trial: Tat Seng Machine Movers Pte Ltd v Orix Leasing Singapore Ltd [2009] 4 SLR(R) 1101 at [76].” (emphasis added)

25     On the facts of Mookan, the learned High Court judge found that the district judge had made a decision on a fact which had not been pleaded, i.e. the defendant’s failure to install an automatic tripping mechanism (at [27]). He held that the lower court’s judgment could not be allowed to stand on an unpleaded point, and accordingly allowed the defendant’s appeal.

26     As the Plaintiff in the present case had pleaded a very specific case, it is necessary to first set out the scope of the Plaintiff’s pleadings.

27     The Plaintiff’s pleaded case as to the cause of his injuries was that he fell through an uncovered opening on the platform. At paragraph [7] of the Statement of Claim, the Plaintiff averred as follows:

“The Plaintiff was instructed by his supervisor to mark spots along the façade on level six (6) of the building, approximately 1.2 metres apart, before beginning to drill the holes at the said spots. The supervisor also instructed the Plaintiff to complete the task in an expeditious manner. At the material time, the Plaintiff was walking along the structure and marking the spots as required, when he fell through an opening on the floor, which was not covered or cordoned off, and landed on the floor of level five (5) with great impact to his left side and back, and his left hand struck the scaffolding pipe, resulting in serious injuries”[note: 13]. (emphasis added)

28     The Defendant’s pleaded position, on the other hand, was that the “Plaintiff lost his footing, slipped and fell about 1 metre to the ground, whilst descending the scaffold ladder from Level 7 to Level 6 at the external scaffold. The root cause of the accident was the Plaintiff’s failure to anchor his safety harness to the runds of the ladder/other available structures whilst he was descending the ladder and/or not descending in a safe manner”[note: 14].

29     In his Reply, the Plaintiff maintained that his injuries were caused when he fell through an unbarricaded opening on the floor. The Plaintiff pleaded at paragraph 3 of the Reply[note: 15]:

“

……

e.    As the said external façade of the building was curved, the external scaffold structure was also set up in a curved manner;

f.     The opening which the Plaintiff fell through was located at a curved part of the external scaffold structure;

g.    Further, at the material time, in order to carry out his instructed task, the Plaintiff had to move sideways with his back facing the said opening;

h.    Also, the supervisor instructed the Plaintiff to complete the task in an expeditious manner;

i.     As such, at the material time, when the Plaintiff was walking (i.e. sideways with his back facing the opening) along the structure and carrying out his instructed task, he fell through an opening which was not covered or cordoned off; and

j.    The Plaintiff fell from a height of approximately 3 meters from the 6th level to the 5th level of the external curved scaffold structure.” (emphasis added)

30     It was thus clear from the Plaintiff’s pleadings that he had run his case based on one version of events only, namely that he fell through an opening on the platform floor that was not covered or cordoned off. A key issue to be determined was therefore whether: (a) the Plaintiff fell through an unbarricaded opening in the platform (the Plaintiff’s pleaded case); or (b) the Plaintiff slipped while descending the monkey ladder (the Defendant’s pleaded case) (the “Disputed Factual Issue”). I will make my finding of fact on the Disputed Factual Issue when analysing whether the Defendant breach its duty of care owned to the Plaintiff.

The Law

Duty of Care

31     In Miah Rasel v 5 Ways Engineering Services Pte Ltd [2018] 3 SLR 480 (“Miah Rasel”), the High Court summarized the four-fold test for the common law tort of negligence as follows: (a) the defendant had to have owed the claimant a duty of care; (b) the defendant’s conduct had to have breached the duty of care by falling below the requisite standard of care; (c) the claimant had to have suffered loss; and (d) the defendant’s breach of duty had to have been a cause of the claimant’s loss. The Workplace Health and Safety Act (Cap 354A, 2009 Rev Ed) and its accompanying regulations intersected with the tort of negligence at the first two stages of the inquiry.

32     The framework for establishing a duty of care is as set out in Spandeck Engineering (S) Pte Ltd v Defence Science & Technology Agency [2007] 4 SLR(R) 100 (“Spandeck”). The test for determining the imposition of a duty of care is a two-stage test comprising of first, proximity (including physical, circumstantial and causal proximity supported by the twin criteria of voluntary assumption of responsibility and actual reliance), and second, policy considerations, which were together preceded by the threshold question of factual foreseeability (Spandeck at [115]).

33     In the case of an employer and employee relationship, there is usually no dispute that a duty of care exists: see The Law of Torts in Singapore (2nd ed, 2016, Academy Publishing) (“The Law of Torts in Singapore”) at [04.010].

34     On the facts of the present case, the Defendant as the employer of the Plaintiff clearly owed the Plaintiff a duty of care. All the requirements in Spandeck had been satisfied in the present case. It was factually foreseeable that injury might be sustained if the duty of care was not observed. There was a sufficiently proximate relationship for the defendant to owe a duty of care qua employer. There were no policy reasons to suggest otherwise.

35     The Defendant was concurrently also the occupier of the worksite. In See Toh Siew Kee v Ho Ah Lam Ferrocement (Pte) Ltd [2013] 3 SLR 284, the Court of Appeal held that occupier’s liability should be subsumed under the general tort of negligence, in line with the approach in Spandeck. VK Rajah JA went on to observe that under the first limb of Spandeck, the vast majority of occupiers having control of the property which they occupy and/or the activities carried out there de jure owed a prima facie duty of care to lawful entrants. The Defendant therefore also owed the Plaintiff a duty of care qua occupier in the present case.

Breach of duty of care

36     The standard of care required of the Defendant is the general objective standard of a reasonable person using ordinary care and skill: see The Law of Torts in Singapore at [06.006].

37     In the present case, the question of whether the Defendant had fallen below the standard of a reasonable person exercising ordinary skill and care is in large part dependent on how the Accident occurred. As noted in the Law of Torts in Singapore, “in contrast to the existence of a duty of care, the question of whether there was a failure on the part of a defendant to take reasonable care towards the plaintiff is a factual, not a legal issue” (at [06.003]). It is only when the manner in which the Accident occurred is established that one can proceed to examine whether the Plaintiff had proven that the Defendant had failed to exercise ordinary skill and care which might have prevented the Accident from happening in the manner that the Plaintiff said that it did. Indeed, the particulars of negligence and breaches of the WSHA pleaded by the Plaintiff were premised on the Plaintiff’s pleaded version of the Accident, namely that he had fallen through a unbarricaded opening in the platform floor: see paragraphs [7], [9] and [10] of the Plaintiff’s Statement of Claim (Amendment No. 1).

38     As the parties have put forward conflicting accounts of how the Accident occurred, in assessing whether the Defendant had breached its duty of care, a key issue to be determined was whether: (a) the Plaintiff fell through an unbarricaded opening in the platform (the Plaintiff’s pleaded case) or (b) the Plaintiff slipped while descending the monkey ladder (the Defendant’s pleaded case).

Finding on the Disputed Factual Issue

39     As the claimant in the present lawsuit, the burden is on the Plaintiff to prove that the Defendant had breached the duty of care owed to it. This would entail proving, as an anterior issue, on a balance of probabilities, that the Accident happened in accordance with his pleaded case.

40     At the conclusion of the trial, I found that the Plaintiff had not proven on a balance of probabilities that the Accident happened in accordance with his pleaded case. I considered the following.

Plaintiff’s Evidence

Plaintiff’s testimony

41     The Plaintiff conceded that the Accident was not witnessed by any person. The Plaintiff’s account of how he fell and sustained his injuries was largely based on his own assertions. The Plaintiff’s account of the Accident would have been more probative if it were backed by a contemporaneous written statement or record containing his account of how the Accident happened. There were no such records in the present case. Instead, there were other contemporaneous written records which supported the Defendant’s version of events, which I will come to shortly.

Photograph of the opening

42     The Plaintiff relied on a photograph at page 45 of his affidavit[note: 16] (the “Photo”) which he claimed showed the opening from which he fell. The Plaintiff claimed that the Photo supported his account that the opening he fell from was not barricaded and/or cordoned off.

43     It transpired at trial that the Photo was taken by Anbumani, the Defendant’s WSHO[note: 17]. However, it became evident following cross examination that the Photo provided an inadequate depiction of the actual state of the opening. In particular, it was not a full view of the opening, capturing instead only a limited portion of the space immediately above the opening. It was thus impossible to rule out, based on the Photo alone, that there could not have been horizontal cross bars serving as barricades further above the opening which was outside the frame of the Photo. This was especially so given that the location of the cross bars at other barricaded openings at the Worksite appeared to be at a significant height above the floor level of the relevant opening: see, for example, the photograph of an unrelated opening at page 43 of the Plaintiff’s affidavit[note: 18]. The Photo therefore did not conclusively show that the opening in the Photo was indeed unbarricaded.

44     Anbumani, who took the Photo, explained that he did not take the picture of the entire space above the opening shown in the Photo as the Plaintiff had told him that he fell from the ladder. Anbumani confirmed that there were protective cross bars above the opening at 1.1 metres above the platform, which would have placed them outside the frame of the Photo. He did not capture the cross bars when taking the photo as he was focusing on the ladder, in light of the Plaintiff’s account to him that he “fell from the ladder”:

45     In this regard, the Plaintiff’s own expert, Yogesvaran, also confirmed that it was inconclusive, from the Photo alone, whether there were protective cross bars at the opening where Plaintiff allegedly fell.[note: 19]

46     Plaintiff’s Counsel submitted that an adverse inference should be drawn against the Defendant for not taking a full shot of the opening and the space above. Defendant’s Counsel’s reply, which I accepted, was that the first communication by the Plaintiff to the Defendant stating that the Plaintiff fell through an unbarricaded opening was when the writ was filed by the Plaintiff on 30 April 2021, some fifteen months after the Accident. Prior to this, the accounts of the witnesses interviewed by the Defendant were that the Plaintiff had slipped while descending the monkey ladder; no issues were raised as to the existence of an unbarricaded opening on the floor. By the time the statement of claim was served, the project had concluded and no other photographs of the opening could be procured.

47     Viewed as a whole. I found that the Photo was neutral and provided limited support for the Plaintiff’s case.

Elavarasan’s testimony

48     Another key evidence relied upon by the Plaintiff was Elavarasan (PW3)’s testimony. As noted above, Elavarasan had corroborated the Plaintiff’s story that: (a) not all opening in the scaffolding structure were barricaded just before the Accident; and (b) after the Accident, on the very same day, WSHO Manikandan (DW2) had instructed the workers to barricade all openings which were not cordoned off. Elavarasan deposed that he personally barricaded the opening at level 6 from which the Plaintiff fell.

49     I acknowledged that Elavarasan had since left the Defendant’s company and thus an argument could be made that he should be treated as an independent witness. However, having observed Elavarasan on the stand, and having regard to his testimony as a whole, I had significant doubts as to Elavarasan’s candour and the cogency and consistency of his evidence.

50     First, Elavarasan had in his affidavit testified that Manikandan (DW3) informed him and his co-workers at the 1.00 pm briefing convened after the Accident (the “1pm briefing”) that the Plaintiff had fallen through “an access opening ... that was not barricaded or cordoned off”[note: 20]. According to Elavarasan, Manikandan however changed tack the next day and directed the workers to maintain that the Plaintiff had fallen while descending the monkey ladder[note: 21]. However, during cross-examination, when it was put to Elavarasan that there was no statement made by anyone at the 1pm briefing that Plaintiff had fallen through an opening, Elavarasan agreed[note: 22]. He conceded that it was just his “guess”[note: 23]. It was further put to Elavarasan that the only thing that occurred at the 1pm meeting was that Manikandan told all the workers that the Plaintiff slipped and fell when descending the monkey ladder as he had not hooked on the harness, and a demonstration was thus given on how to climb or descend a ladder at the 1pm briefing. Again, Elavarasan agreed[note: 24].

51     Secondly, Elavarasan further stated during cross-examination that at the 1pm briefing, Anbumani had asked the workers to barricade all the openings that were not cordoned off[note: 25]. When it was put to him that this statement should have been included in his affidavit if it were indeed true, and that by raising it for the first time at trial he was lying, Elavarasan agreed[note: 26].

52     Thirdly, Elavarasan further deposed at [19] of his affidavit that at the safety meeting held on the day after the Accident, the workers were told to disregard the instructions received at the prior day’s briefing and to “maintain that the Plaintiff had fallen whilst descending the … monkey ladder”. However, when pressed in court, he changed his story and stated that all he and his co-workers were told was that they should “work safely”[note: 27].

53     Overall, Elavarasan’s testimony struck me as being convoluted and inconsistent. He was pre-disposed to give accounts on affidavit that assisted the Plaintiff’s case, only to recant his testimony when challenged during cross-examination. In my view, Elavarasan’s testimony needed to be treated with caution.

Defendant’s evidence

54     The Defendant relied on various contemporaneous documentary evidence to support its case theory.

The Investigation Report

55     As noted above, in the Investigation Report that was prepared on the day of the Accident[note: 28], the description of the incident was stated to be a case where the Plaintiff had slipped while he was climbing down a monkey ladder. The was based on the accounts of the Defendant’s two workers, Bimal and Kulangi, who had signed written statements to that effect[note: 29]. I noted that the Plaintiff had challenged the accounts given by Bimal and Kulangi. However, the fact remained that there was a contemporaneous Investigation Report detailing the cause of the Accident that was in line with the Defendant’s pleaded case. The Investigation Report must necessarily be given due weight, and construed and weighed against the other evidence adduced at trial.

The Medical Reports

56     Two key pieces of evidence relied on by the Defendant were:

(a)     a medical report dated 1 September 2020 by Dr Edgar Azada Salandanan[note: 30] of Changi General Hospital (“Dr Salandanan’s Report”), and

(b)     a medical report dated 2 September 2020 by Dr Yuen Wen Loong Paul[note: 31] also of Changi General Hospital (“Dr Yuen’s Report”) (collectively, the “Medical Reports”).

57     Plaintiff’s Counsel and Defendant’s Counsel had informed the court at the start of the trial that the Medical Reports were agreed as to authenticity and content[note: 32]. It is trite that once a document is admitted as to authenticity and content, the document is accepted as an authentic copy of the original, and is further admissible without having to call the maker of the statement to testify in court. It is, however, still open for parties to challenge the truth and weight of the documents, with the onus on the challenging party to adduce evidence to mount such challenges.

58     In the present case, the Medical Reports contained statements to the effect that the Plaintiff was working on a step ladder when he lost balance and landed on his [left] elbow. Dr Salandanan’s report thus stated in the opening paragraphs as follows:

“… Patient was working and climbing on a step ladder at around 09:10 hours on 07/01/2020 and after finishing scaffolding, he lost balance and landed on his right elbow”

59     Dr Yuen’s Report contained statements to similar effect:

“Patient was admitted to CGH orthopedics from 07/01/2020 to 14/01/2020. He suffered from left elbow injury after falling from ladder at work ….”

60     The Medical reports were prepared based on contemporaneous medical records taken by the attending doctor when the Plaintiff was seen at Changi General Hospital where he was conveyed to after the Accident. They were thus highly probative of the Plaintiff’s account of how the Accident took place, in the absence of evidence to the contrary.

61     The Plaintiff, however, tried to disassociate himself from the cause of the Accident as stated in the Medical Reports by contending that he did not communicate directly with the attending doctor who recorded the medical notes. The Plaintiff claimed that it was Manikandan (DW3), who accompanied him to the hospital, who spoke to the attending doctor. The Plaintiff asserted that Manikandan was conversant in English, and he was not. Thus, Manikandan communicated with the doctor on how the Plaintiff sustained his injuries.

62     Manikandan’s testimony in court was to the contrary. He testified that the doctor had asked the Plaintiff directly how the Accident occurred, and the doctor was accompanied by a Tamil speaking nurse[note: 33].

63     He who asserts must prove. In this regard, I noted that the Plaintiff’s contention that the accounts in the medical notes taken by the Changi General Hospital doctor was provided by Manikandan was based on no more than his bare assertions. The Plaintiff had been working in Singapore since 2006, for some 14 years up to the time of the Accident[note: 34]. I found it improbable that Plaintiff was incapable of informing the doctor in basic English whether he fell from an opening or slipped while descending a ladder, nor that the treating doctor would elicit information on the cause of the Accident from a third party (Manikandan), as opposed to the patient himself.

64     Additionally, the Medical Reports were produced by the Plaintiff himself and relied upon by him[note: 35]. Instead of writing to the doctors or calling them to testify on where they obtained the contradictory version of the Accident from, the Plaintiff merely asserted during cross examination that the version recorded by the doctors was not as communicated by him. Such an explanation lacked persuasion and credibility. On the face of the two Medical Reports, the provider of the information in the reports was prima facie the Plaintiff. Given that the burden of proof was on the Plaintiff, if he disagreed with the version recorded, the Plaintiff ought to have called the two doctors to verify the source of the information recorded by them. He could not attack the contents and accuracy of the Medical Reports without giving the doctors a chance to respond or explain the alleged discrepancies. Not having done so, I found that the Medical Reports supported the Defendant’s case theory that the Plaintiff did in fact fall while descending the monkey ladder.

The Victim Statement

65     The Defendant further relied on the Plaintiff’s victim statement (the “Statement”) purportedly signed by the Plaintiff in which it was stated that the Plaintiff was climbing down a monkey ladder on level 6 when he lost his footing and fell 1 metre [and that] his lanyard was not fully or properly hooked[note: 36]. The relevant extract of the Statement, which was typewritten in both English and Tamil, read as follows:

“On 7/1/2020, I was assigned to perform silicon application of window flashings by my supervisor Periysamy. My fellow workers, including Bimal and Kulangi, and myself attended a tool box meeting at 07:40 hours before work commenced.

At about 09:30 hours, I was climbing down a monkey ladder on L6. I was careless and lost my footing, fell about 1m and landed on my left elbow. Kulangi and Bimal are both nearby and they came over to help me get inside the building from a window at L6.

I was wearing my assigned PPE including the safety harness. I have hooked on both my lanyards and I removed one of them to transfer the hook as I was descending. However, I believe my other lanyard was not fully or properly hooked on when I fell, and I hit the platform ground as a result.

Shortly after, my supervisor Periysamy arrived and helped me to get down to level 1. Anbumani and Manikandan, both superviosrs, send me to Changi hospital for treatment after that.” (emphasis added)

66     The Statement was, on its face, signed by the Plaintiff. The Plaintiff made the bare assertion that the signature on the Statement was not his. In contrast, the Defendant’s site engineer, Manikandan (DW3), was adamant in his testimony that the Plaintiff had signed the Statement at the hospital in his presence and that he had witnessed the same[note: 37].

67     It is trite that the burden of proving that a signature is forged lies on the party alleging the forgery. While the usual civil standard of proof of balance of probabilities applied, the strength and cogency of the evidence that had to be furnished before fraud could be established on this standard was higher than that in most other civil claims. In Super Food Ltd v Mysore Nagaraja Kartik [2019] 4 SLR 692, the High Court observed as follows:

105    The burden of proving forgery is on the party alleging it (Yogambikai Nagarajah v Indian Overseas Bank and another appeal [1996] 2 SLR(R) 774 (“Yogambikai Nagarajah”) at [39]). ….An allegation of forgery amounts to an allegation of fraud. As the Court of Appeal held in Gimpex Ltd v Unity Holdings Business Ltd and others and another appeal [2015] 2 SLR 686 at [183]–[184], the standard of proof in all civil cases – even when fraud is alleged – is proof on the balance of probabilities, nothing higher. The law does not require a party to a civil case who alleges fraud to adduce more evidence in order to tip the balance in his favour.

106    It is true, however, that the law acknowledges that, on the probabilities inherent in the general course of human experience, the more serious an allegation is, the less likely it is that the allegation is true. That is why the law require a person alleging fraud to adduce stronger or more cogent evidence of fraud to discharge his burden than a person alleging, for example, negligence. (emphasis added)

68     Having regard to the evidence as a whole, I was of the view that the Plaintiff had not discharged his burden of showing, on a balance of probabilities, that his signature was forged. I considered the following.

69     First, besides the Plaintiff’s bare allegation that his signature was forged, the Plaintiff did not adduce any evidence to prove his assertion. Given the significance of the admissions contained in the Statement, if the signature on the Statement was indeed forged, the Plaintiff could have appointed a handwriting or other expert to opine and testify as to the authenticity of his signature. The Plaintiff, however, chose not to do so.

70     Secondly, there was nothing on the face of the signature that raised doubts as to its authenticity. Defence Counsel had thus brought the Plaintiff through various signatures he had executed on different documents on different occasions, and showed that there were some variances between the Defendant’s signatures on each occasion.

71     Thirdly, as noted above, Manikandan (DW3) testified that he witnessed the Plaintiff executing the statement after it was read to the Plaintiff in Tamil. Manikandan has since left the Defendant’s employ and thus had no interest in the litigation. As an independent witness, his evidence ought to be given due weight.

72     Fourthly, despite the Statement containing evidence that was so damning to his case, the Plaintiff did not raise any allegations of forgery until he affirmed his AEIC on 10 January 2022, some two years later. Nor did the Plaintiff lodge a police report despite asserting that his signature had been forged in a document that was extremely material to his claim for workmen compensation or in the ongoing litigation, which was a step a reasonable person would likely have taken in similar circumstances.

73     Plaintiff’s Counsel, however, argued that the Plaintiff was repatriated to India long before the Statement was produced by the Defendant in discovery. When the document was disclosed, the Plaintiff was in India and his focus was on getting the necessary medical treatments and giving his evidence in Court when the time arises for the same. The Plaintiff felt that the correct forum to raise his dispute of the Statement was in his AEIC and/or during the trial[note: 38].

74     I did not accept the Plaintiff Counsel’s arguments. These were all evidence from the bar. The transcripts showed that the Defendant’s Counsel had pressed the Plaintiff during cross-examination on why no police report was made, why the authorities to whom the Investigation Report was sent were not notified of the forgery, and why no handwriting expert was engaged. The Plaintiff conceded that he saw the Statement with his signature whilst he was still in Singapore, and that it was his supervisor Periyasamy who showed it to him[note: 39]. The Plaintiff testified that he did inform his legal counsel that the signature was not his, but his legal counsel did not advise him to do anything[note: 40]. The Plaintiff never cited the reasons in [73] for not reporting the forgery to the relevant authorities. Even if the Plaintiff was not in Singapore and could not have attended to the aforementioned steps, there was no reasons why his counsel could not have taken these steps on his behalf if they were genuinely of the view that the Defendant had committed a serious offence of forgery to prejudice the Plaintiff’s claim for compensation before MOM or the courts.

75     As noted above, the burden of proving forgery is on the Plaintiff and is a high one. For the reasons noted above, the Plaintiff had not, in my view, been able to surmount his burden of providing on a balance of probabilities that his signature appearing on the Statement was in fact forged. The Statement should thus be accorded due weight.

76     For completeness, I would deal with the other issues raised by the Plaintiff relating to the Statement to undermine the probative force of the Statement.

77     First, the Plaintiff contended that Anbumani “testified and admitted that the Statement was wholly an afterthought, after Anbumani had completed preparing the Investigation Report” and realized that the Report made no mention of the Plaintiff.[note: 41]

78     I found the Plaintiff’s submission that Anbumani had “admitted” that the Statement was “wholly an afterthought” to be without basis. Anbumani never admitted that the preparation of the Statement was an “afterthought”, which word carried with it connotations of fabrication and lack of reliability. He clearly stated that he decided to prepare the Statement as he realized the Investigation Report made no reference to the Plaintiff’s account of the Accident. Hence, he prepared the Statement based on what the Plaintiff had told him earlier in the day so that the record of the Accident would be complete and the Plaintiff’s account of the Accident could be captured once and for all:

NE 29 July 2022 p. 64 (Cross-examination of Anbumani by Plaintiff’s Counsel)

Q:

Alright. And what was the hurry to get it done on the same day?

A:

That’s because in page 10, that’s because the report found in page 108 [Report], I didn’t mention about Krishnamoorthy [Plaintiff]. That’s why I separately prepared this report, this statement. I, this was based on what he told me and then I gave it to Manikandan [DW3] to explain to him and get it signed. And if it was done on that day itself, the story won’t change, that is one reason. Because some other person could come and advise them to change the story, that was another reason.”

 

(emphasis added)



79     The Plaintiff next contended that Anbumani, who prepared the Statement, had never interviewed the Plaintiff at any time. Anbumani, who drafted the Statement was thus in no position to describe the events in the Statement. The Statement was accordingly a lie and/or afterthought, to cover up the fact that the Plaintiff was never interviewed, and to coerce the Plaintiff to admit to the Defendant’s false narrative of how the Accident happened[note: 42].

80     I found the Plaintiff’s contention that Anbumani had never interviewed the Plaintiff to be incorrect. Anbumani testified that Plaintiff had told him how he fell while they were in the storeroom the Plaintiff was brought to shortly after the Accident:

81     Indeed, when Plaintiff was asked during cross-examination whether he was interviewed by Manikandan and Anbumani while at the Worksite on how the Accident occurred, he answered – “Yes. They did ask” [note: 43]. In his closing submissions, Plaintiff’s Counsel also stated that Plaintiff did “briefly inform” Ambunani while in the storeroom about the cause of the Accident[note: 44].

82     Separately, there were also opportunities for Anbumani to elicit further information from the Plaintiff on the cause of the fall while Plaintiff was being conveyed to Changi General Hospital. Manikandan thus stated that it was possible that the statement was given by Plaintiff to Anbumani when they brought him to the hospital and dropped him off[note: 45]. The Plaintiff himself conceded in his affidavit that he had informed both his supervisors (i.e. Anbumani and Manikandan) on how he got injured while en route to the hospital (albeit he said he fell through an unbarricaded opening)[note: 46].

83     The Statement was further not a long or complex one. It captured the essence of how the Accident happened. I did not see how the preparation of such a statement must be preceded by a lengthy and formal interview. I am thus unable to accept that Anbumani was not in a position to prepare the Statement based on the information made available to him as described above or that the Statement was inherently untrustworthy.

84     The Plaintiff further tried to draw the inference that because the Statement was not included in the final Investigation Report, this suggested that the Plaintiff had in fact refused to sign it, and hence it was not included. To discredit the contents of the Investigation Report further, the Plaintiff contended that it was illogical to prepare an Investigation Report that did not mention the Plaintiff and/or any information provided by the Plaintiff in the Investigation Report[note: 47]. Plaintiff stated that it was even more incredible that Anbumani saw the necessity to include statements from Kulangi and Bimal in the Report but did not see the necessity to include any Statement or information from the Plaintiff himself. The Plaintiff argued that the only logical reason for this was that the Report was prepared in a desperate attempt to cover up the Accident and to push the blame on Plaintiff and further since the Plaintiff refused to sign the Statement.

85     I was unable to accept Plaintiff’s Counsel’s argument. Plaintiff’s Counsel’s argument that it was illogical not to include the Plaintiff’s Statement in the Investigation Report appeared to be based on Plaintiff’s Counsel’s own deduction and no more. The Plaintiff did not provide any evidence of any industry practice or MOM requirement to show that the victim statement must be included in the Investigation Report in all cases. The further deduction that just because the Statement was not included in the Investigation Report that this therefore suggested that the signature on the Investigation Report was forged, appeared to be even more tenuous, being based solely on conjectures. I also noted that save for a single put to Anbumani[note: 48], Plaintiff’s Counsel never questioned Anbumani as to why the Statement was not included in the Investigation Report so as to allow Anbumani an opportunity to explain.

Other evidence

The 1pm briefing

86     As noted above, Elavarasan (PW3) had deposed in his affidavit that a safety briefing was convened by the WSHO, Manikandan, at 1pm on the date of the Accident[note: 49]. Elavarasan (PW3) agreed that the diagram in the Investigation Report at Defendant’s Bundle of Documents (“DB”) at page 108, which showed a person demonstrating how to climb up and down a ladder and to hook on the harness, was taken at the 1pm briefing[note: 50]. Elavarasan (PW3) agreed that the purpose of the 1pm Meeting, which was convened because of the Accident, was to remind the workers “how to climb up and down the ladder and hook on”:

NE 28 July 2022 p. 76-77 (Cross-examination of Elavarasan by Defendant’s Counsel)

Q

A:

Intpr:

87     It was evident from the transcripts that Elavarasan had confirmed that the “only purpose” of the 1pm meeting was to remind all workers how to climb up and down the ladder, and further that the “whole purpose” of the meeting was to tell the workers the proper way to go up and down the ladder. Admittedly, Elavarasan did prevaricate eventually and stated that the issue of the barricading was also discussed. Be that as it way, given the close proximity in time between the 1pm meeting and the Accident, and the fact that an important objective of the 1pm meeting was to brief the workers on the safety aspects of ascending and descending a monkey ladder, this, in my view, lent credence to Anbumani’s testimony that the cause of the Plaintiff’s fall was likely due to accidental slippage while descending the monkey ladder, which was why the 1pm safety briefing was convened to remind the workers of the proper procedures for doing so.

The Plaintiff’s injuries

88     While no accident reconstructionist was called, the court was entitled, when assessing the inherent probabilities and improbabilities of the parties’ respective cases, to take notice of the Plaintiff’s injuries in determining whether the Plaintiff’s account of how he fell was believable as a matter of common sense, logic and common human experience.

89     The Plaintiff’s testimony at the trial was that he was walking sideways towards his left, whilst facing the building, when he stepped into an opening, and he fell from one level to another level “with great impact to my left side, back and left hand, which struck the scaffolding pipe, resulting in serious injuries”. The picture of the spot where the Plaintiff claimed he landed (as shown on DB111 [left and right photos]; or Bundle of Affidavits (“BA”) (Tab A) p. 45) showed the presence of four cross-pipes in close proximity of the monkey ladder at the bottom floor where the Plaintiff allegedly landed.

90     Despite the Plaintiff’s account, the only injury that the Plaintiff suffered was a closed fracture of the left elbow. In court, the Plaintiff confirmed that this was the only injury that he suffered from the Accident. He confirmed that he suffered no other injury, bruise or cut[note: 51]. In my mind, the fact that the sole injury sustained by the Plaintiff was confined to the left elbow, with no other bruises and cuts whatsoever, was wholly inconsistent, as a matter of common sense, logic, and common human experience, with the Plaintiff’s story that he fell “with great impact to my left side, back and left hand, which struck the scaffolding pipe, resulting in serious injuries”.

91     The fact that the Plaintiff sustained only an injury to the left elbow was, on the other hand, more consistent with the Defendant’s case theory that the Plaintiff was climbing down the monkey ladder and slipped on his left step. Such a manner of slipping was likely to lead to the left elbow hitting against the left side scaffolding pipe. I therefore found, on a balance of probabilities, that the injuries were consistent with an accidental slippage when Plaintiff was descending the monkey ladder.

Whether the Plaintiff had proven its pleaded case

92     As noted in my preliminary remarks, the Plaintiff bore the burden of proving his pleaded case, i.e. he fell through an unbarricaded, uncovered opening in the platform floor. Having regard to the fact that no one witnessed the Accident, and the other evidence noted above, I found that the Plaintiff had fallen woefully short of proving his version of the events on a balance of probabilities.

Conclusion on whether the Defendant breached its duty of care

93     As noted above, the Plaintiff’s pleaded case was that he fell through an opening in the scaffolding that was not barricaded or cordoned off. All the particulars of negligence, breach of statutory duty and the risks assessment revolved around the specific version of events pleaded by the Plaintiff. As the objective facts and the proper inferences showed that the Plaintiff had slipped on the monkey ladder whilst descending it, I found that the Plaintiff had failed to discharge his burden of showing that the Defendant breached its duty of care based on the Plaintiff’s pleaded case.

94     As the Plaintiff did not plead an alternate case of accidental slippage while descending the monkey ladder, there was no basis for considering whether the Defendant had been negligent in implementing and enforcing its work procedures as regards the descent and ascent of its workers up and down the monkey ladder. In any event, Plaintiff conceded that Defendant had implemented a 3-point system for ascending and descending the ladder and provided the workers with a safety harness[note: 52]. Plaintiff’s expert, Yogesvaran, also confirmed that the 3-point system was adequate for ascending and descending the ladder[note: 53].

Arguments in relation to the Permit To Work, the Risk Assessment, and the Safety Work Procedures

Permit to Work

95     The Plaintiff argued that the Defendant did not have in force a Permit to Work in breach of Regulations 10 and 11 of the Workplace Safety and Health (Construction) Regulations 2007, and Regulation 20(2) of the Workplace Safety and Health (Work at Heights) Regulations 2013.

96     Both Anbumani and Manikandan conceded that it was necessary to have a Permit to Work for the works done at the relevant time. The Plaintiff submitted that the Defendant, in breach of its duties, failed to implement such a Permit to Work. The Plaintiff pointed out that the Permit to Work at DB73 was for another worksite. Anbumani and Manikandan did not dispute that a wrong Permit to Work had been provided in court[note: 54], but maintained that there would have been a Permit to Work for the works done at the material time. The Defendant, however, made no attempt to tender the correct Permit to Work in court.

97     I agree with the Plaintiff that an adverse inference ought in the circumstances be drawn against the Defendant that they did not have the requisite Permit to Work for the Defendant’s Worksite at the material time in breach of their statutory duties: see Miah Rasel.

Whether the Defendant had carried out and/or enforced a sufficient Risk Assessment

98     The Plaintiff contended that the Risk Assessment exhibited[note: 55] was falsified and tampered with. Plaintiff’s Counsel pointed out that the serial numbers appearing at pages 12 to 16 on the one hand, and pages 17 to 24 on the other, were repetitive, and further did not correspond to the content page at page 1 of the Risk Assessment.

99     Plaintiff further contended that the Plaintiff’s instructed task was not included in the Risk Assessment[note: 56]. Neither DW2 or DW3 were able to highlight any specific parts of the Risk Assessment that specified the Plaintiff’s instructed works.

100    Again, I agree with the Plaintiff that without a credible explanation from the Defendant as to the discrepancies in the Risk Assessment, an adverse inference ought to be drawn against the Defendant that there was not in place a sufficient Risk Assessment at the material time: see Miah Rasel.

Safety Work Procedures

101    The Plaintiff noted that the Defendant had exhibited a Safety Work Procedure at DB50-70. The procedures relating to the Plaintiff’s work on the scaffold is found at DB54-56.

102    The Plaintiff submitted that the scaffold was not certified for use by the Plaintiff at the material time as the Certificate of Supervision provided by the Plaintiff (DB8) had expired in December 2019, and there was accordingly no certificate at the material time of the Accident.

103    Further, under the Safety Work Procedure, the Defendant was required to ensure that there must be sufficient supervision of the Plaintiff by a competent supervisor at the material time. It was undisputed that the Plaintiff was working alone and not under any supervision at the material time. In light of the above, Plaintiff submitted that the Defendant did not implement and/or enforce the Safety Work Procedure at the material time. I agree that such supervision was not borne out by the evidence.

The Permit to Work, the Risk Assessment and the Safety Work Procedures and the Plaintiff’s pleaded case

104    Notwithstanding the above arguments, I agree with Defendant’s Counsel that once it is accepted that the Plaintiff had slipped or fallen whilst descending the ladder, the Plaintiff’s pleaded case must fail in limine. No alternative case based on slipping or falling from a ladder, or any other scenarios, was pleaded by the Plaintiff. As the Plaintiff’s pleaded case – namely that he fell when he stepped into an unbarricaded opening – had not been proven on a balance of probabilities, all of the Plaintiff’s arguments based on the Permit to Work, Risks Assessments, etc. do not advance the Plaintiff’s case any further as they do not go towards proving a breach of duty of care in relation to the Plaintiff’s pleaded case.

105    In any event, there was no link between the alleged breaches of the WSHA and associated regulations and the Plaintiff’s loss. Even if the lack of a Permit to Work, Risk Assessment and Safety Work Procedure were established, they were not shown to be causative of the Plaintiff’s injuries, which I have found, on a balance of probabilities, to be due to accidental slipping while descending the monkey ladder. It was not proven that but for the lack of a Permit to Work, the Risk Assessment and the enforcement of the Safety Work Procedure, the Plaintiff would not have slipped while descending the monkey ladder. More pertinently, the Plaintiff never ran its case based on the accidental slippage while descending the monkey ladder, and consequently did not adduce any evidence to substantiate its claim that the training, supervision and work procedures relating to ascending and/or descending the monkey ladder were deficient or inadequate. As noted in Mookan, the court cannot made a decision on a fact which had not been pleaded.

Conclusion

106    For the forgoing reasons, based on the Plaintiff’s pleaded case, I found that the Plaintiff had not proven that the Defendant had breached its duty of care owed to it. The essential ingredients for establishing a claim in negligence had thus not been made out.

107    I therefore dismissed the Plaintiff’s claim with costs.
