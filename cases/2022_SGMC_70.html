Criminal Procedure and Sentencing – Offences under section 417 of the Penal Code – Young Offender – Sentence

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in MA 9227/2022/01 .]

13 December 2022

District Judge Carol Ling Feng Yong:

Introduction

1       The Accused pleaded guilty to these two charges:

MAC 904940/2022

You, are charged that you, on 13 August 2021, at the Oversea-Chinese Banking Corporation (“OCBC”) branch located in in Paya Lebar Square, 60 Paya Lebar Road, Singapore, did cheat OCBC by deceiving OCBC into believing that you would be the ultimate beneficial owner of the OCBC bank account that you were applying for, a fact which you knew to be false, and by such manner of deception, you intentionally induced OCBC to open OCBC Bank Account Number XXX in your name, which OCBC would not have done if it was not so deceived, and which act is likely to cause harm to the reputation of OCBC, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 417 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

MAC 904974/2022

You, are charged that you, on 13 August 2021, in Singapore, on United Overseas Bank (“UOB”)’s online website, did cheat UOB by deceiving UOB into believing that you would be the ultimate beneficial owner of the UOB bank account that you were applying for, a fact which you knew to be false, and by such manner of deception, you intentionally induced UOB to open UOB Bank Account Number XXX in your name, which UOB would not have done if it was not so deceived, and which act is likely is likely to cause harm to the reputation of UOB, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 417 of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).

2       One charge under section 3(1) read with section 10 of the Computer Misuse Act (Cap 50A, 2007 Rev Ed), for conspiring with another to facilitate unauthorized access to a computer of UOB to perform a function for the purpose of securing access, was taken into consideration in sentencing.

3       The Accused was 19 years old when he pleaded guilty (Date of Birth: 10 October 2002). Both the pre-sentence Probation as well as Reformative Training reports were before me at the point of sentencing. The Accused was found unsuitable for probation but physically and mentally suitable for reformative training.

4       I sentenced the Accused to Reformative Training with Level Two intensity of rehabilitation, as recommended in the report. This was a minimum of 12-months detention in a Reformative Training Centre.

5       As these offences were committed whilst the Accused was undergoing a period of probation imposed in October 2019, breach action was initiated upon the conclusion of this set of proceedings. The learned District Judge before whom breach action was taken, revoked probation and similarly sentenced the Accused to Reformative Training with Level Two intensity of rehabilitation.

6       This is the Accused’s appeal against the sentence in respect of the charges before me[note: 1].

Facts of the Case[note: 2]

7       In July 2021, the Accused came across a message, sent on an unspecified Telegram group by one “Bay”, offering fast cash for the opening of bank accounts. Attracted by the offer of fast cash, the Accused contacted “Bay” in early August 2021. “Bay” told the Accused that he would be given “around $400” if he were to open a bank account with United Overseas Bank (“UOB”) or Oversea-Chinese Banking Corporation (“OCBC”) and hand over the Automated Teller Machine (the “ATM”) card and Personal Identification Number (“PIN”) (the “scheme”).

8       The Accused agreed to participate in the scheme. He informed “Bay” that he would open two bank accounts, one with UOB and another with OCBC, in exchange for $800.

Facts relating to MAC 904974/2022

9       Pursuant to the scheme, on 13 August 2021, the Accused made an application on UOB’s online website to open a bank account with UOB. As part of his application, the Accused declared that he would be the ultimate beneficial owner of the UOB bank account that he was applying for, a fact which he knew at the time to be false.

10     As a result of the Accused’s deception, UOB processed his application and, on 14 August 2021, opened UOB Bank Account Number XXX (the “UOB account”) in the Accused’s name. This was an act which UOB would not have done if it had not been so deceived, and which was likely to cause harm to UOB’s reputation. The Accused subsequently handed the ATM card and PIN linked to the UOB Account to “Bay”.

11     Investigations later revealed that the UOB account had been the subject of at least 27 police reports involving a reported total of at least $129,591.70 in lost monies. The UOB account’s bank statements also showed that it had been used to receive a total of $214,353.38 in 85 separate transactions from 31 August to 2 September 2021. The UOB account was subsequently frozen pursuant to police investigations. By then, the vast majority of the monies had been dissipated and only a balance of $0.67 remained.

Facts relating to MAC 904940/2022

12     Pursuant to the scheme, on 13 August 2021, the Accused visited OCBC’s Paya Lebar Square branch, located at 60 Paya Lebar Road, Singapore 409051, to open a bank account with OCBC. As part of his application, the Accused declared that he would be the ultimate beneficial owner of the OCBC bank account that he was applying for, a fact which he knew at the time to be false.

13     As a result of the Accused’s deception, OCBC processed his application and, on the same day, opened OCBC Bank Account Number XXX (the “OCBC account”) in his name. This was an act which OCBC would not have done if it had not been so deceived, and which was likely to cause harm to OCBC’s reputation. After receiving by mail the ATM card and PIN linked to the OCBC account, the Accused contacted “Bay” and subsequently handed over the ATM card and PIN linked to the OCBC account to “Bay”.

14     Police investigations revealed that the OCBC account had been the subject of at least 12 police reports involving a reported total of at least $21,106.20 in lost monies. The OCBC account’s bank statements also showed that it had been used to receive a total of $29,664.68 in 38 separate transactions from 22 to 23 August 2021. The OCBC account was subsequently frozen pursuant to police investigations. By then, the vast majority of the monies had been dissipated and only a balance of $1.00 remained.

15     The Accused admitted to the Statement of Facts without qualification.

The Appropriate Sentence

16     In October 2019, the Accused was placed on 24 months’ probation for theft in dwelling offences under section 380 of the Penal Code as well as for being armed with a dangerous/offensive instrument under section 22(1)(a) of the Miscellaneous Offences (Public Order and Nuisance) Act, Chapter 184.

17     Notwithstanding the Accused’s previous order of probation, it is well-established that for an offender of the Accused’s age, rehabilitation remained the dominant sentencing consideration. The overall facts and circumstances of the case did not displace the Court’s focus on rehabilitation as its objective in sentencing[note: 3]. With that in mind, I called for a pre-sentence Probation suitability report. Subsequently, I also called for a Reformative Training suitability report.

Accused Found Unsuitable for Probation

18     It was unsurprising that the Accused was not found suitable for probation.

19     As highlighted by the Probation Officer Mr Gilbert Goh, the Accused’s repeated offending behaviour despite being placed on the Guidance Programme and a Probation Order reflected his lack of remorse and internalisation of the consequences of his actions[note: 4]. In 2018, the Accused was placed on the Guidance Programme for theft in dwelling offences but failed to successfully complete the programme due to the commission of offences for which he was subsequently charged in court and placed on probation for in October 2019. He again failed to complete his period of probation when he committed this set of offences before me. As observed by the Probation Officer, the conduct of the Accused demonstrated a poor response to intervention by authorities. His level of risk-taking, pro-criminal attitudes and disregard for the law indicated his lack of consequential thinking[note: 5].

20     The home environment did not appear conducive for the Accused’s rehabilitation. Whilst the Accused acknowledged his parents’ concern for him, he chose only to engage in superficial conversations with them and/or retort by shouting at them or walking away[note: 6]. As assessed by the Probation Officer, the Accused’s parents had been limited in their ability to influence him positively despite their best efforts[note: 7].

21     Even an environment like the military where the Accused was serving his National Service did not seem to influence him positively. As noted by the Probation Officer, since 1 Jan 2022, the Accused had been on medical leave for more than 120 days such that his unit was unable to provide an assessment of his performance. The Accused’s tendency to obtain medical certificates through convenient means (virtual platforms) just because he “did not like the military regime and found the duties in camp to be boring”[note: 8] to avoid returning to camp suggested a lack of motivation to be constructively engaged and disregard for authority[note: 9]. Despite the advice of the Probation Officer, the Accused persisted in obtaining medical certificates to avoid reporting to camp for duties[note: 10].

22     Having perused the Probation report, I did not see any basis to disagree with the findings of the Probation Officer. The viability of putting the Accused on probation had been thoroughly considered by the Probation Officer. The Accused did not challenge or query the contents of the Probation Report when given the opportunity to do so. Other than making an application to serve his term of Reformative Training at a later date, the Accused had nothing to say in mitigation[note: 11].

Reformative Training as the Appropriate Sentence

23     On the other hand, the Accused was found physically and mentally suitable for a term of reformative training. The Reformative Training report recognized that the Accused had multiple needs that required intervention, namely in the areas of his attitudes supportive of crime, his poor relationship with his family, poor academic performance, limited engagement in National Service, alcohol use, unconstructive use of leisure time and poor coping with stress[note: 12]. A Level Two intensity of rehabilitation in the Reformative Training Centre (RTC) was recommended.

24     As re-emphasized in Public Prosecutor v Ong Jack Hong [2016] 5 SLR 166, at [11], “the imposition of reformative training remains within the range of sentencing options for young offenders where the focus is on rehabilitation …….. reformative training too is geared towards the rehabilitation of the offender and hence remains a viable option”. At [14], the Court went on to state: “the choice between reformative training and probation is governed primarily by whether there is in the circumstances a need for a degree of deterrence within an overarching focus and emphasis on rehabilitation”.

25     In this case, I was satisfied that a term of reformative training would be the appropriate sentence for the Accused. Whilst rehabilitation remained as the Court’s primary sentencing consideration, I was of the view that an element of deterrence was warranted.

26     First, the nature of the offences called for a degree of deterrence. The offences had resulted in serious harm. As highlighted by the Prosecution[note: 13]:

(a)     The UOB bank account was the subject of at least 27 police reports involving a reported total of at least $129,591.70 in lost monies. It was also used to receive a total of $214,353.38 in potentially illegal proceeds in 85 separate transactions from 31 August to 2 September 2021[note: 14];

(b)     The OCBC bank account was the subject of at least 12 police reports involving a reported total of at least $21,106.20 in lost monies. The OCBC account was also used to receive a total of $29,664.68 in potentially illegal proceeds in 38 separate transactions from 22 to 23 August 2021[note: 15].

27     Second, there was a need for specific deterrence in view of the Accused’s history of offending, as clearly laid out by the Prosecution[note: 16]:

28     I agreed with the Prosecution that prior interventions had not proven effective in rehabilitating the Accused. As correctly pointed out, his performance during both the Guidance Programme and probation was poor[note: 17]:

(a)     His attendance at the Guidance Programme became irregular from the fourth month onwards. It remained so despite the extension of the Guidance Programme from 7 April 2019 to 7 October 2019. As a result, the Accused received several warnings[note: 18];

(b)     The Accused breached probation conditions on multiple occasions by failing to comply with time restrictions, failing to report to his Probation Officer, and defaulting on his community service. As a result, the accused received two verbal warnings and a written warning[note: 19].

29     I was also satisfied that the recommended term of a Level Two intensity of rehabilitation which carried with it a minimum of a 12-month detention in the RTC would not be a disproportionate sentence.

30     In Public Prosecutor v Saiful Rizam bin Assim and other appeals [2014] 2 SLR 495 at [25], the Court reminded: “It must be borne in mind that reformative training is a special programme put in place for young offenders. Of course it has an element of punishment in it as the young offender is deprived of his liberty. Parliament had, in its wisdom, deemed it fit to impose a minimum period of 18 months in order to ensure that the programme would achieve its objective of rehabilitating the young offender. Thus when the principle of proportionality is being considered, the aim of the reformative programme must not be lost sight of.” The Court in Saiful Rizam considered the fact that the minimum period of reformative training (18 months) [note: 20] was longer than what the District Judge intended to impose on offenders Saiful and Yunus (seven months imprisonment and 14 months imprisonment respectively) and added that that should not have stood as a bar to reformative training being ordered against them.

31     The Court of Appeal in Sim Yeow Kee v Public Prosecutor [2016] 5 SLR 936 also stated: “even if the minimum incarceration period … might not be proportionate to the underlying offence committed by the young offender, the principle of proportionality legitimately takes a back seat with a view to reforming the young offender and deterring him at an early stage” (at [95]).[note: 21]

32     Having regard to the Accused’s character, previous conduct and the circumstances of the offences, I was satisfied that a term of reformative training was necessary to reform the Accused and it would be the most appropriate sentence for this young man.

Conclusion

33     The Accused is currently on bail pending appeal.
