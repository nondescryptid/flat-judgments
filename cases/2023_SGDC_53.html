Lim Chai Hing v Liberty Insurance Pte Ltd
[2023] SGDC 53

Case Number:DC/DC 939 of 2022, DC/SUM 2630 of 2022, DC/RA 3 of 2023
Decision Date:24 March 2023
Tribunal/Court:District Court
Coram: Kow Keng Siong
Counsel Name(s): Ramasamy Chettiar, Samuel Chong, Mark Ho (East Asia Law Corporation) for the Appellant; Anthony Wee, Fendrick Koh (Titanium Law Chambers LLC) for the Respondent.
Parties: Lim Chai Hing — Liberty Insurance Pte Ltd

Insurance – Motor vehicle insurance – Insurer’s liability – Vehicle sublet to driver in breach of term in insurance policy – Driver involved in an accident – Whether insurer’s duty under s 9(1) Motor Vehicles (Third-Party Risks and Compensation) Act 1960 applies – Correct test for determining whether insurer is liable to pay under the legislation – How the phrase “avoid or cancel … the policy” in s 9(1) Motor Vehicles (Third-Party Risks and Compensation) Act 1960 is to be understood

24 March 2023

District Judge Kow Keng Siong:

Introduction

1       Section 9(1) of the Motor Vehicles (Third-Party Risks and Compensation) Act 1960 (“MVA”) imposes a duty on an insurer to satisfy a judgment entered against a person insured under its policy in respect of third-party risks. This duty applies “even though the insurer may be entitled to avoid or cancel … the policy”. Would an insurer be liable under s 9(1) if, in breach of its policy, the insured vehicle is sublet to a driver who is later involved in an accident? This issue is raised in the present appeal.

Background

2       The facts in this case are undisputed.

(a)     In 2015, Lim Chai Hing (“the Appellant”) was injured in a road traffic accident with a car SGQ 3788T (“Vehicle”) while riding his motorcycle.

(b)     At the material time, the Vehicle was driven by one Pang Meng Hee Stephen (“Pang”).

(c)     Pang had hired the Vehicle from Alpha Car Leasing Pte Ltd (“Alpha”).[note: 1] Alpha, in turn, had hired the Vehicle from Elitez Car Rental Private Limited (“Elitez”).

(d)     Elitez had insured the Vehicle in respect of third-party risks with Liberty Insurance Pte Ltd (“the Respondent”). The terms of this policy are in certificate of insurance no. SD15V04291/VPZ/R01 (“Policy”).[note: 2] Elitez is the policy holder and insured party of the Policy.

(e)     The Appellant sued Pang (vide DC/DC 938 of 2018) and subsequently obtained final judgment against him (vide DC/JUD1451/2019) (“Final Judgment”).

3       In 2022, the Appellant commenced action (vide DC/DC 939 of 2022) (“DC 939”) against the Respondent to satisfy the Final Judgment.

4       After pleadings had closed, the Appellant applied for summary judgment against the Respondent vide DC/SUM 2630/2022 (“Application”). The Application was heard by a Deputy Registrar (“DR”), who dismissed it and granted unconditional leave to defend.

5       This is the Appellant’s appeal against the DR’s decision.

Parties’ submissions

6       The Appellant’s case is straightforward. It is grounded solely on s 9(1) of the MVA.[note: 3] According to the Appellant, Pang was an insured driver under the Policy at the time of the accident. This is because he fell within Clause 6 of the Policy as a person “to whom the [Vehicle was] hired”. Pursuant to s 9(1), the Respondent (as the insurer) is obliged to satisfy the Final Judgment even if there are grounds for the Respondent to cancel or avoid the Policy.[note: 4]

7       The Respondent opposes the Application on the following grounds.

(a)     First, s 9(1) is not applicable as Pang is not an insured driver under the Policy. This is because the Policy expressly disallows Alpha from subletting the Vehicle to another party such as Pang.[note: 5] (The DR accepted this submission and dismissed the Application on this basis.)

(b)     Second, the Statement of Claim at [3] alleges that the Respondent is obliged to satisfy the Full Judgment under s 9 of the MVA “and/or the agreement signed by the Motor Insurer’s Bureau” (“MIB Agreement”) (emphasis added). Given that the Appellant has framed his claim in the alternative, this shows that he is not confident of his case under s 9(1) and there are thus triable issues.[note: 6]

(c)     Finally, the Appellant’s claim is vexatious, an abuse of process, and in breach of an implied forbearance to sue. This is because prior to DC 939, the Appellant had already sued the Respondent on the same cause of action vide DC/DC 3233 of 2019 (“DC 3233”). The Appellant withdrew DC 3233 at the trial stage after the Respondent had agreed to his request to waive costs. The Respondent submits that there are triable issues as the Appellant disputes (i) their evidence of how DC 3233 came to be withdrawn and (ii) whether their evidence precludes the Appellant from commencing DC 939.[note: 7]

My Decision

Applicable principles for summary judgment

8       The principles for entering summary judgement are well settled. Such a judgment may be entered if the facts and law needed to determine the claims are not in dispute. If the applicable law is in dispute, courts may still enter summary judgment if (a) the legal controversy can be easily resolved and (b) both the law and the facts are on the side of the plaintiff.

9       Procedurally, the plaintiff must first show that he has a prima facie case for summary judgment. If he fails to do so, then his application will be dismissed with costs. If the plaintiff can show that he has a prima facie case, then the defendant must establish that there is a fair or reasonable possibility that he has a real or bona fide defence. If the court finds that the defence is not credible after having regard to its consistency with contemporaneous documents, its inherent plausibility, and other compelling evidence, then the plaintiff is entitlement to summary judgement: Goh Chok Tong v Chee Soon Juan [2003] 3 SLR(R) 32 at [25].

Section 9(1)

10     Applying these principles to the present case, the first step is to assess whether the Appellant has made out a prima facie case that s 9(1) of the MVA applies. This section stipulates the following:

Duty of insurers to satisfy judgments against persons insured in respect of third-party risks

9.—(1)    Subject to this section, if after a certificate of insurance has been issued under section 4(9) to the person by whom a policy has been effected, judgment in respect of any liability required to be covered by a policy under section 4(1)(b) (being a liability covered by the terms of the policy) is obtained against any person insured by the policy, then, even though the insurer may be entitled to avoid or cancel or may have avoided or cancelled the policy, the following apply to the insurer: …

[emphasis added]

11     The purpose and effect of s 9(1) has been helpfully summarised in Er Kee Jeng v Public Prosecutor [2006] 2 SLR(R) 485 as follows:

37    … Section 9 was enacted to confer upon the third party a direct cause of action against the insurer, a right which did not exist under common law: Nippon Fire and Marine Insurance Co Ltd v Sim Jin Hwee [1998] 2 SLR(R) 77 at [14]. It creates a statutory relationship between the insurer and the third party, which exists irrespective of the contractual position vis-à-vis the insurer and the insured. In order to be discharged from its liabilities under s 9, the insurer must adopt the procedures set out in ss 9(3) to 9(5), which were intended to give third parties adequate notice that the insurer had disclaimed not only its contractual obligations to the insured, but also its statutory obligations to the third party. Failure to follow these procedures only entails the insurer’s continued liability under statute and not contract. …

12     On a plain reading of s 9(1), the Appellant must satisfy four requirements before that provision is engaged.

Three requirements satisfied

13     First, the Appellant must show that a certificate of insurance under s 4(9) of the MVA has been issued vis-à-vis the Vehicle. It is not disputed that this requirement is satisfied in the present case, and that the certificate complies with the prescribed form as set out in Form A in the Schedule to the Motor Vehicles (Third-Party Risks and Compensation) Rules.[note: 8]

14     Second, the Appellant must prove that there is a judgment against Pang in respect of liability required to be covered by a policy under s 4(1)(b) of the MVA. Section 4(1)(b) stipulates the following requirements:

Requirements in respect of policies and securities

4.—(1)    In order to comply with the requirements of this Act, a policy of insurance must, subject to subsection (4), be a policy which —

(a)    …

(b)    insures the person, persons or classes of persons that may be specified in the policy in respect of any liability which may be incurred by him, her or them in respect of the death of or bodily injury to any person caused by or arising out of the use of the motor vehicle in Singapore and in any territory specified in the Schedule.

…

[emphasis added]

Again, it is not disputed that the second requirement in s 9(1) has been satisfied – the Appellant had obtained the Final Judgment against Pang for personal injuries suffered due to the accident.

15     Third, the Appellant must also establish that the terms of the policy issued by the Respondent cover the liability set out in s 4(1)(b). This requirement is again satisfied in the present case. [note: 9]

Whether Pang is a “person insured by the policy”

16     Finally, under s 9(1), the Appellant must show that Pang is a person insured by the Policy.

17     This appeal and the Application hinge on whether the Appellant has satisfied this fourth requirement.[note: 10]

Clause 6

18     The persons who are covered under the Policy in respect of third-party risks are set out in Clause 6 of the certificate of insurance (“Clause 6”). According to this clause,[note: 11] the Policy covers –

Any person who is driving on the Policyholder’s order or with their permission or to whom the vehicle is hired.

[emphasis added]

Policy does not cover Pang

19     As stated earlier, the “Policyholder” in this case is Elitez. On a plain reading of Clause 6, the Policy covers three classes of drivers. The first are those who drive the Vehicle on Elitez’s order. The second are persons who drive the Vehicle with Elitez’s permission. These two classes are irrelevant in the present case – the Appellant has not adduced any evidence to prove that Pang comes within these two classes of drivers.

20     The third class of drivers covered by the Policy are “those to whom the vehicle is hired”. The Appellant submits that Pang falls within this class of drivers on the basis that he had hired the Vehicle from Alpha.[note: 12]

21     I am unable to accept the Appellant’s submission.

(a)     First, reading Clause 6 in context, the words “[a]ny person … to whom the vehicle is hired” (“the Words”) must refer to a person to whom the vehicle is hired from the Policyholder, i.e., Elitez.

(b)     Second, it is unreasonable to interpret the Words so widely – as the Appellant has done – to include any person who happens to hire the Vehicle. On the Appellant’s interpretation of the Words, if a thief were to steal the Vehicle and thereafter hires it to A, then the insurer would be liable if A later collides with B. Such an outcome is unfair and absurd.

(c)     Third, there are two clauses in the Policy that deal with the hiring of the Vehicle. It is clear from these clauses that to come within the third class of drivers, the driver must have hired the Vehicle from Elitez.

(d)     Endorsement V0132 of the Policy states the following:[note: 13]

It is hereby understood and agreed that notwithstanding anything to the contrary contained in this Policy, unless the Motor Vehicle is being driven by or is for the purpose of being driven by him in the charge of the Insured or a person in his employment, the policy shall be operative only whilst the Motor Vehicle is let on hire by the Insured to any person (herein after the “Hirer”) who –

(1)    shall have entered a Hire Contract with the Insured and

(2)    shall have satisfied the Insured that the Motor Vehicle will be driven only by a person duly licensed to drive.

…

[emphasis added]

(e)     As the “Insured”,[note: 14] Elitez is permitted under Endorsement V0132 to hire the Vehicle to another person such as Alpha. There is nothing in Endorsement V0132 to suggest that Alpha may further hire/sublet the Vehicle to another person such as Pang. There is also nothing in the Policy which provides that if Alpha sublets the Vehicle to Pang, Pang will be covered under the Policy.

(f)     In fact, Endorsement V0065 expressly stipulates that further hiring/sub-letting is not permissible under the Policy.[note: 15] This endorsement states –

The vehicle is strictly to be driven by the person to whom it is hired to. The hirer is not allowed to sublet the vehicle to another party, subletting is not covered. If vehicle is rented out to a Company client, the driver must be employee of the Company.

[emphasis added]

22     The Appellant submitted that I should only consider Clause 6 – and disregard Endorsements V0132 and V0065 – in deciding whether Pang is covered by the Policy. This is because these two endorsements do not appear on the face of the certificate of insurance.[note: 16] According to the Appellant –

… in Liberty’s policy jacket … under the sub-heading ‘Authorised Driver & Limitations as to Use’ the words below state ‘As described in the Certificate of Insurance’. No other document is referred and rightly so because the Policy Schedule does not define “authorized driver”. If Liberty intends Pang to be excluded for insurance coverage it must be made known in the COI. It must therefore follow that whether Pang is entitled to drive is based on the information written [wording] in the COI.[note: 17]

23     I do not find the Appellant’s submission to be compelling – for the simple reason that Endorsements V0132 and V0065 appear in the Schedule to the certificate of insurance. The word “schedule” ordinarily means an appendix to a document containing a list of items that affects the contents of the document.[note: 18] In other words, these two endorsements are part of the certificate. It also bears noting that s 4(9) – which stipulates the requirement for a certificate of insurance to be in the prescribed form – expressly recognises that a policy may be subject to certain “conditions”. In the case of the Policy, the particulars of these conditions are set out in the Schedule to the certificate[note: 19] as well as Policy Jacket.[note: 20]

24     Despite the above, the Appellant maintained his submission that Clause 6 is conclusive on whether Pang was an authorized driver – and since Endorsements V0065 and V0132 do not appear in Clause 6, they are irrelevant to the issue. According to the Appellant, the certificate is often relied by the Police to quickly determine whether a driver is authorized to drive a vehicle and hence covered by insurance.

25     I am unable to accept the Appellant’s submission. It bears emphasis that the Appellant’s case for the Application and appeal is based on s 9(1) of the MVA. There is nothing in s 9(1) to suggest that a determination of who an authorized driver is must be confined only to the clauses in a certificate of insurance. Although s 9(1) refers to a “certificate of insurance”, it is merely for the purpose of triggering the statutory duty under that provision.

26     In summary – based on a proper construction of the Policy terms, I find that the Appellant has failed to show that the Policy extended to Pang. Endorsement V0065 makes it abundantly clear that Alpha’s sub-letting of the Vehicle to Pang is not permitted. The Appellant’s submission that Pang is covered under the Policy ignores the unequivocal language in Endorsement V0065 and V0132. If the Appellant’s submission is accepted, this would render these two endorsements otiose.

Fact that Pang had not been charged under s 3(1) of the MVA

27     For completeness, I wish to address the Appellant’s submissions that (a) if Pang had driven the Vehicle without insurance coverage under the Policy, then he would have committed an offence under s 3(1) of the MVA, and (b) the fact that Pang was not charged for the offence raised a “rebuttable presumption” that his use of the Vehicle at the material time was covered by the Policy.[note: 21]

28     I am not persuaded by the Appellant’s submission.

(a)     Section 3(1) of the MVA states that it is an offence for any person to use a motor vehicle in Singapore without there being a policy of insurance in respect of third-party risks that complies with the requirements of the MVA.

(b)     The fact that Pang has not been charged for an offence under s 3(1) of the MVA is neither here nor there. There can be various reasons why Pang has not been charged, including the possibility that (i) the Police does not know whether he had driven the Vehicle without the requisite policy, or that (ii) the Police has assessed that Pang had committed an offence under s 3(1) but has chosen not to charge him because there were extenuating reasons. In my view, it is too speculative to assume that just because the Police did not charge Pang that the Policy had covered his use of the Vehicle at the time of the accident.

(c)     To the Appellant’s credit, he did not push his submission regarding the s 3(1) MVA point. It was candidly acknowledged that “[w]hilst the presumption that Pang did not breach s 3(1) MVTPA can be made, the issue of whether he is covered under the … Policy needs to be established”.[note: 22]

Appellant’s arguments why Policy should nonetheless still apply

29     The Appellant submitted that even if Pang’s use of the Vehicle is a breach of Endorsement V0065, this did not exempt the Respondent from its duty under s 9(1) of the MVA to satisfy the Final Judgment.[note: 23] This is because under s 9(1), an insurer’s duty to satisfy judgments against third-party risks remains “even though the insurer may be entitled to avoid or cancel … the policy” (“the Phrase”).[note: 24]

30     In my judgment, the Appellant’s submission is misconceived.

31     First, it is only where an accident involves a “person insured by the policy” that the statutory duty in s 9(1) and the Phrase kick in.

(a)     The case of Cosmic Insurance Corp Ltd v Ong Kah Hoe [1996] 1 SLR(R) 469 (“Cosmic Insurance”) makes it very clear that where the errant driver is not such a person, s 9(1) does not apply. In that case, the errant driver was under driving suspension when he caused an accident. The insurer’s liability to third parties covered “authorized driver[s]” provided that they were “permitted in accordance with the licensing or other laws or regulations to drive the motor vehicle”. The court held that the errant driver was not an authorised driver for the purposes of the policy and was thus not a “person insured by the policy”. On that basis, the court held that the insurer was not required by s 9(1) to satisfy the judgment entered against the errant driver: Cosmic Insurance at [10] – [14]. See also Overseas Assurance Corp Ltd v Lee Teik Teik [1979–1980] SLR(R) 223, where the High Court held (at [12]) that the insurer there did not have a statutory duty to pay the injured third party because it was not exposed to any liability given that the errant driver was not listed as an authorised driver under the policy.

(b)     Additionally, the Phrase refers to an insurer’s entitlement “to avoid or cancel” a policy. Where an insurer is not contractually obliged under a policy to satisfy a judgment in respect of third-party risks in the first place (e.g., because the errant driver does not fall within the class of persons insured under the policy), then the issue of the insurer “avoiding or cancelling” the policy does not arise at all – the insurer is simply not liable for the third-party risks to begin with. This point was made in Teo Rong Zhi Saimonn v Public Prosecutor [2013] 4 SLR 962 (“Teo Rong Zhi”). In that case, the appellant – who was the husband of the registered owner of a vehicle – had rented out the vehicle to a third party. The vehicle had an insurance policy which explicitly stated that it did not cover “[u]se for hire or reward”. The appellant contended that the insurance policy was in force despite the non-coverage of use for hire or reward because s 9(1) of the MVA obliged the insurer to satisfy judgments in favour of third parties against him. The High Court rejected this contention, holding that s 9(1) was not applicable as there was no insurance policy in force when the vehicle was used for hire or reward:

21    The statutory language of s 9(1) of the Act indicates that the subsection only applies to a “liability covered by the terms of the policy”. If a policy does not cover a certain mode of use, then any liability incurred during that mode of use would not be covered by the terms of the policy. The Policy in question here explicitly stated that it did not cover the use of the Vehicle for hire or reward.

22    Section 9(1) states that an insurer shall remain liable notwithstanding that it “may be entitled to avoid or cancel or may have avoided or cancelled the policy”. There is nothing to avoid or cancel if the policy does not cover certain prescribed modes of use in the first place. The reference to “avoid or cancel” refers to situations where the policy is voidable at the option of the insurers (for instance, on the ground of misrepresentation) or where the policy is liable to be terminated for repudiatory breach (for instance, where premiums are not paid). …

…

24 The net result was that s 9(1) of the Act did not apply. [The insurer] would not have been liable for third-party risks had the Vehicle been used for hire or reward. …

[text in square brackets and emphasis added]

32     Second, I am of the view that it is patently unfair to compel the Respondent to satisfy the Final Judgment when Pang is not a person insured by the Policy.

(a)     On a plain reading, neither s 9(1) nor s 4(1)(b) of the MVA requires insurers to cover against third-party risks every person who may happen to drive an insured vehicle. Insurers are required under the MVA to cover only persons named or specified in their policies: Cosmic Insurance at [9].

(b)     If the legislative intent is that an insurer must be liable so long as an accident involves a vehicle insured by its policy, regardless of whether the errant driver is also insured by its policy or not, then this would have been unequivocally stipulated in s 9(1), for instance, as follows:

9.—(1)  Subject to this section, if after a certificate of insurance has been issued under section 4(9) to the person by whom a policy has been effected, judgment in respect of any liability required to be covered by a policy under section 4(1)(b) (being a liability covered by the terms of the policy) is obtained against any vehicle insured by the policy, then, even though the insurer may be entitled to avoid or cancel or may have avoided or cancelled the policy, the following apply to the insurer: …

[emphasis added]

33     How then should the Phrase be interpreted and applied? In my view, the answer to this question is as follows:

(a)     Where an insured errant driver is involved in a traffic accident, the insurer is contractually obliged to satisfy a judgment obtained against the driver in respect of third-party risks if the terms and conditions of the policy are met.

(b)     Under general contractual principles, the insurer’s obligations under a policy may be avoided for instance (i) where the policy is voidable at the option of the insurer (for instance, on the ground of misrepresentation) or (ii) where the policy may be terminated for repudiatory breach (for instance, where premiums are not paid): Teo Rong Zhi at [22].

(c)     The terms of a policy may also entitle an insurer to cancel the policy in certain circumstances. An example of such a term can be found in the Respondent’s Insurance Policy Jacket at page 12:[note: 25]

6.     Cancellation

The Company may cancel this Policy by giving seven days’ notice by registered letter to the Insured at the Insured’s last known address and will return to the Insured the premium paid less the pro-rata portion thereof for the period the Policy has been in force which amount the Insured has become liable to pay or the Insured may cancel this Policy by giving seven days’ notice to the Company and shall be entitled to a return of the premium paid less the premium computed at the Company’s Short Period Rates for the period the Policy has been in force which amount the Insured has become liable to pay. Provided always that in each event no claim has arisen prior to the cancelation and the Insured shall return the current Certificate(s) of Insurance on or before the date of cancelation and pay the premium due promptly.

(d)     What s 9(1) does is to modify the above contractual rights of insurers to avoid or cancel their policies vis-à-vis their insured drivers. Specifically, insurers must satisfy judgements entered against their insured drivers in respect of third-party risks even though such drivers–

(i)     Have used the insured vehicle in a manner not covered by the policy. See e.g., Pacific & Orient Insurance Co Bhd v Kamacheh a/p Karuppen [2015] 4 MLJ 218 where the court held that pursuant to s 96(1) of the Malaysian Road Transport Act 1987 (which is similar to s 9(1) of the MVA), the insurer was obliged to pay a third party whose injuries was caused by an insured errant rider’s use of a motorcycle to commit an offence under s 391 of the Malaysian Penal Code. This was so even though such a use was not contemplated by the insurer.

(ii)     Have breached procedural requirements under the policy. See e.g., Abuseman bin Jamaluddin v Etiqa Takaful Bhd [2020] 6 MLJ 386 where the court found that the insured driver’s failure to notify the insurer of the accident, failure to notify the police of the accident, and entering negotiations with the third-party rider for the purpose of settlement without written consent from the insurer – in contravention of various terms in the policy – did not exempt the insurer from its statutory duty under s 96(1) of the Malaysian Road Transport Act 1987.

(e)     The modification of the insurers’ contractual rights to avoid or cancel their policies in relation to insured drivers is subject only to the exceptions in s 9(6)(c) and s 9(7) of the MVA.

34     To conclude, I find that the Phrase does not advance the Appellant’s case.

There are triable issues

35     The burden lies on the Appellant to show that he has a prima facie case for summary judgment. For the above reasons, I find that he has failed to discharge this burden.

36     For completeness, I wish to add that even if the Appellant has succeeded in showing a prima facie case for summary judgment, the Respondent has established that there is a fair or reasonable possibility that it has a real or bona fide defence.

(a)     The Respondent contended that the Appellant’s claim is vexatious, an abuse of process, and in breach of an implied forbearance to sue because the Appellant had previously withdrawn the same cause of action in DC 3233 based on certain understanding reached between the parties.

(b)     The Appellant’s disputed the Respondent’s case that there was such an understanding. Specifically, he submitted there was no discussion between the parties regarding the withdrawal of DC 3233, and thus there could not have been any implied term.[note: 26]

(c)     There is plainly a factual dispute between the parties regarding the circumstances in which DC 3233 was withdrawn. A determination of this factual dispute can affect the Appellant’s claim in the present suit. Given this, the matter ought to proceed to trial so that the case and evidence from both sides can be stress-tested before deciding on the merits of the Respondent’s defence.[note: 27]

Appellant’s alternative claim

37     I wish to briefly respond to the Respondent’s submission that there is a triable issue simply because the Appellant had pleaded his case in the alternative. I do not agree with this submission.

38     I fail to see why a court should decline to consider an application for summary judgment where the relevant facts are not contested, and the only controversy is purely on whether a plaintiff’s claim is supported by law.

(a)     It bears noting that trials are necessary only where there is conflicting evidence of facts between the parties – i.e., to resolve the conflict so that the law can be applied on the proved facts in a case.

(b)     Where the facts are not in dispute and the legal dispute can be dealt with summarily, there is no reason why a plaintiff should be denied the benefit of summary judgment simply because, he decides to plead the legal basis for his claim in the alternative.

39     In the present case, given that the Application is grounded solely on s 9 of the MVA, there is no need for me to consider his alternative claim under the MIB Agreement.

Conclusion

40     For the above reasons, I dismiss the Appellant’s appeal.
