1       What is the price of a dream? Consider a young man with a life ahead of him, and dreams to boot. He was (and still is) young. He has (perhaps complacently) never taken concrete steps to pursue those dreams – not at least, any that would produce credible documentation of the sort one would expect in a court of law. One horrific accident later, he finds himself with multiple fractures, massive scars, and one kidney short. His dreams are dashed. His life now is a series of compromises he makes with a new, dimmer reality. Undoubtedly, he has lost something intangible. But he cannot articulate that loss in the law’s language, much less provide evidence of the sort that the law may demand. How does the law provide a remedy, if at all? How would the loss of a dream be priced, if priced at all?

2       These would be the sort of questions implicated in an inquiry for a young plaintiff’s loss of earning capacity. The law in that regard, proved to be somewhat unclear – what is the legal test to be used when determining a young plaintiff’s loss of earning capacity? And beyond that, it was uncertain what was the correct quantification methodology to use in a claim for loss of earning capacity as well. Having had the opportunity to survey the authorities, I offer a modest rationalisation of the law and give my reasons below.

Facts

3       The Plaintiff, a polytechnic student at the time, was involved in a car accident on 28 September 2015.[note: 1] There were multiple, horrific injuries requiring extensive surgical intervention. After some considerable difficulty, the Plaintiff managed to complete his polytechnic education (graduating a year later than his peers), and found employment thereafter. He found his first job out of school (which paid $3,500 per month)[note: 2] too physically taxing due to his injuries[note: 3] and found another job instead.[note: 4] At the new job, he continues to experience difficulty handling some of the more physical aspects of his work due to his injuries. He is aged 26 years old at the time of assessment and is paid $2,400 per month at his present job.[note: 5]

Preliminary housekeeping matters

5       I should make certain matters clear from the outset.

6       First, this matter was transferred to the State Courts pursuant to HC/SUM 4456/2021. A Memorandum of Agreement was filed by the parties on 24 September 2023. There, pursuant to ss 19(2) and 23 of the State Courts Act 1970, the parties agreed to grant jurisdiction to the District Court to hear this matter notwithstanding that the amount claimed in the action exceeds the limits of the District Court’s jurisdiction.

7       Second, on the occasions that I found authorities relevant to my decision/reasoning, I did not necessarily adopt the absolute figures in the awards given. For example, the Courts have frequently referred to the Guidelines for the Assessment of General Damages in Personal Injury Cases (Academy Publishing, 2010) (“the Guidelines”) as a starting point when assessing the proper quantum of damages to be awarded. But the Guidelines are outdated, being more than a decade old. The same goes for certain case authorities, some dating back to the 1990s. The same injury sounding in $5,000 of damages in 1997 should not sound in $5,000 of damages today.

8       Accordingly, I was of the view that these authorities (and the awards of damages proposed therein) ought to be made more relevant by accounting for inflationary pressures: see Tan Siew Bin Ronnie v Chin Wee Keong [2008] 1 SLR(R) 178 at [18] and Quek Yen Fei (by his litigation representative Pang Choy Chun v Yeo Chye Huat and another appeal [2017] 2 SLR 229 (“Quek Yen Fei (CA)”) at [110]. I could not find any authority that offered a principled methodology for doing this. As such, I will be using the Monetary Authority of Singapore’s online inflation calculator (“MAS Inflation Calculator”)[note: 13] in determining what would be the appropriate uplift to give where necessary. When proposed to the parties, no objections were raised.[note: 14]

9       I turn then to the assessment proper.

Pain and Suffering

The applicable legal principles

10     In cases where multiple injuries are suffered, the Court of Appeal in Lua Bee Kiang (administrator of the estate of Chew Kong Seng, deceased) v Yeo Chee Siong [2019] 1 SLR 145 (“Lua Bee Kiang”) held that a two-stage analysis ought to be adopted (see Lua Bee Kiang at [12] – [18]):

(a)     First, the component method should be applied. The component method entails quantifying the loss arising from each item or head of damage separately. This is to ensure that the loss arising from each distinct item or head of damage is properly accounted for: Lua Bee Kiang at [14]. Reference may be made to the Guidelines (since they set out indicative assessment ranges for most types of personal injuries) but these are, of course, no more than guidelines and a “good starting point” for negotiation: Lua Bee Kiang at [15].

(b)     Second, after the component sums have been added up, the global method is applied. All injuries sustained by the claimant are considered holistically to determine whether the aggregate award is reasonable and neither excessive nor inadequate: Lua Bee Kiang at [16]. This exercise is guided by at least two considerations. One is to avoid overcompensation, with the Court taking into account any “overlapping” injuries that either (i) together resulted in pain that would not have been differentially felt by the claimant or (ii) together gave rise to only a single disability: Lua Bee Kiang at [17]. The other is to ensure that like cases are treated alike, by considering and making reference to the appropriate precedents: Lua Bee Kiang at [18].

11     I find the present case to be an appropriate occasion to apply a small upward adjustment at the second stage (where the global method is applied), and set out my analysis in full below.

Kidney injuries

12     I assess and award $87,000 for the kidney injuries. This is a single award for both the retroperitoneal haematoma and the shattered kidney, as well as the medical complications (such as rhabdomyolysis) arising from the injuries.

Whether the retroperitoneal haematoma ought to be considered together with the other kidney-related injuries

13     There was little dispute between parties that the shattered right kidney and the rhabdomyolysis were to be considered together and a single award should be assessed for them.[note: 15] The real question is whether the “large right retroperitoneal haematoma”[note: 16] ought to be considered together with these injuries as well.[note: 17] I take the view that all these should be considered together, applying the principles stated in Seah Yit Chen v Singapore Bus Service (1978) Ltd and others [1990] 1 SLR(R) 490 at [5]: “[…] when the injuries sustained are related to the same part or function of the body, there should only be one award of damages, without any risk of overlapping”.

14     A retroperitoneal haematoma refers to a pool of (usually) clotted blood that forms in the retroperitoneum (ie behind the membrane that lines the abdominal wall, and which covers the organs in the abdomen). The retroperitoneal space houses organs like the kidney and the ureter, and it is unsurprising to see such devastating trauma to the kidney be accompanied by injuries to the areas surrounding the kidney. Indeed, Dr Raj Kumar Menon (“Dr Menon”)’s report referred to the haematoma in the same breath as the kidney injuries, describing the retroperitoneal haematoma as being part of the “severe injuries to his liver and right kidney and active bleeding and clot formation”.[note: 18] So understood, the haematoma is intrinsically tied to the other kidney-related injuries. It relates to the same part of the body and an additional, separate award may well risk overcompensation.

15     As against this, the Plaintiff’s counsel, Mr Caleb Tan (“Mr Tan”), argued that the treatment for the haematoma and the other kidney related injuries were different. The kidney-related injuries required dialysis. The haematoma required laparotomies. They should be treated differently, and a separate award should be given for each, so went the argument. But Mr Tan forgets that all these procedures (separate as they may be), were in relation to injuries to the same part of the body. They relate to the kidney, the loss of function in the kidney, and the associated trauma to the areas surrounding the kidney. These injuries may have been treated with different procedures (as medical necessity required), but were still fundamentally linked. In any case, the Defendant’s counsel, Mr Narayanan Ramasamy (“Mr Ramasamy”), made the point that the laparotomy was not for the haematoma alone. A laparotomy is a surgical incision to reach the internal organs and would conceivably have been part of the broader surgical procedure to treat (amongst other things) the kidney too. This means that there was no separate treatment procedure for the haematoma alone. Absent that factual premise, Mr Tan’s argument simply cannot stand.

16     I am not persuaded by any of Mr Tan’s authorities either.

(a)      Affrin Bin Omar v Goh Beng Kee and another [1994] SGHC 15 was not a case about pain and suffering at all. It was an appeal dealing with the awards for loss of pre-trial earnings and loss of future earning capacity. There was simply nothing in the judgment or its reasoning that I can credibly rely on to determine (a) whether the haematoma ought to be considered together with the other kidney related injuries or (b) what an appropriate award for the haematoma ought to be if it were indeed to be considered separately. Indeed, a casual perusal of the case shows that there was no separate award given for any retroperitoneal haematoma.[note: 19] If anything, it appears to have been grouped under more general heads of injuries (either under “rupture of urethra” or a more broadly framed “internal injuries”). In the circumstances, it was misleading to suggest that this was authority for a separate award for retroperitoneal haematoma sounding in $10,000 of damages.

(b)      Ng Ng Mui v Ibrahim Bin Mohamad [1991] SGHC 196 (“Ng Ng Mui”) is unfit for reliance. Respectfully, there was no meaningful reasoning given for awarding $5,000 for the retroperitoneal haematoma. The bulk of the judgment related to establishing liability (see [1] – [13]). It is difficult to use such an authority when I cannot scrutinise the reasoning for the award given. Even if I were to take Ng Ng Mui and its facts at face value, it appears to be distinguishable from the present case. In Ng Ng Mui, there were no other injuries in the abdominal area. Chan Sek Keong J (as he then was) had no reason to consider whether the retroperitoneal haematoma had to be considered together with some other ostensibly related injury. The haematoma there was indubitably a separate injury to be considered on its own terms, and for which a separate award was (rightly) awarded. That being the case, Ng Ng Mui presented a different set of facts which necessitated a different legal conclusion – a conclusion that the present case does not warrant.

For these reasons, I conclude that the retroperitoneal injury had to be considered together with the other kidney-related injuries.

The proper quantum to be awarded for the kidney injuries

17     The starting point is the Guidelines. Both parties appear to agree that the $40,000 - $80,000 range (for the loss of a single kidney) applies here. I find that $70,000 (in the $40,000 - $80,000 range) would be fair and award $87,000 after adjusting for inflation.

18     This group of kidney injuries commands damages in the higher end of the $40,000 - $80,000 range because of the following reasons:

(a)     There are multiple injuries involved. The plaintiff suffered a “shattered right kidney”[note: 20] and a large retroperitoneal hematoma.[note: 21] I note that although the joint opening statement made reference to a separate “right renal laceration” supposedly described in Dr Menon’s report,[note: 22] that term was nowhere to be found in the report. The closest reference to a kidney-related laceration of any sort was found in Dr Lim Chiao Yuen’s report dated 2 January 2018 and Dr Leanne Leong’s report dated 10 October 2019, but those reports only made mention of a “right kidney laceration” rather than a “renal” one.[note: 23] I take this to mean that references to the “shattered right kidney” and any “right kidney laceration” were one and the same, and that there was no separate “right renal laceration”.

(b)     The injuries were serious, requiring extensive treatment. There was massive blood loss, necessitating a massive transfusion protocol,[note: 24] with the injuries ultimately resulting in the total removal of one kidney (nephrectomy). Needless to say, the nephrectomy was an incredibly invasive surgical operation, and the follow-up procedures (for abdominal washout and change of vacuum dressings) were invasive too, requiring “repeat laparotomies”.[note: 25]

(c)     The recovery was complicated. Post-operatively, he developed rhabdomyolysis (rapid muscle breakdown),[note: 26] and suffered from acute renal failure. This required “3 sessions of haemodialysis because of worsening kidney function”.[note: 27]

19     Mr Ramasamy proposed $60,000 for the kidney injuries (including the retroperitoneal haematoma), and justified it with two reasons. I do not accept either of them:

(a)     True, the injuries have thankfully stabilised by now.[note: 28] But the plaintiff will require “lifelong follow-up for his single functioning kidney given that he is at higher risk of developing end-stage kidney disease”.[note: 29] He will be inconvenienced not only by the hassle of such follow-up reviews/check-ups, but also the adjustments that he has to make to his lifestyle “to prevent chronic kidney disease”.[note: 30]

(b)     True also, is that the lifelong follow-up has in some sense been accounted for by the future medical expenses,[note: 31] which the Defendant has already agreed to foot.[note: 32] But that speaks only to the monetary cost of the follow-up. It makes no provision for the time, effort and psychological weight that comes with arranging appointments and any self-monitoring the Plaintiff will have to do. Mr Ramasamy’s proposal does not make adequate provision for that intangible cost – a psychological dimension perfectly open for the Court to consider in evaluating pain and suffering: Au Yeong Wing Loong v Chew Hai Ban [1993] 2 SLR(R) 290 at [11].

20     I should add that I have also considered but ultimately reject two other arguments made by the Defendant.

(a)     The first was the Defendant’s comparison[note: 33] between the present case and Woon Yok Moy v Ang Lay Tin (Unreported; DC Suit No 559 of 2004 (“Woon Yok Moy”), cited in Carrie Chan et al, Practitioner’s Library – Assessment of Damages: Personal Injuries and Fatal Accidents (Lexis Nexis, 3 Ed, 2016) (“Practitioner’s Library”) at p 507). On that authority, the Defendant invited me to award a similar quantum of damages. I decline that invitation. There was a marked difference in severity between the injuries in Woon Yok Moy and the present case. Just looking at the kidney-related injuries, Woon Yok May only involved a retroperitoneal haematoma (and no information was given about the size or severity of the haematoma in the Practitioner’s Library). The present case involves a large retroperitoneal haematoma, and a shattered kidney that had to be removed. It is just not clear whether Woon Yok Moy involved sufficiently analogous facts.

(b)     The Defendant’s second argument concerned the Plaintiff’s efforts to go for yearly review of the kidney injuries. The Plaintiff did not diligently go for medically mandated reviews for his kidney issues.[note: 34] The suggested inference: the kidney injuries were not so severe, and there was no serious residual pain/suffering resulting from thereon. I decline to draw that inference. There are any number of reasons why someone may not vigorously follow-up with the hospital, even for very serious injuries. One reason might be difficulty in securing an appointment (which the Plaintiff mentioned).[note: 35] One might be fear of spooking potential employers (which the Plaintiff mentioned).[note: 36] And yet another might be financial concerns about constantly seeking specialist opinions (which, again, the Plaintiff mentioned too).[note: 37] Each of these reasons is, in itself, perfectly believable – especially since the Plaintiff was young (ie concerned about first impressions for future employers), not particularly wealthy (ie sensitive to the price of follow-ups, even for matters as important as his health), and was trying to secure follow-ups during the COVID-19 pandemic (ie when hospitals were already stretched). So understood, it seems that there was a complex cocktail of reasons why the Plaintiff did not pursue medical reviews with more diligence. I see no reason to go behind or beyond the Plaintiff’s explanation; they are good enough for me.

21     All in, $70,000 would be a fair estimation of the damages warranted by the kidney injuries. I apply a necessary uplift to account for any inflationary pressures since the time the Guidelines were published, having reference to the MAS inflation calculator. $70,000 in 2010 would be approximately $87,000 in 2023. That is my award of the kidney injuries.

Liver Lacerations

22     I award $20,000 for the liver lacerations. For this injury, the Guidelines are completely unhelpful. None of the descriptions stated at page 42 are applicable:

E. LIVER

 

(i) Severe

Total failure of the liver necessitating a liver transplant. However, the high end of the range of award at $150,000 is appropriate if the injured person underwent a lot of pain and suffering before the subsequent liver transplant, eg, hallucinations, jaundice, blood poisoning, fever, etc, as a result of the liver failure, as seen in the case of Andrea Heidi de Cruz (TV Media Pte Ltd v De Cruz Andrea Heidi [2004] 3 SLR(R) 543). A ruptured liver that requires a liver transplant will fall into this category as well.

In the region of $150,000

(ii) Moderate to mild

Cases include those of lacerations, haematoma, contusions. Full recovery is achieved within a few weeks with no lasting effects.

$2,000 - $3,500



23     The trouble is that the Guidelines offers only two choices: the plainly inadequate, and the ludicrously excessive. This is obviously not a “moderate to mild” type of liver injury. Yes, there were liver lacerations. Yes, lacerations are ordinarily addressed under the “moderate to mild” band of liver injuries. But this was not an ordinary type of liver laceration. For one, it was “complex… with active bleeding”.[note: 38] And at least one report – the ordinary medical report by the doctor who attended to the plaintiff when he first presented at the emergency ward – suggests that there were “multiple liver lacerations”.[note: 39] So profuse was the bleeding that the plaintiff required “hemostasis of the liver lacerations… with abdominal packing and temporary closure”[note: 40] and an “embolization of the right posterior sectoral artery of the liver”.[note: 41] At the same time, the liver injury cannot be described as a “severe” one either. It was not, to use the language of the Guidelines, a “[total] failure of the liver necessitating a liver transplant” and the present case was plainly distinguishable from the horrific liver failure that befell the claimant in TV Media Pte Ltd v De Cruz Andrea Heidi [2004] 3 SLR(R) 543 (“Andrea De Cruz”).

24     The authorities cited by the parties are not helpful either. Mr Ramasamy relied on Woon Yok Moy. Accepting that the liver lacerations here were more severe than the liver contusion in Woon Yok Moy (for which $6,000 was awarded), he proposed $10,000 as a fair award. Mr Tan relied on Andrea De Cruz. Accepting that the liver lacerations here were not as severe as the liver damage in that case, Mr Tan proposed $100,000 – about “half”[note: 42] of what the Andrea De Cruz award would be valued at by today’s standards. Neither Mr Ramasamy’s nor Mr Tan’s proposals are persuasive. $10,000 appears to be too low a sum. The present case involved a complex liver laceration with serious blood loss resulting. A mere $4,000 uplift from Woon Yok Moy’s $6,000 award would be too little, especially considering that Woon Yok Moy involved nothing more than a liver contusion which did not require extensive/separate treatment (see Practitioner’s Library at p 507). But equally, $100,000 seems excessive. No explanation was given for why “half” of the Andrea De Cruz award would be fair. This appears to be a largely impressionistic submission on Mr Tan’s part. It fails to account for the fact that the liver had largely recovered. Dr Glenn Bonney (“Dr Bonney”), the only hepatobiliary surgeon to tender a specialist report, stated that the plaintiff “should [not] have any ill effect to his liver as a result of this injury”, observing that the plaintiff had “normal liver function on blood tests” and a “normal ultrasound scan doppler of this liver”.[note: 43] The damages for a liver with no residual disabilities simply cannot be compared to that awarded for a total failure of the liver, much less be estimated at half of the latter.

25     The authorities being unsatisfactory (and I should add that my own consultation of the Practitioner’s Library proved to be fruitless), and the submissions being unpersuasive, I take a practical approach to determining the appropriate damage for the liver lacerations. The award has to be between $10,000 and $100,000 (at least, based on my reasoning above at [24]). The award also has to be lower than $87,000, since the liver lacerations were objectively less severe than the kidney injuries. All in, given the severity of the injuries, the invasiveness of the surgical procedures, but also the full recovery of the liver and the absence of any residual disabilities, I find that $20,000 would be a fair sum to award. I should add that there is no need to account for inflation in this award, as this is not an award rationalised from old precedents/authorities, but deduced from the facts of this case itself.

Diaphragmatic injury on the right hemi-diaphragm and right vocal cord paresis

26     Dr Kumar identified a diaphragmatic injury on the right hemi-diaphragm in his report.[note: 44] Both parties agreed that $10,000 was a fair sum to award for this injury.[note: 45] I accept this joint proposal. However, I am of the opinion that the diaphragmatic injury has to be considered together with the vocal cord paresis/palsy as well. Accordingly, I grant a small $2,500 uplift as well.

27     The vocal cord paresis/palsy arose because the plaintiff was extubated during the surgeries. This extubating, in turn, was necessitated because of the diaphragmatic injury (“[the plaintiff] had a right chest tube inserted after diaphragmatic injury was noted”).[note: 46] If so, the vocal cord paresis/palsy was not a separate injury caused by the accident, but an unfortunate side-effect of the treatment for injuries caused by the accident.

28     Of course, this does not mean that the defendant is any less responsible for the vocal cord paresis/palsy, and Mr Ramasamy acknowledged as much during his oral submissions:[note: 47]

Ct:

[Mr Ramasamy] just to clarify your position, you’re not opposed to addressing or giving something for these “injuries” but it’s just a matter of where this is housed under, or where it’s addressed.

DC:

That’s right.



Mr Ramasamy’s (sensible) acknowledgment was a complete answer to Mr Tan’s submissions. Mr Tan characterised the Defendant’s position as an attempt to challenge the causation of the vocal cord paresis/palsy.[note: 48] This was a mistaken characterisation of the Defendant’s case. The Defendant fully accepted that she caused the accident and the resulting injuries. She fully accepted that the treatment was necessitated by the resulting injuries. The Defendant was simply making the point that as far as the chain of effect goes, the vocal cord paresis/palsy was one step removed from the accident, being caused by the treatment (ie the extubating), rather than the accident itself. The upshot was that the paresis/palsy had to be considered together with the particular injury that necessitated the extubating. I agree.

29     So understood, the vocal cord paresis/palsy, should not be addressed as a separate injury in its own right, and the proper venue for its consideration ought to be the injury to which it is linked. As explained earlier, the vocal cord paresis/palsy is linked to the diaphragmatic injury and ought to be considered with the same.

30     The question then turns to quantum – what would be fair compensation for the vocal cord paresis/palsy? Mr Ramasamy helpfully referred me to page 13 of the Guidelines.[note: 49] Nerve palsies of the face sound in roughly $7,000 - $15,000 of damages. The present case, being limited to the throat should be less than that. Beyond that, the vocal cord paresis/palsy was temporary and resolved fairly quickly with speech therapy.[note: 50] Within the span of about a month (being the period between the Plaintiff’s first referral to the otolaryngological department and his last review), his “voice returned to normal, and his vocal cord paresis […] also resolved”.[note: 51] In other words, the condition was transient; the recovery, speedy; and the treatment, conservative. The paresis/palsy was relatively mild.

31     In the circumstances, and taking practical reference from the Guidelines, I took the view that $2,000 would be fair compensation for this relatively temporary condition. Adjusting for inflation, this would be $2,500 by today’s standards. I note that Mr Ramasamy was prepared to offer $5,000[note: 52] but that seemed to be too much. $5,000 did not properly account for the fact that the paresis/palsy was, besides being confined to the throat, largely temporary and conservatively treated.[note: 53] All in, I assess the diaphragmatic injuries and the associated vocal cord paresis/palsy at $12,500.

Intestinal obstruction

32     Much of the same analysis in [27] – [29] applies here as well. The intestinal obstruction was “secondary to post-operative adhesions from his emergency trauma surgeries”,[note: 54] and should be rightfully considered together with the other abdominal injuries (the kidney, liver, and diaphragmatic injuries I have earlier discussed).

33     Evidently, this is where the component method (see [10(a)]) reaches the end of its usefulness. The intestinal obstruction could not be considered a separate “injury” in its own right, since it really ought to be considered in tandem with all the abdominal injuries that necessitated the trauma surgeries, which in turn were responsible for the intestinal obstruction. And equally, the intestinal obstruction could not be excluded altogether since it was a legitimate and credible source of pain and suffering for the plaintiff. Indeed, he had to be re-admitted to treat the intestinal obstruction, and spent three days in the wards before being discharged.[note: 55]

34     In my view, the fairest way to account for the intestinal obstruction is to address it at stage two of the analysis, where all injuries sustained by the claimant are considered holistically to determine whether the aggregate award is reasonable and neither excessive nor inadequate: Lua Bee Kiang at [16]. This is to be tied up together with other loose ends such as the potential overlaps in pain and suffering arising from the treatment of the various injuries (see [61] below).

Right pleural effusion

35     I award $5,000 for the right pleural effusion.

36     The precise causal mechanism of the pleural effusion was unclear. Dr Menon merely stated this was either “secondary to [the plaintiff’s] right sided injuries or secondary to a pneumonia”. What was clear, and what Mr Ramasamy accepted at the hearing, was that “without the accident, [the plaintiff] wouldn’t have this condition.”[note: 56] The causal mechanism being unclear, I cannot accept Mr Ramasamy’s proposal to have this considered together with the other abdominal injuries à la the intestinal obstruction and the vocal cord paresis/palsy. It has to be considered on its own terms.

37     The proper award ought to be $5,000 as this was a slightly more severe pleural effusion than that seen in Zhen Zhaoyong v Choon Seng Construction Pte Ltd & Anor (Unreported; DC Suit No 1178 of 2010, cited in the Practitioner’s Library at p 510). The pleural effusion (and abdominal pain) there was treated conservatively. The present case required a pleural tap (to drain the effusion), and this was installed for five days.[note: 57] The Plaintiff here also suffered from “persistent fevers from admission for which no obvious source was found”, and which required “antibiotic therapy”.[note: 58] Thankfully, the fevers and effusion resolved satisfactorily after five days. All in, adjusting for the relative severity of the pleural effusion, the slightly more involved treatment procedure, and any necessary uplift to account for inflation, I award $5,000 for this injury.

Right undisplaced radial fracture and undisplaced ulnar styloid fracture

38     I assess damages for these fractures, which the parties agreed had to be considered together, at $17,500.

39     The starting point again, is the Guidelines. There are four bands of severity listed in the Guidelines and my view was that these fractures should rightly be described as “moderate fractures of the distal radius [and] ulna”:

(a)     These were not “severe and/or compound fractures of the distal radius and ulna in which extensive surgery is required” (Guidelines at p 36F(a)). There were some residual disabilities in the form of reduced range of motions in the wrist. But none of this resulted in a “great loss of function” (Guidelines at p 36F(a)). The plaintiff was, by and large, “managing daily activities well”[note: 59] and there was simply no suggestion that he had lost the use of the wrist in any serious way. Beyond that, there were no complications during the recovery process,[note: 60] “such as malunion and infection” or any “high risk of developing osteoarthritis” either (see Guidelines at p 36F(a)). Mr Tan’s justification for classifying the fractures in the highest band of severity under the Guidelines was that this involved multiple fractures. Only the highest band in the Guidelines could supposedly account for the number of fractures. This is not true. There were other bands of severity such as those at Guidelines pages 37F(c) or 37F(d) that were intended to address multiple (albeit less severe) fractures. The Guidelines at page 37F(c), for example, covers “moderate fractures of the distal radius and/or ulna” (emphasis mine).

(b)     These were not “moderately severe fractures of the distal radius or ulna” either. Neither of the fractures resulted in “significant permanent disability and considerable pain” (see Guidelines at p36F(b)). At best, there was some “discomfort arising from the prominent right forearm plate”.[note: 61] There was some “ache in fracture sites” too.[note: 62] These could not be described as “considerable pain”. And neither could the permanent disabilities (a reduction in the right wrist’s range of motion) be said to be significant. Save for the pronation of the wrist, the other measures of a writ’s range of motion (such as dorsiflexion, palmar-flexion, ulnar deviation, radial deviation, and supination) did not have significant issues. How exactly was “significance” of the reduction in range of motion medically measured? Both Dr Ng Yau Hong (“Dr Ng”) and Dr James Lee Chong Hwa (“Dr Lee”) agreed that one viable way to assess the wrist’s range of motion was to compare the ranges of motion between the injured wrist and its non-injured counterpart.[note: 63] When the difference in ranges of motion between the two are about “10 degrees or so”, the injured wrist’s reduction in range of motion could be said to be medically significant.[note: 64] As of 30 July 2020 (Dr Lee’s last examination of the plaintiff), the plaintiff’s wrist was only experiencing medically significant restrictions in pronating.

That said, I took the view that taken in their totality, the difficulties with wrist movement were not so high as to warrant placing the fractures in the “moderately severe” category. Indeed, when the Guidelines speaks of “significant” permanent disability in relation to wrist injuries, it appears to be distinguishing the various bands of severity by how much functionality of the wrist has been compromised. Hence, “severe” injuries are described as involving “great loss of function”; “moderately severe” injuries, as “considerable loss of function”; and “moderate” injuries as those where the plaintiff is “able to regain substantial function of his wrist”. There being only “some functional limitation” here,[note: 65] these fractures could not be classified as “moderately severe” injuries per the Guidelines.

(c)     Continuing on the same reasoning as above, it seems to me that these were “moderate fractures of the distal radius and ulna”, in that the plaintiff “regained substantial function of his wrist” (see Guidelines at p36F(c)), even as he continued to experience discomfort[note: 66] and aches[note: 67] in his right arm, with “some functional limitation” expected.[note: 68]

40     I decline the Plaintiff’s invitation for me to rely on Balbir Singh s/o Hira Singh & Anor v Chuang Woon Chin Olivia (Unreported; DC Suit No 3936 of 1996 (“Balbir”), cited in the Practitioner’s Library at p 422).[note: 69] True, there was a fracture of the left radius and ulna in that case as well. True also, is that the fractures there were “surgically fixed with metal plate”, similar to the treatment given in the present case. Moreover, the plaintiff in Balbir was “unlikely to suffer permanent disability or osteoarthritis”, suggesting that Balbir was a case with far less severe injuries than the present case. And yet, the plaintiff in Balbir was awarded some $18,000 for these upper limb fractures. To me, this seemed odd and disproportionate. $18,000 – $18,000 by 1996 standards no less – is an incredible amount of money and at odds with the ordinary range of damages described in the Guidelines. Balbir’s award therefore has to be rationalised in one of two ways: either (a) the Practitioner’s Library did not contain sufficient details about the case – perhaps the fractures were excruciating and horrific, necessitating a highly invasive (possibly dangerous) surgery which warranted higher damages; or (b) the decision was simply an outlier that did not follow the general trends compiled in the Guidelines. If it is the former, I am simply unable to use Balbir meaningfully since the facts of that case are not before me. If it is the latter, I must politely decline to rely on a statistical anomaly that I am not bound to follow. Either way, Balbir would be unfit for reliance.

41     The question then turns to where on the range ($6,000 - $15,000) the Plaintiff’s injuries ought to be placed. In my view, the Plaintiff’s injuries fell on the high side of the range:

(a)     The treatment procedure was fairly involved, involving two separate procedures – a manipulation and reduction of the fractures on 14 October 2015, and an open reduction and internal fixation of the fractures on 15 October 2015.[note: 70] Beyond that, occupational therapy was required for the Plaintiff’s hand as well.[note: 71]

(b)     The “restrictions in his physical activity”[note: 72] were “permanent”[note: 73] disabilities. There is little prospect of the wrist injuries getting better, and little chance of the pronation improving either – not at least of their own accord.[note: 74] Put another way, the new state of equilibrium for the Plaintiff is a partial range of motion in his right wrist.

(c)     The pain, while described somewhat mildly as “discomfort” and “aching”, was consistently experienced by the Plaintiff. He consistently reported pain associated with his right wrist/forearm. This was so on the two occasions that the Plaintiff was examined by Dr Lee (10 September 2018 and 30 July 2020),[note: 75] and also the case during his National University Hospital follow up on 14 October 2019.[note: 76] Indeed, Dr Ng went so far as to say that the “main pain was in the forearm”.[note: 77] I have no reason to believe that the Plaintiff would inflate or exaggerate his injuries, self-reported as they may be, and I am not prepared to draw any adverse inferences about the Plaintiff’s credibility, at least from his demeanour at the hearing.

42     Taking the above into consideration, I assess damages at $14,000 (on the scale stated in the Guidelines) and apply the appropriate uplift with reference to the MAS Inflation Calculator. This brings the figure to $17,500.

43     I should add that much of the pain associated with these injuries appear to be connected to the implants put in place during surgery.[note: 78] The implants are there because the Plaintiff has opted to keep the implants. There may have been an argument that the Defendant shouldn’t be made to compensate the Plaintiff for pain which he has brought on himself. But that would, in my view, depend on the reasonableness of the Plaintiff’s election. I note that Dr Lee was specifically invited to give an opinion on the necessity and trade-offs associated with removing the implants. His conclusion was that although leaving the implants in situ would present risks/complications in the future, there was “no absolute need to remove the implants”.[note: 79] This report was not seriously challenged at the hearing, and Dr Lee’s explanations appear quite cogent. As such, I do not begrudge the Plaintiff for his decision to keep the implants and my award does not make any deductions for the same.

Right undisplaced spiral fracture of the mid shaft of the tibia with puncture wound over the mid-shin

44     I assess damages for these injuries, which the parties agreed to have considered together, at $15,000.

45     The relevant band in the Guidelines ought to be that stated on page 49(A)(c)(i). This is for “moderate to minor leg injuries”, and was the most suitable band because of two reasons:

(a)     First, this injury was not a “severe” leg injury of the sort described at the Guidelines at pp 48A(b) – 49A(b). Such injuries would usually involve some aggravating factors like:

(i)       the risk of degenerative changes (eg osteoarthritis: Guidelines at p 49A(b)(ii)),

(ii)       a more serious permanent disability (eg “permanent instability, laxity of the ligaments”: Guidelines at p 49A(b)(iii));

(iii)       extensive surgery, possibly with irreparable consequences (eg gross shortening or muscle wasting: Guidelines at p 49A(b)(i) or extensive scarring: Guidelines at p 49A(b)(iii)); and/or

(iv)       medical complications arising during recovery (eg malunion of the bones: Guidelines at p 49A(b)(iv)).

Ultimately, the determination of whether an injury is “severe” or not is fact sensitive. The presence or absence of any of these aggravating factors described above is not dispositive. In the present instance, the surgery was a fairly standard procedure (“right leg wound debridement and intramedullary nailing of the tibia”),[note: 80] with no complications (“[the plaintiff’s] wounds healed well”).[note: 81] The permanent disabilities, such as the difficulties with “prolonged standing, walking and climbing of stairs”,[note: 82] were not of the severity described under the Guidelines at pp 48A(b) – 49A(b) either. Under these circumstances, I am not prepared to classify this as even the mildest variant of a “severe” injury (see Guidelines at p 49A(b)(iv)).

(b)     Second, among the various sub-bands stated under the “moderate to minor leg injuries” category, the most appropriate one is that stated at p 49(A)(c)(i) of the Guidelines. The present case obviously involved “some residual disabilities” (see Guidelines at p 49(A)(c)(i)) in that the Plaintiff experienced pains after prolonged or vigorous activities.[note: 83] The present case is not a “simple fracture” (see Guidelines at p 50A(c)(ii)) - it was a spiral fracture which required surgical intervention. And of course the present case is not just a scrape or hairline fracture, of the sort contemplated under the Guidelines at p 50A(c)(iii) either.

46     The question then turns to quantum – where on the range of $10,000 - $15,000 would damages for these injuries lie? $12,000 seems appropriate.

47     In my view, the residual disabilities exist but are not necessarily perceptible at all times. They appear to only arise during periods of exertion (such as prolonged walking or intensive exercise).[note: 84] Otherwise, the Plaintiff was, as early as 22 February 2018, able to manage daily activities well.[note: 85] He was able to “ambulate full weight bearing without any walking aid” by 2 November 2015, latest.[note: 86] He was even able to run,[note: 87] even if he could not do this with heavy intensity (“Yes, I did run and lift weights. But it’s not general or full. Weights could be 100kg and running, but not full”).[note: 88] This crucial fact, to my mind, excluded the higher range of this $10,000 - $15,000 band.

48     At the same time, the award has to be higher than $10,000, on the basis that this was a more severe group of injuries than those seen in Woon Hok Fah v Zheng Xiaohua and another [2020] SGDC 55 (“Woon Hok Fah”). There, damages for a right proximal tibia fracture with “residual numbing pain” amounting to “4% permanent disability” were assessed at $10,000 (see Woon Hok Fah at [48] – [53]). The present case, however, arguably involves greater permanent disabilities. The plaintiff’s decrease in range of motion, at least in relation to the plantar flexion of the ankle, was medically significant.[note: 89] The plaintiff can only do “short runs” and “play Futsal for short periods”.[note: 90] And the fact that he cannot stand or walk for prolonged periods[note: 91] without experiencing discomfort, means that activities at a certain level of intensity or duration are no longer an option for him. Moreover, the present case also involved a puncture wound near the shin.

49     All in, I find $12,000 to be a fair amount of compensation. Applying the usual adjustment for inflation (with reference to the MAS Inflation Calculator), this brings me to $15,000.

Undisplaced fracture of the right traverse of the L1 vertebra

50     I award $12,000 for this injury. As is the case with the liver lacerations, the Guidelines are not particularly helpful here. Generally, where back injuries are concerned, the Guidelines categorises different bands of severity based on the nature of the injury. Thus, compression fractures of the lumbar spine sound in $15,000 - $25,000 (see Guidelines at p 23B(b)(i)) while back strains sound in $10,000 - $17,000 (see Guidelines at p 23B(b)(ii)). However, looking at purely the nature of the injury (fracture, sprain or otherwise) may be misleading.

51     Take the present case. There was undoubtedly a fracture. But the $15,000 - $25,000 range is simply inappropriate. That range is for a certain kind of fracture. It is the sort that is accompanied by “significant disabilities” and “constant back pain” (see Guidelines at p 23B(b)(i)). The present case, thankfully, was a fairly mild fracture. It was an undisplaced fracture. There was no mention of any follow-up in any other medical report. Indeed, Dr Lee confirmed that “the fracture had healed without intervention”.[note: 92] And of course, there was no record of any complaints about back pain or back aches either.[note: 93] The better range to apply, notwithstanding that this was a fracture, is the $10,000 - $17,000 range typically used for back strains.

52     I am of the view that $10,000 on that range would be fair. I award the lowest end of this range in view of the lack of any residual disabilities, the conservative treatment involved, and the nature/severity of the injury. Taking inflation into account, this would come up to $12,000. I should add that this award is consistent with Bellal Hossain Lakiyet Ullah v ST Vehicle and Equipment Pte Ltd (Unreported; DC Suit No 1659 of 2003 (“Bellal Hossain”), cited in the Practitioner’s Library at p 800). That case involved two conservatively managed transverse fractures of the vertebrae. $10,000 was awarded (approximately $14,300 by today’s standards). The $12,000 award made here, for a single transverse fracture, is accordingly lower than that given in Bellal Hossain.

Lacerations

53     I award $2,000 for the lacerations. I was not presented with any details about the lacerations and was unable to make an assessment about the nature, extent and severity of the lacerations save that they were on the right forearm and the left shin. In any case, I accept Mr Ramasamy’s submissions that there is a certain degree of overlap between these lacerations and the underlying injuries, such that a lower sum (the range stated in the Guidelines at p 59D(b) is $1,500 - $3,000) would be appropriate. Accounting for inflation and making the necessary uplift, $2,000 sufficed.

Post-traumatic stress disorder (“PTSD”)

54     I award $7,500 for the post-traumatic stress disorder (“PTSD”). The starting point is that there was a formal diagnosis of PTSD.[note: 94] The real question is how severe the PTSD was. In my view, the following three factors suggest to me that the Plaintiff’s PTSD was “moderate” (see Guidelines at p 28B(c)) and ought to sound in $7,500 of damages:

(a)     The persistence of the Plaintiff’s symptoms – The Plaintiff continued to report “intrusive symptoms such as distressing memories of the accident especially when he had sleep difficulties”,[note: 95] some four years after the accident. Indeed, at the psychiatric review on 18 November 2019, Dr Rathi Mahendran (“Dr Rathi”) reported that there were “still some marked alterations in arousal and reactivity in terms of irritability and sleep disturbance”[note: 96] (emphasis mine).

(b)     The severity of the Plaintiff’s symptoms – the medical report dated 18 November 2019 presented a mixed picture. On one hand, there was “evidence of impairment of functioning in social domains”.[note: 97] The Plaintiff was “quiet and this was different from his baseline”[note: 98] – a far cry from the “very active […] and very social”[note: 99] person he used to be. And beyond that, there were “negative alterations of mood”,[note: 100] “alterations in arousal”, and “distressing […] flashbacks a few times a month”.[note: 101] But on the other hand, the clinician administered PTSD Scale for DSM-5 (CAPS-5), assessed the Plaintiff’s Total Symptom Severity Score at only 18 points (Mild/subthreshold to moderate-threshold range).[note: 102] Moreover, the Plaintiff was not deterred from riding his motorbike and “decided to buy another motorbike […] and return[ed] to riding”.[note: 103]

(c)     The follow-up treatment – “specific therapy for PTSD was not needed”,[note: 104] even though Dr Rathi indicated that the Plaintiff “would benefit from PTSD treatment”.[note: 105]

All things considered, the Plaintiff appeared to have either not had a particularly severe case of PTSD (as suggested by the Total Symptom Severity Score) or recovered generally from the same. He continues to have symptoms, but appears to be otherwise able to cope with the demands of daily life, such being the language used in the Guidelines to describe cases of “moderate” PTSD. In view of this, $6,000 (on a scale ranging from $4,000 - $10,000) ought to be fair compensation for the Plaintiff’s PTSD. Applying an adjustment for inflation (with reference to the MAS Inflation Calculator), this brings the figure to $7,500.

55     I should add that there was one other factor which arose during the hearing and in submissions – the Plaintiff’s diligence in pursuing follow-up therapy. One possible suggestion – and in fairness to Mr Ramasamy, his submissions stopped just short of advancing this point[note: 106] - is that a plaintiff who does not regularly/diligently pursue follow-up treatment for his PTSD cannot be said to have a particularly severe case of PTSD. I do not think there is a straightforward relationship between how often someone goes to seek treatment, and how severe the PTSD is. There might be good reasons why somebody does not pursue follow-up treatment for his PTSD. The Plaintiff here, for example, was afraid of being diagnosed with something that could jeopardise his career prospects[note: 107] - a perfectly believable explanation given the potential stigma associated with a diagnosed mental illness. There might also be reasons why somebody goes very frequently for treatment, for reasons that have nothing to do with the severity of the injury. It all depends on the facts, and the explanations provided by a given plaintiff. Here, the explanations were sufficient. I did not think it was appropriate for me to draw any particular inference, negative or otherwise.

Cases such as Valuina v Lim Chee Beng (Unreported; DC Suit No. 374 of 2007 (“Valuina”) cited in the Practitioner’s Library at p 852) and Nick Loh Sze Chiang (formerly known as Loh Ziqiang (Luo Ziqiang)) v Poa Hui Shan, Ivy (A minor) (Unreported; Suit No. 189 of 2013 (“Nick Loh”) cited in the Practitioner’s Library at p 905) made awards of $15,000 and $12,000 for multiple scars, some of which required skin-grafting. And it does not appear that the Plaintiff can have some of these scars surgically treated either. Dr Jane clarified that, at least for the lower limb scars, the surgical risks far outweigh the benefits.[note: 111] In my view, these factors put the present case in the same bracket as precedents like Valuina and Nick Loh, but for different reasons.

59     In fairness to Mr Ramasamy, he candidly revised his initial submission of $8,000 to $10,000 when I raised these factors to him at the hearing. He justified his proposal with two arguments, but I am unable to accept either of them.

(a)     The first argument was that “the scars are minor”, there being “no specific evidence about the scars being itchy or unsightly”.[note: 112] The scars are not minor. The laparotomy scar alone is 30cm long, that being just one of the twenty scars on the Plaintiff’s body. The scars are indeed unsightly, judging by their size and location on the body.

(b)     The second argument was that the cost of treating these scars had in some sense been accounted for by the future medical expenses[note: 113] which the Defendant had already agreed to foot.[note: 114] That misses the point. Future medical expenses are an entirely separate head of claim from pain and suffering. They address different consequential losses arising from a given physical injury. If Mr Ramasamy’s suggestion was that the unsightliness (ie pain and suffering arising) from the scars could be remedied with “other sources of treatment”,[note: 115] that did not hold water either. There was simply not enough evidence as to the efficacy of these other treatment procedures, conservative or otherwise. I was not prepared to assume in the absence of further medical evidence, and drew conclusions based on what was before me – undisputed medical reports documenting extensive medical scarring across a significant portion of the body, that have not faded with time.

60     Therefore, on the range stated in the Guidelines at p 57A(a) ($5,000 - $15,000), this was an appropriate case for an award of $14,000. Adjusting for inflation, again with reference to the MAS Inflation Calculator, I award $17,000.

Further adjustments

61     The foregoing analysis completes stage one of the analysis. As alluded to earlier (see [33] above), the present case is a suitable one for an uplift at the second stage of the analysis. I apply this uplift having regard to three main factors.

62     First, the Plaintiff was a young, 19-year-old man at the time of the accident. For those injuries which involve long-term medical disabilities/consequences, the Plaintiff will necessarily live with them for longer, and his suffering will accordingly be greater. These include:

(a)     The constant monitoring and lifestyle adjustments that the Plaintiff will have to make for his kidney-related injuries (see [19] above);

(b)     The constant pain experience in the right wrist from the surgical implants (see [41(c)] above), the continued risk of implants giving rise to complications (see [43] above), and the permanent restriction in range of motion in his wrists (see [41(b)] above);

(c)     The permanent restriction in range of motion in his right ankle (see [48] above); and

(d)     The unsightliness of the multiple scars on his body, and in particular on his right lower limb (see [57] above).

A separate adjustment is warranted to address the longer time horizon over which the Plaintiff will experience the aforementioned disabilities/consequences.

63     Second, the Plaintiff suffered an intestinal obstruction which (as explained above at [33]) could not be fairly captured under any particular head of injury. The intestinal obstruction was the result of emergency trauma surgery, and that surgery was done in relation to all of the abdominal injuries (the kidney, liver, and diaphragmatic injuries). It similarly could not be regarded as a separate injury in its own right, in that it was inextricably linked to the treatment of the same abdominal injuries. An upward adjustment was warranted for this medical complication, especially considering that the Plaintiff had to be re-admitted to treat the intestinal obstruction, and spent three days in the wards before being discharged (see [33] above)

64     Third, and in opposition to the two points mentioned above, all the abdominal injuries necessarily had to be treated at the same time, at least when the patient first presented at the hospital. The internal organs were accessed by the same procedure (a laparotomy), and one could expect that there was some amount of overlap in the pain and suffering (at least between the various abdominal injuries) during the initial surgery. Follow-up procedures (such as the abdominal washout and change of vacuum dressings) were also related to multiple heads of injuries (ie the abdominal injuries). Again, the pain and suffering experienced by the Plaintiff could be said to have been shared between these heads of injuries. This merited a downward adjustment to prevent overcompensation.

65     Taken in their totality, I find that a moderate uplift of $10,000 would best balance the three competing considerations above. For completeness, I have also considered the precedents cited by parties (primarily those that were discussed above at stage one of the analysis ([12] – [60])) and none of the authorities suggest to me that the aggregate award here is either excessive or inadequate (see Lua Bee Kiang at [18]).

Loss of Earning Capacity

Clarifying the precise nature of the Plaintiff’s claim

66     Before embarking on the analysis proper, I make three observations about the Plaintiff’s claim for loss of earning capacity.

(a)     This is a claim for loss of earning capacity, not loss of future earnings. The Plaintiff has made this amply clear in both his Statement of Claim,[note: 116] and in the joint opening statement filed before the assessment of damages was heard.

(b)     This is a claim for post-trial loss of earning capacity, not pre-trial loss of earning capacity. Though the parties did not make any explicit distinction between the two, the tenor of their submissions (both oral and written) appear to address post-trial loss of earning capacity. In any case, our Courts do not recognise claims for pre-trial loss of earning capacity (Yap Boon Fong Yvonne v Wong Kok Mun Alvin and another and another appeal [2018] SGCA 80 (“Yvonne Yap”) at [42]). I can only assume that the Plaintiff is fully aware of this since he was the one that brought Yvonne Yap to my attention. Therefore, notwithstanding that the Plaintiff pleaded “Loss of Earning Capacity”[note: 117] as a claim under special damages (suggesting that he may have regarded it as a pre-trial loss of sorts), I proceed on the assumption that this is a claim for post-trial loss of earning capacity.

(c)     Though couched in the language of a “loss of earning capacity”, the claim did not offer a consistent characterisation of the loss allegedly suffered. At points, the Plaintiff described his difficulties in applying for jobs (“most places don’t want the liability”),[note: 118] or his uncertainty about being able to carry out his assigned job scope in the future.[note: 119] At other points, he suggested that the loss lay in the fact that he graduated one year later than his peers.[note: 120] At yet other times, the Plaintiff pointed to potential career paths (as a soldier or pilot) that were now closed on account of his physical disabilities.[note: 121] This – the proper characterisation of the Plaintiff’s loss – was a sore point of argument between parties. As I will explain later, the Defendant was right to question the manner in which the Plaintiff has characterised his losses, but the somewhat loose characterisation used by the Plaintiff was ultimately not fatal to his claim.

With this, I turn to the analysis proper.

Whether the Plaintiff is entitled to an award of loss of earning capacity

The loss addressed by a claim for loss of earning capacity

67     An award for loss of earning capacity (“LEC”) addresses “the weakening of [a plaintiff’s] competitive position in the open labour market”: Mykytowych, Pamela Jane v V I P Hotel [2016] 4 SLR 829 (“Pamela Jane”) at [140]; Samuel Chai at [14] citing Smith v Manchester Corporation [1974] 17 KIR 1 (“Smith”) at 8. This – the weakening of a plaintiff’s competitive position – is the lodestone of loss for any LEC claim. Put in concrete terms, this loss may manifest in any number of ways, including (but not limited to):

(a)     an inability to secure stable employment in his pre-accident occupation/job ever again: Lua Bee Kiang at [50]; Ong Zern Chern Philip v Wong Siang Meng [2004] SGHC 256 (“Phillip Ong”) at [26]; Islam Mohammad Rakibul v Masud and another [2022] SGDC 270 at [102] (“Rakibul”); Liu Haixiang v China Construction (South Pacific) Development Co Pte Ltd [2009] SGHC 21 at [25];

(b)     an inability to work at the same intensity or level as before: Pamela Jane at [142]; Chai Kang Wei Samuel v Shaw Linda Gillian [2010] 3 SLR 587 (“Samuel Chai”) at [37];

(c)     a restriction in the range of work that the plaintiff can take up in the future: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [109] and [111]; Mohammad Fadhil Bin Mohammad Anis v Muhammad Faizal Bin Abdullah [2022] SGDC 169 (“Fadhil”) at [78]; Phillip Ong at [24];

(d)     “difficulties in career advancement” or “loss of opportunity for advancement”: Clark Jonathan Michael v Lee Khee Chung [2010] 1 SLR 209 (“Michael Clark”) at [77] and [82]; Lim Yuen Li Eugene v Singapore Shuttle Bus Service Pte Ltd and Another [2005] SGHC 189 (“Eugene Lim”) at [43]); see also Radhakrishnan Naidu s/o Balram v Esa’ri bin Samad [2021] SGDC 246 (“Radhakrishnan”) at [73];

(e)     an increased risk of job loss: Tan Yu Min Winston (by his next friend Tan Cheng Tong) v Uni-Fruitveg Pte Ltd [2008] 4 SLR(R) 825; (“Winston Tan”) at [40]; Eugene Lim at [43]; and

(f)     a reduction in “employability” or “future job prospects”: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [109]; Winston Tan at [38] and Muhamad Ilyas Bin Mirza Abdul Hamid v Kwek Khim Hui [2004] SGHC 12 (“Ilyas”) at [37].

68     So understood, some of the losses alleged by the Plaintiff (see [66(c)] above) may not appear to be true LEC claims at all.[note: 122] His lost dreams of being a pilot or a soldier, for example, are more akin to a claim for loss of chance at becoming a pilot/soldier. He is saying that his ambitions have been dashed and with that, an alternate future has been snatched from him. But that sort of claim – an identifiably different head of loss – would have to be analysed in a completely different way. One would have to scrutinise whether the Defendant’s breach (on a balance of probabilities) caused the loss of a chance to become a pilot/soldier: Tembusu Growth Fund Ltd v ACTAtek, Inc and others [2018] 4 SLR 1213 (“Tembusu Growth”) at [127]. Following that, the court would have to assess whether the chance lost was “real or substantial” (Tembusu Growth at [128]). Only then, could the Court be satisfied of the loss of chance and grant something as compensation. This, unfortunately, was not the Plaintiff’s pleaded case. Likewise, saying that the Plaintiff’s graduation was delayed does not, in itself, suggest a loss of the sort compensable under a claim for LEC. If anything, it seems to be a quibble with the lost year of income. That, as Mr Ramasamy rightly pointed out in his submissions, was already compensated for in the agreed claim for pre-trial loss of earnings.[note: 123]

69     But consider this. The Plaintiff will almost certainly never be able to take up jobs requiring great physical labour. He will not be able to participate in industries or occupations that call on the strength of his legs and the stamina of his body. And one of those occupations may well be a job as a soldier or as a pilot. His loss, if framed not as a loss of opportunity (to secure a better paying job), but as a reduction in the range of jobs available to him, sounds exactly like the sort of competitive disadvantage described above at [67(c)]. Consider also, the true effect of a delayed graduation. It goes beyond just lost income. It represents lost workplace experience too. The Plaintiff, by virtue of starting work one year later, has one less year of workplace experience than he otherwise would have had. This necessarily has implications for his employability and employment prospects – again the sort of competitive disadvantage that a claim for LEC comfortably addresses. Framing the Plaintiff’s losses in this manner would be a complete answer to the Defendant’s quibble with the manner that the Plaintiff has sought to characterise his losses.[note: 124] And so framed, the Plaintiff’s lived experience may be mapped onto, and expressed in the language of LEC.

The applicable test when plaintiffs without incomes at the time of the accident claim for LEC

70     It is, however, usually not enough to simply identify the competitive disadvantage that was occasioned by a defendant’s breach. That competitive advantage must threaten some form of financial damage:

But what has somehow to be quantified in assessing damages under this head is the present value of the risk that a plaintiff will, at some future time, suffer financial damage because of his disadvantage in the labour market. As Orr LJ said in Clarke v Rotax Aircraft Equipment Ltd ([1975] 3 All ER 794, [1975] 1 WLR 1570

It is true, as stated by Scarman LJ in Smith v Manchester Corpn, that the loss of earning capacity has arisen at the time of and in consequence of the accident, but its financial consequences may or may not arise at all or may arise at any future time.'

(Moeliker v A Reyrolle and Co Ltd [1977] 1 All ER 9 (“Moeliker”) at 16; emphasis mine)

71     Therefore the Court must be satisfied of a credible possibility that the competitive disadvantage will indeed occasion financial damage at some point in the future: Rakibul at [76]. Only then, would the Court be able to assess and quantify the “present value of the risk of the financial damage which the plaintiff will suffer if that risk materialises” (Samuel Chai at [36], citing Moeliker at 17; emphasis mine).

72     As such, for plaintiffs who remain employed in their pre-accident job after the accident, they are called to prove that there is a “substantial or real risk” that they will lose this pre-accident job (Samuel Chai at [36]); only then would such plaintiffs be thrust into the open labour market, and only then would any weakening of their competitive position in that market occasion financial damage to him: Rakibul at [74]. Put another way, these plaintiffs are protected from the risk of financial damage because of their employment status at the time of the assessment: Smith at 8. Taking this “protection” into account, the Court requires that plaintiffs prove that there is an actual risk of the competitive disadvantage manifesting as financial damage ie a real risk that the plaintiff might lose his pre-accident job.

73     Other plaintiffs, however, do not have the benefit of such protection. They are either already in the open labour market or have taken up lower paying jobs by the time of assessment. For these plaintiffs, it would make no sense for the law to insist that they prove that there is a risk of losing their pre-accident job – they have already lost it, or have none to lose. And more importantly, the Court need not account for anything that protects them from the financial damage of being prevented from competing for their pre-accident job (see Rakibul at [78] and [85]). Accordingly (and unsurprisingly), the legal test applicable to such plaintiffs is much more straightforward. The two-step approach in Samuel Chai does not apply. There is no need for the first step of proving a real risk that the plaintiff would lose their pre-accident job in the future. For plaintiffs who take up a less well-paying job post-accident, they need only show that they had been prevented from competing in the market for their pre-accident job: Lua Bee Kiang at [50]. And where a given plaintiff is unemployed at the time of assessment, all that remains is for a court to evaluate “whether there is a real risk that the plaintiff will be at a disadvantage in the open employment market because of the injury sustained in the accident”: Pamela Jane at [139]. Proof of these, presumably, demonstrates that the plaintiff would suffer a competitive disadvantage that would thereby occasion financial damage to him.

74     I turn then to the situations “where there is no available evidence of the plaintiff’s earnings to enable the court to properly calculate future earnings.”: Teo Sing Keng and another v Sim Ban Kiat [1994] 1 SLR(R) 340 (“Teo Sing Keng”) at [40(b)]. Typically, these are young plaintiffs like the ones in Quek Yen Fei (CA) or Fadhil; students, like the plaintiff in Michael Clark; or perhaps a mix of both, like the plaintiffs in Ilyas or Eugene Lim. Whatever form they take, these plaintiffs tend to have “no earnings on which to base an assessment for loss of future earnings”: Teo Sing Keng at [40(b)]. In practice, the law has always accepted that such plaintiffs are, in principle, candidates for an LEC award, even as it regarded them as a perhaps special category of plaintiffs where LEC is concerned.

75     However, candidacy for LEC damages does not mean entitlement to the same. For these plaintiffs, there may be no way to arithmetically estimate a proper award for loss of future earnings (“LFE”). But as Samuel Chai cautioned at [20], “lack of sufficient evidence proving loss of future earnings […] cannot, by itself, convert a claim for loss of future earnings into a claim for loss of earning capacity [and] failure to prove loss of future earnings [should not] lead to an award for loss of earning capacity” (emphasis in bold italics, mine; see also Samuel Chai at [22]). Entitlement to an LEC award must still be proven independently.

76     Unfortunately, there does not appear to have been any clearly articulated test for determining that entitlement. Having surveyed the authorities, I would offer the following as my best rationalisation of the law as it stands.

77     First, when the plaintiff has not commenced work at the time of assessment of damages, he would neither be protected from financial damage for the time being (ala Samuel Chai’s plaintiff), nor would he necessarily be exposed to the vagaries of the open labour market just yet (ala Pamela Jane’s plaintiff). In such situations, the approach seems to just involve a general inquiry into whether the plaintiff’s “employability”/ “employment prospects”/ “future job prospects” have been diminished: Winston Tan at [38] – [40], and Ilyas at [37]. There is no need to account for the possibility that financial damage might be averted, or the possibility that he might otherwise be protected from the financial damage occasioned by his competitive disadvantage. The plaintiff has been handicapped before he even enters the labour market. And when he starts looking for a job – as he most certainly will at some point – he will indubitably experience that competitive disadvantage. It is only a matter of time before financial damage is occasioned from the competitive disadvantage.

78     Second, when the plaintiff has commenced work at the time of assessment of damages, there may be multiple bases on which an LEC claim could be founded. The starting point would be whether the plaintiff was, at the time of the accident, engaged in a line of work that broadly corresponds to his pre-accident potential.

(a)     If the plaintiff proves that, at the time of the assessment, he has been forced to work at a job below his pre-accident potential, then he would obviously be entitled to damages for LEC, without more. This is because there is unequivocal evidence that his competitive position has been weakened – the plaintiff is earning less than what he should be earning. And it is abundantly clear that financial damage has resulted from that weakened position. That was exactly the experience in Koh Chai Kwang v Teo Ai Ling (by her next friend, Chua Wee Bee) [2011] 3 SLR 610 (“Teo Ai Ling (CA)”). The plaintiff there was a bright student, who would otherwise have passed her polytechnic course and graduated with a diploma had it not been for the accident. Her failure to obtain a diploma was entirely attributable to the cognitive injuries arising from the accident. She ended up as a data entry clerk earning $800 a month, with nothing but her ‘O’ Level qualifications. Unsurprisingly, the Court of Appeal found that the plaintiff there was entitled to an LEC award:

45    Here, the [plaintiff in Teo Ai Ling (CA)], if not for the accident, would have probably completed the Diploma and started work, whether in the food business or the civil service or any other job. All she has now is her “O” level certificate. Further, there is evidence, and indeed even without such evidence common sense will also tell us, that a polytechnic graduate will command a higher wage than an “O” level holder within most occupations.

[…]

54    … we are we are recognising that, at the minimum, [her] competitive position in the labour market is weakened by her failure to obtain the Diploma...

(b)     If the plaintiff is thankfully engaged in a line of work that corresponds to his pre-accident potential, the court would assess whether the plaintiff has nonetheless experienced some sort of weakening of his competitive position. The type of competitive disadvantage examined here is different from that examined under the situations described at [77] above. The disadvantage here tends to relate to the plaintiff’s work performance as someone already employed, as opposed to say, his attractiveness as a prospective employee. As such, a nursing student who successfully went on to become a nurse despite his accident, was compensated for the anticipated “difficulties in career advancement due to his whiplash-related syndromes” and the “permanent disabilities […] which can interfere with his work performance”: Michael Clark at [77] and [82]. And similarly, an electronic/computer engineering student who successfully went on to become a sales engineer despite his accident, was compensated for the risk that his “administrative or desk-bound job [would] require greater physical input in the future” and the risk that he could “lose the opportunity for advancement”: Eugene Lim at [43].

(c)     Separately, irrespective of whether the plaintiff is earning above or below his pre-accident potential, the plaintiff may well be able to claim for further loss of earning capacity on the basis that he will also be at a disadvantage in competing for the job he is employed in at the time of assessment, and suffer further financial damage as a result. “In principle, it is difficult to see why such a plaintiff should not be able to do so. Such a claim would be entirely consistent with the established premise that an award for LEC is meant to compensate an injured plaintiff for the financial damage which he will suffer in future because of any competitive disadvantage caused by his injuries. It is trite that the general aim of tortious damages is to restore the plaintiff to the position that he would have been had the accident not occurred. If the plaintiff’s injuries would cause him further financial damage because of the disadvantage he will suffer in competing for his post-accident job, there is no reason in principle to impose an absolute bar against such claims”: Rakibul at [88].

I should clarify that this type of claim for further loss of earning capacity is quite different from the sort described at [78(b)] above. Those types of claims address the continuing difficulties that the plaintiff would experience at his job, given that he is handicapped at the time of employment. This may include an inability to take on greater responsibilities at work, an inability to work at as high a level of intensity as one might otherwise have been able to, and the diminishment of career advancement prospects. They are examples of competitive disadvantage of the sort experienced by those who are disabled and already employed. The further claims for loss of earning capacity envisaged in Rakibul are premised on the view that the plaintiff would have more difficulty than an able-bodied person of comparable skill in seeking employment. It is the sort of competitive disadvantage experienced by those who are prospective employees.

As such, if such a claim for further loss of earning capacity is brought, I would agree with the suggestion in Rakibul that the two-step test in Samuel Chai would apply. “The plaintiff must first show a real risk of losing his post-accident job such that he may have to re-enter the open labour market for such jobs in future. Otherwise, if there is no such risk, he would be protected from any further financial damage.”: Rakibul at [89].

79     As is clear from the foregoing, at least part of the analysis would depend on determining the sort of job that the plaintiff would have been employed in, but for the accident. This inquiry obviously engages a high level of speculation, and the speculative nature of this assessment would doubtlessly be amplified the younger the plaintiff is (see also Teo Ai Ling (CA) at [45]). Unsurprisingly, the authorities do not offer a neat legal test for this. But I would imagine that the inquiry is a fact-sensitive one, having regard to common-sensical factors, considered in a common-sensical way. Borrowing from how the authorities have attempted to answer this question in other contexts (eg in determining the appropriate multiplicand for LFE claims brought by young plaintiffs), I would suggest that the following non-exhaustive factors may be usefully considered in the assessment:

(a)      the plaintiff’s education level at the time of the accident: Quek Yen Fei Kenneth v Yeo Chye Huat [2016] 3 SLR 1106 (“Quek Yen Fei (HC)”) at [89] (secondary 3 dropout); Ilyas at [7] and [37] (engineering student at UC Berkeley);

(b)      the plaintiff’s educational trajectory had the accident not occurred: Teo Ai Ling (CA) at [44] – [45] (would have otherwise completed polytechnic course); Lai Chi Kay v Lee Kuo Shin [1981 – 1982] SLR(R) 71 (would have otherwise completed university medical course); Eugene Lim at [39] (would not have otherwise attended university anyway); Danial Syafiq bin Mahbob v Amin Juman bin Abdul Jabbar and another (Mohammed Faizal bin Ismail, third party) [2019] SGHC 282 at [57] (would have otherwise obtained a diploma in electrical engineering); Lee Wei Kong (by his litigation representative Lee Swee Chit) v Ng Siok Tong [2012] SGCA 4 (“Lee Wei Kong”) at [35] and [37] (would have otherwise graduated from junior college and had more than even chance of obtaining a university degree);

(c)      the plaintiff’s previous employment history: Quek Yen Fei (HC) at [85];

(d)      the plaintiff’s demonstrated interest in a particular line of work: Eugene Lim at [41]; Lee Wei Kong at [37]; and

(e)      the presence of injuries from any prior unrelated accidents: Quek Yen Fei (HC) at [90]

80     In summary, the principles I have stated at [67] and [70] - [79] above can be distilled as follows:

(a)     The weakening of a plaintiff’s competitive position in the open labour market is the lodestone of loss for any LEC claim. Practically speaking, this competitive disadvantage may manifest in a variety of ways: see [67] above;

(b)     Beyond establishing that his competitive position has been weakened, a typical plaintiff must also show that the weakened competitive position would occasion financial damage. Depending on the plaintiff’s employment status at the time of assessment, the court may require further proof that financial damage would indeed be occasioned by the loss of competitive advantage: see [70] – [72] above;

(c)     For atypical plaintiffs who have no earnings on which to base an assessment for loss of future earnings, the starting point would be to consider whether they are employed at the time of the assessment. If they are not, their competitive disadvantage will ordinarily be assessed by reference to any loss of employability or loss of future job prospects: see [77] above.

(d)     If they are employed at the time of assessment, there might be multiple bases for granting an LEC claim. For plaintiffs forced to work in fields below their pre-accident potential, they would be entitled to damages for LEC, without more: see [78(a)] above. For plaintiffs engaged in work that corresponds to their pre-accident potential, the court would assess whether the plaintiff has nonetheless experienced some sort of weakening of his competitive position: see [78(b)] above. Separately, irrespective of whether the plaintiff is employed at or below his pre-accident potential, the plaintiff may well be able to claim for further loss of earning capacity on the basis that he will also be at a disadvantage in competing for his post-accident job and suffer further financial damage as a result. The applicable test for such a claim would be the two-step test set out in Samuel Chai: see [78(c)] above.

(e)     The test to determine the type of job that a plaintiff would have been employed in but for the accident, is a fact sensitive one: see [79] above;

Application to the case at hand

81     Of the three bases I have set out at [78(a)], [78(b)], and [78(c)] above, I find that the Plaintiff has only established his claim for LEC on two of these bases (the bases set out at [78(b)], and [78(c)]).

82     At the time of the accident, the Plaintiff was a 19-year-old student in his second semester at Republic Polytechnic.[note: 125] Thankfully, he managed to complete his studies (after taking some time off),[note: 126] and graduated with a Diploma in Engineering Systems and Management.[note: 127] Thereafter, he completed National Service and found employment at Angel Manufacturing as an associate engineer.[note: 128] About seven months later, finding the job at Angel Manufacturing too physically demanding,[note: 129] the Plaintiff left to join SGLabs Pte Ltd (“SGLabs”) as an Assistant Engineer.[note: 130]

83     I find that the Plaintiff’s eventual employment as an engineer broadly corresponds with the type of job that he would have ended up in, accident or not. The Plaintiff was training to be an engineer all along. That was what he did at the Institute of Technical Education (“ITE”), and also the case when he joined the “Common Engineering Program” upon enrolment at Republic Polytechnic.[note: 131] Indeed, he graduated from ITE with a certificate in electrical engineering.[note: 132] Beyond this, the Plaintiff appears to have been a largely average student prior to the accident. He generally received ‘C’s at ITE (attaining a grade point average of 2.183),[note: 133] and was a perhaps middling polytechnic student, even prior to the accident (at least as far as his academic transcript goes).[note: 134] In that regard, it was difficult for me to draw any inferences that he had particularly high employment prospects or that he was a particularly motivated and ambitious student. It was therefore clear to me that, at least on a balance of probabilities, an average student studying to be an engineer (such as the Plaintiff), would have found employment as an engineer, accident or not.

84     The Plaintiff’s main argument in this regard, was that he had never harboured particular ambitions to be an engineer. He had always wanted to be a soldier or a pilot.[note: 135] Evidence was led of how he was an active young man prior to the accident, presumably suggesting that he had an inclination towards a vocation involving high levels of physical exertion.[note: 136] Indeed, his unchallenged testimony was that he had wanted to be a soldier since his secondary school days, and had joined the National Cadet Corps for that express reason.[note: 137] And beyond that, he had hoped to become a pilot, a dream that he had hoped to groom by studying Aviation Management at his polytechnic.[note: 138]

85     I have no difficulties accepting that the Plaintiff genuinely wanted to be a soldier or a pilot. I have no difficulties accepting that the Plaintiff will never be a pilot or a soldier either. He is one kidney short, and has difficulty doing strenuous activities – I cannot see how he could possibly contemplate such physically taxing vocations. However, I have difficulties accepting that the Plaintiff could even have qualified for these jobs in the first place. Quite separate from the fact that this sort of argument should really have been framed as a claim in loss of chance (see [68] above), the Plaintiff did not produce any credible evidence of his prospects at becoming a soldier or a pilot. Joining the National Cadet Corps as a co-curricular activity in secondary school says little about his eligibility to join the armed forces. This is particularly so, given that the Plaintiff’s expressed desires were to join elite vocations within the military (eg the Guards or Commandos), and to serve in leadership capacities (ie as a specialist or as an officer).[note: 139] It was just not clear what were the eligibility requirements for these jobs (the screenshots from the Singapore Armed Forces website that the Plaintiff produced were not helpful),[note: 140] and whether the Plaintiff was on track to fulfilling them prior to the accident.

86     This difficulty was even more pronounced when it came to the Plaintiff’s aviation ambitions. He had no evidence that he stood, in any way, a credible chance of becoming a pilot. His only argument was that he could have taken up the “Aviation Management” specialisation if not for the accident.[note: 141] But it was unclear whether taking this course would necessarily have made the Plaintiff a more qualified candidate to become a pilot, and it was similarly unclear whether taking this course would have materially changed his general career trajectory as an engineer. Indeed, even “Aviation Management” appears to be some sort of sub-specialisation within Republic Polytechnic’s general engineering course.[note: 142] Beyond that, it was unclear whether the accident itself had jeopardised his chances to specialise in “Aviation Management”. Indeed, he acknowledged at the hearing that an application to join the course was “based on merit”,[note: 143] and could not provide any credible basis for his belief that his delay in applying for the course (the delay having been undisputedly caused by the accident) actually jeopardised his chances at taking up the course.[note: 144]

87     For these reasons, I am not prepared to grant the Plaintiff’s claim for loss of earning capacity – at least, not on the basis that he had been competitively weakened into taking a job that is below his pre-accident potential (see [78(a)] above).

88     That said, I am satisfied that the Plaintiff has nonetheless suffered a loss of earning capacity, in that his disabilities affect his work performance and present continuing difficulties for him at work (see [78(b)] above). He cannot take up jobs that “require carrying of weights, prolonged standing and walking and climbing of stairs, ladders, scaffolds and platforms”[note: 145], and cannot carry heavy objects.[note: 146] This has particular implications for him given that SGLabs is a “projects-based company”, and “requires [him] to go to sites”[note: 147] where he will be expected to carry tools or devices. At present, he has to “depend on [his] partner”[note: 148] to handle the heavier items. It is clear that he has suffered a competitive disadvantage, in that the portfolio of work that he can reasonably handle has been reduced by his injuries.

89     Separately, I am of the view the Plaintiff has also suffered a further loss of earning capacity in that there is a real risk of him losing his job, and that he will, because of the injuries, be at a disadvantage in the open employment market (see [78(c)] above). I fully accept that there is no evidence of SGLabs being dissatisfied with the Plaintiff’s performance, or any indication that they find it too difficult to accommodate this disabilities and injuries.[note: 149] If anything, SGLabs hired the Plaintiff knowing full well about his disabilities,[note: 150] and has retained him past his probationary period.[note: 151] In that sense, one could be forgiven for assuming that there is no real risk of the Plaintiff losing his job.

90     But the truth is, an employer is always entitled to change his/her mind. And though the employer does not express any reservations about employing the Plaintiff now, that state of affairs may change in the future. The key question then, is not so much whether the employer is presently dissatisfied/uncomfortable employing the Plaintiff, but what is the probability that the employer would terminate the Plaintiff’s employment at some point, on account of his disabilities. That is the true test. That is what the Court of Appeal meant when it articulated the test in Samuel Chai:

36    … an award for loss of earning capacity (in the context where the plaintiff is currently employed) can only be awarded if there is substantial or real risk that the plaintiff could lose his or her present job at some time before the estimated end of his or her working life and that the plaintiff will, because of the injuries, be at a disadvantage in the open employment market. It is a cumulative test.

(emphasis in italics in original; emphasis in bold italics, mine)

91     In my view, the Plaintiff is not so dissimilar from the plaintiff in Samuel Chai. Like her, he cannot perform the full range of responsibilities that may arise from his job. The plaintiff in Samuel Chai could not “work long hours, attend to patients in wards, and physically assist patients”: Samuel Chai at [37]. He, similarly, cannot carry heavy machinery or “go to the field for a prolonged number of months”.[note: 152] Like Samuel Chai’s plaintiff, he cannot count on job security from long years of employment either. The plaintiff in Samuel Chai was a “fairly new employee” and “not a long-standing part-time employee” at both of the physiotherapy practices that she worked at: Samuel Chai at [37]. The Plaintiff here, similarly, was a relatively new hire who had just passed his probationary period. Taking these facts into consideration, the Court of Appeal in Samuel Chai took the view that there was “clearly some degree of uncertainty” about the plaintiff’s employment, and that she had a real risk of losing her job: Samuel Chai at [37]. That same conclusion, in my view, is warranted by the present case as well.

92     What then, would be the disadvantage suffered if the Plaintiff was compelled to re-enter the labour market? Clearly, he is far less attractive as a potential hire than an able-bodied person of similar skill and ability. He worries about spooking potential employers.[note: 153] His injuries invite questions at job interviews.[note: 154] And I believe the Plaintiff when he says that many prospective employers hesitate to hire someone like him (“most places don’t want the liability”).[note: 155] Taken in totality, considering the risk of the Plaintiff losing his job and the competitive disadvantage he will suffer upon being thrust into the workplace, I am inclined to recognise his claim for LEC on this basis as well (see [78(c)] above). The question then turns to quantum – how will the Plaintiff’s entitlement to damages for LEC be quantified?

The proper quantum to award for the Plaintiff’s loss of earning capacity

The proper methodology for quantifying loss of earning capacity

93     Traditionally, LEC awards have been quantified “in the round” using a “rough and ready” approach (“R&R approach”): Pamela Jane at [141]. That means Courts ordinarily award a global sum after taking into account factors such as the plaintiff’s age, his skills, the nature of his disability, whether he is capable of undertaking only one type of work or whether he is capable of undertaking other types of work as well: Pamela Jane at [141]. LFE awards on the other hand, have traditionally been derived using a multiplier/multiplicand approach (“M&M approach”): Teo Ai Ling (CA) at [37]. As the Court of Appeal explained in Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [42], “[the] multiplicand represents the quantum of loss that the claimant is expected to suffer at periodic intervals in the future [while the] multiplier is the mathematical tool used to calculate the lump-sum present value of the stream of future periodic losses across the period of future loss of the claimant”. Ordinarily, the difference in earnings between the plaintiff’s pre-accident and post-accident income would form the basis of the multiplicand: Lee Wei Kong at [29].

94     That distinction – the usage of the R&R approach in LEC awards; and the M&M approach in LFE awards – was thought to be settled law, with the Court of Appeal in Pamela Jane explicitly pronouncing that:

141    In making awards for loss of future earning capacity, it is not appropriate to use any of the formulas commonly employed to calculate awards for loss of future earnings, such as the multiplicand and multiplier method […] The court must take a “rough and ready” approach (see [Michael Clark] at [91]) and calculate the loss of earning capacity “in the round” (see Smith at 8), ultimately arriving at a figure that it considers reasonable in the particular circumstances to compensate the particular plaintiff for the disadvantage which he faces in the open employment market due to his disabilities…

This echoed the Court of Appeal’s earlier statements in Chang Ah Lek v Lim Ah Koon [1998] 3 SLR(R) 551 (“Chang Ah Lek”) at [27], and the approach taken in cases like Teo Ai Ling (CA) (see [53] – [54]) and Samuel Chai (see [38]). It similarly echoed Prakash J’s (as she then was) observations in Michael Clark that the use of the M&M approach in quantifying LEC awards was “not very satisfactory as it makes the award akin to an award for loss of future earnings”: Michael Clark at [91].

95     However, in seeming contradiction with its earlier pronouncements in Pamela Jane, the Court of Appeal in Quek Yen Fei (CA) later applied the M&M approach to a claim for LEC. There is no discussion in Quek Yen Fei (CA) about the appropriateness of using the M&M approach in quantifying an LEC claim. There is no such discussion in Quek Yen Fei (HC) either. If anything, the Court of Appeal seems to have accepted the High Court’s use of the M&M approach, making only a small adjustment to the multiplier to be applied: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [100]. As such, there now stands a decision of the apex court specifically applying and seemingly approving the use of the M&M approach in quantifying LEC awards.

96     In view of this, I was invited to (politely) disregard Quek Yen Fei (CA) as an “anomalous” authority that “conflated loss of earning capacity with loss of future earnings”.[note: 156] It was suggested that I follow in the footsteps of fellow State Courts deputy registrars – at least two, at last count – that had explicitly declined to adopt the M&M approach.[note: 157] Indeed, my own survey of the authorities suggests that Quek Yen Fei (CA) has received somewhat lukewarm reception at the High Court as well. Though cited in Yeo Chee Siong v Salpac (S) Pte Ltd and another [2017] SGHC 304 ((“Yeo Chee Siong”) at [33]), its M&M approach was ultimately ignored in favour of Michael Clark’s R&R approach when the LEC award was quantified (see Yeo Chee Siong at [42]). In Tan Hun Boon v Rui Feng Travel Pte Ltd and another [2018] 3 SLR 244 (“Tan Hun Boon”), there was some suggestion that the High Court was applying Quek Yen Fei (CA)’s M&M approach (see [130]). Ultimately however, the High Court in Tan Hun Boon did not identify an LEC multiplicand, and granted an award of $150,000 – a round figure precisely of the sort that is derived from an R&R approach. And in Ng Lay Peng v Gain City Engineering & Consultancy Pte Ltd (Ng Peng Boon, third party) (AXA Insurance Singapore Pte Ltd, intervener) [2020] 3 SLR 271 (“Ng Lay Peng”), Quek Yen Fei (CA) was disregarded altogether. Neither Quek Yen Fei (CA), nor its M&M approach to LEC were mentioned at all. Again, the LEC award was derived with the R&R approach, and calculated “in the round” (see Ng Lay Peng at [150]).

97     For all its apparent unpopularity, Quek Yen Fei (CA) remains good law. It is a standing authority from the Court of Appeal that has not been reconsidered or otherwise doubted by our apex court. If anything, its approach was applied (albeit in a slightly different manner) by the Court of Appeal recently in Yvonne Yap (at [72]). I am bound by the decision and will not jettison stare decisis on account of any disagreements I may have with the decision. As it happens, I do not disagree with Quek Yen Fei (CA). I am of the view that it can be rationalised and woven into the juristic tapestry of the law on LEC. I am of the view that the M&M approach, if suitably adapted, can be a meaningful tool in the court’s arsenal when it comes to quantifying LEC claims. And ultimately, I am of the view that practical justice – a theme I will return to later on – can be achieved without sacrificing conceptual or doctrinal hygiene.

98     I support the use of the M&M approach in the context of quantifying LEC claims for three reasons. They are reasons rooted in principle, precedent, and policy.

(A)   Principle

99     A quantification method should only be adopted if it is capable of gauging the particular loss that it seeks to measure. The loss here, in the LEC context, is the weakening of a plaintiff’s competitive position: Pamela Jane at [140]. This loss, it should be appreciated, may be experienced as a one-off event (eg when a plaintiff is fired and thrust into the open labour market, having to compete with more able-bodied candidates for a job), or as a continuing disability (eg when a plaintiff’s injuries place him at a continuing disadvantage compared to his peers in competing for performance related salary increments/promotions). If one had assumed that competitive disadvantages could only be experienced as discrete/one-off events, I would understand the discomfort with quantifying that loss through the M&M approach. An M&M approach inherently contemplates a “period of future loss” and assumes that there is a “stream of future periodic losses” (Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [42]). In that sense, it would be conceptually incorrect to use the M&M approach in quantifying what might otherwise be a one-time loss.

100    But the law now recognises that LEC can be experienced as a continuing disadvantage. A high-school dropout, amputated below the knee, may face enduring difficulties in any sort of manual work that he might be employed to do (eg Quek Yen Fei (HC) at [89]). A human resource consultant may well experience fatigue and have difficulties concentrating for the rest of his working life (eg Tan Hun Boon at [138] and [142]). A labourer, afflicted with a stiff back from a fractured vertebrae, would have continuing difficulties in lifting heavy loads, as may be expected in his line of work (Mutyalu Eswarudu v Lucky Joint Construction Pte Ltd [2020] SGDC 202 (“Mutyalu”) at [50] and [56]). And these sorts of losses, stretched and experienced over a long time frame, would be precisely the sort of losses that an M&M approach is designed to address.

101    So understood, an M&M approach would be well-suited to address at least one variant of the mischief targeted by LEC claims. I recognise that there is some artificiality in saying that somebody would actually suffer periodic losses of say, $100, on account of his continuing competitive disadvantages. But I am prepared to treat this as a necessary myth, no more (and no less) a legal fiction than the suggestion that a lump sum of $10,000 is an actual reflection of the competitive disadvantage suffered by a given plaintiff.

102    More importantly, this – acknowledging the potential applicability of the M&M approach to LEC claims – does no violence to the conceptual distinction between LEC and LFE either. The loss addressed is still the one that lies at the heart of LEC claims (ie the weakening of a plaintiff’s competitive position). If anything, the M&M approach, in deriving a proper multiplicand, is more than capable of taking into consideration the various factual circumstances that affect the extent of a plaintiff’s weakened position (to be explained later). The loss addressed is not the sort of “real assessable loss proved by evidence” (described in Samuel Chai at [12]) that is rightfully framed as an LFE claim. And the M&M approach does not simply “compensate for the difference between the [plaintiff’s] post-accident and pre-accident income or rate of income” (as described in Samuel Chai at [20]).

103    Indeed, although the M&M approach’s roots are in LFE claims, that sort of nostalgia should not preclude its in-principle applicability to LEC claims. Sure, using the M&M approach in LEC claims may make the award “akin to an award for [LFE]”. But to that, I would only suggest that using French culinary techniques does not mean one is preparing French cuisine.

(B)   Precedent

104    I would also defend the use of the M&M approach in LEC claims by addressing two broad categories of authorities. The first is a pair of authorities that have explicitly referenced, doubted and ultimately declined to apply Quek Yen Fei (CA)’s M&M approach in LEC cases. The other is a group of authorities that have explicitly endorsed the R&R approach, to the seeming exclusion of the M&M approach.

105    In the first category of cases, strong reservations were expressed about Quek Yen Fei (CA) and its M&M approach: Fadhli and Lai Mok @ Lee Peng Sung v SBS Transit Ltd and another [2020] SGDC 151 (“Lai Mok”). The nub of the discomfort was that Quek Yen Fei (CA) did not offer any “practical principles or guidance as to how the [M&M approach] ought to be applied in the assessment of LEC”: Lai Mok at [47] (see also Fadhli at [74]). And beyond that, as the learned DR in Fadhli pointed out, “the multiplicand in [Quek Yen Fei] appears to have been derived in a manner virtually indistinguishable from the [R&R approach] advanced in Samuel Chai and [Pamela Jane]”: Fadhli at [75].

106    I recognise that there is no explicit guidance on how to derive the multiplicand in a principled fashion. And there is much force in the observation that deriving the multiplicand under the M&M approach effectively utilises the same heuristics as the R&R approach. But if the difficulty is with identifying the relevant factors for consideration, a solution might simply be to infer/distil them from the facts of the case (and of course, if necessary, to have that corrected on appeal). And if the difficulty is with the qualitative manner in which the multiplicand seems to have been derived, one could equally say the same about the R&R approach – a methodology that Lai Mok and Fadhli had no issues applying. More pertinently, neither of these authorities offer a reason in principle, for rejecting Quek Yen Fei (CA)’s M&M approach. As stated earlier, a continuing competitive disadvantage may well be better estimated with an M&M approach, a methodology which inherently addresses continuing losses, experienced over a certain time horizon.

107    The second category of cases are the authorities that have seemingly endorsed the R&R approach over the M&M approach. In my view, those authorities do not completely close off the possibility of using the M&M approach in quantifying LEC claims.

108    Take Samuel Chai and Chang Ah Leck for instance. Those were simply not cases discussing the appropriateness of the M&M approach or the preferability of the R&R approach. Samuel Chai was concerned with a larger conceptual issue – the distinction between LFE and LEC – and the parts cited by Pamela Jane (Samuel Chai at [15]) were really just part of a broader survey of the English authorities to clarify the distinction between LFE and LEC (see Samuel Chai at [10] – [15]). Chang Ah Leck was concerned with whether LEC ought to have been granted at all and whether the judge below had properly assessed the evidence in rejecting the LFE claim (see Chang Ah Leck at [30] – [31]). These do not appear to be cases where the court was explicitly deciding on the proper quantification methodology to be adopted in LEC claims.

109    The closest that Chang Ah Leck came to discussing the correct quantification methodology to apply, was at paragraphs [27] – [28]. There, relying on Scarman LJ’s observations in Smith, the Court of Appeal stated that:

27    Had the learned judge continued from where she had stopped quoting from Scarman LJ’s judgment [in Smith], she would have read the following paragraph:

It is clearly inappropriate, when assessing this element of loss, to attempt to calculate any annual sum or to apply to any annual sum so many years’ purchase. The court has to look at the weakness so to speak ‘in the round’, take a note of the various contingencies, and do its best to reach an assessment which will do justice to the plaintiff. …

28    Although the learned judge purported to compensate the respondent for loss of earning capacity, she did not to borrow Scarman LJ’s words look at the weaknesses in the round by taking note of the various contingencies and doing the best she could to arrive at an assessment which would so do justice to the respondent; instead she calculated the respondent’s loss by reference to a monthly sum for a 16 years’ purchase that is to say awarded him damages for loss of future earnings.

But on closer scrutiny, the Court of Appeal seems to have disagreed with the manner in which the learned Judge below had used the M&M approach, and not the usage of the approach per se. Her honour had effectively made an LFE award but couched it in the language of LEC. The analysis was centred on the difference in pre-accident and post-accident income. The analysis was focused on the sort of loss that is addressed in LFE awards. The analysis was not directed at the loss which LEC awards are supposed to target (“the weakness in the round”). And so it was no surprise that a wholesale adoption of the M&M approach directed towards LFE-type losses, was criticised. Notably, the Court of Appeal did not foreclose the possibility of the M&M approach being used if it properly addressed the sort of losses contemplated in LEC awards.

110    Then there is Pamela Jane (see [94] above). There, the Court of Appeal cited Michael Clark and Smith for the proposition that “the court must take [an R&R approach] and calculate loss of earning capacity “in the round””: Pamela Jane at [141]. It then cited Samuel Chai and Moeliker as authority for the proposition that it is “not appropriate to use any of the formulas commonly employed to calculate awards for loss of future earnings such as the [M&M approach]”: Pamela Jane at [141] (emphasis mine). But what exactly did the Court of Appeal mean when it said the M&M approach was not “appropriate”?

111     Moeliker, cited by Samuel Chai at [14], and squarely addressing the pronouncements in Smith, is instructive:

Edmund Davies and Scarman LJJ said in [Smith] that the multiplier/multiplicand approach was impossible or 'inappropriate', but I do not think that they meant that the court should have no regard to the amount of earnings which a plaintiff may lose in the future, nor to the period during which he may lose them. What I think they meant was that the multiplier/multiplicand method cannot provide a complete answer to this problem because of the many uncertainties involved. The court must start somewhere, and I think the starting point should be the amount which a plaintiff is earning at the time of the trial and an estimate of the length of the rest of his working life.

(Moeliker at 6; emphasis in bold, mine)

112    So understood, Moeliker clarifies that Scarman LJ regarded the M&M approach as “inappropriate”, only in the sense that it is not an exhaustive or comprehensive way to quantify LEC claims. That makes perfect sense. An M&M approach may appear to offer a misleadingly tidy solution to what is clearly a fact-sensitive inquiry. Embedded in it, is a certain kind of mathematical heuristic that seems suspiciously neat, and apt to produce only spurious precision. The potential arrogance of such an arithmetic approach may have been what unsettled the English Court of Appeal. This would be supported, albeit obliquely, by Scarman LJ’s opinion in Smith itself:

It is clearly inappropriate, when assessing this element of loss to attempt to calculate any annual sum or to apply to any annual sum so many years' purchase. The court has to look at the weakness so to speak "in the round", take note of the various contingencies, and do its best to reach an assessment which will do justice to the plaintiff…

(Smith at 8; emphasis in bold, mine)

The concern, it would seem, was that the M&M approach would not sufficiently take cognizance of the various qualitative factors that would go into assessing someone’s loss of earning capacity.

113    In that sense, Pamela Jane’s references to Moeliker must mean that the Court of Appeal adopted Scarman LJ’s observations in Smith, in a manner befitting of its context. When it described the M&M approach as “inappropriate”, it must be a criticism of the pitfalls of the M&M approach, and not a complete rejection of the same; a caution, rather than a prohibition.

114    This, in my view, would be the reading that best reconciles the Court of Appeal’s use of the M&M approach in Quek Yen Fei (see [95] above), alongside its previous statements that seemingly disapprove of its usage in the LEC context.

(C)   Policy

115    Finally, something ought to be said of the broader policy of the courts when it comes to matters of lost future earnings (whether it be future earnings or future earning capacity). The law has always recognised that in some cases, a plaintiff may not be able to provide sufficient evidence of his loss to establish a claim for LFE. That, of course, is no excuse or justification for an award of LEC (see Samuel Chai at [20] and [22]), but has often been the occasion for the Court to consider an award for LEC. This represents the law’s dynamism, and readiness to ensure that all loss – even if incapable of precise quantification – is addressed meaningfully. Being somewhat ineffable, LEC has been quantified with a correspondingly loose (and possibly imprecise) methodology – the R&R approach. But that is practical justice. And more importantly, that does not represent a pedantic attachment to an (admittedly) impressionistic methodology.

116    That being so, I cannot see why LEC awards must be quantified with the R&R approach, to the exclusion of any other methodology. So long as the M&M approach is suitably tailored to address the loss that lies at the heart of LEC claims, I am of the view that the law’s impulse for practical justice can be supported without sullying conceptual correctness. This brings me to the manner in which the M&M approach may be used in quantifying an LEC award.

How the M&M approach may be used in claims for loss of earning capacity

117    In general, the authorities suggest that there are two ways in which the M&M approach may be used when quantifying claims for loss of earning capacity:

(a)     First, the multiplier/multiplicand method may be used to confirm the reasonableness of a figure derived using the R&R approach.

(b)     Second, where the particular competitive disadvantage is experienced on a continuing basis, the M&M approach may be used to directly quantify the LEC award.

(A)   Situation 1: Confirmatory use of the M&M approach

118    First, it is relatively uncontroversial that a court, having arrived at a provisional figure for a LEC award using the R&R approach, may then apply the multiplier/multiplicand method to ascertain the reasonableness of this provisional figure.

119    In Winston Tan, the High Court observed (at [45]):

… the court [could], merely for the purpose of doing a very rough check, determine if the amount that was assessed “in the round” was within a reasonable range (i.e. neither manifestly inadequate nor too excessive). That check could be achieved by computing what that lump sum amount to be awarded would have in effect produced, if it was distributed as a stream of income per month in the future over the period of the working life of the plaintiff, assuming a certain rate of interest to be used as a discount rate for the computation. The result from such a simple mathematical computation or annuity analysis provides, in my view, a useful parameter to make a very rough check on whether or not the assessment of the lump sum “in the round” so to speak, could have gone simply too far wrong either way. I believe it would help avoid an overly excessive or manifestly inadequate estimate of a ball-park figure by the court for the loss of earning capacity assessed primarily on the relevant facts and circumstances of the case.

120    Thus, in Winston Tan, the High Court arrived at a provisional figure of $100,000 for LEC after making an assessment “in the round” (at [49]). It then went on to confirm the reasonableness of this amount using the multiplier/multiplicand approach. Working backwards, the High Court calculated that an award of $100,000 would equate to an award of approximately $530.30 a month (the multiplicand) over 35 years (the multiplier) based on a discount interest rate of 5.33% per annum. The High Court was satisfied that this was reasonable and ultimately made an award of $100,000 for LEC.

121    The Court of Appeal used the multiplier/multiplicand method in a similar, albeit not identical, way in Yvonne Yap. The Court of Appeal accepted the factual findings made by the High Court, but observed that the LEC award had been made without “[providing] any specific reasons” (at [72]). The Court of Appeal confirmed the reasonableness of this figure by applying the multiplier/multiplicand method (at [72]-[75]). Unlike the High Court in Winston Tan, the Court of Appeal in Yvonne Yap did not work backwards from the figure of $80,000 but set about to determine afresh an appropriate multiplier and multiplicand. It alighted on an (annual) multiplicand of $5,733 and a multiplier of 15 years and observed that the ensuing aggregate sum of $85,995 was “close to” the amount awarded by the High Court: Yvonne Yap at [75]. The Court of Appeal thus upheld the High Court’s award of $80,000 for LEC.

(B)   Situation 2: Direct quantification of continuing competitive disadvantage

122    Beyond its use in a confirmatory sense, the M&M approach may also be used to directly quantify an award for loss of earning capacity. As alluded to earlier (see [100] above), there are species of competitive disadvantages that may be experienced on a continuing basis by the plaintiff. These could be directly quantified by a methodology that contemplates future loss being experienced over a certain time horizon. In these situations, the multiplicand would be a proxy for the extent of loss; and the multiplier, a proxy for the period for which that loss might be experienced. Determining the multiplicand would be a fact sensitive exercise (as suggested by the approach taken in Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [109]) and the multiplier would simply be the “remaining working life of the claimant but for the accident”: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [92]. It would be useful, at this point, to illustrate the approach by reference to the authorities that have thus far applied the M&M approach to LEC claims.

123     Quek Yen Fei (HC) (and the appeal arising from it) involved a plaintiff who suffered serious injuries from a road traffic accident. His right leg had to be amputated below the knee as it was severely manged, and he suffered a fracture to his right collarbone as well. At the time of the accident, he was a Secondary Three dropout who had been shuttling between various part-time jobs prior to National Service enlistment. He had also fractured both his wrists in a prior unrelated accident. These facts suggested to the Judge that the plaintiff was likely, accident or not, to have been employed for manual work: Quek Yen Fei (HC) at [89]. Therefore, the physical injuries would directly impact his ability to carry out such manual work and he would have been “likely to receive a lower income than an able-bodied person doing the same job”: Quek Yen Fei (HC) at [89]. That said, allowance was also made for the fact that he had already been handicapped in a prior unrelated accident: Quek Yen Fei (HC) at [90]. In other words, the disadvantage he was likely to experience was not entirely attributable to the accident at hand. All this was estimated at a monthly multiplicand of $750.

124    As for the multiplier, the High Court placed primary emphasis on the fact that the plaintiff was a 24-year-old man at the time of assessment, and relied on Teo Seng Kiat v Goh Hwa Teck [2003] 1 SLR(R) 333 (“Teo Seng Kiat”) where a multiplier of 18 years was applied to a 28-year-old man. This multiplier was, according to the court in Teo Seng Kiat, “the current trend in relation to “healthy young men””. Quek Yen Fei (HC) accordingly adopted a multiplier of 18 years. On appeal, this figure was increased to 20 years. Having regard to the statutory minimum retirement age of 67 years old, the Court of Appeal concluded that the plaintiff would have a “remaining working life of 43 years”: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [94]. The Court adopted the annualised discount rates that were used in calculating the LFE multipliers in Lee Wei Kong and in Teo Ai Ling (by her next friend Chua Wee Bee) v Koh Chai Kwang [2010] 2 SLR 1037 (“Teo Ai Ling (HC)”). Those were cases where the plaintiffs were similarly (a) “young persons who had long remaining working lives of over 40 years at the time of their assessments of damages” and (b) “likely to have worked in Singapore for the remainder of their working lives”: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [99]. As such, the same annualised discount rate as that used in Lee Wei Kong and in Teo Ai Ling (HC) was used to derive a multiplier of 20 years: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [100].

125     Mutyalu applied Quek Yen Fei’s M&M approach to directly derive an LEC award of $10,000. Multiplicand wise, the Court valued the competitive disadvantage at $50 per month: Mutyalu at [56]. Though no specific reasons were given for that figure, it may be useful to note that the plaintiff there had been repatriated by the time of the assessment. Back at home, he was working at a farm, drop feeding pellets for 5,000 INR a month (approximately S$100): Mutyalu at [54]. The multiplicand, presumably, was drawn up with reference to the lower average salaries (translating to lower losses in earning capacity) that one could expect in India. And as for the multiplier, a discount rate of 4.8% per annum was applied to the plaintiff’s remaining working life of nearly 34 years. The final sum, working out to be $10,077.49, was rounded down to $10,000.

126    And finally, there is Yvonne Yap. This case, as discussed above, was not strictly one where the LEC award was directly quantified using the M&M approach. The M&M approach was used to confirm the figure that the learned judge had assessed in the proceedings below That said, the Court of Appeal did quantify the LEC award afresh and it would be instructive to examine how that was done. With regard to the multiplicand, the Court of Appeal observed that “mobility and the ability to stand for longer durations would likely be advantageous albeit not crucial or necessary” for her job as a course facilitator: Yvonne Yap at [73] (emphasis mine). Therefore, The plaintiff’s permanent physical incapacity having been assessed at 14%, the Court found it appropriate to peg the multiplicand to half of that. Applying 7% to her last-drawn annual salary of $81,900, the Court derived an annual multiplicand of $5,733: Yvonne Yap at [73]

127    As for the multiplier, the Court of Appeal in Yvonne Yap recognised that the plaintiff had about 27 years remaining in her working life (assuming she retired at the re-employment age ceiling of 67). After applying the usual discount rate to account for her accelerated receipt of damages, as well as contingencies that would affect the length of her remaining working life, the Court alighted on a final multiplier of 15 years.

128    Each of these cases involved some sort of continuing competitive disadvantage that would be experienced by the plaintiffs for a certain future period. Quek Yen Fei’s plaintiff would doubtlessly be afflicted by a missing leg for the rest of his working life as a manual labourer. The same goes for Mutyalu’s plaintiff (a physical labourer) and his vertebrae fracture. And Yvonne Yap’s plaintiff would be handicapped in conducing her courses and facilitating them as she was not able stand for long durations or move around freely. That – the continuing nature of the competitive disadvantage that is experienced by these plaintiffs – is the golden thread that runs through the cases that have adopted the M&M approach in quantifying LEC awards.

129    So understood, and having examined the authorities, I am guided by the following principles:

(a)     The number of years of working life that a plaintiff may have, would ordinarily be derived by deducting the plaintiff’s age at the time of assessment from the statutory retirement age. However, the statutory retirement age is just one factor, and “[owing] to such factors as the particular characteristics of the claimant and the nature of the work concerned, he may be expected to retire before, at or after the statutory retirement age”: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [92].

(b)     Beyond the discount for accelerated receipt and mortality risks that curtail the expected period of future loss (Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [43]), the multiplier may well take into account other contingencies such as the fact that someone might not work “in Singapore for the remainder of his working life”: Quek Yen Fei (CA) at [99], citing Poh Huat Heng Corp Pte Ltd v Hafizul Islam Kofil Uddin [2012] 3 SLR 1003 (“Poh Huat Heng”) at [59].

(c)     Deriving the multiplicand would ultimately be a fact sensitive exercise but regard must always be had to the competitive disadvantage that might actually be experienced by the plaintiff in the future. Someone working as a manual labourer would doubtlessly be affected by an amputated knee (Quek Yen Fei), but that same injury may not have the same impact on a course facilitator who will not rely so heavily on her physical strength in the course of her work. Needless to say, tibia fractures, though an impediment, would perhaps have a limited impact on her actual earning capacity as a course facilitator (Yvonne Yap at [73]).

130    Finally, in obiter, I would suggest that the M&M approach may also be used to directly quantify a LEC award in situations where LEC overlaps with LFE as a head of damages. The possibility of such an overlap requires some explanation.

131    On a conceptual level, LFE and LEC are different heads of damages intended to compensate for different losses. In Samuel Chai, the Court of Appeal expressed the distinction between LFE and LEC in the following way (at [20]):

… These two heads of damages are meant to compensate for different losses – loss of future earnings compensates for the difference between the post-accident and pre-accident income or rate of income, while loss of earning capacity compensates for the risk or disadvantage, which the plaintiff would suffer in the event that he or she should lose the job that he or she currently holds, in securing an equivalent job in the open employment market. …

132    It follows that there is no necessary overlap between LFE and LEC. Depending on the facts, a plaintiff may well be entitled to separate awards for both. The Court of Appeal specifically addressed this possibility in Samuel Chai at [24]-[25]:

24    Finally, there is one other general comment which we would make. There is nothing in principle which bars a plaintiff from being entitled to claim for both loss of future earnings and loss of earning capacity, provided that the necessary evidence is present. …

25    To reiterate again, loss of future earnings and loss of earning capacity compensate different losses. We can best illustrate the point by an example. Suppose an injured person was taken back by his pre-accident employer to do a less demanding job due to his disabilities but at a lower pay. If the employer cannot guarantee how long he will be so employed but will do so as long as possible, it seems to us that the injured victim should be entitled to awards based on both heads of damages.

133    Nonetheless, in certain factual situations, LFE and LEC may well overlap in that the losses they seek to address are one and the same. The possibility of such an overlap is illustrated by the cases of Rakibul and Chang Ah Lek.

134    In Rakibul, the plaintiff was working in Singapore as a construction worker at the time at the time of the accident. As a result of his injuries, he was working in lower-paying odd jobs and menial jobs in Bangladesh at the time of the assessment. The Deputy Registrar awarded the plaintiff $27,792 for LFE (see [38]-[66]) but, despite finding that he had also satisfied the applicable test for LEC (see [101]-[106]), declined to make an additional LEC award. This was because the LFE award had already compensated the plaintiff for the difference between: (a) his future earnings in the construction industry in Singapore and Bangladesh had he not been injured; and (b) his future earnings now that he was confined to doing lower-paying odd jobs and other menial jobs in Bangladesh. As this represented the full extent of the financial damage the plaintiff might suffer because of his competitive disadvantage in the construction industry, an additional LEC award would lead to overcompensation ([107]-[132]).

135    Similarly, in Chang Ah Lek, the plaintiff was an ironmonger working as a subcontractor dealing with iron works at the time of the accident. As a result of his injuries, he was working in a lower-paying job as an overseer at the time of the assessment. The Assistant Registrar appeared to award the plaintiff $51,000 for LFE and $17,000 for LEC. The High Court set these awards aside and dealt with the issues afresh. On further appeal, the Court of Appeal commented that, having awarded the plaintiff $51,000 for LFE, the AR was wrong to have also awarded $17,000 for LEC because “[t]his was two awards for the same item of loss” (at [22]).

136    Where LFE and LEC overlap, the M&M approach typically used to quantify LFE may also be usefully applied to quantify LEC. This view is consistent with, and may explain, the Court of Appeal’s use of the M&M approach to quantify a LEC award in Quek Yen Fei (CA). To recap (see [123]), the plaintiff was in National Service at the time of the accident. He had earlier dropped out of school three months into his repeat year for the Secondary Three Normal (Technical) course. In the four years before he commenced his National Service, he had been unable to secure a stable full-time job and shuttled between part-time jobs. The plaintiff was unemployed at the time of the assessment. The Court of Appeal assessed the plaintiff’s entitlement to damages for LFE and LEC in the following way. First, the Court of Appeal determined that a multiplier of 20 years would be appropriate (at [92]-[101]). Second, turning to the appropriate multiplicand, the Court of Appeal rejected the plaintiff’s claim for LFE because it was unclear, from his employment history, whether he could even have obtained employment and continued in such employment. There were therefore insufficient objective facts or evidence to enable a reasonable assessment of LFE (at [102]-[105]). However, the Court of Appeal agreed that the plaintiff was entitled to damages for LEC and determined that a multiplicand of $750 per month would be appropriate for this purpose (at [108]-[112]). The Court of Appeal thus awarded the plaintiff $180,000 for LEC, computed by multiplying the multiplicand of $750 a month by the multiplier of 20 years.

137     Quek Yen Fei, therefore, could be understood as a situation where LFE and LEC would have overlapped had the evidence permitted an assessment of LFE. But for the accident, the plaintiff would likely have been engaged in manual work given his relatively low level of education. However, his injuries had significantly limited his ability to do manual work. Had an award for LFE been possible – ie had the plaintiff been able to demonstrate that he could have obtained employment and continued in such employment – it would have compensated the plaintiff for the difference between: (a) his future earnings in the manual work industry had he not been injured; and (b) his future earnings in the manual work industry now that he was injured. This would have represented the full extent of the financial damage the plaintiff might suffer because of his competitive disadvantage in the manual work industry. Given the overlap between LFE and LEC, it is perhaps unsurprising that the Court of Appeal adopted the multiplier/multiplicand method even when quantifying its award for LEC.

138    I turn then to the facts of the present case.

The quantum that the Plaintiff is entitled to

139    As stated above, the Plaintiff has suffered a loss of earning capacity in two broad senses. The first, is the competitive disadvantage that comes with being unable to take on a full portfolio of responsibilities at work (see [88] above). This, I take to be a continuing disadvantage which is amenable to the M&M approach. The second, is the risk that he would lose his present job and be thrust into the open labour market, having to compete against his more able-bodied peers (see [89] above). This, I do not regard as a continuing disadvantage, in that it would be a discrete event that occasions financial damage only on the contingency that he is thrust into the open labour market. I take each of these in turn.

(A)   The First Type of Competitive Disadvantage

140    First, I assess the number of years of working life that the Plaintiff has ahead of him. The Plaintiff is 26 years old at the time of assessment. The re-employment age ceiling (having reference to s 7A(11) of the Retirement and Re-employment Act 1993, the Retirement and Re-employment (Prescribed Re-employment Age) Notification 2022, and s 4(1) of the Retirement Age Act), is now 68 as of 1 July 2022. This means that the plaintiff has 42 years of working life ahead of him.

141    The appropriate annualised discount rate would be in the range of that applied in Lee Wei Kong (4.27% per annum; 43 years of remaining working life), Teo Ai Ling (HC) (4.21% per annum; 42 years of remaining working life), and Quek Yen Fei (unspecified discount rate, but in the range of 4.2% - 4.3% per annum; 43 years of remaining working life). These were all young persons with long remaining working lives of over 40 years at the time of assessment. In my view, an average discount rate of 4.25% would be suitable, giving a multiplier of 20.26 for the present case.

142    However, I am of the view that 20.26 should not be the final multiplier. In truth, the Plaintiff will only experience this particular competitive disadvantage for as long as he works at a job that involves some degree of physical labour. In those jobs, he will be handicapped in that he will not be able to handle the full portfolio of work. If he finds a more office-based job, I am not sure that his physical disabilities will necessarily be such an issue. Indeed, Dr Lee (the orthopaedic surgeon that the Plaintiff visited most frequently) took the view that the Plaintiff “should be able to do jobs, which are sedentary in nature and [do] not require carrying heavy objects”, and would only be “affected in jobs/occupations that require carrying of weights, prolonged standing and walking and climbing of stairs, ladders, scaffolds and platforms.”.[note: 158]

143    The better multiplier, therefore, should be 11. This would take into account the usual discounts given for accelerated receipt of lump-sum damages, and the mortality risks that might cut short the Plaintiff’s remaining working life. It would also take into account the contingency that the Plaintiff finds himself an office-based job – and he has consistently expressed his desire for one (“I am really pushing towards an office-based job”)[note: 159] – and the fact that finding such a job would minimise the otherwise continuing competitive disadvantage he would experience at his job. This contingency, it should be borne in mind, is not a hypothetical one. The Plaintiff has graduated (albeit with some difficulty) with a diploma. He is capable and, at least in my limited interaction with him, appears to be a personable man. Those qualities go some way towards his securing of a job more suited for his present condition.

144    I am fully aware that this might be experienced as a kind of unfairness – the Plaintiff should not have had to consider his disabilities in his job hunt at all. But these are the cards that the Plaintiff has been dealt. I can only grant awards based on the loss that he might actually experience, and must take into consideration contingencies such as these. In that regard, I simply cannot peg the Plaintiff’s multiplier here to those used in cases like Teo Ai Ling (HC), Quek Yen Fei (CA) and Lee Wei Kong (other cases with young plaintiffs that had over 40 years of working life ahead of them). These were all cases where the plaintiffs had no further options. Their job prospects were shackled, as it were, to their injuries. There was no chance that Teo Ai Ling’s plaintiff was going to escape her eye injuries, hearing loss, and cognitive injuries by taking on a different job. There was no chance that Lee Wei Kong’s plaintiff would escape the effects of his loss of motor function, residual spasticity, mood instability, blindness in one eye, and cognitive impairments, by taking on a different job. In fact, he did attempt to pivot into a different career. He joined the LaSalle College of the Arts (“LaSalle”) hoping to pursue something he had an interest in, and hoping to become a painter or to work in some adjacent field. Those efforts were in vain. It turned out that he was unlikely to ever succeed as a painter or an art instructor: Lee Wei Kong at [34] and [38]. And for Quek Yen Fei’s plaintiff, his amputated knee would forever haunt him in whatever manual labour he would pick up. These plaintiffs did not have the benefit of a contingency – an escape plan from their circumstances. The Plaintiff does.

145    Finally, I am mindful that the approach laid out in Quek Yen Fei (CA), and to some extent, the observations in Poh Huat Heng at [48] – [56], have been superseded by the introduction of the “Actuarial Tables with Explanatory Notes for use in Personal Injury and Death Claims” (“the Tables”). I have chosen not to use the Tables because they do not account for the contingency that I have described above. In any case, the State Courts Practice Directions 2013 (“the PDs”) clearly state that I am free to depart from the Tables if the “facts of the case and ends of justice” require me to. Of course, the PDs do not “empower” me to deviate from the Tables; but then again, the Tables were never binding on me in the first place.

146    I turn then to the multiplicand. My starting point is Quek Yen Fei, a case that similarly involved a young plaintiff with no serious income prior to his accident, and the only case to my knowledge where the Court of Appeal has expressly endorsed an LEC multiplicand for such a plaintiff. The monthly multiplicand used there was $750. That comes up to an annual multiplicand of $9,000. I cannot use that figure here. The Plaintiff here is far less impacted by his injuries. The medical evidence suggests that the Plaintiff does not suffer such a serious disability in the work that he does. His tibia fractures are really only perceptible in moments of exertion (see [47] above). For his one permanent disability (the partial restriction in range of motion in his wrist, as described at [41(b)] above), the extent of its impact on his work was never explored at the hearing/in his affidavit evidence-in-chief.

147    Beyond that, and without discounting or demeaning the horrific nature of his injuries, the kidney, diaphragm and lung related injuries do not appear to have any meaningful implications for his ability to work either. Indeed, Dr Leanne Leong (the nephrology expert) only advised the Plaintiff to avoid “strenuous sports” and to “adhere to a low sodium diet […]”.[note: 160] She further noted that “apart from that, [the Plaintiff was] expected to be fit to work.”[note: 161] Dr Bonney (the hepatobiliary surgeon) was likewise of the opinion that “from the liver point of view there should be no long-term result of this on his occupation”.[note: 162]

148    The better annual multiplicand, therefore, would be $6,000. That would take into account the actual impact that his injuries have on the portfolio of work that he can take up. It would also take into account the actual financial damage that the Plaintiff stands to suffer on account of his reduced ability to work. He is certainly more qualified than Quek Yen Fei’s plaintiff. He stands, on average, to earn a higher salary. And in that sense, he stands to lose more as well. $6,000 would, in my view, be a fair annual multiplicand that accounts for these two distinguishing factors.

149    I should make clear that the Plaintiff’s suggestion of $1,000 as a monthly multiplicand (coming up to $12,000 as an annual multiplicand) was excessive and founded on conceptual confusion. Excessive, for the reasons I have stated above; and conceptually unsound, because it seems to be loosely based on the difference between his salary from Angel Manufacturing ($3,500/month), and his salary from SGLabs ($2,400/month):

(a)     If the Plaintiff’s view is that he would have otherwise earned $3,500/month but is now resigned to earning $2,400/month, he should have framed his claim as one in LFE. There was nothing to stop him from doing so. Young plaintiffs like the Plaintiff have sought – and succeeded – in claims for LFE, notwithstanding that they had no pre-accident income from which to derive an LFE multiplicand: Lee Wei Kong at [33]. That, unfortunately, was not the Plaintiff’s pleaded case.

(b)     If the Plaintiff’s suggestion is that he has already lost $1,100/month from taking up a lower paying job at SGLabs, that is a clearly definable and quantifiable loss. More importantly, it is a loss in the past and should have been claimed as pre-trial loss of income. I note that parties have already agreed on the appropriate award for pre-trial loss of income.

(c)     If the Plaintiff’s suggestion is that I should consider the kind of multiplicand that I might have used in assessing an LFE claim to derive an LEC multiplicand, that would be plainly wrong. I would be committing the same mistake that the Court of Appeal roundly criticised in Chang Ah Lek.

150    Even taking the Plaintiff’s case at its highest, I can only accept that the factors which go towards deriving an LFE multiplicand for young plaintiffs might be considered in deriving the Plaintiff’s LEC multiplicand. These factors would be found in cases like Peh Diana and another v Tan Miang Lee [1991] 1 SLR(R) 22, Lai Chi Kay v Lee Kuo Shin [1981–1982] SLR(R) 71 (“Lai Chi Kay”), Teo Ai Ling (HC) and Lee Wei Kong. Those were all instances where, owing to the nature of the plaintiffs, the multiplicand had to be derived “from scratch”. There was no pre-accident income with which to compare the post-accident income. As such, the Courts used career models (that is, the likely salary from the career that the young plaintiff might have otherwise embarked on) as a heuristic substitute for pre-accident income. For example, Lai Chi Kay used a doctor’s career model to estimate what the medical student-plaintiff might otherwise have earned but for the accident. If such a clear career trajectory cannot be identified, some reasonable estimate such as the average starting salary of a university student, or the average salary of a civil servant was taken instead (see Lee Wei Kong at [39] and Teo Ai Ling (CA) at [32] – [36]).

151    Here however, there was just no need to rely on any career model. As I found above at [83], the Plaintiff was training to be, and did in fact become an engineer. And beyond that, by the Plaintiff’s own admission, the “median gross starting salary of a post National Service graduate in Engineering Systems and Management in 2018 was about $2,315”.[note: 163]Both of his jobs post-graduation were higher than that. In fairness, $2,315 is a figure from 2018. But when asked if he had a more updated statistic, the Plaintiff simply had no answer.[note: 164]

152    In any case, Lee Wei Kong and Teo Ai Ling (HC) were all cases where the plaintiffs suffered serious injuries that directly affected their ability to work. Lee Wei Kong’s plaintiff could not graduate from junior college. He was barely coping with his studies at LaSalle (an option he took up after the accident derailed his “A” level studies) and there was every indication that LaSalle would be awarding him a Diploma in Arts “out of sympathy”: Lee Wei Kong at [34] and [38]. Teo Ai Ling (HC)’s plaintiff dropped out of mainstream education altogether, resigning herself to a data entry job with her only educational qualifications – an ‘O’ level certificate from an earlier time when she had fuller faculties. The multiplicands used for each of these cases were clearly appropriate given the competitive disadvantage that the plaintiffs would actually face. Compared to that – and only in comparison – the Plaintiff’s competitive disadvantage is relatively mild.

153    Taking all this together, I find that the Plaintiff’s loss of earning capacity (at least with regard to the first type of competitive disadvantage) would be $6,000 x 11 = $66,000.

(B)   The Second Type of Competitive Disadvantage

154    This refers to the risk that the Plaintiff would lose his present job and be thrust into the open labour market, having to compete against his more able-bodied peers (see [89] above). In my view, only a small sum ought to be awarded for this given that it would be a one-time event, and given that the Plaintiff may well be competing for office-based jobs where his disabilities will not pose a serious interference with his work. $4,000, bringing the total LEC award to a round $70,000, ought to suffice.

Conclusion

155    In sum, I assess damages at $356,153.1 and will hear parties on costs. I should conclude by thanking the counsel involved in this matter – Mr Joshua Lim (Plaintiff’s instructed counsel), for his good sense and temperament; Mr Tan, for his diligence and passion; and Mr Ramasamy, for his candour and reasonableness. I did not have the opportunity to hear Mr Donald Spencer at length, but am nonetheless appreciative of his submissions and assistance as well. This was a horrific accident, involving a searching examination of the fairest remedy for an unfortunate victim. In that regard, the sensitivity and competence of the counsel involved was of tremendous assistance to the Court.
