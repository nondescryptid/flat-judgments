Public Prosecutor v Lingasamy Selva Pandi
[2023] SGMC 18

Case Number:Magistrate's Arrest Case No. 911084 of 2021, Magistrate's Appeal No. 9046 of 2023-01
Decision Date:24 April 2023
Tribunal/Court:Magistrates Court
Coram: Christopher Goh Eng Chiang
Counsel Name(s): Sunil Nair (Attorney-General's Chambers) for the Prosecution; A Revi Shanker (ARShanker Law Chambers) for the Accused.
Parties: Public Prosecutor — Lingasamy Selva Pandi

Criminal Procedure – Convicted after trial – Sentencing – Affray

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in MA 9046/2023/01.]

24 April 2023

District Judge Christopher Goh Eng Chiang:

Introduction

1       The accused had claimed trial to a single charge of affray under s 267B of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) (“PC”). He was convicted after trial and was sentenced to seven days’ imprisonment.

2       The accused, being dissatisfied with the sentence I have imposed, now appeals against it. He did not appeal against his conviction.

The Charge

3       The charge against the accused is as follows:

“ …. that you, are charged that you, on 21 July 2021, at or about 4.15pm, along Buffalo Road, Singapore, which is a public place, did disturb the public peace by fighting with one Subramaniam s/o Veerasamy, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under section 267B of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev. Ed.).”

Summary of the facts

4       The following is a summary of the facts from the case as I found them to be at the conclusion of the trial and is derived from the agreed statement of facts[note: 1], the two sets of CCTV footage[note: 2] and the evidence of the witnesses. The summary of facts is as follows:

(a)     The incident occurred at about 1615 hours on 21 July 2021 along Buffalo Road.

(b)     Prior to the incident, the accused had been drinking and he was intoxicated. P4 shows the accused standing on Buffalo Road, oblivious to the danger posed by moving traffic. He then kicked a metal railing on the opposite side of the road before walking towards Subramaniam s/o Veerasamy (“PW3”).[note: 3]

(c)     PW3 was standing at the railing in front of the shop he was working at when the accused approached him. While they were talking, the accused hit PW3 on the head twice before PW3 retaliated by slapping the accused twice on the face. Thereafter, the CCTV footage[note: 4] showed the accused pulling PW3’s arm towards the railing. There was a minor scuffle with the accused and PW3, each on either side of the railing before they moved out of sight of the CCTV.

(d)     Whilst they were out of the field of view of the CCTV, both the accused and PW3 continued with their scuffle.

(e)     The third and final part of their continued scuffle was captured by a POLCAM[note: 5]. This occurred nearby, a short while later, on the opposite side of Buffalo Road. The footage showed that the accused and PW3 standing near a carpark gantry, having apparently ceased their hostilities. Together with them was one Subramaniam A/L Rajindra (“PW6”), a friend of the accused, who was standing between the two protagonists, trying to calm things down.

(f)     It is the accused who once again initiate the fight. From the POLCAM footage, the accused can be seen suddenly closing the distance between him and PW3, rushing past PW6, and swinging his right hand, hitting PW3 on his face.[note: 6] This caused PW3 to stumble backwards. PW3 then retaliated by picking up a wooden plank nearby and using it to hit the accused on his head.

(g)     Both the accused and PW3 suffered injuries. The accused suffered a laceration on his head that was caused by being hit by the wooden plank. PW3 suffered a nasal nose fracture and was granted medical leave from 21 – 26 July 2021.[note: 7]

Submission on sentence

Prosecution’s submission on sentence

5       The Prosecution submitted that the custodial threshold was crossed and sought a sentence of seven to 14 days’ imprisonment. The following reasons were submitted for this sentence:

(a)      The accused was on bail. When this offence was committed, the accused was on bail for other offences, including one for affray.

(b)      The accused was the aggressor.

(c)      The age discrepancy. The accused was 27 years of age at the time of the offence while PW3 was 66 years of age.

(d)      Injuries suffered. While PW3 suffered a fractured nose, it was conceded by the Prosecution that the accused was also badly hurt.

(e)      Public disquiet. The fight took place on a weekday afternoon along a moderately busy street.

(f)      Lack of remorse. The accused disputed the charge and embellished the account of the incident. He had also attempted, albeit unsuccessfully, to interfered with the evidence of PW6.

Defence’s sentencing submissions

6       The Defence submitted that a fine of $4,000 would be appropriate in the present case. The following reasons were submitted:

(a)      No group element. This was a fight between two individuals and no group element was involved.

(b)      Short duration. The altercation and scuffle only lasted for a short while.

(c)      No premeditation. The accused never intended to injure anyone, and the incident was spontaneous.

(d)      No weapons were used. The accused was unarmed.

(e)      Co-operation with the police. The accused was co-operative with the police throughout this incident.

(f)      Regret and remorse. The accused was extremely regretful and remorseful.

(g)      Minor injuries suffered. The Victim suffered relatively minor injuries compared to the accused. These were not life threatening and were not sustained on a vulnerable part of the victim.

Decision on Sentence

Punishment prescribed by law

7       The punishment prescribed by law for an offence of affray under s 267B of the PC is a term of imprisonment of up to one year and a fine of up to $5,000 or to both.

Custodial threshold for the offence of affray

8       In PP v Goh Jun Hao Jeremy [2018] SGHC 68 (“Goh Jun Hao”), the offender pleaded guilty to a charge of affray which occurred outside a night club. He had fought with one Heng by punching and kicking him. Heng suffered a nasal bone fracture and other minor injuries. The offender also suffered some minor injuries occasioned by a fall. The offender was fined $2,000 by a district court. The High Court allowed the Prosecution’s appeal and substituted the fine with a two-week imprisonment term.

9       At [36] – [37] See J held that, in determining whether the custodial threshold had been crossed, the court had to first analyse the level of harm and culpability as well as have regard to other mitigating and aggravating factors that do not relate to the commission of the offence per se. A fine would suffice where there is a low level of harm caused and a low level of culpability. On the other hand, a custodial sentence would be warranted where there is a higher level of harm and culpability.

Whether the custodial threshold had been crossed in the present case

10     Following from Goh Jun Hao, I proceeded to analyse the facts in the present case:

(a)      Degree of harm. The harm suffered by PW3 is similar to that suffered by Heng in Goh Jun Hao,[note: 8] namely a nasal bone fracture. Accordingly, I would similarly assess the degree of harm to be at least moderate to high. As highlighted by the High Court in Goh Jun Hao, a nasal bone fracture would be classified as grievous hurt under s 320(g) of the PC. I therefore disagreed with the Defence that the injuries caused to PW3 were minor.

(b)      Culpability. In Goh Jun Hao, the offender initiated the fight as Heng was walking away. He punched Heng’s face and kicked him before Heng retaliated. See J assessed the offender’s culpability as being significant.[note: 9] In the present case, the was drunk and also took needless offence at a perceived provocation from PW3. From the CCTV footage (P4) and the POLCAM footage, it was pertinently obvious that it was the accused that was the initiator of the fight in both instances. However, the accused’s acts against PW3 were, in my view, less violent than that of the offender in Goh Jun Hao. Accordingly, whilst I was prepared to assess the accused’s culpability as being significant, I found it to be less than the offender in Goh Jun Hao.

(c)      Aggravating factors. The most significant aggravating factor, in my view, was the fact that the accused was on bail for a similar offence of affray when he committed the present offence. The accused was charged for the present offence on 13 December 2021. However, he was originally charged on 18 December 2019 for four PC offences including one for affray under s 267B, two for voluntarily causing hurt on grave and sudden provocation under s 334 (including one with common intention) and one for voluntarily causing hurt under s 323. The accused pleaded guilty to the offences of affray and voluntarily causing hurt on grave and sudden provocation with common intention on 17 February 2022 and was sentenced to a fine $2,000 and an imprisonment term of five days respectively.

(d)      Other factors. The Prosecution had highlighted other aggravating factors in their sentencing submission. My assessment of these factors are as follows:

(i)        The age difference. An age difference is usually highlighted because the younger person would usually have an advantage over the older one in a fight. In the present case, the CCTV and POLCAM showed that the age difference did not appear to disadvantage PW3 in anyway. For example, it did not stop him from using the wooden plank to hit the accused on the head. Also, the actual fight itself was of an extremely short duration and my assessment is that this factor did not give the accused any significant advantage. I hence put little weight on this factor.

(ii)        Public disquiet. While the fight took place on a weekday afternoon, the CCTV and POLCAM footage does not appear to show any impact on public disorder.

11     Accordingly, having looked and the harm caused, the accused’s culpability and the aggravating factors, I assessed that the custodial threshold in the present case was crossed.

12     I next considered the mitigating factors highlighted by the Defence and my views. These were as follows:

(a)      No weapons were used. I note that no weapons were used by the accused. In the same breath, I note that PW3 had used a wooden plank to hit the accused as seen in P5.

(b)      Short duration. While the confrontation between PW3 and the accused stretched over a period of time, I agreed that the actual altercation lasted only for a short duration. There was no prolonged fight between the two of them and it did not require anyone to break it up. This is unlike in Goh Jun Hao where the fight between the offender and Heng appeared to carry on and required the intervention of the bouncers from the night club to stop it;[note: 10]

(c)      No premeditation. Generally speaking, offences of affray are committed without premeditation, hence I put little weight on this.

(d)      No group element. Whilst I accept that there was no group element in the present case, this was, in my view, not a mitigating factor but simply a lack of an aggravating factor.

(e)      Co-operation with the police. While co-operation with the police is generally considered as a mitigating factor, the facts of the case were, in my view, relatively straight forward. There was ample objective evidence to show what really happened. Therefore, while I did consider this factor, I put very little weight on it.

(f)      Remorse and regret. In my assessment, the supposed feelings of remorse and regret felt by the accused were simply because he had been convicted for the offence whilst the co-accused, PW3, was administered with a stern warning.

13     Having considered the mitigating factors in the present case, I found that there was nothing to warrant a downward adjustment of the sentence to one that was below the custodial threshold.

Conclusion

14     All considered, I was therefore of the view that the acts of the accused in the present case crossed the custodial threshold.

15     In my view, the circumstances in the present case were less egregious than in Goh Jun Hao, where the offender therein was sentenced to two weeks imprisonment. I adjudged that the appropriate sentence in the present case to be:

seven days’ imprisonment.

16     Further, as highlighted by the Prosecution, this sentence is not out of line with the median imprisonment term imposed on offenders where the custodial sentence has been held to have been crossed.

17     The accused, being dissatisfied with the sentence I have imposed now appeals against it. He is presently on bail pending the outcome of this appeal.
