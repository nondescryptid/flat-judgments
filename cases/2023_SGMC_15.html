District Judge Christopher Goh Eng Chiang:

Introduction

1       The accused claimed trial to one outrage of modesty (“OM”) charge under s 354(1) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed) (“PC”). The accused was the Chief of Operations at Doyle Dane Bernbach Worldwide Pte Ltd (“DDB”), and was alleged to have outraged the victim, an employee with Tribal Worldwide Pte Ltd (“Tribal”), a sister company of DDB, during DDB’s Dinner and Dance.

2       At the conclusion of the trial, I acquitted the accused of the said charge. The Prosecution, being dissatisfied with the order of acquittal, now appeals against it. The reasons for my order of acquittal have been set out in my Brief Oral Grounds (“Oral GD”) dated 25 November 2022. The following is an expansion of those Grounds.

The Charge

3       The charge against the accused is as follows:

“ …. that you, on 29 January 2020, at or about 10pm, at Grand Shanghai Restaurant located at 390 Havelock Road, Singapore, did use criminal force to one, Victim, Female / 25 years old (at the material time of the offence), to wit, by touching and patting the right side of the said Victim’s hips with your right hand, knowing it to be likely that you will thereby outrage her modesty, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 354(1) of the Penal Code (Cap 224, 2008 Rev Ed).”

7       The accused’s table was towards the rear of the restaurant. The CCTV footage showed that he was playing a card game with the other persons on his table just before the incident.

The accused began to make his way to the stage from his table. In doing so, he walked past the Victim. As he passed the Victim, he is alleged to have touched and patted the right side of the Victim’s hip with his right hand, knowing that his act would outrage the Victim’s modesty.

9       The Victim filed a police report on 31 January 2020 at about 1745 hours (see Exhibit P1).

Analysis of the Evidence

44     In the present case. The accused was charged with committing the offence by:

(a)     touching and patting the right side of the Victim with his right-hand hand (the actus reas), and

(b)     knowing it to be likely that he would thereby outrage her modesty (the mens rea).

45     While in most cases involving the outrage of modesty, it is essentially the evidence of the victim, against that of an accused with no independent eyewitness(es). In the present case there are three witnesses sitting at the next table who appeared to have an unobstructed line of sight of the incident and more importantly, objective evidence in the form of CCTV footage from two cameras (referred to as CH05 and CH06).

46     I will deal with the actus reus and the mens rea of the offence in turn.

Actus reus of the of the offence

47     It is the Prosecution’s case that the accused touched and patted the right side of the victim’s hips with his right hand. I note that the Prosecution witnesses have sometimes referred to this location as the buttocks. I will, however, use the description “hips” as referred to in the charge. Further, it is also the Prosecution’s case this contact was captured on CCTV and seen by the three Prosecution’s witnesses, PW1[note: 15], PW3[note: 16] and PW4, corroborating the Victim’s account.

48     Figure 2 below shows where the three Prosecution witnesses were seating and their relative line of sight. Screenshot is taken from CH06.[note: 17]

49     I accepted that the accused had placed his hand on the Victim’s right hip. The CCTV footage clearly showed that this touching occurred and in fact, the Defence has conceded that the accused has accepted this. The main dispute is whether the accused patted the Victim’s right hip.

Analysis of the CCTV footage

50     The Prosecution submitted that the CCTV footage of the incident was not clear, therefore, the court should rely on the evidence of the Prosecution’s witnesses.

51     In my opinion, the quality of the CCTV footage was actually very good. However, because the CCTV which captured the alleged act of molest was CH05, this meant that the scene was captured from some distance away. As such, because when the relevant portion of CCTV footage required zooming in, the resulting footage was not as clear as the original. However, in my view, it was not as bad as that as what the Prosecution seem to suggest. My analysis of what happened that evening is primarily based on the CCTV footage (both CH05 and CH06)[note: 18] which had been reviewed numerous times and at different speeds during the course of the trial.

53     From my review of the CCTV footage, I am unable to see where the supposed patting occurred. The accused’s first point of physical contact with the side of the Victim’s hips appears to be at 2:10/2:18 (CH05/CH06 footage) and he hand leaves the side of her hips at about 2:12/2:20 (CH05/CH06 footage) as he walks past. At no time during these two seconds (probably less than that) do we see any movement from the accused’s hand that suggested that he patted her hips no matter how many times we reviewed that particular portion of the CCTV footage and no matter how much we slowed down the footage (this refers to the footage from CH05).

54     The only evidence that the accused patted the Victim appeared to be the evidence of the three Prosecution witnesses, namely PW1, PW3 and PW4, and the Victim herself.

Evidence of the Prosecution’s witnesses

55     With respect to at least two of the Prosecution’s witness, namely PW1 and PW3, I have doubts as to the quality of their evidence as to what they saw. Both witnesses had given evidence that gave the impression that the passageway was more than adequate for the accused to pass through, without touching anyone, and in my view, implying that the accused had perhaps deliberately walked close to the Victim so that he could touch her. Further, their evidence was that they had clearly seen the accused pat the Victim’s hips.

56     However, the objective evidence, namely the CCTV footage clearly showed otherwise. CH06 showed that there was inadequate space for the accused to walk past the Victim without any contact being made as the passage was blocked by a waiter. It seemed curious that, on one hand, whilst PW1 and PW3 have given evidence that they had clearly seen the accused pat the Victim’s hip, they did not see the waiter blocking the passage. In fact, they only realised that the passageway was blocked by the waiter upon seeing the footage in CH06. They were unable to explain why they did not notice the waiter.

57     For example, PW1 was only about three to four metres away and her line of sight to the waiter was not obstructed (see figure 2 at [48]), yet it is somewhat surprising she does not recall the waiter blocking the passageway. PW3, on the other hand, tried to explain that she did not see the waiter as the accused was blocking her line of sight. However, a look at figure 2, showed otherwise. From her position beside PW1, she could clearly see if the passageway was blocked, yet inexplicably, she did not see the waiter. In her text message to the Victim (for example, see Exhibit D4, message timed at 18:53 and 19:01), she had even indicated that there was “plenty of space” and that there was no reason for the accused to squeeze past the Victim. Looking at the CCTV footages, we know that this was not the case.

58     Only PW4 gave evidence that she saw the accused pat the Victim’s hip and that the passageway to the stage was blocked by the waiter. However, when the CCTV was reviewed frame by frame, she seemed unsure when the pats on the Victim actually occurred.[note: 19] In my view, this is not surprising, PW4 was seated the furthest away of the three witnesses. While in theory, she had a direct line of sight to the scene, it must be noted that PW4 was seated and not standing When the accused hands moved from the Victim’s forearm to where her hips are, this would be below the height of the back of the restaurant’s chair and in her direct line of sight would be where PW1 was seated. While her view of the incident may not be totally blocked, it would have been partially obscured, especially when the accused walks by the witnesses’ table and the people are turning around, looking at the accused and applauding him for winning one of the top prizes, as can be seen from the CCTV footage.

59     As an aside, I note that the reactions of the three Prosecution witnesses in the CCTV footage suggested that something had happened when the accused walked past the Victim. The only question was what. Although the Prosecution witnesses have described their reaction as being “shocked”, for example, the CCTV footage suggests otherwise. Immediately after the accused had walked past the Victim (see CH06, timestamp approximately 2:15 to 2:19), both PW1 and PW3 can be seemed smiling or laughing. This appeared to be a rather strange reaction after the shock seeing a colleague supposedly just having been inappropriately touched. Perhaps the only witness who can be described as being remotely “shocked” was PW4.

60     However, if we were to assume that the witnesses were shocked because the accused had patted the Victim’s hip, this shock reaction does not gel with their reaction barely 30 seconds later. From the CCTV footage, (see CH06, timestamp 2:34 to 2:39), we can see the accused walking past the Victim and the witnesses’ table as he headed back towards his own table. As he walks past behind the Victim, she does not notice him.[note: 20] The same cannot be said for PW1 and PW4 who appear to be looking at him as he walks past their table. Yet, this does not elicit a reaction from anyone. This is strange considering that they had, barely a minute ago, been shocked by the accused’s inappropriate touch on the Victim’s hip.

61     As such, I was not convinced that they had seen the accused pat the top of the Victim’s hips area as they have claimed. In particular, PW1 and PW3’s recollection of the incident appeared premised on the fact that there was more than ample space for the accused to have walked past the Victim without any form of contact but that he had deliberately sought to squeeze past the Victim in order to touch the Victim inappropriately.

The Victim’s evidence

62     Based on the Victim’s evidence, the incident occurred just when she moved to the side to allow the accused to go past her.[note: 21]

63     I have some doubts as to whether there was any patting for the following reasons:

(a)     The Victim did not see the accused pat her hips as he had walked past behind her. As mentioned, the CCTV footage from CH05 does not appear to bear this out and there was no discernible hand movement(s) from the accused to suggest any patting occurred. In my assessment, what the Victim had described as “patting” may have been caused by the Victim moving to her right hip at that same moment as the accused squeezed past between her and the waiter in the opposite direction. This may have caused the Victim’s hip to hit against the accused’s hand as his hand moved down from her forearm to her hip area, giving her the impression that the accused had “patted” her hip.

(b)     Perhaps the Victim formed this view because, like PW1 and PW3, she had the impression that there was a lot of space behind her because she was unaware there was someone behind her,[note: 22] and that the accused had deliberately chosen to pass as close as possible to her to touch her.

(c)     I further note that in the Victim’s own evidence, she told her senior female colleague a few seconds after the incident the accused had “touched”[note: 23] and not that he had “patted” her.

64     Therefore, I was not convinced by the Victim’s evidence that the accused had “patted” (or “tapped”) her right hip or buttocks at all, let alone three times, as the Victim claimed.

The accused’s statement

65     The Prosecution had submitted[note: 24] that the accused had admitted to patting someone on the waist area in his statement of 7 February 2020[note: 25]. The following is the extract from his statement for Answer 1:

“… I remember as I was making my way up to the stage I had probably used by dominant hand, which was my right hand, by patting around the waist area to move out of the way. I could not remember how many times I had to pat each person to move out of the way.” (Emphasis added)

66     In my view, this by itself was not an unequivocal admission of his guilt as suggested by the Prosecution. Even if the accused vaguely conceded that he patted someone around the waist, I think this must be read in context of first, the entire statement and then, the entire statement. In the very next sentence of this same answer, the accused refers to the patting of each person to move out of the way and earlier in the same statement, the accused refers to the unintentional “touching” of the Victim.

Conclusion

67     Having reviewed the evidence, in particular, the CCTV footage, I am unconvinced that the Prosecution had proved that the accused had patted (or tapped) any part of the Victim’s right hips. At its very highest, I am only able to conclude that the accused’s right hand did touch the Victim’s right hip as he walked past her.

The mens rea of the offence

68     As highlighted by the Prosecution, the accused was charged under the knowledge limb of s 354(1) of the PC and not that he did not possess the intention to outrage the victim’s modesty. Therefore, the central issue of the trial was whether the accused had the necessary knowledge that his action would likely outrage the Victim’s modesty.

The legal test

69     Both the Prosecution and the Defence agreed that the legal test for the knowledge limb is set out in PP v Mohd Taufik bin Abu Bakar and another appeal [2019] SGHC 90 (“Taufik”). This test is set out at [37] – [40], where the High Court held that:

“ …….

37    To prove that the accused had sufficient knowledge for the purposes of the OM charge, it must be shown that the accused committed his acts on the victim whilst “knowing it to be likely that he will thereby outrage the modesty of that person” [emphasis added] (s 354(1) of the Penal Code). Here, the knowledge that is relevant is that of the accused, and the unexpressed feelings and thoughts of the victim is often irrelevant for the purposes of proving that knowledge. Furthermore, the accused’s knowledge must be assessed in relation to the alleged victim, as can be gleaned from the focus on the outrage of modesty of “that person”.

38    Hence, context is everything. In assessing the knowledge of the accused in the commission of the act, the objective circumstances shedding light on the accused’s state of mind must be read against the backdrop of the appropriate context.

39    For example, if an accused person touches the private parts of a stranger, the objective circumstances, read against the context of the non-existent relationship between the parties, would suggest that the accused must have known that his acts would outrage the victim’s modesty. Unless the accused can avail himself of one of the relevant exceptions in the Penal Code (such as the unsoundness of mind), any allegations by the accused that he did not know that his acts would outrage the victim’s modesty would fly in the face of logic and human experience.

40    However, in cases where the context discloses a relationship (whether romantic or not) between the parties, the objective circumstances in which the alleged OM offence was committed must be carefully assessed against the context to determine whether the accused knew at the material time that his acts would likely outrage the victim’s modesty. This is a two-stage process:

(a)    First, the objective circumstances that are patent to the accused must be determined.

(b)    Secondly, the objective circumstances in (a) must be assessed against the context in which the acts were committed.”

The objective circumstances and the context in which the touches were made.

70     The objective circumstances as well and the context in which the touches were made are mostly taken from the CCTV footage. The following is my assessment:

(a)     It is the company Dinner and Dance and the accused had won a major prize in the Lucky Draw and was walking towards the stage to collect it before it is forfeited. There are people clapping for him and looking at him as he goes to collect his prize. The path to the stage goes straight past the Victim;

(b)     Just as he reaches the place where the Victim is standing, a waiter steps behind the Victim and blocks the passageway. The Victim congratulates the accused, and he thanks her by touching her upper arm with his right hand. In my view, this appeared to be a friendly gesture.

(c)     As the passageway was blocked, his hand moves to the Victim’s right hip as he tries to get past between the Victim and the waiter. There are no obvious signs of any patting or tapping. From the CCTV footage, we can see that he had to nudge the waiter aside to get past. I accept that that the accused was trying to gently push the Victim to one side. Further, I note that the accused had the front of his body facing the back of the Victim’s body in close proximity. It may not be something that the accused was consciously aware of, but this act of placing his hand on the Victim’s hip may have been to ensure that there was no other body contact with her. This contact lasted only slightly more than two seconds.

(d)     After collecting his prize, the accused returns by the same way to his table barely 30 seconds later. He walks past the back of the Victim without noticing the Victim.

71     Having reviewed the CCTV footage, in particular, how the accused made his way past the Victim, I assessed that his movements were consistent with someone who was intent on making his way to the collect a prize and thereafter back to his table to continue with his card game.

72     His actions seen on the CCTV footage are consistent with someone who was trying to squeeze through a blocked passage. He gently pushed the Victim, a colleague, aside whilst trying to prevent any further bodily contact, and nudged the waiter, a stranger, aside. Further, the Victim’s own evidence is that the accused uttered “sorry” as he went past the Victim. In my view, this gives more credence to the accused’s description of what happened and seen together with the CCTV footage.

73     I was satisfied that while the accused did put his hand on the Victim’s hip, he did not have the knowledge that this act would outrage the Victim’s modesty.

Conclusion at the end of the trial

74     At the end of the day, the onus is always on the Prosecution to prove these allegations beyond a reasonable doubt. It is not for the accused to prove otherwise. The allegation against the accused was serious and it was fortunate that there was CCTV footage available to assist the court in coming to its conclusion.

75     In summary, I concluded the following:

(a)     That the accused touched the Victim’s forearm and put his hand her right hip as he walked past her. His touch to her hip was necessitated by the fact that the passageway to collect his prize was blocked. There was no patting.

(b)     That the accused did not have the requisite knowledge that his act would outrage the Victim’s modesty.

76     While the Victim in the present case strongly felt that her modesty had been outraged by the accused, in my view, the objective evidence told a different story. As such, I found that a conviction would be unsafe.

77     Accordingly, I found the accused not guilty and acquitted him of the charge. The Prosecution, being dissatisfied with my decision, now appeals against it.
