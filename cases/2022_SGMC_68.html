Li Peng v Seman Bin Joyo and another
[2022] SGMC 68

Case Number:Magistrate Court Suit No. 7395 of 2021
Decision Date:09 December 2022
Tribunal/Court:Magistrates Court
Coram: Vince Gui
Counsel Name(s): Muhammad Ridwan Bin Abdul (Riaz LLC) for the plaintiff; Tan Chee Kiong (Seah Ong & Partners) for the defendant.
Parties: Li Peng — Seman Bin Joyo — AAK Logistics Services Pte Ltd

Tort – Negligence – Contributory negligence – Whether motorist has duty to keep a lookout and guard against vehicles running red light

9 December 2022

Judgment reserved.

District Judge Vince Gui:

Introduction

1       The 1st Defendant’s vehicle collided into the Plaintiff’s vehicle at a traffic junction in breach of a red-light signal. At the time of the collision, the Plaintiff entered the junction in response to a green-light signal ahead of him. It is undisputed that the traffic signal had likely turned red against the 1st Defendant and green in favour of the Plaintiff for about 6 to 9 seconds before the collision. The 1st Defendant accepts that his failure to abide by the red-light signal was the primary cause of the accident. He however argues that the Plaintiff was also blameworthy for failing to notice and take evasive action against his errant driving. The central issue in this case is whether the Plaintiff was contributorily negligent.

Background facts

2       The background facts to this case are largely uncontested. The junction at which the collision took place was located along Ayer Merbau Road and Jurong Island Highway. Both Ayer Merbau Road and Jurong Island Highway were straight dual-carriage ways that laid perpendicular to each other. Ayer Merbau Road had two lanes in each direction divided by a continuous white line. Jurong Island Highway had four lanes in each direction, save that the first and last lane diverged into an additional lane respectively near the junction to house vehicles turning left and right into Ayer Merbau Road. The collision took place on a sunny afternoon of 17 July 2020. Visibility was good and the road surface was dry. This is a satellite image of the junction obtained from Google Maps:[note: 1]

3       Just before the collision, the Plaintiff’s vehicle (a lorry) was travelling along Ayer Merbau Road on the right lane. He stopped before the junction before a red-light signal. The Plaintiff’s vehicle was the first vehicle before the junction. When the traffic signal turned green, the Plaintiff moved his vehicle into the junction. It is undisputed that it would have likely taken him about 1 to 2 seconds to engage the manual gear and bring his vehicle into motion. The video footage taken from the vehicle showed him travelling for about 4 seconds without incident. At around the 5 second mark, the video blacked out. It transpired that the vehicle driven by the 1st Defendant (a trailer), travelling along Jurong Island Highway, collided into the right side of the Plaintiff’s vehicle while the Plaintiff’s vehicle was making its way across the junction.

4       I pause here to note that while the vehicle that collided into the Plaintiff’s vehicle was driven by the 1st Defendant, its registered owner was the 2nd Defendant, AAK Logistics Services Ltd. The 1st Defendant was under the employ of the 2nd Defendant at the time of the collision. For the purposes of this judgment, nothing turns on that distinction. Both Defendants were represented by the same counsel and took a unified position on liability. No arguments were made either to exempt the 2nd Defendant’s liability (for authorising the 1st Defendant to drive the vehicle as its servant and/or agent) or to apportion liability between the two Defendants. As such, purely for the sake of convenience and simplicity, I will henceforth refer to this vehicle as “the Defendant’s vehicle” and the 1st Defendant as “the Defendant”.

5       The post-accident photographs showed that the right side of the Plaintiff’s vehicle was badly damaged. The front portion of the Defendant’s vehicle (including the windscreen, hood and bumper) was also badly smashed. The impact of the collision was so huge that the Plaintiff’s passenger seated to his left was flung out of the vehicle. Both the Plaintiff and his passenger were seriously injured. The Plaintiff and his passenger received 49 and 21 days of medical leave respectively.

6       No video footage from the Defendant’s vehicle was adduced in evidence. The following facts on the Defendant’s errant driving were nevertheless evident from the Plaintiff’s video footage and the parties’ testimony.

(a)     The Defendant entered the junction in breach of a red-light signal.

(b)     In fact, the signal had turned red against the Defendant for about 6 to 9 seconds at the time of the collision. This can be deduced from the following undisputed factual premises: (1) the Plaintiff’s vehicle had travelled for about 5 seconds before the collision; (2) the Plaintiff would have taken about 1 to 2 seconds to engage the manual gear before moving off; and (3) common experience tells us that the traffic light signal against the Defendant likely turned red for about 1 to 2 seconds before the traffic light signal turned green against the Plaintiff.

(c)     According to the Defendant, he was travelling at about 50 km/h when he approached the junction. The Plaintiff did not dispute this and took the position that there is insufficient evidence to prove otherwise.

(d)     The Defendant’s vehicle was travelling on the third rightmost lane of Jurong Island Highway as he approached the junction. In other words, there were two lanes to his right and three lanes to his left. The upshot of this is that the Plaintiff’s vehicle would have crossed at least three lanes of Jurong Island Highway after entering the junction – in terms of distance – before the Defendant’s vehicle collided into him.

7       After the collision, the Defendant was charged and pleaded guilty to the offence of Dangerous Driving causing Hurt under s 61(1) read with s 64(2B)(A) of the Road Traffic Act 1961 (2020 Rev Ed). The Statement of Facts to which the Defendant pleaded guilty to stated that he was driving on the third lane of Jurong Island Highway, and that the Plaintiff was travelling in response to a green light signal while the Defendant ran a red-light signal when the collision occurred.

Parties’ submissions

8       The Plaintiff submitted that the collision was entirely the Defendant’s fault. When the Plaintiff moved off in response to the “green” traffic signal, the Defendant was nowhere close to the junction. At 50 km/h and based on the 6 to 9 seconds timeframe, the Defendant’s vehicle was likely 83 to 125 metres away from the point of collision when the traffic signal first turned red against the Defendant. At that distance, a reasonable motorist in the Plaintiff’s position would have assumed that the Defendant would bring his vehicle to a stop before the junction. It was not realistic for the Plaintiff to have stopped and waited for vehicles to his left and right to come to a stop before entering the junction. The Plaintiff submitted that the Defendant should bear full liability for the collision.

9       The Plaintiff also cited English cases and the case of Soh Lay Lian Cherlyn v Kok Mui Eng [2015] 5 SLR 53 involving similar situations of signal-abiding motorists being collided into by errant motorists running the red light. In all these cases, the court found the errant motorist fully liable for the collision. The Plaintiff cited these cases for the proposition that a motorist entering a junction with the signal lights green in his favour is entitled to assume that no fresh traffic would enter the junction from roads which the signal lights are red. His sole duty would be to look out for traffic already in the junction to the extent reasonable.

10     The Defendants submitted that there was an appreciable risk of vehicles running the red light. Referring to the majority decision in Asnah bte Ab Rahman v Li Jianlin [2016] 2 SLR 944, the Defendants submitted that motorists, like pedestrians, cannot rule out the possibility of other motorists running the red light not just shortly after the red light had turned on but also several seconds later. Further, rules 72 and 114 of the Highway Code (Cap 276, R 11, 1990 Rev Ed) (“Highway Code”) requires motorists to check left and right not only right before moving off but also right before they enter the junction. Had he seen the Defendant’s approach, he would have applied brakes to stop the vehicle and avoid the collision or minimise its impact. The Plaintiff had a clear line of sight, since there was no concrete divider to the Plaintiff’s right. The Plaintiff testified that he did not see the Defendant’s vehicle approaching until the collision. This, the Defendants submitted, must mean that he did not keep a proper lookout for the Defendant’s vehicle as he entered the junction. The Defendant’s running the red light constituted an “imminent danger” which the Plaintiff ought to have guarded against, and for such failure he should be held contributorily negligent.

Discussion

11     The main issue to be determined is whether the Plaintiff is contributorily negligent for failing to notice and take evasive action against the Defendant running the red light. Contributorily negligence connotes a claimant’s failure to take reasonable measures to guard against a foreseeable harm (Asnah at [18], read with s 3(1) of the Contributory Negligence and Personal Injuries Act (Cap 54, 2002 Rev Ed)). To succeed in establishing contributory negligence, the Defendants will have to show that the Defendant running the red light was not only reasonably foreseeable but also reasonably avoidable.

12     The distinctive feature of the present case is that the Defendant ran the red light not shortly after it had turned red but rather a whopping 6 to 9 seconds later. It was also not disputed that the Defendant was travelling at around 50 km/h when he was approaching the junction. The implication of this is that, based on the established speed and time lapse, the Defendant would have been around 83 to 125 metres away from the point of collision when the traffic signal turned red against him and green against the Plaintiff.

13     The Defendants submitted that the Plaintiff did not notice the Defendant’s vehicle approaching the junction when the traffic signal turned green against the Plaintiff. I accept that this is borne out by the evidence. While the Plaintiff testified that he had looked left and right as he moved off, he also admitted that he did not notice the Defendant’s vehicle before the collision. It was also clear from the video footage that he took no evasive action, which is consistent with his testimony that he did not notice the Defendant’s vehicle approaching.

14     Was the Plaintiff negligent in his failure to notice and guard against the Defendant’s vehicle approaching from around 83 to 125 metres away from the junction and take evasive action against the possibility of him running the red light?

15     Counsel for the Defendants, Mr Tan Chee Kiong (“Mr Tan”) referred to rule 114 of the Highway Code which exhorts drivers to do as follows when the traffic signal turns “green”:

Look to the right and left to ensure it is safe before you proceed.

He submitted that this rule requires motorists like the Plaintiff to watch out for vehicles running the red right.

16     Mr Tan also cited rule 72 of the Highway Code which states as follows:

At a junction look right, then left, then right again. Do not move off until you are sure it is safe to do so.

17     Both rules do not expressly spell out how drivers should go about ensuring “it is safe” to cross the junction. Parties also did not tender extrinsic materials on the legislative intent behind these rules. No reported decisions on the interpretation of these rules were also tendered. This appears to be the first reported decision issued on the interpretation of these rules. In my view, these rules exhort drivers to exercise their judgment call and do what is reasonably practicable depending on the circumstances of each case. The exact measures required would depend on the facts of each case. For instance, if the driver is put on notice of an imminent collision close to the junction, it may well be prudent to wait for the other vehicle to pass, slow down or come to a standstill. But if the other vehicle is far away from the junction travelling at normal speed, common sense dictates that the driver should not be concerned unnecessarily by its approach. His primary focus should be placed on the road situation directly ahead of him. Placing undue focus on vehicles travelling from afar will take his attention away from what is ahead of him which could in turn make him a road hazard.

18     In the present case, the Defendant was 83 to 125 metres away from the junction when the signal turned green against the Plaintiff. In my judgment, the Defendant’s vehicle was too far away from the junction to reasonably put the Plaintiff on notice of the possibility of a collision and take reasonable evasive action against such collision. It was not reasonably practicable for the Plaintiff to guard against the Defendant’s vehicle at such a distance. At such a great distance, it would not have been sufficient to take a quick glance at the Defendant’s vehicle. To gauge its safe approach, the Plaintiff would have had to stall his vehicle at the junction and observe the Defendant’s approach for at least several seconds. In fact, the Plaintiff would have had to wait around 9 seconds for the Defendant to clear his path, bearing in mind also that the Defendant was driving a trailer with containers. Such precautionary measures are in my view quite extraordinary and inconsistent with the realities of driving. Drivers are expected to move off shortly after traffic signals turn green. Some allowance can be given for drivers to engage their gear and look out for imminent danger. But it would be quite unrealistic to expect drivers to stall their vehicles at traffic junctions for many seconds to guard against the minute chance of a freak accident happening. Drivers should be afforded the comfort and confidence to drive through traffic signals in ordinary circumstances, subject to the caution sounded in rule 114 of the Highway Code. To hold otherwise would not only make life unbearable for drivers but also bring vehicular traffic to a gridlock.

19     Counsel for the Defendants, Mr Tan, also relied on the Court of Appeal decision of Asnah for the proposition that the risk of vehicles running red lights many seconds after it had turned red is not only foreseeable but also reasonable to guard against. While the majority in Asnah held that such risks are foreseeable and avoidable, that was in the context of pedestrians. There is a world of difference between pedestrians and drivers. In my view, it is not appropriate to conflate them both.

(a)     Pedestrians can slowly walk through the pedestrian crossing while casting their peripheral vision on the side lines. Drivers do not necessarily have the same luxury. Drivers, unlike pedestrians, are placed in a position to endanger other road users because a vehicle can turn into a lethal weapon when not operated with care. It is therefore incumbent on drivers to focus on the road situation ahead of them and look out for imminent dangers.

(b)     This problem is compounded by the fact that vehicles move much faster than humans. When one moves at the faster pace, it is only natural to focus on the situation ahead and afford less attention to the peripheral surroundings.

(c)     Drivers are also expected to move the vehicle quickly to avoid hindering motorists behind them. In these premises, it would not be reasonably practicable for the Plaintiff to continuously check for the Defendant’s approach while driving his vehicle through the junction. To do so would unduly take his attention off the road situation ahead of him which could ironically endanger other road users. Such conduct can become a recipe for disaster considering the Plaintiff was driving not just any ordinary vehicle but a heavy load lorry.

20     Looking at the matter another way, it has sometimes been said that calibrating the appropriate standard of care requires one to balance the likelihood of injury, the gravity of injury and the cost of taking precautions to guard against such risks (Asnah at [39]). In the present case, the cost of guarding against the risk of a motorist like the Defendant running the red light outweighed the likelihood of such risks eventuating. This is not a case where there was “no difficulty”, “no expense” and “no disadvantage” involved in avoiding the risk (Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v The Miller Steamship Co Pty Ltd [1967] 1 AC 617 at 643–644, cited with approval in Asnah at [40]). The costs of precautions were quite extraordinary and inconsistent with the realities of driving.

21     Mr Tan also submitted that the Plaintiff should have checked for oncoming traffic about 3 seconds after moving off. By that time, the Plaintiff’s vehicle would have gone past not only the stop line but also a pedestrian crossing located beyond the stop line. The reason for imposing this duty, he explained, was that “inevitably at traffic light junctions, traffic will build up due to red lights and there will likely be a queue of vehicles”. He submitted that to avoid rendering the duty to check for safety under rule 114 “redundant” for vehicles in the queue, rule 114 should be interpreted to read purposively to read as follows:

Look left and right to ensure it is safe before you proceed to enter the junction.

22     I have several difficulties with this argument.

(a)     First, the word in italics do not appear in rule 114; they were added by the Defendants.

(b)     Second, even assuming the provision should be read with those words added, the Defendants tendered no authorities to show that “the junction” in the present case should be demarcated by the pedestrian crossing and not the stop line before the pedestrian crossing at which the Plaintiff’s vehicle stopped.

(c)     Third, the situation of drivers in a queue moving off does not concern us; the Plaintiff was the first vehicle to move off.

(d)     Fourth, the Defendants are effectively saying all drivers have a duty to automatically look left and right for approaching traffic as they traverse the junction, even when their vehicles are in motion. As I discussed above at [17]-[19] above, this would be a tall order in ordinary circumstances. The driver should ordinarily be focussed on the road situation ahead and not be unnecessarily distracted by background traffic on the side lines. In the ordinary circumstances, it is illogical for the drivers to minimise the already slim chance of vehicles running red lights, only to significantly enlarge the risk of colliding into vehicles or pedestrians ahead of them. The alternative to this measure is to stop the Plaintiff’s vehicle once more after it has gone past the stop line and pedestrian crossing to look out for vehicles approaching from the left and right. I do not understand the Defendants to be asking this court to impose such a duty. Doing so would be wholly impractical and inconsistent with the institution of traffic signals. Indeed, as Phang JC (as he then was) held in Ong Bee Nah v Won Siew Wan (Yong Tian Choy, third party) [2005] 2 SLR(R) 455, there is – in the absence of clear and compelling circumstances to the contrary – no legal duty on a driver to slow down automatically each time he or she approaches a junction if the lights are in his or her favour (at [95]).

23     Finally, the Defendants cited the following passage in Au Jia Chuan v Chew Choon Keong [2020] SGMC 39 (“Au Jia Chuan”) at [32]:

The upshot of these observations made by the Court of Appeal [in Hum Weng Fong v Koh Siang Hong [2008] 3 SLR(R) 1137 at [23]] is that green light signals are not the be-all and end-all. They give motorists the right of way, not the right to ignore road hazards. In the face of an impending danger, motorists should take steps to avoid a collision where reasonably practicable. A motor vehicle is a dangerous weapon capable of inflicting untold harm onto other road users. With those dire consequences comes the responsibility to operate the vehicle with reasonable care.

24     It would be apparent from my foregoing analysis that the Defendant’s vehicle hardly qualifies as an “impending danger” at the time the traffic signal turned green in favour of the Plaintiff. Further, it would not have been “reasonably practicable” to avoid the collision in the present circumstances for the reasons mentioned. This was a freak accident which could not have been reasonably avoided by the Plaintiff. The facts of Au Jia Chuan are vastly different. In that case, the drivers concerned were put on notice of the collision course. The defendant in that case conceded that he noticed the traffic signal to his right (meant for vehicles travelling in the same lane as the plaintiff) turning green in conjunction with the traffic signal ahead of him and that he should have kept a proper lookout in those circumstances (at [30]). The plaintiff also conceded that the defendant’s vehicle was within his direct line of sight and his in-car video footage showed the defendant’s vehicle moving forward as the plaintiff was about to leave his stationary position (at [39]). Contributorily negligence was established in that case because both drivers could have taken reasonable steps to avoid the collision. The same cannot be said for the Plaintiff in the present case.

25     I would also add my decision is consistent with the outcome arrived at prior cases involving defendant-drivers running red lights. In all these cases, the court found the defendant wholly liable for the collision. I will discuss them briefly.

26     The first case is Soh Lay Lian Cherlyn v Kok Mui Eng [2015] 5 SLR 53 (“Soh Lay Lian Cherlyn”). The defendant’s vehicle in that case collided into the right side of the plaintiff’s vehicle when the defendant’s vehicle was making a right turn at a traffic junction with the lights in the defendant’s favour. The District Court at first instance found that the defendant, being the first vehicle turning into the junction in response to the green arrow signal, ought to have contemplated the possibility of vehicles in the opposite direction running the red light, which was not out of the course of normal human experience. The District Court went on to find the defendant 10% contributorily negligent. Lai Siu Chiu SJ reversed that decision on appeal. Lai SJ reasoned that the District Court overlooked the fact that the defendant’s vehicle had crossed three lanes of the road by the time of the collision. This meant that the red light against the plaintiff must have come on for some time and a reasonable motorist in the defendant’s position would not have expected an oncoming vehicle to run the red light (at [17]-[18]).

27     The facts of Soh Lay Lian Cherlyn are almost identical to the present facts. The Plaintiff was similarly responding to a green light signal and was the first vehicle to move into the junction. The Plaintiff’s vehicle was also turning right into Jurong Highway. The collision took place when the Plaintiff’s vehicle had already crossed about three lanes of Jurong Highway.

28     The Plaintiff also cited several English cases where the courts refused to find contributorily negligence against drivers entering junctions when the lights were in their favour. I will discuss a few.

(a)     In Joseph Eva Limited v Reeves [1938] 2 KB 393, Greene MR of the English Court of Appeal held that the respondent was entitled to assume that traffic approaching the crossing would abide by the traffic signal and was not bound to provide for the case of vehicles running the red light. He went on to say that “it does not, of course, mean that, if he had noticed [the appellant’s vehicle] in time, it was not his duty to take all reasonably possible steps to avoid coming into collision with it notwithstanding that [the appellant’s vehicle] was acting in breach of the regulations”. On the facts of that case, the respondent did not see the appellant’s vehicle and it could not be said that he was under any obligation to assume the possibility of its presence.

(b)     In Sudds v Hanscombe [1971] RTR 212, the defendant failed to notice a change in light signals and proceeded across a junction without regard for traffic coming from the main road where the lights were at green. The plaintiff entered the junction when the lights were at green in his favour, and in so crossing, he collided with the defendant’s vehicle. Widgery LJ of the English Court of Appeal held that the plaintiff was entitled to assume that no fresh traffic would enter the junction when the lights had already changed in his favour. He held that the plaintiff’s duty was to look to his front and see if there is an obstruction to his path and keep an eye to his front to watch where he was going, behaving as a reasonably careful motorist would, to look out for traffic already in the junction which might perhaps obtrude into his line of traffic (at 215).

(c)     In Hopwood Homes v Kennerdine [1975] RTR 70, the plaintiff was involved in an accident at a junction controlled by traffic lights. He entered the junction when the lights were green for him. The lights turned amber just as he passed them. He took up position with the front of his vehicle angled towards his right, intending to turn right. The plaintiff saw the defendant coming towards him. He was at that time stopped, with his vehicle angled and his offside trafficator flashing. The plaintiff thought the defendant had ample time to stop. He moved forward about four to five feet. When he realised the defendant had no intention of stopping, even though the lights were against the defendant, he braked. Just as his vehicle came to a halt, the two vehicles collided. The court at first instance found the plaintiff contributorily negligent and reduced his recoverable damages by 10%. The English Court of Appeal overturned the ruling on appeal. Edmund Davies LJ held that there was no reason why the plaintiff should have apprehended that so reckless an act as the defendant here indulged in would be executed by him (at 85). Scarman LJ held that bearing in mind the exposed position of the plaintiff’s vehicle in the middle of the junction, and the need to make with all expedition his way out of that exposed position, the plaintiff correctly judged that it was not only safe for him to move across the junction but also reasonable to do so. When he realised the defendant had no intention of stopping, the plaintiff braked and stopped, so that his vehicle only travelled four or five feet, and by then there was ample width of road that would have enabled the defendant, had it driven properly, to circumnavigate it without accident (at 85-86).

29     The Defendants argued that Soh Lay Lian Cherlyn and the English cases should be reassessed on the basis that they were decided before the Court of Appeal decision of Asnah, where the majority held that drivers running red lights is a reasonably foreseeable risk. As I have explained above at [19], Asnah does not assist the Defendants. The majority’s decision deals squarely with the duty of pedestrians. There was no suggestion that drivers would be subjected to the same duty that is placed upon pedestrians. In any event, rule 22, which requires pedestrians to wait for vehicles to “come to a standstill”, is worded differently from rule 114 which requires drivers to ensure “it is safe” before they proceed in response to a green-light signal. While rule 114 did not appear to have considered in those decisions, whether a claimant has complied with rule 114 is ultimately a factual question that turns on the facts of each case. On the present facts, I have explained above that I am not persuaded that the Plaintiff was contributorily negligent in failing to take evasive action against the risk of the Defendant’s vehicle running the red light.

Conclusion

30     For these reasons, I find the Defendants fully liable for the collision. Parties asked for liability to be decided first with the quantum of damages to be assessed thereafter. I will hear parties on the issuance of directions for the assessment of damages and costs.
