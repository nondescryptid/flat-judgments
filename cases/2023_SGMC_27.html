Judah Jay v Gwendoline Goh Cuixian and another
[2023] SGMC 27

Case Number:Magistrates' Court Suit No 13261 of 2020, HC/DCA 5/2023
Decision Date:03 May 2023
Tribunal/Court:Magistrates Court
Coram: Jonathan Toh Jun Hian
Counsel Name(s): Phipps Jonathan (LegalStandard LLP) for the plaintiff; Fong Mun Yung Gregory John (Fong & Fong LLC) for the defendants.
Parties: Judah Jay — Gwendoline Goh Cuixian — Jasper Lee

Tort – Misrepresentation – Fraud and deceit

Restitution – Unjust Enrichment – Doctrine of ministerial receipt – Total failure of consideration

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in HC/DCA 5/2023.]

3 May 2023

District Judge Jonathan Toh Jun Hian:

1       The plaintiff made a failed investment in a product known as “Ufun”, which turned out to be a pyramid scheme. He sued the defendants for introducing him to Ufun. He claimed that they knowingly made false representations to induce him to invest.

2       After the trial, the plaintiff filed closing submissions which abandoned his claim in fraud. He advanced arguments solely for a claim in unjust enrichment. Neither the claim in unjust enrichment nor the abandoned fraud claim was made out on the evidence and I dismissed the plaintiff’s claim. The plaintiff has filed an appeal and I set out my detailed grounds of decision.

Introduction

3       The plaintiff is a businessman and the principal of the company Fire Terminator International Pte Ltd, whose main business is manufacturing and selling firefighting products.

4       The defendants are husband and wife. The plaintiff and the 2nd defendant came to know each other because the plaintiff’s company used the 2nd defendant’s employer for freight forwarding services. The 2nd defendant later introduced his wife, the 1st defendant, to the plaintiff in the context of the UFun scheme. The 2nd defendant was himself invested in the Ufun scheme, and was the plaintiff’s “upline”. This meant that within the scheme, the 2nd defendant was the one who introduced the plaintiff and benefitted from the plaintiff’s participation.

5       The plaintiff’s witnesses were himself and his brother-in-law, one Mr Anthony Cruze. The gist of Mr Cruze’s evidence was that the plaintiff invested SGD 15,000 on Mr Cruze’s behalf in the Ufun scheme and Mr Cruze witnessed the plaintiff hand this money in cash to the 2nd defendant.

6       The defendants appeared as witnesses and also called the 2nd defendant’s father, one Mr Lee Ser Tiong, as a witness. The gist of Mr Lee’s evidence was that he was the 2nd defendant’s “upline”. He received the plaintiff’s investment of SGD 14,500 from the 2nd defendant and handed it to the Malaysian principal of the Ufun scheme.

7       Participants in the Ufun scheme purchased a digital currency called Utokens. Utokens could be used to purchase goods and services on the Ufun platform. Utokens were also expected to appreciate in value and be exchanged for cash.

8       Existing participants in the Ufun scheme could earn bonus Utokens by opening new accounts or sponsoring new participants to open accounts.

9       On 24 April 2015, the plaintiff received news that the Ufun scheme was an illegal pyramid scheme. Thereafter, Ufun collapsed and Utokens became worthless.

The parties’ respective cases

The plaintiff’s case

10     The plaintiff’s primary pleaded claim was in fraud. He claimed that there was a first meeting at a restaurant in Clarke Quay initiated by the 2nd defendant where the defendants made representations about the Ufun scheme to persuade him to invest.[note: 1] The defendants allegedly represented that they and their family members successfully invested in the Ufun scheme with significant returns, that the Singapore Stock Exchange had approved the listing of the Ufun scheme, and that there would be a substantial increase in its valuation after its listing.[note: 2]

11     A few days after the first meeting, the 1st defendant called the plaintiff to request for a further lunch meeting on 22 December 2014. At this meeting, the 1st defendant repeated the earlier representations and made further representations that:[note: 3]

(a)     if the plaintiff invested USD 10,000 through the 1st defendant, she would open an investment account for him;

(b)     the 1st defendant was closely involved with the owners and promoters of Ufun;

(c)     the plaintiff would be able to withdraw his capital and returns at any time;

(d)     the minimum investment sum was USD 10,000;

(e)     there was a guaranteed return on investment of 20% per annum;

(f)     Ufun was scheduled to be listed within three to four months;

(g)     the investment sum would be transferred immediately to the fund with a receipt from the fund confirming the purchase of shares sent to the plaintiff.

12     Relying on these representations, the plaintiff decided to invest in the fund. The plaintiff’s case of what he paid to the defendants changed significantly over the course of the trial:

(a)     The plaintiff originally pleaded that he paid a total sum of SGD 37,000.[note: 4] The first payment was made by way of the plaintiff’s DBS cheque no. 024233 dated 22 December 2014 in the sum of USD 11,500. The balance payments were not specifically pleaded, save that they were made by the plaintiff to the defendants in his office.

(b)     In the plaintiff’s affidavit of evidence-in-chief (the “plaintiff’s AEIC”), he maintained that the total sum paid was SGD 37,000. The first payment made was by way of cheque dated 22 December 2014 in the sum of USD 11,500.[note: 5] The second payment was made approximately one week after the first payment, the 2nd defendant met the plaintiff in his office where his brother-in-law, one Mr Andrew Cruze, was introduced to the 2nd defendant. At that meeting, the plaintiff handed USD 10,000 in cash to the 2nd defendant.[note: 6]

(c)     Following the first two payments, the plaintiff stated in his AEIC that he made further payments amounting to USD 15,500 to the defendants.[note: 7] He gave no details about when or how these payments were made.

(d)     During his oral examination-in-chief at trial, the plaintiff changed his case on the sums that he paid to the defendants. He testified that he invested USD 37,000 and not SGD 37,000. On this basis, he claimed that he invested a sum of SGD 44,000 based on an SGD to USD exchange rate of 1.45.[note: 8] This was not mathematically correct, as USD 37,000 multiplied by 1.45 resulted in SGD 53,650 and not SGD 44,000.

(e)     The plaintiff then stated that the three payments were wrongly pleaded. He paid three sums in SGD and not USD.

(f)     The first sum was SGD 14,500 paid to the 1st defendant on 22 December 2014.[note: 9] He explained that after giving the pleaded cheque for USD 11,500 to the 1st defendant, she informed the plaintiff that cheques were not accepted. As such, the plaintiff gave her SGD 14,500 in cash.

(g)     The plaintiff testified that the second sum was SGD 14,500[note: 10] and not USD 10,000. According to the plaintiff’s AEIC, the sum was handed over to the 2nd defendant in cash in the plaintiff’s office about a week after the first payment in the presence of Andrew Cruze.[note: 11] In closing submissions, the plaintiff changed his case yet again. He took the final position that a week after 22 December 2014, the 2nd defendant followed the plaintiff to the bank where this second sum was withdrawn and handed to the 2nd defendant.[note: 12] There was no longer any mention of Andrew Cruze.

(h)     As for the third payment, the plaintiff changed his evidence from USD 15,500 in his AEIC to SGD 15,000 during his examination-in-chief.[note: 13] No explanation was provided for this change. The plaintiff also provided no evidence on when or how this sum was paid. It was the plaintiff’s witness, Anthony Cruze, who testified that he saw the plaintiff hand the cash of SGD 15,000 to the 2nd defendant at the plaintiff’s office between 17 and 20 April 2015.

13     The plaintiff’s case was that he later learnt that the representations made to him were untrue and that the defendants made the representations fraudulently.[note: 14] He claimed for the return of SGD 44,000 on the basis of fraud and total failure of consideration.[note: 15]

The defendants’ case

14     The defendants denied making the alleged representations to the plaintiff.

15     The defendant’s case was that the 2nd defendant initially told the plaintiff about the Ufun platform as a way to promote the plaintiff’s company’s products.[note: 16] This was in response to the plaintiff’s complaint that he had spent a substantial amount to showcase his company at an exhibition, without significant returns.[note: 17] The 2nd defendant told the plaintiff that there were seminars organised by Ufun for interested participants and that he should attend the seminars to decide whether to participate in Ufun.[note: 18]

16     The plaintiff decided to attend a Ufun seminar and requested that the 2nd defendant accompany him, which he did.[note: 19] The next day, the plaintiff told the 2nd defendant that he was convinced of Ufun’s viability as an investment and informed the 2nd defendant that he wished to invest USD 10,000, which was the equivalent of SGD 14,500.[note: 20] The plaintiff initially wanted to pay by cheque. The 2nd defendant informed him that Ufun did not accept cheques. The parties then went to the bank where the plaintiff handed SGD 14,500 in cash to the 2nd defendant.[note: 21]

17     After receiving the sum of SGD 14,500, the 2nd defendant handed it to his father, Lee Ser Tiong, for the purpose of handing it to Ufun’s management or administrative personnel.[note: 22]

18     The 2nd defendant claimed that he made no representations to the plaintiff about Ufun. The plaintiff was independently convinced by what he heard at the seminar and decided to invest.[note: 23]

19     All of these events occurred before the meeting between the 1st defendant and the plaintiff on 22 December 2014. The 1st defendant met with the plaintiff at the 2nd defendant’s request. The purpose of the meeting was to show the plaintiff how to use the Ufun platform.[note: 24] At the meeting, the 1st defendant informed the plaintiff on the 2nd defendant’s behalf that he was officially enrolled in the Ufun scheme and assisted the plaintiff with accessing his Ufun account online.[note: 25] As such, this was not a meeting where the 1st defendant made representations to induce the plaintiff to invest in Ufun.

20     According to the defendants, the dinner at Clarke Quay took place only after the plaintiff had attended the seminar and joined the Ufun scheme.[note: 26]

The factual issues

21     Given the differences in the parties’ cases, the following factual issues had to be determined:

(a)     Whether the pleaded representations were made by the 1st and/or 2nd defendants to the plaintiff to persuade him to participate in the Ufun scheme.

(b)     If the pleaded representations were made, whether they were false, and whether the defendants made them fraudulently.

(c)     How much money did the plaintiff pay to the defendants for the Ufun scheme.

My findings

Were the pleaded representations made?

22     I preferred the defendants’ account of events as it was unchallenged in cross-examination and more consistent with the objective evidence.

23     First, the defendants’ account of how the plaintiff was introduced to the Ufun scheme went unchallenged at trial. In cross-examination, the plaintiff’s counsel did not confront the 1st and 2nd defendants about the alleged representations made by them to the plaintiff. He also failed to challenge the defendants’ AEIC evidence on this point. He did not challenge the 1st defendant’s evidence that she had no contact with the plaintiff prior to him investing in Ufun.[note: 27] He also did not challenge the 2nd defendant’s evidence that he merely accompanied the plaintiff to a Ufun seminar and did not entice the plaintiff to invest in Ufun.[note: 28] The entire issue was completely left out of the cross-examination.

24     Second, the plaintiff abandoned his claim in fraudulent misrepresentation in his closing submissions filed after trial on 20 May 2022. He did not make submissions as to: (a) whether the representations were made, (b) why they were false, and (c) why they were made fraudulently. Instead, the plaintiff’s closing submissions were focused solely on the cause of action in unjust enrichment for total failure of consideration. The thrust of the plaintiff’s submissions was that he paid SGD 44,000 to the defendants but never received any consideration for the payment.

25     On the other hand, the plaintiff was confronted by the defendants’ counsel in cross-examination on his assertions about the alleged representations made to him. The plaintiff confirmed that apart from the defendants and him, no one else was present at the meetings where these alleged representations were made. Further, he conceded that he had no documentary or circumstantial evidence to prove the representations.[note: 29] It was nothing more than the plaintiff’s word against the defendants’.

26     The objective evidence supported the defendants’ version of events.

(a)     In support of their assertion that the plaintiff had attended a Ufun seminar, the defendants produced sample presentation slides that they said were used at the seminar.[note: 30] This suggested, at minimum, that there were such seminars where attendees would learn of the Ufun scheme.

(b)     On 22 December 2014 when the plaintiff and the 1st defendant met, the 1st defendant sent an email to the plaintiff with an attachment containing the plaintiff’s identity card number, username and password of his Ufun account, email address, and mobile number.[note: 31] It also stated that it was for the package “Four Stars $10,000”.

(c)     This showed that before this email was sent, the plaintiff had already met with at least one of the defendants and had provided his personal particulars and details. This must have been done with sufficient time for the username and password of the plaintiff’s Ufun account to be generated.

(d)     The provision of the username and password also indicated to me that the plaintiff’s Ufun account had already been opened. This was consistent with defendants’ case that the plaintiff had already decided to invest and had paid the sum of SGD 14,500 (equivalent to USD 10,000) to the 2nd defendant prior to the meeting on 22 December 2014. The purpose of this meeting was for the 1st defendant to assist the plaintiff to access the Ufun platform.

27     Given that the objective evidence was consistent with the defendants’ version of events, which remained entirely unchallenged at trial, I found their version of how the plaintiff was introduced to Ufun and opened his Ufun account as set out at [14] – [20] above to be true on a balance of probabilities. In summary, I found that the plaintiff came to invest in Ufun after attending a seminar conducted by Ufun, and that the defendants did not make the alleged representations to persuade the plaintiff to invest in Ufun.

The sums paid by the plaintiff to the defendants to invest in Ufun

28     I move on to the next set of factual issues relating to how much was paid by the plaintiff to the defendants.

29     To recap the parties’ positions, the plaintiff’s final case was that he paid the following sums to the defendants.

(a)     On 22 December 2014, a sum of SGD 14,500 in cash to the 1st defendant.[note: 32]

(b)     In the following week, a sum of SGD 14,500 in cash to the 2nd defendant at the bank.[note: 33]

(c)     Between 17 and 20 April 2015, a sum of SGD 15,000 to the 2nd defendant in the plaintiff’s office in the presence of Anthony Cruze.[note: 34]

30     The defendants’ case was that they only received one sum of SGD 14,500 from the plaintiff.[note: 35] This was paid on the day after the plaintiff attended the Ufun seminar. The 1st defendant accompanied the plaintiff to the bank to receive the money in cash. The defendants did not receive the second or third payments as alleged by the plaintiff.

31     I again preferred the defendants’ case and found that the plaintiff only paid one sum of SGD 14,500 to the 2nd defendant, which was acknowledged by the 1st defendant.

The first payment of SGD 14,500

32     The plaintiff’s account was not trustworthy and it appeared to be an ex-post rationalisation of the evidence adduced by the defendants.

33     As a starting point, as I detailed at [12] above, the plaintiff’s account evolved in the course of trial.

(a)     The plaintiff stated in the Original SOC filed on 22 December 2020 and in his AEIC sworn on 28 May 2021 that he made the first payment of USD 11,500 by way of cheque at a meeting between him and the 1st defendant on 22 December 2014. This was very different from his evidence at trial that he paid SGD 14,500 in cash to the 1st defendant. His explanation in cross-examination was that his memory of the true events was only jogged later.[note: 36] However, there was no explanation of what jogged his memory between the swearing of his AEIC and the trial.

(b)     There was also no mathematical connection between USD 11,500 and SGD 14,500. The plaintiff’s evidence was that he used an exchange rate of USD 1: SGD 1.45. However, the numbers did not work out mathematically. SGD 14,500 would have been USD 10,000, not USD 11,500.

34     In contrast, the defendant’s case remained consistent. The 2nd defendant’s evidence was that he received a sum of SGD 14,500 in cash from the plaintiff on the day after they attended the Ufun seminar. This was the defendants’ consistent pleaded position from the original Defence filed on 28 January 2021 at [4]. It was maintained in the 2nd defendant’s AEIC and at trial.

35     The defendants’ case was also consistent with the objective evidence.

(a)     The defendants relied on an email from the 1st defendant to the plaintiff dated 22 December 2014 stating that she received a sum of SGD 14,500 from the plaintiff. The 2nd defendant explained that he asked the 1st defendant to send this email to acknowledge receipt of the plaintiff’s funds that were earlier paid to the 2nd defendant.[note: 37]

(b)     The attachment in the email sent by the 2nd defendant to the plaintiff on 22 December 2014[note: 38] contained the username and password of the plaintiff’s Ufun account and indicated that it was for the package “Four Stars $10,000”. This indicated that the payment was already earlier made prior to the meeting on 22 December 2014 and the plaintiff had already provided his personal particulars and there was sufficient time in between for his Ufun account to be opened and the username and password to be generated.

36     In conclusion, I preferred the defendants’ version of events and rejected the plaintiff’s account.

The alleged second payment of SGD 14,500

37     The plaintiff’s final case in closing submissions on this alleged second payment was that a week after 22 December 2014 where the 2nd defendant followed the plaintiff to the bank and the sum was withdrawn.[note: 39]

38     I could not accept this.

39     First, the plaintiff’s evidence on this point changed significantly throughout the course of trial.

(a)     In the plaintiff’s AEIC, he claimed that a week after the first payment, he handed USD 10,000 in cash to the 2nd defendant in his office in the presence of the plaintiff’s brother-in-law, Andrew Cruze.[note: 40]

(b)     His testimony at trial was that he handed cash of SGD 14,500 and not USD 10,000 to the 2nd defendant. He did not change the other details.[note: 41]

(c)     In closing submissions, the plaintiff submitted that he paid this second sum to the 2nd defendant who had accompanied the plaintiff to the bank. In other words, the plaintiff was no longer maintaining that the sum was paid in his office in the presence of Andrew Cruze.[note: 42]

(d)     This new submission was entirely at odds with the plaintiff’s pleaded case, his AEIC evidence and his evidence at trial. The plaintiff’s eventual case was devoid of credibility.

40     Further, the plaintiff had no evidence apart from his bare assertion that this second payment was made. He did not call Andrew Cruz to give evidence. He did not show any bank or other financial statements supporting a payment of SGD 14,500 on or around 29 December 2014. There were no references to this second payment in the WhatsApp exchanges or emails between the parties.

41     This struck me as odd for the following reasons:

(a)     First, the plaintiff was an educated individual and an experienced businessman. He was careful to document his dealings in relation to the first payment. According to his evidence at trial, at the meeting between him and the 1st defendant on 22 December 2014, he took pains to have her fill in a payment voucher recording receipt of USD 11,500.[note: 43] According to the 1st defendant, at the meeting, the plaintiff asked her to send him an email acknowledging the receipt of SGD 14,500 from him.[note: 44] She sent the email dated 22 December 2014 accordingly.[note: 45] The plaintiff also relied on this email as evidence that he paid SGD 14,500 to the defendants.[note: 46]

(b)     Given the care that the plaintiff took to ensure that the first payment of SGD 14,500 was documented, it appeared odd that there was absolutely no documentation for the second payment. There were no emails or other written correspondence such as mobile messages to show that the plaintiff had handed a second sum of SGD 14,500 to the defendants for the Ufun scheme.

42     Given the plaintiff’s unreliable evidence, I found on the balance of probabilities that this alleged second payment of SGD 14,500 was never made.

The alleged third payment of SGD 15,000

43     The plaintiff’s case was that between 17 and 20 April 2015, the third payment of SGD 15,000 was paid to the 2nd defendant in the plaintiff’s office in the presence of Anthony Cruze.[note: 47] The defendants denied receiving this payment.

44     I was unable to find that the plaintiff proved his case.

45     As a starting point, the plaintiff adduced no objective evidence of the payment having been made. That being the case, much turned on the credibility of the parties and their witnesses.

46     I start with the plaintiff’s evidence.

(a)     In the plaintiff’s AEIC at [15], he alleged that he made “further payments amount to USD 15,500 to the [d]efendants”. The phrase suggested that the payments were made over several tranches. His AEIC did not specify how the payments were made.

(b)     This contradicted his own witness’ evidence. Mr Anthony Cruze’s AEIC stated that he witnessed the plaintiff handing SGD 15,000 to the 2nd defendant at a meeting between 17 and 20 April 2015 at the plaintiff’s office.[note: 48]

(c)     At trial, the plaintiff changed his evidence. He aligned himself with Mr Anthony Cruze’s evidence and stated that he paid SGD 15,000 in a single tranche to the 2nd defendant at a meeting in his office. No good reason was given for this change. It appeared to me that the plaintiff was simply changing his evidence to match Mr Cruze’s.

47     The defendants’ position was that this payment was never made and they had no knowledge of it.

48     There was no objective evidence of this alleged third payment. There was no record that the plaintiff had withdrawn this money from any bank account to pay to the 2nd defendant. There was no mention of the payment in any of the WhatsApp chats or emails between the parties. For the same reasons stated at [41] above, I found this odd.

49     Mr Anthony Cruze’s evidence is nothing more than a bare assertion on his part that contradicted the plaintiff’s initial evidence. Given the changes in the plaintiff’s case, I found it untrustworthy and preferred the defendants’ case.

50     In the circumstances, I found on the balance of probabilities that this alleged third payment was not made as well.

The plaintiff’s pleaded claim in fraudulent misrepresentation

51     The elements of the tort of deceit are as follows (Panatron Pte Ltd v Lee Cheow Lee [2001] 2 SLR(R) 435 at [14]):

(a)     there must be a representation of fact made by words or conduct:

(b)     the representation must be made with the intention that it should be acted upon by the plaintiff, or by a class of persons which includes the plaintiff;

(c)     it must be proved that the plaintiff had acted upon the false statement;

(d)     it must be proved that the plaintiff suffered damage by so doing; and

(e)     the representation must be made with knowledge that it is false; it must be wilfully false, or at least made in the absence of any genuine belief that it is true

52     The plaintiff’s pleaded cause of action was in the tort of deceit. He pleaded that:

(a)     Representations were made to him by the defendants (SOC (A1), [3], [5]).

(b)     The representations were made to persuade him to invest in Ufun (SOC(A1), [3]).

(c)     He acted upon the representations by investing SGD 44,000 in Ufun (SOC (A1), [6]).

(d)     He later discovered that the representations were untrue (SOC (A1), [9]).

(e)     The defendants made the representations knowing that they were false or recklessly not caring whether they were true or false (SOC (A1), [10]).

53     As stated above at [24], the plaintiff abandoned the pleaded claim in fraudulent misrepresentation:

(a)     The plaintiff’s opening statement made no mention of whether the alleged representations made by the defendants were untrue, or whether the defendants had made them fraudulently or recklessly.

(b)     The defendants’ case that they made no representations to the plaintiff to induce him to invest in Ufun, and that the plaintiff had attended a seminar conducted by Ufun and was independently convinced of the viability of the investment was not challenged in cross-examination. The plaintiff’s case on fraudulent misrepresentation was not put to the defendants.

(c)     The plaintiff’s closing submissions did not address the claim in fraudulent misrepresentation.

54     In any case, I already found that the plaintiff came to invest in Ufun after attending a seminar conducted by Ufun, and that the defendants did not make the pleaded representations to persuade the plaintiff to invest in Ufun (see [27] above). There was also no evidence that even if the representations were made, the defendants had made them knowing that they were false, or recklessly, not caring if they were false.

55     While the standard of proof of a claim in fraud is the civil standard on a balance of probabilities, more evidence is required than would be the situation in an ordinary civil case (Chua Kwee Chen and others (as Westlake Eating House) and another v Koh Choon Chin [2006] 3 SLR(R) 469 at [39]). The plaintiff fell far short of the standard required in this case.

The plaintiff’s claim in unjust enrichment

56     The plaintiff’s claim in unjust enrichment is pleaded at SOC (A1), [13], which reads:

In the premises, the consideration for the payment of the said sum of S$44,000 has wholly failed, and the said sum of S$44,000 is repayable by the Defendants to the Plaintiff.

57     In his closing submissions, the plaintiff elaborated that the failure of consideration was due to the fact that the plaintiff was not granted access to the UFUN account or platform.[note: 49] However, this material fact was not pleaded.

58     To succeed in a claim for unjust enrichment, a plaintiff must prove the following elements (Skandinaviska Enskilda Banken AB (Publ), Singapore Branch v Asia Pacific Breweries (Singapore) Pte Ltd [2011] 3 SLR 540 at [110]):

(a)     the defendants were enriched;

(b)     the enrichment was at the plaintiff’s expense;

(c)     an unjust factor is present which makes it is unjust to allow the defendant to retain the enrichment; and

(d)     the defendant has no defences available to it.

59     I begin with the issue of whether the defendants were enriched.

Were the defendants enriched?

60     The first issue is whether the defendants were enriched by the payment of SGD 14,500 by the plaintiff to the 2nd defendant.

61     It is not disputed that the sum of SGD 14,500 was paid by the plaintiff to the 2nd defendant. The defendant’s case was that they were not enriched because they did not receive a personal benefit from the money, but promptly handed it on for the purpose that it was intended. According to the 2nd defendant, he promptly handed the money to Lee Ser Tiong, who was his father and “upline” within the Ufun scheme, for the purpose of handing the sum to Ufun’s management or administrative personnel.[note: 50] Subsequently, at the meeting of 22 December 2014, the 1st defendant met with the plaintiff and showed him how to access his account. As such, the plaintiff obtained the benefit of his investment in Ufun.

62     The High Court in Zhou Weidong v Liew Kai Lung and others [2018] 3 SLR 1236 at [54] summarised the doctrine of ministerial receipt as follows:

A defendant who receives assets as an agent and passes them to his principal may be able to escape liability in unjust enrichment on the basis that he received the assets ministerially, and not for his own use and benefit (Chua Kwee Sin v Venerable Sek Meow Di [2013] SGHC 265 at [54]; Goff & Jones: The Law of Unjust Enrichment (Sweet & Maxwell, 9th Ed, 2016) (Charles Mitchell, Paul Mitchell & Stephen Watterson eds) (“Goff & Jones”) at para 4-68). This has also been referred to as the agency defence (Andrew Burrows, The Law of Restitution (Oxford University Press, 3rd Ed, 2011) (“Burrows”) at p 559).

63     The sum of SGD 14,500 was never meant to be paid to the defendants for their benefit. It was paid by the plaintiff to the 2nd defendant for the purpose of paying it to Ufun in order for the plaintiff to open the Ufun account and obtain Utokens.

64     I found that the 2nd defendant did receive the sum of SGD 14,5000 ministerially and assisted the plaintiff in sending the money on, and that the plaintiff did receive a Ufun account where his Utokens resided. As such, the defence of ministerial receipt was established. I elaborate below.

65     First, the 2nd defendant and Lee Ser Tiong both gave evidence that the sum of SGD 14,5000 was received by the 2nd defendant and passed to Lee Ser Tiong. Lee Ser Tiong then passed the sum to a representative from Ufun.

(a)     The 2nd defendant’s evidence was that the sum was passed to his father and “upline”, Lee Ser Tiong.[note: 51] Lee Ser Tiong came to court to give evidence was that he received the cash from the 2nd defendant and handed it to Ufun.[note: 52] He explained that under Ufun’s system, he would assist to collect money from his “downlines”. The personal particulars of these new investors would be recorded and the money collected. He would then hand the money and the particulars to representatives from Ufun. The accounts would then be opened for the new investors.[note: 53] After the accounts were opened, the new investors would be given their username and password.[note: 54]

(b)     In particular, Lee Ser Tiong dealt with a specific individual from Ufun’s finance department known to him as “Manson”. Manson would send individuals from Malaysia to Singapore to collect cash from the new investors through Lee Ser Tiong.[note: 55] An average sum of SGD 100,000 in cash was handed over by Lee Ser Tiong to these individuals two to three times a week.[note: 56]

(c)     Mr Lee did not produce any documents or records relating to the SGD 14,500 or other sums handed to Ufun. When questioned, he explained that CAD had investigated the Ufun scheme and had taken away his entire computer.[note: 57] He also explained that he had lost his phone and no longer had access to old WhatsApp or WeChat messages in 2014 to 2015.[note: 58]

(d)     I pause to note that in cross-examination, the plaintiff’s counsel sought to ask Lee Ser Tiong if he was aware that bringing more than SGD 20,000 in cash over the border was a crime in Singapore. The defendant’s counsel objected to the question on the basis that no claim was being made against Lee Ser Tiong. I allowed the objection and disallowed the question on the basis that I did not think it relevant to the issues in dispute. In particular, in my view, whether or not Lee Ser Tiong knew that bringing SGD 20,000 in cash over the border was a crime was not relevant to the question of whether the 1st and 2nd defendants were personally enriched by the payment of the SGD 14,500.

66     The 2nd defendant and Lee Ser Tiong’s account of events was supported by the fact that the plaintiff’s Ufun account was created and the plaintiff was provided with his username and password.

(a)     The defendants’ evidence was that the plaintiff’s account was created, and the plaintiff’s username and password was given to him by the 1st defendant’s email of 22 Dec 2014.[note: 59] By as early as 22 December 2014, the plaintiff was provided with the username and password for his Ufun account.

(b)     On 24 April 2015, after the news of Ufun being a pyramid scheme broke out, the plaintiff asked the 2nd defendant over WhatsApp chat for his password for his Ufun account. The 2nd defendant responded on the same day at timestamp 10:36:39 to provide a user ID and password (which was now different from the original password provided), and also asked the plaintiff: “Can log in?”.[note: 60]

67     These exchanges showed that the plaintiff’s Ufun account existed and that his log in details were provided to him.

68     The plaintiff maintained at trial that he never received the benefit of the Ufun account and was never able to log in with the details provided to him. This was not borne out by the evidence.

(a)     After the 2nd defendant asked the plaintiff “Can log in?”, the plaintiff responded on the same day at timestamp 14:38:43. He did not say that he could not log in. Instead, he told the 2nd defendant “Jesper pls stop selling fire terminator product on UToken…” and “may I know you told me that I can return my Utoken to the company…”.[note: 61]

(b)     The 2nd defendant responded at timestamp 15:36:15 pm to say, “…you can sell whatever TRADABLE UTOKENS you see in your screen” and proceeded to give the plaintiff instructions to do so.

(c)     At timestamp 15:37:40, the plaintiff said “Ok pls do that for me Bro tks lot”. The 2nd defendant replied at timestamp 15:38:00 “How many tradable utokens u have?” At timestamp 15:39:40, the plaintiff replied “Bro I want to liquidate my position and come out but not for 5000”.[note: 62]

(d)     Later in the day, at timestamp 16:03:34, the plaintiff again sought assistance from the 2nd defendant to “sell all what I have”.[note: 63] The 2nd defendant then responded to say that he was “super not free” and told the plaintiff to “Log in and go to UTRADE: Selling process as below”.

69     These exchanges revealed the following points:

(a)     The 2nd defendant did not know how many tradeable Utokens the plaintiff had in his account.

(b)     The 2nd defendant had gave the plaintiff his password and log in details, as well as instructions on how to sell Utokens.

(c)     In response to the 2nd defendant asking the plaintiff for the number of tradeable Utokens the plaintiff possessed, the plaintiff was able to answer that if he liquidated his position, he would only have $5,000.

(d)     This showed that the plaintiff had access to his Ufun account as he could check the number of tradeable Utokens that he had, and could tell the 2nd defendant what the value of his Utokens would be if he sold them.

(e)     These exchanges showed that the plaintiff not only had an account containing UTokens that he could access, he also knew that he was selling his company’s products on the Ufun platform.

70     Given that the plaintiff did have a functioning Ufun account which contained Utokens, it was likely that the SGD 14,500 that was handed to the 2nd defendant was handed on to Lee Ser Tiong for the purpose of passing the sum on to Ufun.

71     In the circumstances, I found that the defendants were not enriched by the sum of SGD 14,500.

Was there a total failure of consideration?

72     While the above finding was sufficient to dispose of the plaintiff’s claim in unjust enrichment, for completeness, I address his main point: that he did not benefit from his Ufun account because he could not log in despite the passwords that he was given.

73     I did not think that this was true.

74     First, as set out at [69] above, the WhatsApp exchanges between the plaintiff and 2nd defendant revealed that the plaintiff had checked his Ufun account on 24 April 2015 and knew what his Utokens were worth then.

75     Second, the entire set of WhatsApp exchanges between the plaintiff and the 2nd defendant revealed that the plaintiff did not at any time say to the 2nd defendant that the username and password that was provided to him was not working.

76     Instead, what the plaintiff did was to allege, on various occasions, that:

(a)     The 2nd defendant was handling the investment on trust for the plaintiff: WhatsApp message timestamped 24/4/15, 17:59:35.[note: 64]

(b)     The plaintiff had entrusted his account passwords to the 2nd defendant: WhatsApp messages timestamped 10/12/15, 15:46:28;[note: 65] 14/12/15, 22:34:10 and 22:41:21.[note: 66]

(c)     The 2nd defendant had guaranteed payment from the plaintiff’s Utokens: WhatsApp messages timestamped 29/11/16, 13:41:35 and 5/12/16, 23:50:24.[note: 67]

(d)     The 1st and 2nd defendants defrauded the plaintiff by taking his money and giving him a certificate which had no value, while guaranteeing payment in their personal capacity: WhatsApp messages timestamp 20/2/17, 01:12:13.[note: 68]

77     The various allegations show that the plaintiff was simply grasping at straws and casting about for a way to recoup his failed investment from the defendants. If he was genuinely unable to log in to his Ufun account and had no access to his Utokens, he would have said so earlier, given his prolific and wide ranging allegations against the 2nd defendant.

78     I also gave little weight to the plaintiff’s bare and belated explanation in cross-examination that he communicated verbally over the phone that he could not log in with the password. This was neither pleaded nor stated in his AEIC. If his main point was that he could not log into his Ufun account, then one would have expected this to be made front and centre in his pleaded case and explained in his AEIC.

79     In summary, I found that the plaintiff had a Ufun account and had access to Utokens within the account. The SGD 14,500 that he paid to the 2nd defendant was transferred as described by the 2nd defendant to Lee Ser Tiong who transferred it on to Ufun representatives. The problem for the plaintiff was his Utokens became worthless due to the collapse of Ufun. However, he has not shown any basis for the defendants to be made responsible for his loss.

Conclusion and costs

80     In conclusion, I dismissed the plaintiff’s claim with costs.

81     The defendants submitted costs to be fixed at $12,000 plus reasonable disbursements, on the basis that the trial was not conducted on a simplified basis, but went on for two days with five witnesses. The plaintiff submitted costs fixed at $8,000 as the affidavits were fairly brief.

82     On balance, given that this was a Magistrates’ Court suit, I fixed costs at $10,000 plus reasonable disbursements to be agreed or fixed.
