23 December 2022

District Judge Kow Keng Siong:

Introduction

1       Under s 6(1) read with s 28A(1)(a) the Employment Agencies Act (Cap 92, 2012 Rev Ed) (“EAA”), a director is guilty of an offence if he has consented to his company carrying on an “employment agency” without the requisite license. The Act defines an employment agency as an agency carried on “for or in connection with the employment of persons in any capacity”: s 2 of the EAA. On conviction, the director can be punished with a fine (up to $80,000), or to imprisonment (up to two years), or to both: s 6(4)(a) of the EAA.

2       Should a director be sentenced to imprisonment when his company did not engage in any deception or exploitative conduct when carrying on an unlicensed employment agency? Should the director be imprisoned when the unlicensed employment agency activities have benefitted private education institutions (“PEIs”), their foreign students, and Singapore firms? In determining the director’s sentence, is it correct to assume that just because he is also a 95%-shareholder in the company, he must have received 95% of the company’s profits from the unlicensed employment agency activities? These are some of the key issues raised in the present case.

3       Benedict Yeo Tian Ming (“Yeo”) is a director of Edupal Associates Pte Ltd (“Edupal”). Between April 2017 and July 2019 (“Material Period”), Edupal (a) had assisted PEIs to place their foreign students on industrial attachments with Singapore firms, and (b) had assisted these firms to apply for training work passes (“TWPs”) as well as to provide aftercare services for the students (“emplacement services”). As a result of providing these services, Yeo and Edupal were charged as follows:

Charge against Yeo

You, […] are charged that you, [during the Material Period], as a director of [Edupal], had consented to [Edupal] carrying on an employment agency in Singapore when [Edupal] was not the holder of a license issued by the Commissioner of Employment Agencies authorising [Edupal] to carry on such an agency, which offence was committed with your consent, and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 6(1) read with Section 28A(1)(a) of the [EAA] and punishable under Section 6(4)(a) of the [EAA].

[text in square brackets added]

Charge against Edupal

You, […] are charged that you, [during the Material Period], in Singapore, did carry on an employment agency, when you were not the holder of a license issued by the Commissioner for Employment Agencies authorising you to carry on such an agency and you have thereby committed an offence under Section 6(1) of the [EAA], punishable under Section 6(4)(a) of the [EAA].

[text in square brackets added]

4       Yeo and Edupal claimed trial to their charges. Their Counsel submitted that no offence was disclosed because the emplacement services were outside scope of work performed by an “employment agency”. At the end of the trial,[note: 1] I found that the charges were made out and convicted them accordingly.

6       Dissatisfied with Yeo’s sentence, the Prosecution filed a notice of appeal. In these grounds of decision, I will explain why I had imposed only a fine on Yeo – instead of a fine and custodial sentence as submitted by the Prosecution.

Undisputed facts

7       Before providing my reasons, it is useful to first set out the facts. As stated earlier, the charges arose from the provision of “emplacement services” by Edupal. The company was able to find a market for such services because PEIs needed to place their students on industrial attachments with Singapore firms. Altogether, 175 foreign students from 13 PEIs were emplaced with seven Singapore firms on TWPs. These students were employed and remunerated by the firms at all material times.[note: 3] The details of Edupal’s emplacement services, which have been set out in an Agreed Statement of Facts (“ASOF”), are as follows:

17.    During the Material Period, Edupal, with the consent of Yeo, conducted activities for and in connection with the employment of the 175 foreign students from the 13 PEIs as TWP holders with the Firms. These activities included:

a.    informing the PEIs that Yeo and Edupal could arrange for foreign students to be employed as TWP holders with various Firms;

b.    calling the Firms to ask if the Firms wanted to employ TWP holders;

c.    arranging for interviews after the foreign students were shortlisted by the Firms […] Yeo also provided the biodata of the students to the Firms;

d.    guiding the Firms on the process of applying for TWPs; and

e.    providing the Firms with the documents required for the application, i.e., copies of the student’s passport and student pass, the PEI’s training program as well as confirmation letter from the PEIs, which Yeo and Edupal had obtained from the PEIs.

18.    […]

19.    Once the application for a TWP was approved by MOM and an In-Principle Approval (“IPA”) issued, Edupal would then forward the IPAs to the PEIs and inform the PEIs to cancel the foreign students’ student passes. Edupal arranged accommodation for the foreign students, and informed the foreign students to register their addresses with MOM. Additionally, Edupal brought the foreign students to MOM for thumb-printing to complete the TWP application. Further, Edupal purchased air tickets for the students to return to their home countries after completion of their 6-months attachment with the Firms. During the Material Period, the Firms employed and paid the TWP holders for their services.

8       During the Material Period, Edupal was not a licensed employment agency. It is the policy of the Ministry of Manpower (“MOM”) that only employers are allowed to make TWP applications. Employment agencies – even if they are licensed – are not allowed to make such applications.

Prosecution’s submissions for custodial sentence

9       Having set out the background facts, I will now summarise the Prosecution’s reasons for seeking a sentence of three weeks’ imprisonment and $30,000-fine against Yeo. They are as follows:

(a)     Edupal had received payments totalling $202,230 from its offence. Ninety-five percent of these payments (i.e., $192,118.5) would have gone to Yeo since he owned 95% of Edupal. Even if the maximum fine of $80,000 is imposed on both Yeo and Edupal, such fines ($160,000) would not be sufficiently deterrent as they would have failed to fully disgorge their illicit profits.[note: 4]

(b)     Yeo’s offence is serious. It had (i) undermined the regulatory framework in the EAA, (ii) “[taken] advantage of licensed and law-abiding [employment agencies]”, and (iii) caused financial losses to the firms as they should not have paid for the emplacement services.[note: 5] Yeo’s offence was also committed over more than two years and on a significant scale.[note: 6]

(c)     Finally, Yeo had similar antecedents. Between June 2006 and June 2007, he had abetted the employment of foreign students at various restaurants without a work pass. As a result, he was charged with 27 counts under s 5(1) read with s 23(1) of the Employment of Foreign Workers Act (Cap 91A, 1997 Rev Ed) (“EFWA”). In 2008, he pleaded guilty to 14 of these charges and consented to the remaining charges to be taken into consideration for the purpose of sentencing. Yeo was sentenced to a total fine of $91,200.[note: 7]

My decision

10     I will now explain why I had rejected the Prosecution’s submission to sentence Yeo to imprisonment. I will first set out my views on (a) the mischief sought to be addressed by s 6(1) of the EAA and (b) the sentencing factors for the offence. Thereafter, I will show why the factors raised by the Prosecution do not justify sentencing Yeo to imprisonment.

Mischief to be addressed

11     This is Yeo’s first contravention of s 6(1) of the EAA. As such, he is liable to a fine not exceeding $80,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or both: s 6(4) of the EAA.

12     Prior to 2011, the prescribed punishment for contravening s 6(1) is only a fine of up to $5,000. In 2011, the prescribed punishment was enhanced to a maximum fine of $80,000, or imprisonment up to two years, or both. During the second reading of the Employment Agencies (Amendment) Bill, Mr Lee Yi Shyan, Minister of State for Manpower, provided the following explanation for the amendment (Parliamentary Debates, Official Report (11 January 2011, Vol 87, column 2289 to 2290) (“Second Reading Speech”):[note: 8]

Sir, the Government takes unlicensed EA activities seriously. They undermine the regulatory framework by competing with licensed law-abiding EAs. They also tend to exploit the vulnerable workers from developing countries by charging them exorbitant fees and may not give them the promised jobs in return.

The current penalties pale in comparison to the potential profits from illegal or errant EA activities. The existing maximum penalty for first-time offenders under the Act is $5,000. But foreign workers, as you know, are willing to pay agency fees of between $3,000 and $10,000 in order to land a job in Singapore. … Moreover, licensed EAs would have to furnish a security deposit of up to $60,000 and the same requirement is not there for unlicensed EAs. Therefore, all these add to strong incentives for unlicensed EAs to remain unlicensed.

To stem the supply of unlicensed agencies, we will raise the penalties for unlicensed agencies to ensure that they are commensurate with the potential gains from malpractices. The most significant increase in the maximum penalties under the Act will be for operating an unlicensed EA in section 6. The first offence will warrant a fine of up to $80,000 and/or up to two years of imprisonment, and subsequent offences are liable for penalties of up to twice that amount. …

[emphasis added]

13     The above statements clearly show that sentences imposed for contraventions of s 6(1) of the EAA should (a) deter unlicensed employment agency activities given that huge profits can be made and (b) punish those who exploit vulnerable foreigners. In determining how a sentence can meet these twin objectives of deterrence and retribution, all the facts of the case must be considered: Public Prosecutor v Kho Jabing [2015] 2 SLR 112 at [51(a)]. This would include considering factors that go towards (a) the harm from the offence and (b) the offender’s culpability. I will elaborate of this shortly. Suffice to note at this stage, a deterrent sentence need not always take the form of imprisonment and that a high fine can still be deterrent: Chia Kah Boon v Public Prosecutor [1999] 2 SLR(R) 1163 at [15]; Public Prosecutor v Cheong Hock Lai [2004] 3 SLR(R) 203 at [42].

Offence-specific sentencing factors

14     I will now state my views on the sentencing factors for an offence under s 6(1) read with s 28A(1)(a) and s 6(4) of the EAA.

15     I start by setting out the offence-specific sentencing factors for operating an unlicensed employment agency. A non-exhaustive list of these factors is summarised below:

Factors relating to harm

(a)     Extent of pecuniary loss caused to the victim, if any.

(i)       In many cases, a job seeker or an employer will have paid an offender for providing services for or in connection with employment. It bears emphasis that the mere fact of such payment does not necessarily mean that the job seeker or employer has suffered pecuniary loss equivalent to the quantum of the payment. This is because these persons may have received the benefits promised by the offender.

(ii)       It is only when the offender has failed to deliver on his promise that it can be said that the victim has suffered pecuniary loss. An example where such losses have been considered in sentencing is Public Prosecutor v Ng Chai Hock [2021] SGDC 105 (“Ng Chai Hock”).

(b)     Extent of other harm caused to the victim and others, if any.

Apart pecuniary loss, the victim may also suffer other harm. For instance, a foreign job seeker may be left stranded in Singapore or are put in dire straits: Public Prosecutor v International Placements (S) Pte Ltd [2019] SGMC 52 at [87]; Ng Chai Hock at [58].

Factors relating to culpability

(c)     Motive for contravening s 6(1).

(i)       Generally speaking, the fact that an offender has committed an offence for financial gains is an aggravating factor: Vasentha d/o Joseph v Public Prosecutor [2015] 5 SLR 122 at [51]. In the case of a contravention of s 6(1) of the EAA, I am of the view that this general principle should not be rigidly applied. This is because most offenders prosecuted for contravening s 6(1) would have been motivated by some form of financial and material gains to do so. Put in another way, the mere presence of such a motivation does not render the offence materially any more serious, or the offender any more culpable, than other cases of unlicensed employment agency activities. A similar approach has been adopted for the offence of drug trafficking and importation (Adri Anton Kalangie v Public Prosecutor [2018] 2 SLR 557 at [82]; Loo Pei Xiang Alan v Public Prosecutor [2015] 5 SLR 500 at [27]) as well as money-laundering (Huang Ying-Chun v Public Prosecutor [2019] 3 SLR 606 at [92] – [94]).

(ii)       In the exceptional case where an offender is motivated by reasons other than financial or material gains in contravening s 6(1) (e.g., to help a foreign worker secure employment out of altruism), then these reasons would have to be scrutinised closely to assess whether they aggravate or mitigate the severity of the offence.

(d)     The manner of contravention: International Placements (S) Pte Ltd v Public Prosecutor [2020] 4 SLR 1195 (“International Placements”) at [69].

(i)       The fact that an offender has engaged in deception and/or exploitative conduct when contravening s 6(1) is an aggravating factor: Ng Chai Hock. Indeed, the exploitation of vulnerable foreigners has been specifically highlighted in the Second Reading Speech as a matter of concern: see [12] above.

(ii)       An example where the absence of deception or exploitation in the contravention of s 6(1) of the EAA have been considered in sentencing is Public Prosecutor v Lina Khoo Ling Ling [2013] SGDC 58 (“Lina Khoo”). In that case, the offender initially possessed a license to run an employment agency. Through her negligence, she did not renew the license when it expired and continued to carry on an employment agency for about eight months. The Prosecution submitted for a $70,000 fine given that 38 clients were involved and $45,600 had been collected as agency fees. The court imposed a fine of $15,000 instead. In coming to this decision, the court noted that the offender (i) had matched her clients with prospective employers, (ii) had assisted in crediting her clients via GIRO, and (iii) had sourced potential employers for her clients. Other than operating an employment agency without a valid license, everything else that the offender had done was above board. She carried out her duties efficiently and professionally. The agency fees that she charged were within the guidelines set under the Employment Agency Rules. No wrongful loss was caused to any of the offender’s clients. The Prosecution did not appeal against the sentence: Lina Khoo at [16] – [18], [20], [30] – [32].

(e)     The extent of contravention: International Placements at [69]; Ng Chai Hock at [59].

Relevant factors include (i) the number of foreigners involved (International Placements at [70]; Ng Chai Hock at [53]), (ii) the number of employers and other entities involved, and (iii) the period of offending.

(f)     Steps were taken to avoid detection.

An example of this can be seen in Ng Chai Hock at [59], where the offender used the particulars of another person to set up the employment agency.

16     Where a director is convicted of having consented to his company operating an unlicensed employment agency, in addition to the above, other sentencing factors are also relevant. These include the following:

(a)     The degree of the director’s involvement in his company’s offence.

(b)     The extent of the director’s profits and other benefits from his offence.

Offender-specific sentencing factors

17     Apart from the offence-specific sentencing factors, aggravating and mitigating factors which relate to the offender must also be considered. The nature of these offender-specific sentencing factors is well established. They include (a) the number of charges taken into consideration, (b) the lack of remorse, (c) the relevant antecedents demonstrating recalcitrance, and (d) a timeous plea of guilt. The mere fact that the offender had claimed trial is a neutral factor: International Placements at [71].

Why custodial threshold is not crossed in Yeo’s case

18     I will now show why the factors raised by the Prosecution do not justify sentencing Yeo to imprisonment.

19     A key plank for the Prosecution’s sentencing position is that even if Yeo is sentenced to the maximum fine of $80,000, this will not be sufficiently deterrent given that Edupal had received $202,230. There are two premises to this submission. First, the entire sum of $202,230 represented Edupal’s profits. Second, Yeo had received 95% of the $202,230 based on his shareholding in the company. In my view, both premises are erroneous.

Prosecution’s first premise – Edupal’s profits

20     The Prosecution’s contention that Edupal had made a profit of $202,230 is disputed. Counsel submitted that although Edupal had received this amount from the commission of its offence, its profits is lesser. This is because Edupal had incurred expenses in providing the emplacement services – it had paid for the students’ insurance (about $133.75/student) and air-tickets (about $300/student). Given that Edupal had incurred expenses of about $433.75/student, Counsel submitted that its profits were only $126,323.75 (i.e., $433.75 x 175 students).[note: 9]

21     The Prosecution urged me to reject Counsel’s submission. First, whether Edupal had incurred the alleged expenses cannot be verified (“Prosecution’s first reason”). Second, even if such expenses had been incurred, this is not a mitigating factor in principle (“Prosecution’s second reason”).[note: 10]

22     I am unable to accept the Prosecution’s submission.

(a)     Regarding the Prosecution’s first reason, there is in fact evidence to support Edupal’s claim that it had to incur expenses pursuant to the emplacement services. According to cl 6 of the management agreements which Edupal had entered with the Singapore firms, Edupal “[undertook] to be responsible for the onward repatriation of the [foreign student]” upon completion of his contract: see ASOF at Annex 5.1 to Annex 5.6.

(b)     As for the Prosecution’s second reason, I note that it is inconsistent with case law. In Public Prosecutor v Su Jiqing Joel [2021] 3 SLR 1232 (“Joel Su”), the High Court had held that as a matter of principle, “necessary expenses” – i.e., “expenses the sole purpose of which is to enable the offender to commit the offence” – should be deducted when computing the amount of disgorgement fine to impose. This is because the purpose of disgorgement is to negate the benefits that an offender has enjoyed from his criminal enterprise. To ignore the necessary expenses incurred would render the disgorgement (a) disproportionate to the benefits enjoyed and (b) “inherently punitive”. This, in turn, will undermine the distinction between the disgorgement component and punitive component of a fine: Joel Su at [60] – [63], [76(b)] and [76(c)].

23     In my view, payments for the foreign students’ insurance and air-tickets fall within the category of “expenses the sole purpose of which is to enable the offender to commit the offence” under s 6(1) read with s 6(4) of the EAA. These payments are necessary as they are an integral part of Edupal’s emplacement services. If Edupal did not make such payments, the firms (a) would not have entered into the management agreements with Edupal, or (b) would have paid a lower management fee to the company. In the case of (b), the payments to Edupal would have been less than $202,230.

24     In my mind, the issue is not whether payments for insurance and air-tickets by Edupal ought to be deducted in the calibration of the disgorgement fine. Instead, the real issue is how much Edupal’s profits is. The burden is on Yeo to prove the relevant amount: Joel Su at [76(d)]. In this regard, Counsel had tendered one “debit note” dated 9 October 2018 from one Ins-Solution Agency Pte Ltd to Edupal.[note: 11] According to this note, Edupal was to pay to $267.50 in relation to –

Insured: A & P Maintenance – 2 FW

09-10-2018

08-12-2019



25     I am of the view that even if the debit note is proof that Edupal had paid insurance premiums of $267.50 in relation to two foreign students, the note does not prove that it had paid insurance premiums at $133.75/student for all 175 students involved in the offence. As for the payment of air-tickets, apart from Counsel’s bare submission that the cost is about $300/student, there is nothing else to substantiate this claim. In other words, there is no evidential basis for me (a) to deduct $75,906.25 (being [$133.75 + $300] per student x 175 students) as the amount for the “necessary expenses”, and (b) to find that Edupal’s profits were only $126,323.75.

26     To summarise – while I accept that Edupal had made substantial profits, given that there is evidence of (a) Edupal being obliged to incur “necessary expenses” to repatriate the foreign students upon completion of their industrial attachment, and (b) Edupal having in fact incurred such expenses for at least two students, I am unable to accept the Prosecution’s submission that Edupal’s profits from the offence are $202,230.

Prosecution’s second premise – Yeo’s profits

27     There is another difficulty with the Prosecution’s submission that Yeo had made substantial profits. It is well established that there must be an evidential basis for imposing a particular sentence: Chang Kar Meng v Public Prosecutor [2017] 2 SLR 68 at [38] and [39]; Rupchand Bhojwani Sunil v Public Prosecutor [2004] 1 SLR(R) 596 at [17] and [18]; Public Prosecutor v Kang Seong Yong [2005] 2 SLR(R) 169 at [21].

28     If a custodial sentence is to be imposed on the basis that Yeo had made substantial profits and that a $80,000-fine is insufficient to disgorge these profits, then the Prosecution must adduce the relevant company/bank records or other evidence to prove the amounts of profits that he had made. No such evidence was produced before me.

29     In my view, it is too speculative for the Prosecution to submit that just because Yeo owns 95% of Edupal, he must have obtained 95% of Edupal’s illicit profits. Under s 403(1) of the Companies Act 1967, no dividend is payable to shareholders except out of profits. It is trite law that a company is under no duty to pay dividends even if it makes profits. A company may plough all its profits into its business rather than pay them out to its shareholders. It is also well established that shareholders may share in the company’s assets only when it is wound up and after all the company’s creditors have been paid in full: Industrial Equity Ltd v Blackburn (1977) 17 ALR 575 at 583 – 584 (endorsed in Public Prosecutor v Lew Syn Pau [2006] 4 SLR(R) 210 at [203]); Ernest Ferdinand Perez De La Sala v Compania De Navegacion Palomar, SA [2018] 1 SLR 894 at [119].

30     Given the above, I disagree with the Prosecution’s submission that a custodial sentence is warranted because Yeo had made $192,118.5 in profits and that the maximum fine is insufficient to disgorge these profits.

31     Before proceeding to address the Prosecution’s other reasons for imposing a custodial sentence, I wish to digress to make some observations.

Prosecution’s submission for $30,000-fine

32     The first concerns the Prosecution’s submission that in addition to a custodial sentence, a $30,000-fine be also imposed on Yeo. `

33     The Prosecution did not explain how it had arrived at the quantum of its proposed fine. If the disgorgement of Yeo’s profits is of such critical importance for the Prosecution, it is curious why they had chosen to seek a fine of only $30,000 and not the maximum fine of $80,000. This is especially so when the Prosecution had contended that Yeo had made $192,118.5 in profits. The Prosecution’s proposed fine is only about one-sixth of such profits.

Ng Chai Hock

34     If the maximum fine of $80,000 is imposed to disgorge Yeo’s illicit profits, then his sentence would be three weeks’ imprisonment and $80,000-fine. Such a sentence is very similar to that imposed in Ng Chai Hock (i.e., four weeks’ imprisonment and $80,000-fine). Such a sentence is difficult to reconcile with Ng Chai Hock. This is because the difference in sentence between these two cases is only one week’s imprisonment. Yet, Yeo’s offence is far less egregious than that in Ng Chai Hock. The offender there had deceived 755 foreign job seekers, exploited their vulnerability, and caused them to suffer harm. In sentencing the offender, the court had considered the following:

(a)     The offender had created an online portal to enable the online distribution of resume for job seekers. Through his advertisements however, the offender had created the impression to job seekers that he was offering employment agent service, and that interested applicants were to attend an interview at his office. The offender had then used the interview to extract payments (usually $350) from the job seekers without explaining the services that he was offering: Ng Chai Hock at [54].

(b)     The offender had also exploited the job seekers’ sense of anxiety and hope by making misleading representations that he was going to find employment for them. The offender had collected $291,135 from the job seekers. Once payment was made, the offender would dissuade the job seekers from backing out by making empty assurances that refunds were possible: Ng Chai Hock at [51].

(c)     The offender had also placed a condition in the contract with the job seeker that threatened anyone who lodged a police report against him with a sanction of $1,000. To the lay person, the cloak of legitimacy and the threat of financial sanction effectively discouraged any job seeker from making a complaint: Ng Chai Hock at [52].

(d)     Even when the offender was under MOM investigations, he had persisted in his illegal activities to make substantial profits: Ng Chai Hock at [55].

35     It is clear from the decision in Ng Chai Hock that the above aggravating factors were the reasons why the court had imposed a custodial sentence on the offender. (The maximum fine was imposed to disgorge his illicit profits.) Such aggravating factors are absent in Yeo’s case. See Annex A for a comparison of the relevant sentencing factors between these cases.

Prosecution’s charging approach

36     My last observation is on the Prosecution’s decision to prefer one charge against Edupal (and for that matter, Yeo as well) for contravening s 6(1) of the EAA. It bears noting that the charge covered activities committed over two years, and which involved 175 foreign students, 13 PEIs, and seven firms. Because there is only one charge against Edupal, a fine of only $80,000 could be imposed. This is despite Counsel’s submission that Edupal had received $126,323.75 as profits.

37     It is puzzling why the Prosecution had preferred only one charge against Edupal when there is nothing in either the EAA or the Criminal Procedure Code 2010 that requires them to do so. An offence of carrying on an unlicensed employment agency is made out so long as an offender has carried on an agency that does work “for or in connection with the employment of persons”: see definition of “employment agency” in s 2 of the EAA. The word “agency” is not defined in the EAA. Based on its ordinary meaning, an “agency” is a business or an organisation that provides a particular service especially when representing other businesses or organisations.[note: 12]

38     I note that the Prosecution had framed more than one charge in other cases where the contravention of s 6(1) of the EAA is extensive: see e.g., Lina Khoo; Public Prosecutor v Tan Chin Boon [2021] SGDC 66. If the Prosecution had adopted a similar charging approach against Edupal, then a more deterrent sentence in the form of higher fines could have been imposed.

Efficacy of MOM’s EAA policy not seriously undermined

39     I now return to the Prosecution’s submission for a custodial sentence against Yeo. A second reason that they had provided for such a sentence is that Yeo’s offence can undermine the efficacy of MOM’s EAA policy:[note: 13]

8.    […] the offences undermined the regulatory framework put in place by the Ministry of Manpower (“Ministry”) for employment agencies. The offences also frustrated the policy objectives of the Employment Agencies Act of enforcing high standards and accountability in the industry.

9.    It is difficult for the Ministry to monitor and regulate unlicensed employment agencies such as Edupal as they are not registered with the Ministry as employment agencies. They fall outside the regulatory framework and stay under the radar until they are surfaced to the Ministry’s attention.

40     In my view, the mere fact that Yeo’s offence can undermine the EAA regulatory framework does not justify the imposition of a custodial sentence. All contraventions of s 6(1) can undermine the EAA regulatory framework. The Prosecution must show something more to justify why Yeo should be imprisoned when (a) s 6(1) provides for a discretion to sentence him to a substantial fine of $80,000, and (b) other offenders have been sentenced to fines.

41     It is clear from the Second Reading Speech that Parliament’s key concern is with the harm caused by unprofessional, deceptive and/or exploitative conduct of unlicensed employment agencies. In this regard, the Minister of State for Manpower specifically highlighted that the amendments to the EAA were intended –

(a)     to target unlicensed employment agencies that “tend to exploit the vulnerable workers from developing countries by charging them exorbitant fees and [who] may not give them promised jobs in return”: Second Reading Speech at column 2289 to 2290,

(b)     to ensure “[v]ulnerable rank-and-file workers, including foreign workers, will enjoy greater protection against unethical practices of unlicensed and errant players”: Second Reading Speech at column 2295, and

(c)     to promote the development of an employment agency industry “that is better qualified, more professional and effective in matching employers and workers”: Second Reading Speech at column 2287.

42     In my view, a fine in Yeo’s case will not be inconsistent with the legislative intent behind s 6(1).

(a)     First, Yeo’s offence did not involve deceptive or exploitative conduct. In fact, the evidence disclosed that Yeo and Edupal had acted above board at all material times. For instance –

(i)       Edupal had signed written agreements with six PEIs for its emplacement services.[note: 14]

(ii)       Edupal had also signed written management agreements with seven Singapore firms. These agreements spelt out Edupal’s duties vis-à-vis the provision of emplacement services as well as the fees payable for these services.[note: 15] There is nothing to suggest that the terms in the agreement are unfairly in favour of Edupal.

(iii)       Further, the emails between Edupal/Yeo with the Singapore firms and Singapore PEIs revealed that Edupal had carried out its duties relating the emplacement services professionally and transparently: Annex 7.1 to Annex 9.12.

(b)     Second, Yeo’s offence did not cause any harm to the foreign students, PEIs, and the Singapore firms. As such, Yeo’s offence did not engage the concerns behind the EAA amendments.

(c)     On the contrary, Edupal’s emplacement services appear to have resulted in a win-win arrangement for all parties concerned. Edupal and Yeo were able to (i) effectively match the students with the firms, (ii) competently assist the Singapore firms to make the relevant TWP applications, and (iii) provide aftercare services for the foreign students.[note: 16] I accept the following submissions by Counsel[note: 17] –

Edupal had provided a valuable service to them by assisting them in emplacements with companies. The industrial attachment was a crucial part of their curriculum without which they could not obtain their diplomas. Edupal also cared for the students’ welfare by entering into management agreements with the various firms. This ensured that the students did not face abuse or were exploited, in line with Parliament’s intentions.

(d)     Finally, by giving courts the option to impose a fine of up to $80,000 for a contravention of s 6(1) of the EAA, Parliament had clearly envisaged that there would be cases where a high fine is appropriate. It has repeatedly been emphasised that a fine would suffice where there is a low level of harm caused and a low level of culpability: see e.g., Public Prosecutor v Goh Jun Hao Jeremy [2018] 4 SLR 1438 at [37]. A custodial sentence should not “be lightly or readily imposed as a norm or a default punishment” unless the nature of the offence justifies its imposition on the basis that general or specific deterrence is called for: Yang Suan Piau Steven v Public Prosecutor [2013] 1 SLR 809 at [31].

43     The Prosecution submitted that the Singapore firms had suffered harm because they had made payments to Edupal when there was no need to.[note: 18] I do not find this submission persuasive. The clear evidence is that these firms had paid Edupal for certain services. There is no evidence of any complaint by these firms about the services provided. Given that the firms had obtained tangible benefits provided by Edupal, the Prosecution’s submission that the firms had suffered harm comes across as hollow.[note: 19]

44     The Prosecution had also submitted that Yeo and Edupal “had taken advantage of law-abiding employment agencies which are precluded from carrying out [emplacement services]”.[note: 20] I accept that Yeo and Edupal had secured an unfair competitive advantage over the licensed employment agencies by providing services which these agencies could not legally provide. In my view, an appropriate sentencing response to this would be a fine. There is no compelling reason why the mere fact that Edupal had secured a competitive advantage and thus obtained pecuniary gains means that Yeo must be imprisoned.

Yeo’s antecedents

45     I now come to the Prosecution’s last justification for a custodial sentence against Yeo – namely, that he had antecedents in 2008 for offences under the EFWA.

46     Counsel urged me not to place weight on these antecedents “as they related to a different offence altogether”. According to Counsel[note: 21] –

36.    […] At the time, Yeo was involved with placing 42 students on work attachments at restaurants so that they could complete their MOE-approved courses. Yeo had informed the restaurants that the foreign students would not be considered as the restaurants' employees and were merely on industrial attachment and could therefore work for them. These foreign students, who were paid monthly salaries of between $400 to $800, did not hold valid Work Permits to work in the restaurants. It turned out that MOE’s approval did not make the work attachment legal. As such, Yeo was charged for abetting their illegal employment. He had disputed the charge, but eventually was pressured into pleading guilty as the students were unable to return home until the matter was resolved. He also faced pressure from the students’ parents who were concerned for their welfare. It was subsequent to this incident that MOM introduced the TWP scheme for students to be placed in industrial attachments. The charge involving illegal employment is very different from acting as an employment agency without license.

[emphasis added]

47     I am unable to accept Counsel’s submissions to disregard Yeo’s 2008 antecedents.

48     First, it is well established that antecedents for a different offence may be considered in sentencing if they share broad similarities with the offence being sentenced: Leong Mun Kwai v Public Prosecutor [1996] 1 SLR(R) 719 at [18] and [19]; Sentencing Principles in Singapore (Academy Publishing, 2019) at [21.141] – [21.150]. Although the 2008 antecedents are under a different piece of legislation, I am of the view that they share common factual and legal similarities with Yeo’s current offence.

(a)     Both sets of offences are factually similar. They involved Yeo being engaged in placing foreign students on industrial/work attachments with firms in Singapore.

(b)     Both sets of offences are also legally similar. The placement of students on industrial/work attachment would have constituted “employment” under the EAA. Such an activity is regulated under both Acts – the employer is required to apply for a TWP (under the EAA) and a work pass (under the EFWA) for the foreign student.

(c)     Finally, the close nexus between these two sets of offending conduct is evident as Counsel had submitted that it was after Yeo’s 2008 convictions “that MOM introduced the TWP scheme for students to be placed [on] industrial attachments”.

49     I also disbelieve Counsel’s submission that Yeo was “pressured” into pleading guilty to the 2008 offences.

(a)     First, this submission is based on a bare assertion unsupported by any evidence.

(b)     Furthermore, Yeo’s claim did not make sense. Counsel had failed to explain why the mere fact that the students were enrolled in MOE-approved courses meant that they could work in restaurants and draw “monthly salaries” during their employment at the restaurants with MOM-issued work passes.[note: 22]

(c)     Finally, the prescribed punishment for an offence under s 5(1) of the EFWA in 2008 is a minimum fine of not less than 24 months’ levy. According to the Statement of Facts for the 2008 antecedents, the levy payable for each student was $240/month. This means that the minimum sentence is $5,760/charge. Yeo pleaded guilty to 14 charges and was eventually sentenced to a fine of $91,200. It is inconceivable that Yeo would be prepared to incur such a substantial fine if he had disputed the EFWA charges.

50     Although the 2008 antecedents are relevant, I note that they are 14 years old. In Kavitha d/o Mailvaganam v Public Prosecutor [2017] 4 SLR 1349 at [19], the High Court made the following observations regarding dated antecedents:

It is a well-established principle that the length of time that an offender has stayed clean must be taken into account when assessing the weight to be given to his antecedents (PP v NF [2006] 4 SLR(R) 849 at [70]). When this period of time is substantial, and it is due to the offender’s own efforts at rehabilitation, then only minimal weight ought to be given to his past offences when determining sentence. As put in Kow Keng Siong, Sentencing Principles in Singapore (Academy Publishing, 2009) at para 21.202:

It is obvious that the older a conviction becomes, the less relevance it has in predicting the offender’s future conduct. An offender who has for a substantial period of his life since his last offence stayed clear from crime suggests that he is a reformed man, to be judged afresh.

51     In my view, it is unnecessary to sentence Yeo to imprisonment for the purpose of specific deterrence. There is no evidence to suggest that he has a propensity to re-offend. Since his 2008 antecedents, Yeo had remained crime-free for the next 14 years, which is a long time. Courts have given very little weight to antecedents of similar “vintage” as Yeo’s in other cases: Kavitha d/o Mailvaganam v Public Prosecutor [2017] 4 SLR 1349 at [19] (14-year-old antecedent); Public Prosecutor v Leong Soon Kheong [2009] 4 SLR(R) 63 at [51] (13-year-old antecedent); Public Prosecutor v Marzuki bin Ahmad [2014] 4 SLR 623 at [40] (ten-year-old antecedents); Public Prosecutor v Fizul Asrul bin Efandi [2018] 5 SLR 475 (eight-year-old antecedent).

52     Even if weight is to be given to Yeo’s 2008 antecedents, this does not mean that a custodial sentence must be imposed for his current offence. This is because Yeo is to be punished solely for his current contravention of s 6(1) of the EAA, and the escalation principle must not have the effect of resulting in a sentence that (a) is disproportionate to the gravity of his current offence or (b) violates the double jeopardy rule by also punishing him for 14-year-old crimes for when he had already paid his dues: Sim Yeow Seng v Public Prosecutor [1995] 2 SLR(R) 466 at [9]; Public Prosecutor v NF [2006] 4 SLR(R) 849 at [66]; Public Prosecutor v Boon Kiah Kin [1993] 2 SLR(R) 26 at [31]; Public Prosecutor v Low Ji Qing [2019] SGHC 174 at [1], [2], [56] – [66] and [74] – [81].

Summary of sentencing considerations

53     In summary, I have considered the following in imposing the maximum fine of $80,000 on Yeo:

Aggravating factors

(a)     Edupal’s offence was committed over two years and on a grand scale – it involved 175 foreign students, 13 PEIs, and seven firms.[note: 23]

(b)     Edupal had received $202,230 from its offence. Most of this amount represents Edupal’s profits.

(c)     Yeo had been instrumental in Edupal’s contravention of s 6(1) of the EAA. He was solely in control of the company at the material time.[note: 24] Yeo had approached PEIs and Singapore firms to offer Edupal’s emplacement services. As a result, he was appointed by PEIs as an “industrial attachment officer”. Yeo had also entered into several management agreements with Singapore firms to provide the emplacement services.[note: 25]

Mitigating factors

(d)     Neither Yeo nor Edupal had engaged in deception or exploitative conduct.

(e)     The foreign students, PEIs and Singapore firms did not suffer pecuniary loss or other harm arising from Yeo/Edupal’s offence.

(f)     There is no evidence that Yeo/Edupal had taken steps to avoid detection of their offences. That said, such offences are inherently difficult to detect as they involve private arrangements with Singapore PEIs and firms. There is no evidence to show that MOM was aware of these offences. If MOM had been aware, enforcement action would have been taken, and Yeo/Edupal would not have been able to continue with their offences for such a long period.[note: 26].

Neutral factors

(g)     It is unclear to what extent Yeo had profited from his offence.

(h)     I reject Counsel’s submission that Yeo had committed his offence due to an ignorance of the law which was contributed by a lack of clarity on the law and MOM’s policy.[note: 27] It is trite that ignorance of the law is not a mitigating factor: Krishnan Chand v Public Prosecutor [1995] 2 SLR 291 at [7]; Public Prosecutor v Tan Seo Whatt Albert [2019] SGHC 156 at [48]. In any event, I agree with the Prosecution that (1) MOM’s employment licencing conditions “clearly and unequivocally” state that employment agencies are not allowed to conduct any activities for or in connection with placing a foreigner in a training program or obtaining a training work permit for the foreigner, and (2) the need to comply with the existing regulations should not and cannot be subjugated to “business sense’.[note: 28]

(i)     I did not take the fact that Yeo had claimed trial as an aggravating factor. I accept Counsel’s submission that court’s time was not wasted. Yeo had claimed trial because he had felt that there was a legal issue which required a legal determination. To Yeo’s credit, he did not protract the hearing by raising unnecessary factual issue and had instead agreed for the legal issue to be resolved by way of a comprehensive ASOF.[note: 29]

(j)     Yeo has similar antecedents. These are however dated.

54     I am of the view that the sentence that I have imposed on Yeo is in line with the sentencing precedents: see Annex A.

Conclusion

55     The above are my reasons for Yeo’s sentence.
