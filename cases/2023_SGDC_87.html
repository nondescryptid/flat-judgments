District Judge Christopher Goh Eng Chiang:

Introduction

1       The Accused pleaded guilty to three charges under the Road Traffic Act (Cap 276, 2004 Rev Ed) (“RTA”). Two additional charges were taken into consideration for the purposes of sentencing (“TIC”).

2       He was sentenced to a total of 16 weeks' imprisonment, a fine of $800, in default four (4) days' imprisonment and was disqualified from holding or obtaining all classes of driving licences (“DQAC”) for a total period of five years with effect from the date of his release from prison.

3       The Accused, being dissatisfied with the sentence and the order of disqualification I have imposed, now appeals against them.

The charges

4       The three charges that the Accused pleaded guilty to are summarised as follows:

(a)      DAC-909298-2021 (“C1”). Inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt (repeat offender). The Accused drove a car without due care and attention. He had failed to keep a proper lookout on the road, and collided into a pedestrian crossing the road, thereby causing grievous hurt to the said pedestrian. Further, as he had previously been convicted of an offence of speeding which conviction had not been set aside, the Accused was considered a repeat offender. This is an offence under s 65(1)(a), read with (“r/w”) s 65(8)(b) and punishable under (“p/u”) s 65(3)(b) r/w s 65(6)(d) of the RTA;

(b)      DAC-909299-2021 (“C2”). Failing to stop after an accident. The Accused failed to stop after colliding with the pedestrian. This is an offence under s 84(1), r/w s 84(7) and p/u s 131(2)(a) of the RTA;

(c)      DAC-909300-2021 (“C3”). Failing to render assistance. Having been involved in an accident that where serious injury[note: 1] was caused, the Accused failed to render such assistance that was reasonable in his power to render. This is an offence under s 84(3) r/w s 84(7) p/u s 84(8) r/w s 84(9) of the RTA.

5       The Accused had two TIC charges. These were:

(a)      DAC-909294-2021 (“C4”). Inconsiderate driving simpliciter (repeat offender). This offence occurred on a different day, prior to the present offences, namely on 19 December 2019 at 0139 hours. The Accused had failed to keep a proper lookout when making a discretionary left turn into Victoria Street and collided into a cyclist who was riding across the road at a designated pedestrian crossing when the traffic light signal was green in the favour of pedestrian. As the Accused was a repeat offender, this was an offence under s 65(1)(b) p/u s 5(b) of the RTA; and

(b)      DAC-909301-2021 (“C5”). Removing his vehicle from the scene without the authority of a police officer. This offence is connected with the offence to which the Accused pleaded guilty to. Having been involved in an accident that caused a person to be seriously injured, he had moved his vehicle without the authority of a police officer. This was an offence under s 84(4) r/w s 84(7) and p/u s 131(2)(a) of the RTA.

Summary of the facts

6       The Accused admitted to the Statement of Facts (“SOF”) without qualification. There are also two video footages that captured the accident. The first was from a Land Transport Authority (“LTA”) camera, and the second from the in-car camera of a vehicle. Both video footages were taken from the opposite side of the road.

The Junction

7       The accident took place at about 2311 hours on 9 July 2020 at the T-junction of East Coast Road and Frankel Avenue (“the Junction”).

8       The Junction is a signalized traffic light junction. There is a pedestrian crossing across Frankel Avenue but only one pedestrian crossing across East Coast Road as seen in Figure 1 (see arrows above). East Coast Road in both directions are dual carriageways.

The accident

9       On 9 July 2020, at about 2310 hours, the Accused was driving his car along East Coast Road in the direction of Upper East Coast Road.

10     Meanwhile, the Victim, an 85-year-old man, can be seen from the video footage emerging from the compound of a house along East Coast Road. He was about to cross East Coast Road, on the side of Frankel Avenue, just before the stop line. This was on the side of the Junction without a pedestrian crossing (see Figure 1). The Victim was also crossing East Coast Road within 50 metres of a pedestrian crossing (hence, he should have been using the pedestrian crossing) and while the traffic lights were showing green in favour of vehicles travelling along East Coast Road.

11     The video footage showed that when the Victim began crossing East Coast Road, the Accused’s vehicle can be seen about three lamp posts away, on the right lane of two lanes.

12     As the Accused approached the Junction, he drove without due care and attention, and failed to keep a proper lookout, colliding into the Victim. He only took evasive action when his car was almost upon the Victim. He then swerved slightly to his left to avoid hitting the Victim but failed to do so. The video footage showed that the front right portion of the car and the right-wing mirror hit the Victim[note: 2] and the impact of the collision caused the Victim to be thrown into the air and he landed slightly in front of the stop line, at the centre of East Coast Road. The collision occurred when the Victim was almost midway across the right lane of the East Coast Road.

13     The following are stills from the video footage from the LTA camera.

14     After the collision, the Accused slowed down his car briefly, but did not stop. Instead, he proceeded to drive away from the scene without rendering assistance to the Victim. The video footage showed the Victim subsequently being attended to by a passer-by.

15     The Accused lodged a police report online the next morning, on 10 July 2020, at about 1152 hours. He stated that he had been driving along East Coast Road towards Upper East Coast Road and heard a sound at the right side of his car, when he arrived home, he noticed that the right-side mirror was damaged.

16     At the time of accident, road surface was wet, there was a slight drizzle, traffic volume was light, and visibility was good.

Injuries suffered by the Victim

17     The Victim was later conveyed to Changi General Hospital (“CGH”). His medical report dated 12 October 2020 and 19 November 2020 stated that he suffered the following injuries:

(a)     Pelvic fracture with active bleeding and an expanding pelvic hematoma;

(b)     Right thigh hematoma;

(c)     Suspected bladder rapture;

(d)     Left scalp frontoparietal hematoma;

(e)     Lumbar spine L2 compression fracture;

(f)     Left foot 3rd and 4th metatarsal shaft fracture;

(g)     Left fibula closed mid-shaft fracture; and

(h)     Right medial malleolus closed fracture.

18     The Victim was given 43 days of hospitalisation leave from 10 July 2020 to 21 August 2020. He required the use of a wheelchair from the date of his discharge until 29 September 2020.

Repeat offender

19     Further, before the commission of the said offence, on 31 October 2018, the accused was convicted in State Court No 21 for an offence of speeding under s 63(4) of the RTA and was fined $900 and six months DQAC. Accordingly, the Accused was considered a repeat offender by virtue of s 65(8)(b) of the RTA and liable to be p/u s 65(3)(b) r/w s 65(6)(d) of the RTA.

Sentencing submissions and plea in mitigation

Prosecution’s sentencing submission

21     The Prosecution sought a global sentence of four months’ and three weeks’ imprisonment, a fine of $800 and five years DQAC.

Inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt (repeat offender)

22     The Prosecution submitted that the applicable sentencing framework is the harm-culpability framework applied by the High Court in PP v Sue Chang [2022] SGHC 176 (“Sue Chang”). However, as the Accused is a repeat offender by virtue of his previous speeding conviction, the punishment prescribed by law is double that of an inconsiderate driving charge under s 65(3)(a) of the RTA. Therefore, an appropriate uplift to the Sue Chang sentencing framework should apply.[note: 3]

23     The Prosecution applied the Sue Chang framework as follows:

(a)     The first step, considering the offence-specific factors and identify the level of harm caused and the Accused’s culpability:

(a)        Culpability. The Prosecution assessed the Accused’s culpability as being low to moderate. Although the Victim was crossing in an undesignated area and was within 50 metres from a designated pedestrian crossing, the Accused nonetheless failed to keep a proper lookout and more so, when he was approaching a signalised traffic light junction. He should have exercised more care and attention as there were houses on either sides of the road. Furthermore, from the video, the Victim could clearly be seen crossing the road at a slow and steady pace;

(b)        Harm caused. The Victim suffered multiple fractures and was on hospitalisation leave for 43 days up to 21 August 2020 and was wheelchair bound until 29 September 2020. Although he was treated conservatively, he had to undergo invasive treatment of angioplasty and embolization to stop bleeding from the pelvic fracture and his right thigh. The Prosecution therefore assessed the harm caused to the Victim as being moderate.

(b)     The second step, identifying the indicative sentencing range. Based on the assessment of the harm caused to the Victim and the Accused’s culpability, it was submitted that the indicative sentencing range was a fine or up to eight months’ imprisonment;

(c)     The third and fourth steps, namely, to identifying the appropriate starting point within the indicative sentencing range and to adjust this starting point to take into account offender-specific factors respectively. It appeared that the Prosecution has combined these two steps. It was submitted that the court should put little weight on the Accused’s plea of guilt as the incident was clearly captured on video. However, allowing for some downward adjustment, the Prosecution submitted a sentence of at least four months’ imprisonment plus the mandatory minimum sentence of five years DQAC.

Failing to render assistance

24     The Prosecution submitted that for the hit and run charge, where serious injury is caused, the benchmark sentence is a custodial sentence of three weeks imprisonment and 18 months DQAC (per PP v Lee Meng Soon [2007] 4 SLR(R) 240 (“Lee Meng Soon”) at [33] and Fernandez Joseph Ferdinent v PP [2007] 3 SLR(R) 65 (“Fernandez Joseph”) at [17].[note: 4]

25     The Accused had failed to stop and render assistance despite knowing full well that he had collided into the Victim. Despite this, he had “assumed” that the Victim would be fine and drove off without rendering assistance.

26     For this charge, the Prosecution submitted that the appropriate sentence was a sentence of at least three weeks imprisonment and 18 months DQAC.

Failing to stop

27     The Prosecution sought a fine of $800 for this charge.

Total sentence

28     The Prosecution further highlighted the Accused had an inconsiderate driving simpliciter charge that was TIC. This offence was committed on 19 December 2019, less than seven months prior to the present offence.

29     The court was urged to run the sentence for the inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt charge and the failing to render assistance charge consecutively to “properly capture the totality of the Accused’s egregious behaviour”. This would give a total sentence of four months’ and three weeks’ imprisonment, a fine of $800 and five years DQAC.[note: 5]

Defence’s sentencing submission / plea in mitigation

Inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt

30     The Defence agreed that the sentencing guideline for inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt was laid down by the High Court in Sue Chang.

31     The Defence submitted that the Accused’s culpability was low. This was due to the following reasons:

(a)     The traffic light was green in his favour;

(b)     The Victim should not have crossed where he did. It was one to two metres before the stop line and was within 50 metres of a designated pedestrian crossing;

(c)     The Victim appeared to have started crossing the road without looking out for oncoming vehicular traffic;

(d)     The Accused had attempted to avoid the Victim by braking and swerving but was unsuccessful;

(e)     There is no allegation that the Accused was speeding.

32     The harm caused by the Accused was assessed by the Defence to be between the low and moderate bands. The fractures were managed conservatively without the need for surgery and was likely to make a full recovery.

33     The Defence acknowledged that the punishment prescribed by law in the present case was double that in Sue Chang as the accused was a repeat offender. It was submitted that the court should apply the sentencing ranges set out in Sue Chang, which in this instant was a fine or up to four months’ imprisonment, and then provide an uplift to the indicative starting sentence as the Accused was a repeat offender, instead of increasing the sentencing ranges as set out in Sue Chang. [note: 6]

34     The Defence submitted that the speeding offence that caused the Accused to be considered as a repeat offender should be on the lower end of the antecedent offences and a one-week uplift would be sufficient.[note: 7] The Defence therefore submitted that the appropriate sentence was three weeks’ imprisonment.

35     For the offence of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt, the law prescribes a mandatory disqualification term of five years under s 65(6)(d) of the RTA. The Defence highlighted that the court should not ignore the fact that the Accused’s driving licence has been suspended by the traffic police since 13 July 2020. It was submitted that this amounted to a special reason and a period of disqualification of less than five years should therefore be imposed.[note: 8]

Failing to render assistance and failing to stop

36     The Defence submission for the two offences above are as follows:

(a)      Failing to render assistance.[note: 9] Three weeks’ imprisonment;

(b)      Failing to stop.[note: 10] A fine.

Mitigating factors

37     The Defence has highlighted the following mitigating factors:

(a)      Financial consequence. The Accused may lose his job as a consequence of the sentence and consequently, his income. He would then have difficulty paying his two sons’ educational fees;

(b)      Medical issues. The following were highlighted:

(a)       He suffers from psychiatric issues, including “adjustment disorder” and “anxious distress”;[note: 11]

(b)       He also suffers from pain and numbness in his right leg and has had heart surgery.[note: 12]

Global sentence

38     As the offence of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt and failing to render assistance arose out of the same accident, the Defence submitted that the two sentences of imprisonment should run concurrently. Therefore, the total sentence submitted by the Defence was three weeks’ imprisonment, a fine and a DQAC term of less than the mandatory minimum of five years.

Decision on sentence

39     I will deal with each offence in turn.

Inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt (DAC-909298-2021).

Punishment prescribed by law

40     The punishment prescribed by law under s 65(1)(a), r/w s 65(8)(b) p/u s 65(3)(b) r/w s 65(6)(d) of the RTA for an offence of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt for a repeat offender is a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding four years or to both and to a mandatory minimum term of five years DQAC.

(a)      The Prosecution’s approach. Although the Prosecution did not set out their proposed sentencing framework in a three-by-three sentencing matrix, their submission suggested that each individual sentencing range would be doubled. I have reproduced an adapted three-by-three sentencing matrix based on their submissions[note: 13];

(b)      The Defence’s approach. The first step was to utilise the sentencing matrix set out in Sue Chang to determine the indicative starting sentence and then to analyse the offence that rendered the offender a repeat offender to come to an appropriate uplift for that offence.[note: 14]

43     I was of the view that the court should adopt the adapted sentencing matrix for repeat offenders as submitted by the Prosecution. This will allow the sentencing court to utilise the full sentencing range prescribed by law. The offence that rendered an offender a repeat offender would subsequently be considered in order to determine whether or there should be a downward (or upward) adjustment to the sentence reached.

Determining the appropriate sentence

44     In Sue Chang, at [83] – [108] the High Court adopted the Logachev-hybrid approach as this would provide “a more structured guidance to sentencing judges in arriving at the appropriate sentence to impose …. [and] ….. promotes a consistency of approach, which is better able to translate to consistency of outcome between like cases”.[note: 15] The Logachev-hybrid five-step approach set out below:

(a)     Identify the level of the offender’s culpability and harm;

(b)     Identify the indicative sentencing range;

(c)     Identify the starting point within the indicative sentencing range;

(d)     Adjusting the starting point, taking into account offender-specific factors;

(e)     Make further adjustments to take into account the totality principle.

(1)   The first step: identifying the offender’s culpability and harm caused to the Victim

45     The first step is to identify the offender’s culpability and harm caused to the Victim.

(A)   Culpability.

46     In Sue Chang at [93], the High Court held that the degree of culpability generally refers to the relatively blameworthiness disclosed by an offender’s actions and could be measured. The video footages provided the court with the objective evidence of the accident. From this footage, I considered the following culpability reducing factors:

(a)     The Victim did not cross the road at a designated pedestrian crossing and he had crossed it within 50 metres of a designated pedestrian crossing (see Figure 1);

(b)     Further, I was prepared to accept the Defence’s submission that it appeared that the Victim had crossed the road without looking out for oncoming traffic which he should have, especially since East Coast Road is a major road, notwithstanding that the Prosecution had highlighted that there were houses on either side of the road[note: 16], perhaps to suggest that this could be a residential area.

47     In my view, the culpability enhancing factors were as follows:

(a)     When the Victim began crossing the road, the Accused’s car was about three lampposts away (see Figure 2). East Coast Road is a relatively straight road and there was no obstruction between the Accused’s car and the Victim. Neither were there any other vehicles. If the Accused had indeed maintained a proper lookout, he would have seen the Victim. On the contrary, it appeared that the Accused did not notice the Victim until almost at the very last minute. This is evident from the video footage when he swerved slightly to his left to avoid hitting him. In fact, it was fortunate that he did so and this last-minute manoeuvre, in my view, prevented an even more serious accident from occurring;

(b)     The Defence had submitted that the raindrops on the vehicle’s windscreen had compromised visibility.[note: 17] The rain could be seen from a second video clip taken from an in-car camera of the vehicle on the opposite side of the junction. Notwithstanding this, I was of the view that visibility was relatively good, and the Victim should have been seen by the Accused. Further, as the Accused was approaching a traffic junction, and he should have been relatively more alert. Assuming that his visibility had been compromised by the rain, this is all the more reason for the Accused to be driving at a slower speed than he was when approaching this junction.

48     Having considered these factors, I assessed the Accused’s culpability in the present case to be on the high end of the low range.

(B)   Harm caused

49     In Sue Chang at [87], the High Court held that the degree of harm would generally refer to the degree of the grievous bodily injury caused to a victim and the levels of harm could thus be broadly divided based on the severity of the grievous hurt suffered. These are as follows:

Degree of harm

Description

Low

The hurt caused can be managed with conservative treatment, with no or short periods of hospitalization and/or medical leave. The harm occasioned would typically involve minor fractures / dislocation at less vulnerable parts of the body.

Moderate

The injuries are of a more permanent nature and/or involve more complex fractures / dislocation which necessitate some surgical procedures with a moderate period of hospitalisation and / or medical leave.

Serious

The injuries are of a very serious / permanent nature and/or necessitate significant surgical procedures. The victim’s daily living is usually permanently and severely affected. This includes injuries resulting in loss of limb, sight, hearing, member or other major bodily functions or paralysis.



50     The Victim’s injuries included numerous fractures. These included a pelvic fracture with active bleeding and an expanding pelvic hematoma, a lumbar spine L2 compression fracture, a left foot 3rd and 4th metatarsal shaft fracture, a left fibula closed mid-shaft fracture and a right medial malleolus closed fracture. No surgery was required for the fractures. However, he required angiography and embolization to stop the bleeding from the pelvic fracture and from a right thigh hematoma.

51     In all, the Victim was given 43 days of hospitalisation leave from 10 July 2020 to 21 August 2020. He also required the use of a wheelchair from the date of his discharge until 29 September 2020.[note: 18]

52     I note that the Victim has generally recovered well from the injuries suffered and there is no evidence that any of the Victim’s injuries were of a permanent nature.

53     For the above reasons. I assessed that the harm caused in this case to be in the low end of the moderate range. This is generally in line with the assessment of both the Prosecution and the Defence.[note: 19]

(2)   The second step: the indicative sentencing range

54     As I have mentioned earlier in these grounds at [43], above, I accepted the adapted sentencing matrix for repeat offenders as submitted by the Prosecution. This sentencing matrix for a repeat offender convicted of an offence of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt can be found at [42(a)], above.

55     Having assessed the culpability of the Accused as being in the high end of the low range, and that the harm caused by the Accused to be at the low end of the moderate range, the indicative sentencing range based on the adapted sentencing matrix would be a sentence of a fine or a term of imprisonment of up to eight months’ imprisonment.

(3)   The third step: the indicative starting sentence within the range

56     Both the Prosecution and the Defence did not submit where the indicative starting sentence within this range would be. Although the indicative sentence range does include the provision of a fine only (with a DQ term), it appears that the Defence has conceded that the custodial threshold was crossed in the present case.

57     Based on the assessment of the Accused’s culpability and the harm caused to the Victim, I adjudged that the indicative starting sentence would be in the region of about 12 weeks’ imprisonment. It should be noted that in determining the indicating starting sentence, I have taken into account that the offence which caused the Accused to be considered a repeat offender was a speeding charge under s 63(4) of the RTA and that the offence was relatively recent, with the Accused having been convicted of this offence on 31 October 2018.

(4)   The fourth step: adjusting the starting point, taking into account offender-specific factors

58     I next considered the offender-specific factors that would warrant an adjustment of the indicative starting sentence.

(A)   Mitigating factors

59     The following mitigating factors were considered:

(a)      Pleading guilty. The main factor in the Accused’s favour is that he has pleaded guilty to the charges;

(b)      The Accused’s medical condition(s).[note: 20] The Defence had highlighted the Accused’s medical (including psychological) conditions. While I was prepared to accept these as mitigating factors, I did not put much weight on them;

(c)      Financial consequences. It was highlighted that, as a consequence of the sentence, he could lose his job, his only source of income. I put no weight on this. In PP v M Raveendran [2022] SLR 1183 (“Raveendran”), the High Court held at inter alia at [43] that “ … if the court were to place weight on factors such as the financial consequences of a particular sentence, and reduce an offender’s sentence on that basis, it would result in the more favourable treatment of certain individuals” and at [48], that “ …. such extrinsic factors will generally have no mitigating weight in sentencing”.

(B)   Aggravating factors

60     While I have considered that the above factors would warrant a downward adjustment of the sentence, the flip side would be to consider whether there were any aggravating factors that would, conversely, warrant an uplift adjustment to the indicative starting sentence. In this respect, I considered the Accused’s long list of compounded traffic offences.

(a)      The Accused’s driving history. Since 1 November 2019, under s 139AA of the RTA, for the purpose of determining the appropriate sentence, a court may take into account any offence that has been compounded (whether before, on or after the date of commencement of the amended RTA on 1 November 2019) as an aggravating factor. It states:

For the purpose of determining the appropriate sentence for an offence committed by a person under this Act, a court may take into account, as an aggravating factor, any offence that has been compounded (whether before, on or after the date of commencement of section 21 of the Road Traffic (Amendment Act 2019) under this Act before the date of the sentencing.

In the present case, the Accused has eight compounded driving offences (see the list of compounded offences at [20], above). I was of the view that the compounded driving offences should be taken into consideration in determining sentence as they showed the Accused’s propensity for committing traffic offences.

(b)      The TIC charges. The Accused faced two TIC charges. One of these TIC charges was for an offence of inconsiderate driving (repeat offender) under s 65(1)(b) of the RTA. This offence was committed on 19 December 2020, less than seven months before the present offence. The Accused had turned left at a signalised traffic light junction and collided with a cyclist cycling across the pedestrian crossing and who had the right of way.[note: 21] This reinforced my view that the Accused’s driving manner left much to be desired.

61     Taking all these offender-specific factors together, in particular the aggravating factors, I was of the view that there was no need to adjust the indicative starting sentence downwards (or upwards). Hence, I adjudged that the appropriate final sentence for a repeat offender committing the offence of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt to be 12 weeks’ imprisonment.

(5)   The fifth step: make further adjustments to take into account the totality principle.

62     For the fifth step, I will return to this after determining the sentences for the remaining two charges that the accused had pleaded guilty to.

(6)   Disqualification term

63     The present offence also prescribes a mandatory term of five years DQAC under s 65(6)(d) of the RTA unless there are special reasons. The Defence had submitted that, as the Accused had already been suspended by Traffic Police since 13 July 2020[note: 22], this amounted to a special reason and urged the court to impose a disqualification period of less than the mandatory minimum period of five years.

64     The meaning of “special reasons” was considered by the High Court in PP v MV Balakrishnan v PP [1998] 2 SLR(R) 846 (“Balakrishnan”) and was followed in Muhammad Faizal bin Rahim v PP [2012] 1 SLR 116 (“Faizal”) and referred to in Prathib s/o M Balan v PP [2018] 3 SLR 1066 (“Prathib”). The High Court has held that the phrase “special reasons” has a narrow meaning. Only reasons connected to the offence and not reasons related to the offender are to be considered.

65     The Defence had submitted that the fact that the Accused’s driving licence was suspended by Traffic Police was a reason that was not connected to the offence nor the offender and that the categories should not be closed.[note: 23] I disagreed with the submission. The fact that Traffic Police had suspended the Accused from driving was not a reason connected the offence. It was a reason that was related to the offender and is therefore not a special reason. Hence, at the very minimum, the court is required to impose the mandatory period of five years DQAC. In the present case, I was of the view that the mandatory disqualification period was appropriate, and it was not necessary to impose a longer term of disqualification.

Sentence imposed

66     For the aforesaid reasons, the sentence imposed by the court for a repeat offender committing the offence of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt is 12 weeks imprisonment and five years DQAC.

Failing to stop after an accident (DAC-909299-2021)

Punishment prescribed by law

67     The punishment prescribed by law for the offence of failing to stop after an accident is provided for under s 131(2)(a) of the RTA. For a first offender, the punishment prescribed is a fine not exceeding $1,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding three months. In addition, under s 42(1) of the RTA, the court has the discretion to impose a disqualification period for life or for such period as the court may think fit.

Sentence imposed

68     Both the Prosecution and the Defence agreed that the appropriate sentence for this offence was a fine. I did not disagree. Accordingly, I sentenced the Accused to a fine of $800, in default, four (4) days’ imprisonment.

Failing to render assistance (DAC-909300-2021)

Punishment prescribed by law

69     This offence is sometimes referred to as a “hit and run charge”. Under s 84(8) of the RTA, a first offender who fails to comply with s 84(3) in circumstances where he has caused serious injury or death to another person is punishable with a fine of up to $3,000 or imprisonment for up to 12 months and mandatory disqualification of at least one year unless there are special reasons.

Determining the appropriate sentence

70     Both the Prosecution and the Defence agree that the appropriate sentence for this offence was three weeks’ imprisonment.[note: 24] In addition, the Prosecution cited two cases to support their submission. These were Fernandez Joseph Ferdinent v PP [2007] SGHC 60 (“Fernandez Joseph”) and PP v Lee Meng Soon [2007] SGHC 129 (“Lee Meng Soon”).

71     In Fernandez Joseph, the offender was convicted after trial for several RTA offences. For the offence of failing to render assistance, the offender was sentenced to six weeks’ imprisonment and 18 months DQAC. The offender’s car hit the victim’s motorcycle from the rear as he was negotiating a bend along a flyover. The impact flung the victim off the motorcycle. The offender only stopped by another motorist about 500 m away. His appeal against conviction and sentence was dismissed.

72     In Lim Meng Soon, the offender was also convicted after trial for several RTA offences. For the offence of failing to render assistance, the offender was sentenced to four weeks’ imprisonment and two years DQAC. The offender had collided with the rear of a motorcycle at a signalised cross-junction. Both the rider and his pillion suffered serious injuries. The offender drove away but was later apprehended. The Prosecution’s appeal against sentence for this offence was dismissed.

73     At [33], Lee Sieu Kin J observed that for the offence of failing to render assistance involving serious injury or death, the range of imprisonment sentences have been for three to six weeks’ imprisonment and a disqualification period of 18 months. In addition, the factors affecting the severity of sentence include, amongst other thing, (a) the degree of injury or property damage; (b) evidence of alcohol consumption; (c) failing to stop to evade arrest; and (d) prior antecedents.

74     In this present case, I considered the following factors:

(a)     The injuries suffered by the Victim. I have since described the Victim’s injuries earlier in these grounds. They were not minor;

(b)     Accused’s actions after the accident. While the Accused did not fail to stop to evade arrest in the sense that he was not being pursued by the police, his subsequent actions thereafter was very telling. In the Statement of Facts at [6], it was revealed that the Accused “…. lodged a police report online on 10 July 2020, at about 11:52 am, stating that he had been driving along East Coast Road towards Upper East Coast Road and heard a sound at the right side of his car, when he arrived home, he noticed that the right side mirror was damaged.” In my view, this clearly showed that the Accused was attempting to evade responsibility. A review of the video footage clearly showed that the Victim had crossed the entire frontage of his vehicle and that he had taken evasive at the very last moment. I was incredulous that the Accused claimed that he did not know that he had collided with someone.

75     In Lim Meng Soon at [34], Lee Sieu Kin J highlighted that “ …. hit-and-run offences must be severely dealt with as it constitutes a reprehensible abdication of the fundamental moral obligation to render assistance to a victim of an accident engineered by one’sown negligence or recklessness…”. Further, in the event where life threatening injuries are suffered by a victim, any assistance rendered, even from the offender, could well mean the difference between life and death for that victim. Hence, any sentence imposed seeks not only to punish an offender, but to deter any future offender from committing such an offence.

Sentence imposed

76     All considered, I was of the view that the appropriate sentence in this instance was four weeks’ imprisonment and 24 months DQAC.

Consecutive or concurrent sentences

77     The Prosecution submitted that these two sentences of imprisonment should run consecutively while the Defence submitted for concurrent imprisonment sentences as the offences arose from the same accident.[note: 25]

78     I note that s 84(10) of the RTA from provides the following:

(10)  Where at one trial the driver of a motor vehicle is convicted and sentenced to imprisonment for —

(a)    an offence involving the use of the motor vehicle by him or her whereby any serious injury or death is caused to another person; and

(b)    an offence under subsection (7) arising from his or her failure to comply with subsection (3),

an offence under subsection (7) arising from his or her failure to comply with subsection (3),

79     As the Accused has fulfilled the above two conditions in s 84(10) of the RTA, I ordered the sentences of imprisonment imposed for the offences of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt (repeat offender) and failing to render assistance to run consecutively.

Final sentence imposed

The last look

80     Before confirming the final sentence, I proceeded to take a final review of the individual sentences imposed to determine whether any further adjustments were required. I concluded there were none.

81     I next reviewed the total sentence imposed, in particular for the offence of inconsiderate driving causing grievous hurt (repeat offender), to consider Step 5 of the Logachev-hybrid five-step sentencing approach (see [62] above) to consider whether there was a need to further adjustments to take into account the totality principle. I likewise considered that there were none.
