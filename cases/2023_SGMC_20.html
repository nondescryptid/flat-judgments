5       The reasons I have given to the parties at the time of imposition of the aforesaid sentences on the AP on 17 April 2023 were as follows:

PROBATION INAPPROPRIATE

(a)     Sentencing considerations laid down by the High Court indicate that where severe emotional and reputational harm is caused by an accused to a victim, rehabilitation must yield to deterrence as the dominant sentencing objective (PP v Koh Wen Jie Boaz [2016] 1 SLR 334 at [30])(“Boaz Koh”). In the context of voyeurism offences under s 377BB(4) PC, a three-judge coram of the High Court recently held that a deterrence sentence is usually warranted for such offences as they “often inflict significant emotional harm on the victim” (Nicholas Tan Siew Chye v PP [2023] SGHC 35 (“Nicholas Tan”) at [44]). More pertinently, a different three-judge coram of the High Court held that general deterrence is likely to be the overriding sentencing consideration where an offender distributes intimate images or recordings of another person without that person’s consent (PP v GED and other appeals [2022] SGHC 301 (“GED”) at [106]). This remains so in this case, notwithstanding the accused’s age at the material time.

(b)     Although the AP was 17 to 20 years old when he offended, rehabilitation is eclipsed by deterrence for four key reasons.

(i)       Evidence had been adduced by the prosecution to show that the AP had thoroughly humiliated and traumatised, XXXX, the victim (V), causing her severe emotional and reputational harm by posting V’s nudes online in pornographic websites and sent them to a mutual friend where V’s private parts and face were visible and these nudes were viewed or shared thousands of times as of 2021. In fact, more than one of the videos posted by the AP on xHamster were re-shared on various other pornographic websites which only serves to compound the grave harm suffered by V. Even if the AP had indeed deleted V’s nudes from xHamster, that is not a sign of repentance as he went on to send a nude video of V to a mutual friend, YYY. Further, the AP deliberately assumed V’s persona in the caption for each Tumblr post which were all accompanied by degrading captions that portrayed V as a promiscuous woman who openly invited others to sexualise her and who had posted her own nudes online. More egregiously, some of the captions even played into pornographic tropes and rape fantasies, which would have compounded V’s trauma and humiliation.

(ii)       Deterrence must take centre stage as the AP had committed serious offences (Boaz Koh at [30]) of distributing another person’s nudes which brings deterrence to the fore. The maximum punishment under s 292(1)(a) PC is three months’ imprisonment and a fine, but this belies the gravity of the AP’s offending. To reflect the severity of such offences, Parliament has since enacted s 377BE PC, which provides for a maximum imprisonment term of five years and caning. The strong legislative response to offences involving the distribution of intimate images and recordings reflects Parliament’s intention to place deterrence at the fore for such offences (see, in this vein, Nicholas Tan at [48]).

(iii)       Deterrence is the predominant sentencing consideration as evidence had been adduced by the prosecution to show that the AP is recalcitrant (Boaz Koh at [30]). The AP’s first charge alone, for possessing 14 nude videos and photographs of V that he uploaded on Tumblr, spanned a period of about a year. He then posted V’s nudes on xHamster and sent them to a mutual friend, YYY on four separate occasions in 2019. Unrepentant, he illicitly took photographs and videos of other women in compromising positions on three distinct occasions in 2020, before he was finally arrested and found to be in possession of obscene films. With each passing year, not only did the AP not gain insight into his wrongdoing, but he also evinced time and again his total disregard of the privacy of the women who had been intimate with him.

(iv)       The AP should be treated as an adult offender to whom the presumptive primacy of rehabilitation did not apply, notwithstanding that he was below the age of 21 when he offended (A Karthik at [34] and [58]). The AP was 17 years old when he first offended, but he continued offending until he was just two months shy of turning 21 years old and there is also no evidence of the AP’s rehabilitative progress since the time of his offending. There is nothing to suggest that he has taken any active steps post-offence to leave his errant ways behind (PP v Siow Kai Yuan Terence [2020] 4 SLR 1412 (“Terence Siow”) at [55(a)] and [56(b)]). It does not appear that he has sought treatment or counselling to address the root causes of his offending. Moreover, some of the videos in MAC-910356-2021 were pornographic videos that the AP had downloaded online, with no evidence that he has stopped or limited his consumption of pornography.

(c)     The AP’s offending cannot be dismissed as mere youthful folly. There is no innocent excuse for posting a girlfriend’s or ex-girlfriend’s nudes on Tumblr with lewd captions, or on xHamster, a pornographic website. It would also have been obvious to anyone – and especially to a 20-year-old – that uploading V’s nudes on a pornographic website would cause her significant distress. What the AP’s offending reveals is not immaturity, inexperience or indiscretion, but a callous indifference to V’s ensuing humiliation.

(d)     The AP’s persistent offending over three years also indicates that his rehabilitative prospects are not exceptional (Praveen s/o Krishnan v PP [2018] 3 SLR 1300 at [30(b)]). The AP’s good performance in National Service and university did not evince his capacity for reform as he was able to compartmentalise his wrongdoing from other well-functioning aspects of his life, much like the respondent in Terence Siow (Terence Siow at [33] and [77]). He should not be allowed to come to court to say that he has a bright future in view of his good university performance and must serve the sentence for his offences first, as stated in the Law Minister’s Ministerial Statement on the Review of Sentencing Framework for Sexual and Hurt Offences on 5 March 2021.

(e)     I have considered the defence submissions and rejected them for the following reasons as submitted by the prosecution:

(i)       The defence submission that the AP “could not” delete V’s nudes from his Tumblr account as he “no longer has access” to the account glossed over the fact that the AP did have access to his own Tumblr account from May 2017 (when he first uploaded V’s nudes) all the way until at least 2021. At no point in time over that period of at least three and a half years did the AP delete V’s nudes from his Tumblr account or delete his Tumblr account entirely. It was by the AP’s choice that V’s nudes remained accessible on his Tumblr account for years, and he is thus responsible for the resulting harm to V.

(ii)       The defence submission that specific deterrence is not a key sentencing principle because the AP offended when he was below the age of 21 and is now more mature do not militate against the primacy of specific deterrence in the present case as the AP had offended repeatedly over three years and up until his arrest at the age of 20 years and ten months and his persistent offending brings specific deterrence to the fore.

(iii)       The defence submission that for the s 30(1) Films Act offence, the need for general deterrence is proportionate to the number of obscene films in an offender’s possession cannot be the case as general deterrence aims to deter other like-minded members of the general public. It is the type and/ or circumstances of a particular offence (eg, whether the offence was premeditated or committed against vulnerable victims; the prevalence of the particular offence; or whether the offence affected public safety) that is relevant to the need to deter other members of the public (PP v Law Aik Meng [2007] 2 SLR(R) 814 at [24]–[25]), not the minutiae of the number of obscene films in an offender’s possession.

(iv)       The defence argument that the “like-minded members of the general public” who might need to be deterred are teenagers between the ages of 17 and 20, and that such teenagers should not be punished harshly” is mistaken as general deterrence targets like-minded members of the public which comprise would-be offenders of any age who are tempted to commit similar offences. The defence submission that teenagers between the ages of 17 and 20 should not be dealt with harshly is also a non-sequitur as this court is concerned only with how the AP in the present case should be dealt with since every sentencing matter should be based on its own facts and circumstances.

(v)       Contrary to the defence submission that as the AP was below the age of 21 when he offended, his rehabilitative potential and remorse should be considered for a probation report to be called for him to determine his suitability for probation, it is trite law that when sentencing an offender who was at or below the age of 21 at the time of offending but above the age of 21 at the time of sentencing, the court cannot and should not focus exclusively on the offender’s age at the time of offending. Instead, in deciding whether the offender should be treated as a youthful or adult offender, the court must examine all the facts, including the offender’s age at both material points in time, the length of the delay between both points, and any evidence of the offender’s rehabilitative progress in the intervening period (A Karthik v PP [2018] 5 SLR 1289 at [58]).

(vi)       The AP is who is now 23 and a half years old, is an adult offender who should be treated as such and the main sentencing principle engaged is deterrence, not rehabilitation, and the appropriate sentence is therefore imprisonment. If anything, it is highly worrying that the AP was capable of such cruelty even before he was 21 years old. Many youths are immature, but few invite the sexual objectification of their ex-girlfriends by posting their ex-girlfriends’ nudes on pornographic websites. Equally few upload their ex-girlfriends’/ girlfriends’ nudes on social media websites with degrading captions that play into pornographic tropes. Such disturbing misconduct cannot be met with a slap on the wrist in the form of a probation order.

(vii)       As regards the defence assertion that the AP’s good performance in National Service and university evinced his capacity for reform, the evidence adduced by the prosecution showed that the AP has not made any rehabilitative progress. He offended while being a student leader at Ngee Ann Polytechnic and a national karate athlete, and while performing well at an aerospace engineering internship. In other words, he was able to compartmentalise his good performance in other areas of his life from his offending behaviour (see PP v Siow Kai Yuan Terence [2020] 4 SLR 1412 at [33] and [77]). His performance in National Service and university were thus not probative of his rehabilitative potential.

(viii)       The defence had understated the AP’s transgressions as a misguided exploration of sexuality as what his offences reflected is a man who was callously indifferent to the foreseeable suffering of his ex-girlfriend/ girlfriend, who disregarded the privacy of other women with whom he had been intimate, and who prioritised the satisfaction of his sexual urges above the dignity of women. Such behaviour is not mere teenage sexual curiosity, but a consistent pattern of misconduct that the court must stem by imposing a deterrent sentence of imprisonment.

(ix)       On the defence claim that the AP is remorseful, this is a bare assertion with no tangible evidence of his remorse as the AP has taken no steps to remedy the root causes of his offending and gain further insight into his offending behaviour.

(g)     In arriving at the above individual and global sentences for the s 292(1)(a) PC offences, I agreed with the prosecution submissions on the various aggravating factors in the present case as follows:

(i)        Premeditation in crafting lewd captions in V’s voice for each Tumblr post and use of his Tumblr username – “aversgirl” to bolster the illusion that it was V herself who had shared her nudes. By uploading V’s nudes on a pornographic website, xHamster, the AP expressly intended for other viewers to objectify and sexualise her for their gratification;

(ii)        Persistent offending as the AP had posted V’s nudes on Tumblr not once, but repeatedly, over a year. The next year, he uploaded her nudes on xHamster on three occasions and sent a nude video of her to a friend.

(iii)        Abuse of trust as V was his girlfriend who had consented for him to make intimate recordings of her as she had trusted that those recordings would remain strictly within the confines of their relationship during and after their relationship ended. She never expected that the AP would betray her trust by sharing recordings of her most intimate and private moments online, both during their relationship (from May 2017 to end 2017) and after they had parted ways (from the start of 2018 to mid-2018).

(iv)        Multiple obscene images and recordings were transmitted as the first charge involved the transmission of 12 obscene photographs and two obscene videos, while the second charge involved the transmission of several obscene photographs and videos.

(v)        Recordings were widely circulated, viewed and shared in Tumblr and xHamster and reposted on other pornographic websites thousands of time as of 2021 with one post attracting 6,849 “likes” and shares. The AP’s Tumblr account remained active until at least 2021 which meant that V’s nudes were accessible on his account for about four years. As of 2021, one such video of V, which was reposted on multiple pornographic websites, was viewed 11,982 times on one pornographic website alone. The more widely an intimate recording is distributed, the more the harm is generally caused (GED at [53]). V’s nudes have been so widely circulated that it is now practically impossible to erase all traces of them online.

(vi)        Recording was distributed to V’s friend as the AP had sent a video of V having sex with a man to her friend who is someone known to V and such targeted distribution of an intimate recording to V’s friend may cause just as much, if not more, harm to V than distribution to many strangers on a pornographic website (GED at [53]).

(vii)        Grave violation of V’s privacy of the highest order due to the extremely intimate nature of the recordings disseminated where V’s all private parts were visible and the videos depicted V masturbating, having penetrative sex, or fellating a man with the videos capturing both movement and sound (unlike still images).

(viii)        V is identifiable in the photographs and videos as her face is partly visible in one of the videos that the AP posted on xHamster and her friend actually identified her in several of her nude videos that had been shared on pornographic websites (see the Statement of Facts at paragraph 10). The more identifiable the victim, the greater the harm and distress the victim will likely suffer (GED at [52]).

(ix)        V did not consent to the transmission of her nude images and recordings as the AP committed the offences after their relationship ended and it was only in about June 2020 that V found out from a mutual friend that her nude videos were circulating on pornographic websites and filed a police report.

(x)        3 similar TIC offences under s 377BB(3) p/u s 377BB(7) PC which, much like the s 292(1)(a) PC offences, involved a grave violation by the AP of women’s privacy, a wanton abuse of trust, and a blatant indifference to his victims’ humiliation.

(xi)       The above aggravating factors are objective aspects of the harm caused by the dissemination of intimate images and recordings which can serve as indicia from which the court can objectively infer the gravity of the harm caused to V. (GED at [55], recently affirmed in Nicholas Tan at [71]).

(h)     I accepted the prosecution submissions that there are no compelling mitigating factors in the present case :

(i)       The AP’s plea of guilt is of little weight. He only pleaded guilty on the first day of trial. Furthermore, the evidence against him was strong and he had little choice but to plead guilty. He was caught red-handed for the s 30(1) FA offence, and the evidence for the s 292(1)(a) PC charges pointed to him as the culprit since he was the only person who possessed so many of V’s nudes. His plea of guilt is hence hardly mitigating (PP v BDB [2018] 1 SLR 127 at [74]).

(ii)       Although the AP has no antecedents, he should not be treated as a first time offender as he committed multiple offences over a long period of three years (Chen Weixiong Jerriek v PP [2003] 2 SLR(R) 334 at [15] and [17]).

(i)       I am mindful of the High Court exhortation that no two cases can or will ever be completely identical or symmetrical, the sentences cannot be determined with mathematical certainty but must not be arbitrary and must fit the crime. (Dinesh Singh Bhatia s/o Amarjeet Singh v PP [2005] 3 SLR(R) 1 at [24]). I note that while both parties have relied on the case of PP v Koh Kwok Lun, Samuel [2016] SGDC 130 where the offender was sentenced to ten weeks’ imprisonment for a single charge under s 292(1)(a) PC with no TIC charge as sentencing precedent, each party had tried to distinguish it on the facts to show the differences. I agreed with the prosecution that the present case is more egregious as the offender in the aforesaid Samuel Koh’s case committed an one-off with no repeated offence and transmitted a single (and no multiple) obscene photo image which showed only the victim’s exposed breasts to a friend not known to the victim. Samuel Koh’s case is less egregious than our present case as in that case, there was no grave abuse of trust, no full private parts, no highly sexual and private acts (such as fellatio, masturbation, and penetrative sexual intercourse) shown in at least 4 videos with intensely demeaning captions posted on Tumblr and xHamster which attracted hundreds, if not thousands, of views, and which were widely re-shared on other Tumblr accounts and pornographic websites. Contrary to the defence submissions, both the offender in Samuel Koh’s case and the AP did not profit from his offences and that the offences did not constitute revenge pornography and V was in fact identified by her friend in several of these videos.

(j)     As for the s 30(1) FA charge which concerned the AP’s possession of 44 obscene films, I agreed with the following prosecution submissions when I imposed a 2 weeks sentence for this charge:

(i)     There are two factors which heighten the AP’s culpability:

(1)       The 44 obscene films comprised not only pornographic videos that the AP had downloaded online, but also voyeuristic videos of women engaging in sexual acts that he had personally recorded. The AP was not a wide-eyed, sexually curious teenager – he was a voyeur who satiated his sexual urges at the expense of multiple women’s dignity, and who could not care less about respecting the privacy of women at their most intimate and vulnerable moments.

(2)       The 44 obscene films were found across three devices (two iPhones and a hard disk), which suggests that the AP likely obtained them over an extended period and not all at once. This is aggravating as it speaks to his commitment to possessing obscene material.

(k)     As regards the defence argument that “[a]part from the videos/images canvassed in the 1st – 3rd Charges, there is no evidence that [the AP] circulated the other videos/images”, the images and videos that formed the subject matter of the first to third charges (under s 292(1)(a) PC) did not overlap with the 44 videos that were the subject matter of the s 30(1) FA charge. In other words, besides posting 12 nude images and two nude videos of V on Tumblr (MAC-910350-2021), posting three nude videos of V on xHamster (MAC-910351-2021) and sending a nude video of V to a mutual friend (MAC-910352-2021), the AP was also in possession of another 44 obscene films (MAC-9103562021). It is not mitigating that the AP did not circulate the 44 obscene films in his possession. The gravamen of an offence under s 30(1) FA lies in the possession, not distribution, of obscene films. The offender in PP v Chong Hou En [2015] 3 SLR 222 (“Chong Hou En”), who was convicted of an offence under s 30(1) FA (among other offences) and sentenced to 4 weeks imprisonment, likewise did not distribute the obscene films in his possession.

(l)     As for defence argument that the AP did not profit from the possession of the 44 obscene films, which were mainly for his personal consumption, the absence of an aggravating factor is not a mitigating factor and the offender in Chong Hou En similarly possessed obscene films for his own consumption and without profiting.

(D) CONCLUSION

6       In my view, the aforesaid global sentence of 13 weeks’ imprisonment which I have imposed on the AP was not manifestly excessive or inadequate, but entirely appropriate and consistent with the principles of proportionality and totality without the effect of a crushing sentence on the AP. I extended the AP’s current bail and allowed his application to complete the second semester of his 1st year NTU undergraduate Mechanical Engineering course and to commence serving his sentence on 15 May 2023 in order to avoid disrupting his studies.
