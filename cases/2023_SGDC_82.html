Introduction

1       This judgment arose from a cross-appeal by both the Plaintiff and Defendant against the decision of the learned Deputy Registrar Vince Gui Chuan Cheng (the “DR”) in SUM 2196/2021 (“SUM 2196” or the “Plaintiff’s Striking Out Application”) and SUM 4011/2021 (“SUM 4011” or the “Defendant’s Striking Out Application”).

Background Facts

2       The salient background facts and procedural history are highlighted below.

3       The Plaintiff is a sole proprietorship registered in Singapore and its primary business is the provision of, inter alia, freight forwarding services. The Defendant is a Singapore incorporated company and its primary business is the installation of industrial machinery, equipment, and mechanical engineering works.

4       Between 2013 to 2020, the Plaintiff’s freight forwarding services were engaged by the Defendant. The Plaintiff specifically claimed that parties had entered into an agreement for the said services by way of a letter and email dated 11 January 2013 (the “Agreement”), subject to terms and conditions.[note: 1] The letter and email dated 11 January 2013 have not been disclosed by the Plaintiff.

5       On 4 November 2020, the Plaintiff filed a writ against the Defendant, claiming for the following:

(a)     A principal sum of S$188,471.11 as payment for invoices issued by the Plaintiff in relation to the carriage of goods as set out at paragraphs 29 and 33 of the Statement of Claim (the “Plaintiff’s 2020 Invoices”). The Plaintiff’s 2020 Invoices and supporting documents are exhibited to the Plaintiff’s Affidavit filed in support of SUM 2196 dated 1 June 2021.

(b)     In the alternative, the Plaintiff sought payment of a reasonable sum for the services rendered to the Defendant on a quantum meruit basis.

(c)     Contractual interest pursuant to clause 3 of a document titled “Credit T&C” dated 11 January 2013[note: 2] (the “Plaintiff’s 2013 Document”).

6       From November 2020 onwards, the parties engaged in negotiations, attempting to reach a settlement.

7       On 8 July 2021, the Defendant paid the principal amounts for the Plaintiff’s invoices amounting to S$219,126.11. Proof of the payment is exhibited in the Defendant’s Supporting Affidavit of one Mr Darren Seet, the Director of the Defendant for SUM 4011 dated 6 October 2021.[note: 3] The payment is not disputed and in fact, the Plaintiff acknowledged this in his Reply dated 23 July 2021 at paragraphs 1 and 2:

1.    We can confirm that payment of SGD 219,126.11 has been received on the 17/07/21. This amount is solely the outstanding invoices – the amount of the current case and additional invoices some noted in the claim as well.

2.    Per the attached chart supplied by the defendant in 01/21, we believe this amount makes up most if not all the outstanding amount owed by the defendant the plaintiff. However, all rights are reserved should additional outstanding invoices arise in the future.

8       On 30 July 2021, the Defendant sought and obtained leave to amend the Defence. The Defence (Amendment No. 1) was filed on 13 August 2021.

9       On 27 August 2021, the Defendant sought and obtained leave to amend a typographical error in the Defendant (Amendment No. 1) (i.e. the misspelling of the date “13th day of August 2020” which should have been 13 August 2021). The Defence (Amendment No. 2) was filed on 4 September 2021.

10     SUM 2196 (i.e. the Plaintiff’s S/O Application) was fixed on 12 October 2021.

11     On 6 October 2021, SUM 4011 (the Defendant’s S/O Application) was filed. The hearing date on 12 October 2021 was vacated and the hearing date for both SUM 2196 and SUM 4011 was fixed on 17 November 2021.

12     At the hearing for SUM 2196 and SUM 4011 on 17 November 2021, the Plaintiff produced, for the first time, the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document[note: 4] in support of his claim for contractual interest. In light of this development, parties were ordered to file further affidavit and submissions.

13     Pursuant to the Defendant’s request seeking an extension of timelines and hearing date dated 30 November 2021, the hearing of SUM 4011 and SUM 2196 were refixed to 17 December 2021.

14     However, the Plaintiff did not appear at the hearing on 17 December 2021 and the DR struck out the Plaintiff’s claim in its entirety and ordered the Plaintiff to pay the Defendant costs.

15     The Plaintiff subsequently claimed that he had no notice of the hearing on 17 December 2021. The DR then refixed the hearing of SUM 4011 and SUM 2196 to 16 February 2022.

The decision of the learned DR

16     At the hearing on 16 February 2022, the DR ruled that:

(a)     The Plaintiff’s S/O Application (in SUM 2196) was to be dismissed with no order as to costs.

(b)     The Defendant’s S/O Application (in SUM 4011) was to be partially allowed with no order as to costs. In this respect, the Plaintiff’s claim for the principal sum and alternative claim for payments on a quantum meruit basis were struck out. The Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest was preserved.

17     In DC/RA 17/2022 (“RA 17”), the Plaintiff appealed against the entirety of the DR’s decision in relation to SUM 2916 and SUM 4011.

18     In DC/RA 18/2022 (“RA 18”), the Defendant appealed against the DR’s decision on a narrower basis in relation to: (i) the DR’s decision not to strike out the Plaintiff’s claim in SUM 4011 in its entirety, and in particular, for contractual interest; and (ii) the cost orders in respect of SUM 2196 and SUM 4011 save for the cost order amounting to $1,000.00 imposed on the Plaintiff for his absence from the hearing for SUM 2196 and SUM 4011 on 17 December 2021 without good reason.

19     Alternatively, for leave to be granted to the Defendant to amend the Defence (Amendment No. 2).

The Main Issues before the Court

20     The main issues that arose from RAs 17 and 18 were as follows:

(a)     Whether the Plaintiff’s claim for the principal sum should be struck out;

(b)     Whether the Plaintiff’s claim for quantum meruit should be struck out;

(c)     Whether the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest should be struck out;

(d)     If the conclusions to (a) to (b) above is in the negative, whether the Defence should be struck out in relation to the Plaintiff’s subsisting claim(s);

(e)     Whether the Defendant should be granted leave to amend the Defence;

(f)     Whether the costs orders for SUM 2196 and 4011 were rightly imposed by the DR.

The Law on Striking Out

21     The law on striking out pleadings is trite. In general, the threshold for striking out is a high one, and it will typically only be invoked in plain and obvious cases. The claim must be obviously unsustainable, the pleadings unarguably bad, and it must be impossible, not just improbable, for the claim to succeed before the court will strike it out (see Manas Kumar Ghosh v MSI Ship Management Pte Ltd and others [2021] 4 SLR 935 at [43] to [44]).

22     Under O 18 r 19(1) of the Rules of Court (Cap 322, R 5, 2014 Rev Ed) (“ROC”), the court may strike out a pleading if:

(a)     It discloses no reasonable cause of action or defence, as the case may be;

(b)     It is scandalous, frivolous, or vexatious;

(c)     It may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial of the action; or

(d)     It is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court.

23     In Gabriel Peter & Partners (suing as a firm) v Wee Chong Jin & Ors [1997] 3 SLR(R) 649 [at para. 18], it was held that the power of striking out should only be invoked in plain and obvious cases. It should not be exercised by a minute and protracted examination of the documents and facts of the case in order to see if the plaintiff really has a cause of action. The practice of the courts has been that where an application for striking out involves a lengthy and serious argument, the court should decline to proceed with the argument unless, not only does it have doubts as to the soundness of the pleading, but in addition, it is satisfied that striking out will obviate the necessity for a trial or reduce the burden of preparing for a trial.

24     The guiding principle in determining what a “reasonable cause of action” is under O 18 r 19(1)(a) connotes a cause of action which has some chance of success when only the allegations in the pleadings are considered. As long as the statement of claim discloses some cause of action, or raises some question fit to be decided at the trial, the mere fact that the caser is weak and is not likely to succeed is no ground for striking it out: Gabriel Peter [at para. 21].

25     I shall first deal with the striking out application of the Plaintiff’s claims.

The Plaintiff’s claims

A.   Whether the Plaintiff’s claim for the principal sum should be struck out

26     It was not disputed that the Defendant has paid the principal sum arising from the Plaintiff’s invoices amounting to S$219,126.11 on 8 July 2021. This has also been acknowledged by the Plaintiff at paragraphs 1 and 2 of his Reply dated 22 July 2021, where it was confirmed that payment of SGD 219,126.11 has been received on the 17 July 2021, and that this amount was solely the outstanding invoices – the amount of the current case and additional invoices some noted in the claim as well. I further note that the Plaintiff also stated that they believe this amount made up most, if not all the outstanding amount owed by the Defendant to the Plaintiff.

27     Hence, given that the Defendant has already paid the principal sum for the Plaintiff’s invoices, the Plaintiff’s claim is obviously unsustainable (be it legally or factually), and quite clearly, it would be frivolous and vexatious to allow the Plaintiff to procced on this claim.

28     I note that the Plaintiff’s argument in relation to the principal claim appears to be that the Defendant has no justification in making the delayed payment on 8 July 2021. However, this is irrelevant in so far as the principal sum is concerned, as any delay in paying the principal sums would be a consideration in the award of costs.

29     Accordingly, I was of the view that the Plaintiff’s claim for the principal sum was rightly struck out by the DR.

B.   Whether the Plaintiff’s alternative claim for quantum meruit should be struck out

30     I next turn to the question of whether the Plaintiff’s alternative claim for quantum meruit should be struck out.

31     A claim for a reasonable sum for work done based on the doctrine of quantum meruit in a situation where there was no contract (whether express or implied) found its source in what was known as quasi-contract. It is now generally acknowledged that the doctrine of quantum meruit is more appropriately classified under a claim in restitution or (perhaps more accurately) unjust enrichment (see Eng Chiet Shoong and others v Cheong Soh Chin and other and another appeal [2016] 4 SLR 728 at [32] & [33]).

32     In the present case, the Plaintiff asserted that parties have entered into the Agreement subject to the terms and conditions contained in the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document. As such, the Agreement read together with the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document would govern the contractual relationship between the Plaintiff and the Defendant and provide for the payment of the Plaintiff’s services at the material time (see Statement of Claim at para 3). Therefore, it follows from the Plaintiff’s assertion that contractual principles should govern and that the law of restitution for unjust enrichment has no part to play in this matter.

33     Accordingly, I was of the view that the Agreement precludes the Plaintiff’s claim for services rendered to the Defendant on a quantum meruit basis. The Plaintiff’s invoices provided for the payment for work done in relation to the carriage of various goods. Any payment would be governed by the four corners of the Agreement and its corollary terms and conditions (whether is it the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document as contended by the Plaintiff, or the EnerMech’s Terms and Conditions as contended by the Defendant that applies), which stipulates the terms of payment.

34     Given these circumstances and considering that the claim for principal sum has been paid by the Defendant, the Plaintiff could not instead opt to claim these payments from the Defendant on a quantum meruit basis.

35     Accordingly, I was of the view that the DR has rightly struck out the Plaintiff’s claim for quantum meruit.

C.   Whether the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interests should be struck out

36     I now turn to the question of whether the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interests should be struck out.

37     In this respect, the main area of dispute centred on the applicable terms which governed the parties’ contractual relationship – i.e. whether the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document or the EnerMech’s Standard Terms and Conditions as stipulated in the Defendant’s purchase orders applies.

38     The Defendant contended that the “Plaintiff’s claim in contractual interest is clearly unmeritorious on the facts and the law and doomed to fail”. In support of the argument to strike out the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest on the grounds that it is frivolous or vexatious under O 18 r 19(1)(b) of the ROC, the Defendant raised several arguments as to why clause 3 of the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document was unenforceable.

39     For reference, clause 3 of the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document provides as follows:

40     Having considered the said clause in question, I was of the view that the arguments raised by the Defendant do not satisfy the high threshold of striking out the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest based on the evidence before the court. Quite the contrary, the issues raised by the Defendant in this case merit a hearing.

41     First, the Defendant alleged that the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document does not substantiate the Plaintiff’s claim as it does not provide any indication that it relates to the Plaintiff’s 2020 Invoices, nor does it identify which of the Defendant’s invoices it pertains to.

42     However, in my view, it would be unrealistic for the document to detail information of subsequent invoices issued after the Defendant has contracted for the Plaintiff’s services. It is also clearly stated that the terms in the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document “apply to all shipments from this date forward and remain in place at all times until otherwise amended and agreed to by both parties in writing”. The Defendant’s signature is also appended on the document.

43     Secondly, whether the Plaintiff had, by his conduct, waived his right to enforce the contractual interest clause (i.e. clause 3) found in the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document raises a triable issue.

44     The Plaintiff argued that the terms contained in the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document are the “only terms and conditions that were in effect to cover the work performed by the Plaintiff and amounts owed by the Defendant for the said work are the Plaintiffs terms and conditions which are acknowledged and agreed to by both parties and have remined (sic) in effect”.

45     In contrast, the Defendant argued that there would have been a waiver by estoppel of the Plaintiff’s right to enforce the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document on account of the Plaintiff’s inaction and “lack of assertiveness” to enforce such a right in relation to invoices issued between 2018 to 2020 based on several course of events.

46     For a start, the Plaintiff did not demand for payment of interests despite the Defendant repeatedly taking about 60 days to pay for invoices since 2018. Further, the Plaintiff has never once mentioned the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document in any correspondence. The Defendant had relied on the Plaintiff’s inaction and continued to take 60 days to make payment for invoices, labouring under this mistaken belief that it was entitled to in accordance with Clause 10.4 of EnerMech’s Terms and Conditions.

47     The Plaintiff would have known of his own right to claim interest on any unpaid invoices after the credit term of 30 days has lapsed. The Plaintiff would also have been aware that the Defendant had been under this belief that the Plaintiff would not pursue his rights under clause 3 as evidenced by the Defendant’s subsequent act of making payments long after 30 days, in breach of the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document. Yet, despite this knowledge, the Plaintiff continued providing the Defendant its services and did not once bring up clause 3 of the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document or his right to demand for interest on payments exceeding 30 days.

48     Quite clearly, all these points appear to demonstrate that the Plaintiff has consistently given the impression that clause 3 of the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document is inapplicable, and it is at least arguable that the Plaintiff has waived his rights by his inaction in not enforcing interest payments he would have been ostensibly entitled to under the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document.

49     Nevertheless, any eventual finding as to whether the Plaintiff has indeed waived his contractual rights (assuming that the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document is applicable) should be decided only by a close and careful examination of all the relevant circumstances and factual matrix. At the striking out stage of the proceedings, it would not be appropriate for the court to render any conclusive finding on whether there has been a waiver or otherwise based solely on affidavit evidence alone, especially on the issue of whether the Plaintiff simply stood by knowing full well that the Defendant was labouring under a mistake as to his rights, which is not plain and obvious based solely on the evidence presently tendered before the court.

50     Thirdly, whether clause 3 in the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document would constitute a penalty clause and would not be enforceable similarly raises a triable issue.

51     The Defendant contended that clause 3 in the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document constitutes a penalty clause due to its large quantum of S$75,604.40 due as at the date of payment (i.e. 8 July 2021) as compared to the statutory rate of 5.33% per annum which would only amount to S$8,927.36. Further, the contractual interest is not based on any estimate of loss and is calculated arbitrarily.

52     In so far as the total amount of contractual interest is said to be a large sum, that in and of itself does not invariably mean that clause 3 is a penalty clause. Likewise, the comparison to the statutory interest rate of 5.33% per annum is immaterial in determining whether a clause is a penalty clause as it would be open to parties to decide upon an interest rate to apply to their commercial dealings. Ultimately, whether a clause is penal hinges upon whether it provides a genuine pre-estimate of loss.

53     The next argument to consider is that EnerMech’s Terms and Conditions have been incorporated by the previous course of dealing between the parties or has in fact superseded the terms in the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document.

54     Suffice for me to note, these are matters which are more appropriately dealt with at trial, after the process of discovery has been exhausted, and where the evidence would have to be tested at trial. In my view, the points raised by the Defendant are not supported by clear evidence to show that high threshold of striking out has been met and that it is plain and obvious that the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interests is unsustainable.

55     Upon careful analysis, many of the issues raised by the Defendant are arguably of the nature and character which would involve a minute and protracted examination of the documents and the facts of the case (see Gabriel Peter at [18]). I am mindful that engaging in such an exercise could effectively be usurping the proper function of the trial court, and conducting a trial of the case in Chambers on affidavits only, without discovery and without the evidence tested by cross-examination, and ultimately, deprive a litigant of the opportunity to have his claim tried by the court.

56     Accordingly, I was of the view that the DR was correct in not striking out the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest.

D.   Whether leave to amend the Defence should be granted

57     The Defendant has also submitted that if the court is not minded to strike out the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest, leave should be granted to amend the Defence. Having carefully considered the matter, I was of the view that the Defendant should be allowed to amend its Defence.

58     Under O 20 r 5 of the ROC, the court has a wide discretion to grant leave to amend pleadings. At the end of the day, the most important question which the court must ask itself is, are the ends of justice served by allowing the proposed amendment. In this respect, pleadings should not be used as a means to punish a party for his errors or the errors of his solicitors. All relevant circumstances should be considered by the court before it exercises its discretion whether it would allow an amendment.

59     Considering that the stage of proceedings in the present case is relatively early since trial has not commenced, and any amendment to the Defence at this stage would not result in significant prejudice (if any) to the Plaintiff, in a way which could not be compensated by costs, I was of the view that the Defendant should therefore be allowed to amend its Defence.

60     While the Defendant alleged that the disclosure of the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document on 17 November 2021 was belated, the salient terms and conditions from the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document were already reproduced in the Statement of Claim at paragraph 4. As such, any alleged prejudice suffered by the Defendant is minimal as the disclosure of the said document is a matter that would be left to discovery.

61     I shall next deal with the striking out of the defence.

The Defence

Whether the Defence should be struck out

62     Given that only the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest remains, the question then is whether the Plaintiff has shown that it is plain and obvious that the Defence to the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest is unsustainable.

63     In this respect, the main thrust of the Defence in relation to the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest is that (i) clause 3 of the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document is an unenforceable penalty clause; or (ii) the contractual relationship between the Plaintiff and Defendant is governed by the EnerMech’s Standard Terms and Conditions, which contains an entire agreement clause.

64     Suffice for me to note, the evidence provided by the Plaintiff did not establish the high threshold of proving that the Defence is plainly and obviously unsustainable. The Plaintiff has not adequately addressed the applicability of EnerMech’s Standard Terms and Conditions aside from the fact that these terms were contained in purchase orders issued “ex post facto”.

65     In my assessment, the Defendant would clearly have an arguable case if parties had in fact agreed that the EnerMech’s Standard Terms and Conditions would henceforth apply to the parties’ contractual relationship moving forward. That would certainly revoke the terms under the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document and obviate the Plaintiff’s right to any contractual interest. The evidence tendered by the Plaintiff is not unequivocal to show that parties have only agreed to be bound by the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document to the exclusion of the EnerMech’s Standard Terms and Conditions.

66     At this juncture, the evidence of the parties’ intentions and the circumstances surrounding the Agreement and the Plaintiff’s 2013 document, as well as the basis to support the Plaintiff’s claim in contractual interest is unclear. Based on the affidavit evidence presently submitted to this court, the EnerMech’s Standard Terms and Conditions are in fact specified in the Defendant’s purchase orders issued to the Plaintiff.

67     Quite clearly, whether the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document or EnerMech’s Standard Terms and Conditions apply to the contractual relationship between parties, and the details of any such agreement would be issues of fact which should be more appropriately tested at trial. It would not be appropriate for this court to speculate as to the parties’ commercial dealings without any basis on which to do so.

68     Accordingly, I was of the view that the DR was correct to rule that the Defence in respect of the claim for contractual interest could not be summarily determined and ought not to be struck out.

Costs

69     Having decided that the DR’s decisions should not be disturbed, I was of the view that the substantive appeal for both RAs 17 and 18 should be dismissed. I next turn to the issue of costs decided by the DR.

70     Costs are entirely at the discretion of the court. An appellate court would be slow to interfere with the DR’s costs order and the exercise of his discretion unless he has erred in law or in principle or he has taken into account some matter which he should not have taken into account or has omitted to take into account some matter which he should have taken into account, or if the appellate court is of the opinion that the DR’s decision is plainly wrong and therefore must have been reached by a faulty assessment of the weight of the various factors which he has had to take into account (see Goh Chok Tong v Jeyaretnam Joshua Benjamin and another action [1998] 2 SLR(R) 971 at [60]).

71     As a preliminary point, the Plaintiff broadly relied on O 59 r 18A of the ROC Court to assert that he should be awarded costs for acting as a litigant-in-person. O 59 r 18A provides that on a taxation of the costs of a litigant-in-person, there may be allowed such costs as would reasonably compensate the litigant for the time expended by him, together with all expenses reasonably incurred. Therefore, if a Plaintiff-in-person is successful in his claims, he is entitled to a claim against the Defendant for compensatory costs under O 59 r 18A for the time expended by him for the action, together with other reasonable expenses (see Aldabe Fermin v Standard Chartered Bank [2010] 3 SLR 722 at [130]).

72     However, O 59 r 18A does not entitle the litigant-in-person to costs as of right. The fundamental principle that costs follow the event is not displaced by O 59 r 18A (see Ong Wui Teck (personal representative of the estate of Chew Chen Chin, deceased) v Ong Wui Swoon and another and another appeal [2019] SGCA 61 at [74]).

73     I shall deal with the respective costs orders made by the DR.

Costs in relation to SUM 2196 – the Plaintiff’s S/O Application

74     The DR declined to make any costs orders for SUM 2196 (which was dismissed) for the following reasons:

(a)     In relation to the claim for principal sum, the Plaintiff should be entitled to costs from the filing of SUM 2196 on 1 June 2021 to before payment of the principal sum was made by the Defendant on 8 July 2021. On the other hand, the Defendant should be entitled to costs thereafter, since the Plaintiff ought to have withdrawn SUM 2196 in respect of that claim having received payment.

(b)     As for the claim for contractual interest, the Plaintiff should be entitled to costs for the period between the filing of SUM 2196 on 1 June 2021 and the filing of the amendments to the Defence on 13 August 2021. While the Defendant should be entitled to costs thereafter, as the Plaintiff should have withdrawn SUM 2196 by then after perusing the Defendant’s amended Defence.

75     The Defendant argued that it is entitled to more costs than the Plaintiff because, inter alia, the Plaintiff’s entitlement to costs is for a shorter period than the Defendant and that it had to file an affidavit and submission, and had to make arguments.

76     While the period in which the Defendant is entitled to costs seems to be lengthier, it is not the case that the Defendant has spent a significant amount of time and effort in its preparation of SUM 2196 – both parties have similarly filed an affidavit and submission for SUM 2196.

77     All considered, I was of the view that the Defendant has not raised any substantial grounds to dispute the DR’s decision in not awarding costs to either party for SUM 2196.

Costs in relation to SUM 4011 – the Defendant’s S/O Application

78     As for the cost orders made by the DR in relation to SUM 4011, the DR declined to make any costs orders for SUM 4011 (which was dismissed) for the following reasons:

(a)     In relation to the claim for principal sum, the Plaintiff should be entitled to costs before the Defendant made payment of the principal sum on 8 July 2021. However, the Defendant should be entitled to costs thereafter.

(b)     As for the claim for contractual interests, the Defendant should pay the Plaintiff costs. The Defendant only partially succeeded in SUM 4011 as the Plaintiff’s claim for contractual interest was still subsisting. The Defendant also failed in its bid to stay the proceedings on the basis of forum non conveniens.

(c)     The Defendant had without good reason filed SUM 4011 late in the day on 6 October 2021, resulting in the vacation of the first hearing date on 12 October 2021.

79     The Defendant contended that the belated filing of its S/O Application was due to the Plaintiff not producing any document to support its claim in contractual interest and as such, “the Defendant was entitled to have that portion of the claim struck out”. This was despite the Notice to Produce filed on 29 July 2021, requesting that the Plaintiff disclose the document that contains the Terms and Conditions referred to in paragraph 3 of the Statement of Claim.

80     However, in my view, the fact that the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document was not disclosed earlier did not constitute as good reasons for the late filing of SUM 4011. The Defendant was aware of the salient terms and conditions relied upon by the Plaintiff as reproduced at paragraph 3 of the Statement of Claim dated 26 November 2020. The Defendant would have ample time to assess its case and to decide whether to apply for a striking out application well before 6 October 2021.

81     Even if the Defendant required time or more information to assess the merits of its case, the Defendant could have applied for an order for production for inspection under O 24 r 11(1) or (2) of the ROC after receiving no response to its Notice to Produce filed on 29 July 2021. The Defendant did not do so. Having chosen to file SUM 4011 late in the day, the Defendant should not lay the blame on the Plaintiff.

82     In short, neither the Plaintiff nor the Defendant has shown that the DR was plainly wrong in his costs order.

Costs of $1000/- for the Defendant for the rehearing of SUM 2196 and SUM 4011

83     Next, I shall deal with the issue of whether the cost order of $1,000/- that was imposed against the Plaintiff should be overturned.

84     Having carefully reviewed the DR’s Minute Sheet on 16 February 2022, I was of the view that the cost order was rightly imposed on the Plaintiff for his absence from the hearing on 17 December 2021 (“17 December 2021 Hearing”) without good reason.

85     The DR had disagreed that the Plaintiff’s absence was necessitated by the Defendant. Further, the Plaintiff clearly had notice of the 17 December 2021 Hearing, and it could not be seriously contended that he was not informed of the hearing date when he had replied on the same day to the Defendant’s email of 1 December 2021 informing the refixing of the hearing.

86     Notwithstanding the Plaintiff’s assertions that he needed to attend matters with MOM, he had not informed the Defendant beforehand that he was unavailable for the hearing, nor had he sought an adjournment beforehand on account of his unavailability.

87     Accordingly, there was nothing to indicate that the DR considered matters which he should not have, or that he was plainly wrong in his costs order. Simply put, there was no basis to interfere with the DR’s decision to order the Plaintiff to pay costs of $1,000/- to the Defendant for his absence and the rehearing of the Summonses.

Costs of the RAs 17 and 18 of 2022

88     In respect of the costs to be awarded for RAs 17 & 18, in the Defendant’s Cost Submissions dated 28 October 2022, the Defendant submitted that no order as to costs should be made in respect of the RAs as both parties have not succeeded in their appeals and that the decision of the DR on 16 February 2022 has been affirmed in toto.

89     On the other hand, in the Plaintiff’s Submissions for Costs dated 28 October 2022, the Plaintiff claimed cost of $60,000/- based on a conservative number of hours spent and expenses incurred. Essentially, the Plaintiff repeated many of his arguments and allegations against the Defendant for the striking out application and submitted that he had expended considerable time and costs during the hearings to address the many issues raised by the Defendant. Further, the Plaintiff submitted that the Defendant sought to prejudice the Plaintiff’s case and circumvent court procedures, including raising a new defence which was not pleaded. As such, the Plaintiff submitted that the Defendant should be made to bear the costs for the entire action surrounding the RAs, which were due solely to their own actions and were forced upon the Plaintiff.

90     Given that both the Plaintiff’s appeal and the Defendant’s appeal have been dismissed, and that the Court has affirmed the decision of the DR given on 16 February 2022, I agreed with the Defendant’s submission that costs should follow the event. Accordingly, I was of the view that it would be appropriate to make no order as to the costs for the RAs.

Costs of Amendment of Defence

91     In respect of the Defence’s application to amend the Defence, which leave was granted, there could be no serious dispute that the Defendant would be liable to pay costs of the application, and any costs consequent to the amendments. Order 20 rule 5(1) of the ROC provides that the court may allow an amendment “on such terms as to costs or otherwise as may be just and in such manner (if any) as it may direct”. It is trite that the party amending would be required to pay the costs of and occasioned by the amendment (see Singapore Court Practice 2021 at paragraph 20/8/9).  

92     The Defendant submitted that the costs of the amended Defence should be deferred and addressed by a Pre-Trial Conference Judge after the extent of consequential amendments (if any) by the Plaintiff are disclosed, in essence for costs to be reserved.

93     However, I was of the view that this is unnecessary and costs for the amended Defence and consequential amendments (if any) could be ordered at this stage of the proceedings, in the round. Considering the limited scope of the amended Defence, and that leave to amend the Defence was allowed at the hearing of the RAs, a deferment of costs to be assessed by the PTC Judge would not be warranted.

94     As for the quantum of costs payable, I note that the latest amendments to the Defence introduced new legal and factual defences (i.e. waiver by estoppel and that the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document was superseded), and they were alluded to in the Defendant’s written submissions for RAs 17 and 18. However, the salient terms and conditions from the Plaintiff’s 2013 Document were in fact already reproduced in the Statement of Claim. The amended pleadings to the Defence did not technically require disclosure of the 2013 Document.

95     In my assessment, costs of around $1,000/- would be adequate and I ordered the Defendant to pay the Plaintiff cost to be fixed at $1,000/-.

Conclusion

96     In the final analysis and in my judgment, the DR’s decision for RAs 17 and 18 should not be disturbed. Both the Plaintiff and the Defendant have not shown that their respective striking out applications bear sufficient merit to the extent that it warrants overruling the DR’s decisions.

97     Accordingly, I affirmed the decision of the DR and dismissed both RA 17 and 18 of 2022.

98     The Plaintiff being dissatisfied has filed an appeal against the decision and the costs order. The Defendant has not filed any appeal.
